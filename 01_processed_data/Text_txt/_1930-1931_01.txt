THE LIGHT THROWN ON ANCIENT
CHINESE HISTORY BY RECENT
ARCHEOLOGICAL DISCOVERIES
By DUDLEY BUXTON, M.A.
School of Anthropology, Oxford University
The archæological discoveries discussed in this paper
are due very largely to the energy and enthusiasm of
Dr. Andersson, who, during his tenure of the position of
Geological Adviser to the Chinese Government, discovered,
while investigating the recent geological history of China,
remains of the early civilizations discussed below. He was
able to communicate his enthusiasm to the Directors of
the Chinese Geological Survey, who sanctioned the produc-
tion of a magnificent series of Memoirs (Palæontologica
Sinica, Series D) which contain accounts of the various
excavations. My excuse for bringing them to the notice
of those whose interests lie mainly in sinological studies
is that the human remains associated with the archæo-
logical deposits have been shewn by Dr. Black to be of
the same type as modern Chinese.
It is unnecessary in this context to discuss except very
briefly the earliest archæological finds which have been
made in China. It will be sufficient to say that the work
of a small band of students, notably Pères Teilhard and
Licent, have shewn that man was living in China in
Paleolithic times, almost certainly before the great plain
was covered with the loess which gives it its present
remarkable form and equally remarkable fertility. The
Neolithic age is beginning to be better known, but at
present there are big gaps, and the Chalcolithic civiliza-
tion, which is the subject of this paper, appears suddenly
from beginnings which at least in China itself are at
present quite unknown. It is a culture which, with
certain exceptions, did not appear to know of the use
of metals, but which at the same time attained a very
high degree of civilization. The sites which have so

remains, differing somewhat from the rest, have been
found all along the northern border of Kansu, at Fuku
in Shensi, at Paote in Shansi and as far east as Sha
Kuo T'un in Fengtien province, a series of discoveries
which suggest that the culture certainly extended along
the whole of the northern border of China. Further to
the south only one province has at present yielded any
results, namely Honan, in which four sites have been
explored-Chih Kou Chai and Ching Wang Chai in Ho Yin
district and Yang Shao T'un and Pu Chao Chai in Mien
Chih district. There is sufficent difference in the sites
to suggest that, even at present, when the subject can
hardly be yet said to have been more than scratched,
fairly representative discoveries have been made.
The finds in Kansu include both the remains of habit-
ations and cemeteries. Most of the work done so far
has been in the west of the province, particularly round
the city of Lanchow. The pottery from this region is
abundant and well made, especially those pieces which
have been excavated from cemeteries. On the other
hand implements are comparatively rare and of poor work-
manship. It is on the pottery therefore that Andersson
has based his preliminary classification of a number
of sub-periods, of which the upper, and therefore later
ones, belong to the Copper or early Bronze Age, while the
three lowest appear to correspond culturally, not neces-
sarily in time, with the sites from other parts of China.
As elsewhere the domestic pig, so typical of China to-day,
occurs very commonly. The burial customs of these
people are not uninteresting. A large number of the
bones had been covered with red ochre, a custom which
is widely spread, and which occurs in Europe even as
early as the Palæolithic period. Most of the bodies were
laid in an extended position, with the feet to the south,
but in two graves the skeletons lay on the left side in
a contracted position. Whether this was due to accident,
owing to the grave being too small or whether it was
done on purpose does not appear in the reports.
The excavations in Fengtien were made in a small
cave, which lay close to the railway, about half-way
between Tientsin and Mukden. The cave, which was
twenty feet long and half that breadth, appeared to have
been occupied for a considerable time, as the deposits were

over six feet in depth and consisted of layers of charcoal
alternating with bands of clay. The bands of charcoal
were very productive of human remains. About half-way
down there was a layer of human bones, most of them
broken and burnt, amongst which Black counted the
remains of at least forty-five individuals.
In contrast to this small site the inhabited area at
Yang Shao T'un in Honan was very extensive, being about
600 yards long and 500 yards broad. Under a thin
surface stratum of ploughland there were fertile deposits
ten feet thick. The original surface beneath the deposits
had been excavated into large pits. A few graves were
found but most of the site consisted of the debris of a
large settlement. In general culture there is no reason
to believe that the Honan sites differed from the Feng-
tien site.
The pottery discovered on these sites naturally forms
the most important guide to the date and culture of these
ancient peoples. Although some of the wares are crude,
most of the material is of considerable beauty and the
skill shewn in the manufacture provides ample evidence
that we are dealing with a relatively advanced civilization.
The crude ware is not unlike that which has been found
by Torii in Manchuria and may possibly represent a
survival of a local ware for purely domestic purposes,
side by side with the more beautiful other ware introduced
from elsewhere. This other ware is very fine and is
usually decorated with painted designs. Sometimes the
paint has been applied directly on to the finished surface
of the pot. In other cases this surface has first been
treated with a clay wash, technically called a "slip."
The pots are made of loess clay, a raw material
available over all northern China. As this material
cannot be obtained south of the Yangtze it would appear
likely that if a similar culture is ever found in the south
the wares would differ in technical details. Little stress,
however, must be laid on this point, as the potters have
used the most obvious material which came to hand.
The clay has been thoroughly cleaned and freed from
all impurities and then probably worked into a sort of
paste. Such a paste before firing is usually too weak
to support the weight of a pot, which would fall to pieces
during the making. The clay has therefore been strength-
ened with a small addition of quartzite or some flinty
material. Probably some iron-oxide was added to im-
prove the colouring, but some of the differences in colour-

ing are almost certainly due to differences in firing, which
it seems as the potters were sufficiently masters of their
craft to be able to control. In any case the differences
in colour do not seem to be of importance for classification.
Red and black paints were used to add the ornament,
either both together, or red alone. Most of the pottery
is wheel-made.
Although in most cases only fragments have been
discovered, the general form of the pottery has been classi-
fied fairly accurately. The unpainted vases include a
series of tripod jars, and pots with narrow necks of a type
probably not unlike the water pots used over much of the
East to-day. Others seem to have had pointed bottoms.
The painted pottery has usually been made in the shape
of bowls of various types, but also includes a series of
pear-shaped vases.
The ornamentation on the painted pots is of great
interest and importance. In the Kansu ware there are
more or less realistic drawings of animals. The ware
from the other sites is ornamented with conventional
designs only, although the Kansu ware also includes such
conventional patterns as the continuous and the inter-
rupted meander pattern. The ornaments include spiral
bands or lines, triangles, sometimes of fantastically
exaggerated form, discs-that is, circles which have been
filled in with solid colour-plain circles, concentric circles
and so on. The most characteristic ornament is the
spiral, but hardly less characteristic is a kind of lattice
pattern which is used either to fill in a triangle or as a
band round a pot.
The geographical distribution of these forms and of
the types of ornament is extremely instructive (see Map).
The tripod-like vessel occurs as early as the first city of
Troy in the West and is not uncommon in south-eastern
Europe. It has been suggested that the fragment dis-
covered by Torii and others in Manchuria and elsewhere
in the Far East may really consist of tripod legs. If this
is really the case it is of importance, as the tripod pot
would have a much wider distribution than the other
painted forms. Little can be judged from the geograph-
ical distribution of the pots with painted bottoms, as
they occur in many places-India, Troy, Egypt and else-
where. A type with a perforated bottom occurs in Troy,
Persia, Egypt, and elsewhere. The small bowls are like
pots which have been found in the earlier strata at Anau
and Susa. Further, although there are differences in the

Painted pottery was made over a wide area in south-
eastern Europe and western Asia at a date which was
probably between the fourth and third millenium B.C.,
though there are wide divergencies on the point of date.
There can be little reason to doubt that the Chinese
painted pottery is of the same class, or that this type of
pottery had a wide distribution in Eurasia during the
period of transition between the Stone and the Bronze
Ages. Although it is an immense journey from the topless
Towers of Ilium to the Yellow River, there have been
trade routes right across Asia from very early times,
probably even in the Neolithic period, and it is possible
that the deserts which now constitute such a formidable
barrier may then have been more easily traversed. On
the other hand the differences in the design of the wares
have led some archeologists to doubt whether the Chinese
finds should definitely be associated with the western
painted pottery, which itself falls into more than one
group.
In the extreme West painted pottery has been report-
ed from Tripolje in south-east Russia, from Petreny
in Bessarabia, from Schepenitz in Bukovina, and from
various other sites in Moldavia, Galicia and Transylvania.
It extends as far south as Thessally. This area, the
region of what is usually known as the Danube-Dnieper
culture, is characterized by a pottery to which the Chinese
wares bear a striking resemblance both in the forms and
in the types of decoration.
In western Asia also close parallels can be found.
Here there are two separate areas, a northern and a
southern. In the north, at Anau near Askabad in Tur-
kestan, Pumpelly found a series of different cultures of
which the fourth was of Iron Age date. The earliest,
however, is closest to the Chinese, for copper was here
relatively rare and the pots, which were abundant, were of
the polished and painted and decorated with geometrical
designs. Lattice designs are found here as in China,
and in the straight line patterns there seems to be a
close agreement; at Anau, however, curves are very

rare and circles do not occur, both of which features are
extremely characteristic of the Chinese wares. It should
be noted that the earliest Anau culture seems to have had
a fairly wide distribution, as similar finds have been
made in Transcaucasia (near Nachitzevan) but in this
case the culture appears to represent a highly developed
Copper Age, in which point it differs both from the similar
stratum at Anau and also from the Chinese sites.
The southern area in western Asia includes Persia,
Baluchistan, and Mesopotamia. The closest resemblance
to the Chinese wares, with the possible exception of some
pots from Mesopotamia, is found in the pottery from
Baluchistan, which Noetling has published. I am also
informed by Sir Aurel Stein that he has seen sherds in
Seistan which are extremely like the Chinese. Of the
Persian sites the finds from Tepe Mussain about a hundred
miles west of Susa shew the closest analogy with the
Chinese wares and here all the motives which appear on
the Chinese wares occur, except the meander, which it
will be remembered only occurs on the Kansu pottery,
This site is a little later than the earliest Susa finds
(Susa, I) which also shew a certain kinship to the Chinese
wares, but in all these cases we are dealing with a fully
developed Copper Age, not a transitional Chalcolithic stage
as in China.
Painted pottery is widely spread in Mesopotamia. It
has been found at Eridu by Campbell Thompson, near Ur
by Woolley, and at Kish and a neighbouring site by Lang-
don and other members of the Oxford (Weld) Field
Museum expedition. These finds serve to link up the other
discoveries from western Asia, and while conclusively
proving that there existed a closely linked culture extend-
ing from the Caucasus to the Persian Gulf, and into India,
certainly do not establish an absolutely complete link with
China. Striking as the resemblances are, there are certain
differences which have made many scholars reticent to
accept the unity between the eastern and the western
finds. It has been suggested that some of the Chinese
finds (except the later strata from Kansu) should be con-
sidered as belonging to a Stone Age culture and that China
is the original home of copper working, which spread
thence. This seems a very improbable hypothesis. Second-
ly, Frankfurt is inclined to believe that there is an ana-
logy between the Persian ware and some of the finds
from Kansu, but that the spiral design is a local Chinese
development, "due possibly to a technique acquired from

outside." Arne holds a similar opinion, but somewhat
more elaborate. He believes in two waves of immigration,
the first being either so early that it preceded a knowledge
of copper, or else rapidly lost touch with its original
home, so that copper did not come through by trade. The
second, he suggests, may have been more closely associated
with copper, and ascribes the copper finds in the graves at
Sining and Chenfan in Kansu to this later date.
Any definite opinion on the problem must wait until
further evidence is forthcoming. It seems clear, however,
that we must ascribe the culture of Chalcolithic culture in
China to the great culture so widely spread and charac-
terized by the presence of painted pottery. The differences
which are found in China may easily be explained as a
local development, due to isolation. It is not improbable
that the expansion of this culture eastwards may have
started relatively early (but see chronology later) and in
that case the difference between the Chinese wares and
those from the West could be explained on the grounds of
parallel evolution. It is clear that the expansion of culture
was, on the whole, not a definite racial movement.
The skeletons associated with the discoveries in China
belong to a racial type which closely coincides with the
modern Chinese inhabitants of the same area. It is more
than possible that the painted pottery culture was carried
by a certain number of alien invaders, but they were not
sufficient in numbers to impress their racial type on the
people of the land. While therefore there is every reason
to believe that there was a great migration of culture
right across Asia, there is no reason to suggest that it
was associated with a great racial migration. That some
racial elements may have been absorbed into the Far East
at this time is quite possible, but these elements did not
dominate the physical type in the way that the culture
entirely dominated the previous culture of the area.
seems further likely that considerable isolation took place
so that the Chalcolithic civilization of China was able to a
greater or lesser degree to evolve along lines which are
peculiar to itself.

The date of the Chalcolithic period in China is import-
ant in this connexion. Teilhard has recently drawn
attention to the discovery in Honan itself of inscriptions
written on ivory or shell which belong to the Yin or Shang
dynasty and may probably be dated at about 1760 B. C. If
this date be correct, obviously the painted pottery must be
considerably earlier, at least before the end of the third

millenium B.C., since that culture knows nothing of writ-
ing, nor of richly decorated bronzes. In any case the date
suggested for the end of the Chalcolithic period must be
received with a good deal of caution. It seems reasonable
to suggest that while such a date may apply to the Kansu
finds, the other discoveries must be considerably earlier.
The earliest possible date is even more difficult to
determine. It will depend to a large extent on the dates
ascribed to the painted pottery in the West, a subject on
which unfortunately there is by no means any general
agreement. Gordan Childe holds that in Thessally and
the Ukraine the date is probably not much before 3000
B.C., while in Elam and Egypt he would go back another
two thousand years. In Mesopotamia most scholars sug-
gest the middle of the fourth millenium B.C., but some
prefer a much earlier date. Child pessimistically says
that the earliest Chinese finds are undateable. Arne,
believing that the Chinese finds are more or less syn-
chronous with Susa, 8, I-an assumption not really justified
by the evidence-suggests a date about 3000 B.C. In spite
however, of the uncertainty of his analogy it would seem
that Arne's date was the most likely which has so far been
suggested. It must, however, never be forgotten that
there is a definite break both between the Chinese Palæo-
lithic and Neolithic and between the Neolithic and the
Chalcolithic. Nor have we any means of judging how long
it took for the culture to migrate across Asia. We must
allow a considerable period for this and also sufficient
time for it to develop in China itself. If we accept
Childe's earliest date for the existence of the culture in
Elam as 5000 B.C., two thousand years allows ample time
for the spread and development of the culture in China
itself, perhaps too long, but no further exactness seems
possible at present. The Kansu finds must in any case be
later, and it would seem probable that we have yet to find
another culture, possibly the precursor of the Chalcolithic
culture, possibly a transitional age between the Neolithic
and that culture.

that it is more or less directly descended from it. It
seems extremely probable that China, and indeed the

At present there is equal inconformity between the earli-
est Chinese culture and the painted ware culture and the
periods which precede the latter. All that we can say at
present is that the Chalcolithic culture is the earliest civili-
zation of China, as opposed to the crude culture of the
Neolithic period. We can also say that it was practised
by people who were physically akin to the modern
inhabitants of northern China. We cannot say one way
or the other whether modern Chinese culture developed
from it or not, nor does it at present throw any definite
light on Chinese origins. The brilliant work of Andersson
has, however, opened a new chapter of Chinese history.
How that chapter will link on to the subsequent chapters
must depend on future excavations, and it is to be hoped
that they will be as fortunate as these pioneering digs
have been.
