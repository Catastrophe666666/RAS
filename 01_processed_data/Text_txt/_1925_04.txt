THE LIFE AND TIMES OF CONFUCIUS
By J. WHITSED DOVEY
At this late day it is difficult to throw any fresh light
on a life which has been so closely and critically studied
as that of Confucius. The present article and the accom-
panying illustrations in particular are based on the series
of stone tablets at Ch'ufu in Shantung depicting the
principal scenes in the life of Confucius; and the descrip-
tion of the incidents given on each tablet has been com-
pared with the standard authorities on the subject in
order to present a fairly complete and ordered story of
the life of the Sage.
Some 570 years ars before the birth of Confucius, Wu
Wang the founder of the Chow dynasty had assigned the
various states of the empire, which comprised little more
than the valley of the Yellow River, to the relatives and
adherents of the reigning family. In this way the empire
really came to resemble the one united family to which
it has so often been likened; but ere long the family bonds
became looser and the nominal central government lost
all control over the various states. There were in the
days of Confucius thirteen principal states and a large
number of smaller ones. The rulers of these states, corres-
ponding to the barons in mediæval Europe, quarelled
and fought amongst themselves and barely acknowledged
allegiance even to the Emperor. Inside each state there
existed a similar condition of affairs on a smaller scale,
for the powerful hereditary families were continually
striving amongst themselves for the reins of power.
In the state of Lu in which Confucius was born we
find that three such families, the Chi, the Meng and
the Shu had reduced the nominal ruler to a condition of
virtual dependency. The general condition of the country
may be summed up in the words: "In those days there was
no king in China, and every prince did what was right in
his own eyes."

In spite of the political disorder a commencement
had been made in evolving an educational system and
each court had its historians and musicians. Progress
had been made too in arts and manufactures and the
bronze sacrificial vessels of these early periods show con-
siderable skill in their design and workmanship. Yet the
period was one of widespread suffering, for the people as
a whole were ground under the heel of the tax-gatherer,
were dragged from their fields and set to labour and fight
for their rulers in whom they had little interest and from
whom they derived no benefit.
The religious consciousness of the times was practi-
cally confined to a vague belief in Heaven, as a Supreme
Power, worshipped through the Emperor, the Son of
Heaven, as advocate for the people; and the worship of
ancestors. The latter was the special concern of each
family and individual and in addition we find worship of
the powers of nature residing in mountains, the soil,
woods, streams and the principal grains. The practice
of divination by means of the Pa Kwa, marked tortoise
shell, etc., was already in vogue and funeral ceremonies
had been elaborated till they were a real burden on the
survivors and in the case of heads of families or rulers of
states they called for both human and animal sacrifice,
varying with the dignity of the departed.
According to some genealogical tables the ancestry
of Confucius is traced back as far as 2637 B.C. but for our
purposes it is sufficient to note that one of his ancestors
had been made ruler of the state of Sung, embracing the
eastern part of the present province of Honan, in the
11th century B.C. by the second Emperor of the great
Chow dynasty. The branch of the family to which
Confucius belonged subsequently moved to the state of
Lu and here we read of the martial exploits of the father
of Confucius. Being a man of exceptional strength and
courage he had on one occasion raised and held up the
massive porticullis of the city of Peih-yang enabling his
fellow-soldiers to make good their escape from the trap
which had been laid for them.
The name Confucius, by which the Sage is known
throughout Western lands, is the latinised form of
"Kung-fu-tsz," which means literally "K'ung the
learned." As we proceed with the story of his life the
character and outlook of Confucius will reveal itself and
we shall gather some of the salient points of his teachings
which have made so profound an impression on the minds

of succeeding generations of Chinese. It is not our
purpose to discuss at length the philosophy he formulated
but rather to present some of the outstanding incidents
of a life which has appealed so strongly to the imagination

The father of Confucius, Shu Liang Ho, whose
personal qualities we have already noted had married
when young but his wife brought him only daughters-to
the number of nine in succession—and he still waited for
a son. Being now 70 years of age the old soldier sought a
secondary wife from the Yen family, marrying the
youngest daughter. The great disparity in age made it
very doubtful whether the long desired son would yet be
given to him and according to the legends either he or she
or both of them ascended Mt. Nie Kʻiu to pray for the boon
and when this was obtained the fact was commemorated
in the names K'iu and Chung Nie which were given to
the boy. (FIG. 1).
One legend relates that as she ascended the mountain
the leaves of all the trees and plants stood erect and
drooped downwards on her return. That night a spirit
appeared to her saying "You shall have a son, a sage,
and must bring him forth in a hollow mulberry tree."
When she asked her husband if there was such a thing
in the neighbourhood he told her of a cave of that name
not far away and here in the following year Confucius
was born, a spring breaking out for his bath and dying
away immediately.
Shortly before the birth of Confucius one of the
supernatural "lin" which are believed only to appear on
the advent of a sage was found outside the father's house
in the town of Tsou. The creature was covered with
scales like a dragon and from its mouth it brought forth
an inscribed tablet of precious stone, which read "The
son of water will appear. He is to supersede the declin-
ing Chow dynasty, and be a throneless King." This
tablet it presented to his mother who tied a piece of
embroidered ribbon to its horn, kept it for two nights and
then set it free.
On the 21st day of the 11th month of the 22nd year
of the reign of Duke Hsiang of Lu, or in 551 B.C. of our
chronology, Confucius was born. On the night when his
birth took place two dragons were circling round to ward
off evil influences and five old men, the spirits of the five
planets, came from heaven to the courtyard of his father's
house.

At the time of his birth a chorus of song was heard
in the sky "Heaven is moved by the troubles on earth,
hence the enlightened son is sent into the world." The
child was of an extraordinary appearance we are told,
with a mouth like the sea, lips like an ox and a back like
a dragon. On the top of his head was an unusual pro-
tuberance, which together with the legends concerning
his mother's prayer at Mt. Nie K'iu led to his being given
the "personal" name of K'iu (lit. a mound).
His father died when Confucius was three years old
and according to some accounts his mother married again.
While but a child of five or six he used to play with
sacrificial vessels, spreading them out in imitation of the
important festivals when the ruler sacrified in the ances-
tral temple. He also amused himself by imitating the
ceremonial postures used on such occasions. The tablet
states that this very unusual trait was due to his Heaven-
conferred nature which caused him to know intuitively
what was right and proper in such things. His example
soon influenced other children, till this little group of
ritualists became the talk of the whole countryside. Thus
early he began his career as a teacher. According to
tradition he was sent at the age of seven to the school
kept by a Mr. Yen Pʻing Chung.
At the age of nineteen he married, his wife coming
from the Ping Kuan family in the neighbouring state
of Sung. About this time he received his first official
appointment being made keeper of the stores of grain
for the Chi family. Although of royal blood he was
probably prompted by poverty to accept this rather lowly
office. The following year he was put in charge of the
public lands and herds. (FIG. 2).
Mencius in commenting on these appointments says
that while the superior man may at times be compelled
to accept such appointments through poverty, yet he
should be content with positions that are but poorly paid
and aim at nothing but absolute rectitude in the discharge
of his duties. According to him, Confucius said that as
keeper of the stores of grain, "My calculations must all
be right, that is all I have to care about"; and later as
keeper of the public lands and herds, he said "The oxen
and the sheep must be fat and strong and superior, that
is all I have to care about."
When he was in his 24th year his mother died.
Confucius buried her alongside his father and then as was
customary retired from office for 27 months to mourn.

When Confucius was 29 years old we read that he
studied the lute (Chʻin) under a noted musician named
Hsiang. On one occasion he kept to one lesson for ten
days without attempting to pass on. When it was sug-
gested that he should go on to the next, he replied: "But
I cannot find out the meaning of this one." After a while
he was again urged to proceed and gave the same reply.
A third time he was asked and replied: "I must find out
who composed this tune." He then sat thinking deeply,
looking straight before him. At last he exclaimed: "I
know the man: his face is black, he is of great height and
his sight can reach out to the very ocean. The only man
who could compose such a tune is Duke Voong." When
Hsiang heard this he rose from his seat, fell down on
his knees and exclaimed, "you are indeed a Sage, it was
truly Duke Voong's tune.”
It has been said that a love of music was the only
accomplishment which Confucius possessed and this he
studied less as an accomplishment than as a necessary
part of education. “It is by the odes that the mind is
aroused" he said; "It is by the rules of propriety that
character is established and it is music which completes
the edifice."
The enthusiasm which Confucius manifested in
learning all that was possible regarding the ancient rules
of propriety was demonstrated on a certain occasion
when he was present at the sacrifices in the Grand
Temple. His persistence at length provoked the comment
from one of the attendants, "Who will say that the son
of Tsou knows the rules of propriety? He has come to
the Grand Temple and enquires about everything."
It is at this period of his life that his famous meeting
with Lao Tzu, the founder of Taoism, took place at the
court of Chow in the present province of Honan. His
desire was to make enquiries regarding ancient ritual and
music and it is recorded that he was profoundly impressed
by what he saw of the services at the Imperial Temple.
While on this visit he made enquiries of a certain
teacher, Chʻang Huang, regarding the principles of music.
After he had gone Ch'ang Huang exclaimed to a friend,
"Surely Confucius has all the marks of a sage: river
eyes, dragon forehead, long arms, a back like a tortoise,
and an unusual stature. His profound knowledge of the
ancient writings, his humility, his wide knowledge and
good memory all fit him for such an office."

From his 22nd year Confucius began to attract a
group of earnest enquirers, who dissatisfied with the
prevailing practices, aspired to an understanding of the
ancient doctrines of propriety. While Confucius made
no charge, his pecuniary position was such that he was
glad to accept whatever presents his disciples could
afford. He never refused to give instruction if a student
was too poor to pay anything, the one condition he
required being a receptive eager mind. "I do not open
up the truth" said he "to one who is not eager to get
knowledge nor help any one who is not anxious to explain
himself. When I have presented one corner of a subject
to any one, and he cannot from it learn the other three,
I do not repeat my lesson." It is said that his band of
disciples grew till it numbered some three thousand, with
an inner circle of seventy-two.
When Confucius was visiting a certain temple his
attention was drawn to a swinging vessel leaning over to
one side, “I have heard" said he "that an empty vessel
is always leaning and may be set upright by putting
something right in the middle. But beware of putting
too much for then the vessel will surely be upset.
is a good lesson for a ruler to learn."

Confucius used his disciples in digesting the results of

which have made Confucius famous were completed
during this period, though the majority probably belong

The literature of China goes back long before the
days of Confucius and his genius lay in gathering
together, revising and editing the ancient records and
making them available for his own generation and pos-
terity in a clear and compact form. Amongst his most
famous works were the Book of Odes, a selection of
national ballads which throws much light on the condi-
tions of the country in those days; the Book of History,
a collection of ancient records; the Canon of Changes, a
book of divination; and the Spring and Autumn Annals.
The last named was his only original work and sets forth
the history of his own native state of Lu.
The Book of Changes is founded on the Yi Ching, an
enigmatical work on the "Pa Kwa” or eight diagrams,
amplified into sixty-four double diagrams. This book
which treats of the heavenly bodies, tells one's fortune and
forecasts the future, also interprets the Seal characters
and explains their meaning in connection with the
celestial phenomena and diagrams. The subject is one
that had a peculiar fascination for Confucius for we are
told that the leather strings with which his volume was
bound were broken three times on account of his frequent
reference to the book. He even said that if some years
could be added to his life he would give fifty to the study
of the Yi and then he might come to be without great
faults. Confucius edited and tried to rationalise these
mystic diagrams which date from some six hundred years
before his time, but neither he nor any one else has suc-
ceeded in giving them much practical value. (FIG. 4).
While still a young man, Confucius had studied
archery in which he attained considerable proficiency.
As a true sportsman it is said he always gave his bird its
chance, only shooting it on the wing. Once he attended
a village archery contest. Looking on, he said with a
sigh, "To order oneself before engaging in any work, and
to guard against departing from the correct course; how
can such attainments be acquired by one who is unworthy?
This is a quality which is only found in a truly worthy
man." Hence the verse in the Book of Odes, "If the
bull's eye thou shouldest hit, the rank of nobility thou
shalt get."

One of the most conspicuous causes of the disordered
condition of the state of Lu was an officer named Yang Ho.
On one occasion he desired to see Confucius, but Confucius
would not go to see him. On hearing this he sent a
present of a pig to Confucius, who having chosen a time
when he knew Yang Ho was not at home, went to pay
his respects for the gift. He met him, however, on the
way. "Come let me speak with you," said Yang Ho.
"Can he be called benevolent who keeps his ability in his
bosom and leaves his country to confusion?" “No,”
replied Confucius. “Can he be called wise who is anxious
to be engaged in public employ, and yet is constantly
losing the opportunity of being so Confucius again
replied, "No." Yang Ho continued, "The days and
months are passing away, the years do not wait for us."
"Right," said Confucius, "I will go into office."
The tyranny under which the people suffered is
illustrated by an incident which occurred while Confucius
was passing by Mt. T'ai Shan on his way to the neigh-
bouring state of Chʻi. By the side of the road was a
woman weeping and wailing by a grave. Confucius lent
forward in his carriage and after listening for some time
sent his disciple Tzu Lu to enquire the cause of her grief.
She replied that her father-in-law, her husband and now
her son had all been killed in succession by a tiger. "Then
why do you not move somewhere else since this place is
so dangerous?" "No," said the woman, "There is no
oppressive government here." When Confucius was told
of this he turned to his disciples and said "Remember
this, my children, oppressive government is worse than
a tiger."
When Confucius arrived at the court of Ch'i, the
ruler, Duke Ching, treated him with a considerable
amount of honour. He declined to proceed ahead of
Confucius, but the latter drew back and insisted that the
Duke should go first. The Duke declined again and
again and then said in the hearing of his court, "I con-
sidered it a great honour to have you as my guest. Your
drawing back indicates that you wish to break off our
intercourse. Why should you wish to do this, I have not
committed any fault?"
"Your manner of treating an officer of another state
is much appreciated by me," said Confucius, "but as I
am only one of the common people, I dare not precede a
ruler of your rank. Even although you wish me to do so,
I cannot commit such a breach of etiquette."

In common with the age in which he lived and
succeeding generations, Confucius shared the animistic
belief that natural objects are the abode of living spirits.
While a well was being dug for Mr. Chi Huan a peculiar
stone was discovered shaped like an animal. A servant
was sent with it to Confucius to enquire what it was.
When he saw it Confucius declared, "Everything on
earth has its own distinctive spirit. This is a spirit of
the earth shaped like a sheep with a large head."
After his long retirement we find Confucius at the
age of fifty again in official life as governor of the town
of Chung Doo in the state of Lu. He now had an oppor-
tunity of putting his theories of government to the test
and in a year had caused a marvellous transformation.
He enacted laws providing that the living should be
properly cared for and the funeral rites for the dead
properly observed; that the old and young should eat at
separate tables; and that the strong and the weak should
each have their responsibilities.
Under his rule men and women walked on opposite
sides of the road; things dropped on the road were not
picked up by passers by; no faked carvings were offered
for sale and goods were sold at fixed prices. He ordained
that inner coffins were to be 4 inches in thickness and
outer ones 5 inches. Graves were to be made on the
sides of the hills and thus the old method of raising a
mound and planting trees around it became unnecessary.
So remarkable was the change under his rule, that the
rulers of all the neighbouring states felt constrained to
follow his methods.
From governor of Chung Doo, Confucius was speedily
promoted, first to a position as superintendent of Works
and then as Minister of Crime. According to his chroni-
clers the effect of this appointment was that there ceased
to be any crime. Nevertheless we read of a few incidents

One day as Confucius was seated beside the Duke of
Lu, the latter offered him a peach and some rice. Con-
fucius took the rice first and then the peach, whereat all
the attendants smiled. The duke explained that it was
correct to eat the peach before the rice. “I know that,"
replied Confucius, "but rice is first amongst the five
grains and serves as a dish amongst the offerings in the
Ancestral Temples. There are six kinds of fruits and
the peach is of the lowest rank. It is seldom used in
sacrifice. I have heard that a gentleman should take a
cheaper kind of food before a dearer one and it is im-
proper to take the dearest food first. I dare not therefore
follow the custom of taking the dearer before the cheaper
kind of food."
Amongst other personal characteristics we are told

cut up or accompanied by the proper kind of sauce. He
insisted that there must always be ginger on the table
and while abstemious in his eating of meat and rice, he
fixed no limit to the amount of wine he permitted himself,
though we are assured he never allowed himself to be
confused by it. He would not sit down if his mat were
not straight and in his actions as well as his words he
avoided all appearance of haste.

On one occasion Yen Yuan, the favourite disciple,
asked his master the meaning of Virtue. The Sage

A character such as Confucius, which in later years
made such a strong appeal to the imagination of his
countrymen was bound in the course of time to be en-
veloped in a mass of legends and traditions and it is not
easy to separate the authentic from the legendary. The
following incident probably belongs to the latter class:
In the court of Ch'i one day there perched a one-
footed bird spreading its wings and hopping about. The
Duke of Ch'i was much surprised at this and sent to ask
Confucius about it. Confucius said, "That is called a
Siang Yang bird, which appears only on the eve of heavy
rains. Once I saw a number of boys hopping on one foot
and singing, "Great rain will come, Siang Yang is hop-
ping." "Now," he continued, “as that bird has appeared
in Ch'i, it is an omen of rain." Accordingly he instructed
the people to prepare ditches and repair the dykes. A
little while after very heavy rains came which spread to
all the neighbouring states causing great damage and loss
to the people. Only Chʻi was saved from this disaster by
its preparations.
Although Confucius himself met with many rebuffs,
he had the satisfaction towards the close of his life of
seeing many of his disciples occupying positions of in-

As soon as he heard of the death of the Sage, the
Duke of Lu ordered a temple to be erected in his honour

sacrifices being offered at the four seasons of the year.
From that day to this, save for the brief reactionary
period under Ch'in Shih Huang, the name and teachings
of Confucius have been honoured by his countrymen.
After the several states were welded into a compact

and a hog as sacrifice and visited the grave, thereby
setting the Imperial approval upon the authority of one
whose teachings and influence had been so bitterly assailed
by the previous ruler of the Ch'in dynasty.
