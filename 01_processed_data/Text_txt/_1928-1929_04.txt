THE GREAT WALL OF CHINA
By L. NEWTON HAYES, M.A., B.S.
It is probable that no existing monument of human
intellect and skill has so captured and held the imagina-
tion of people of all nations as has the Great Wall of
China. Its colossal length, its venerable age, and the
almost superhuman toil involved in its construction have
made it the outstanding wonder of the ancient world
that remains to us to-day. The Pyramids of Gizeh may
claim a more venerable age; the Parthenon at Athens
may excel it in beauty; the Coliseum of Vespasian at
Rome may exhibit finer engineering skill; but the Wall
of Ch'in Shih Huang Ti surpasses them all in the
magnitude and daring of its undertaking and the industry
and patience expended upon its construction.
Mere contemplation of its age and length and of
the toil involved in the Herculean structure, makes a deep
impression upon the imagination. An opportunity to
observe the Great Wall, like a titanic saurian crawling
up out of the sea at Shanhaikwan and mounting the hills
west of the city, where it loses its head, as it were, among
the peaks high in the distance while its tail dangles
behind in the watery depths, or an opportunity to study
sections of the Wall at Nankow, or Kalgan, or Kupehkou,
gives the traveller a thrill that is not soon forgotten;
but to know the Wall, to understand it, to feel its power,
one must live with it day after day; one must travel
far along its course, sometimes on its smooth-paved top,
high above its base, and sometimes on the ground below,
either on the inside (facing China proper) or on the
side which fronts the former hostile world. One must
see it in the early morning hours, perchance when the
first rays of the rising sun strike it far above on a sky
line of dizzy height, and at mid-day, and at night, under
the mellow influence of the moon, when the spirits of
the myriad ghosts of the builders entombed in the
structure itself come out to play. Truly to know and

Astronomers tell us that the Great Wall is the
only object of man's construction that would be visible
to the human eye from Mars. It is to China what the
Pyramids are to the land of the Pharaohs. When
students of history think to-day of ancient Egypt their
thoughts instinctively turn to the pyramids that point
skyward in the Valley of the Nile, and when they think
of China's past they find that the outstanding object
(persisting to this day) that symbolizes the departed
centuries is the barrier erected by China's first Emperor.
The Great Wall begins its westward course from
Shanhaikwan, on the sea (lat. 40° North, long, 119°, 44′
East), and extends to Kaiyükuan (long. 98° 14′ East),
a distance which, in a straight line, works out at
approximately 1,145 miles. Anyone who has ever observ-
ed how the great structure twists and turns and often
doubles back upon itself, knows that a measurement by
longitude calculations must be very far from accurate.
If therefore we allow for the various sinuosities of the
main wall we should probably have a central line of
defence approximately 1,700 miles in length.
The traveller who follows the Great Wall for long
distances observes that it occasionally flings out at right
angles long arms or, in parallel formation, one or more
loops of barricade to increase the protection which the
principal wall was designed to render. The longest of
these arms is situated near the provincial border that
separates Chihli from Shansi, and the largest loops are
found just north of Peking and north of Lanchow, the
capital of Kansu.
Most of the supplementary walls are not indicated
on any map. Their location is usually in the vicinity
of much-travelled passes, or near those sections of the
Wall that guard important cities. If, in our study of
the Great Wall, we should also take account of these
reinforcing arms and loops, we should undoubtedly have
a total length of no less than 2,500 miles.
The Chinese name for the greatest achievement of
their country's first Emperor (to distinguish it from
other similar but shorter structures of ancient times)
is Wan Li Ch'ang Ch'êng, "The Wall of Ten Thousand
Li." We recognize, of course, that the name "Ten
Thousand" is here employed in a figurative sense, and

In order to enable us to appreciate the colossal extent
of the Great Wall, it is helpful for us to think of its
length in terms of the distance between certain points
with which we are fairly familiar. The seventeen
hundred miles of the main wall (that is, ignoring all
the reinforcing loops and arms) if transported elsewhere
would extend in a straight line: From Moukden to the
most southern tip of China proper, opposite the island
of Hainan; or from Nanking to Lhassa; or from London
to Leningrad; or from Paris to Bucharest; or from New
York to Denver.
A member of Lord Macartney's Mission that visited
China in 1790 and saw the Great Wall at Kupehkou,
estimated that more brick and stones were employed in
its construction than were used in all the buildings of
the United Kingdom of that day. Another historian,
who desired to convey to his followers a comprehensive
idea of the mass of materials involved, declared that, if
rebuilt, the brick and stone and earthwork were sufficient
to construct a barrier eight feet high and three feet thick
that would girdle the globe at the equator.
Ch'in Shih Huang Ti's triumph begins at sea level
and, after climbing to great heights and descending again
to low levels in its tortuous course, finally mounts at its
Western terminal to an altitude of nearly a mile above
the level of the sea. Instead of ending abruptly against
the side of some lofty mountain, or losing itself in the
sandy plains (as we might easily imagine it does), the
Great Wall comes to an impressive and fitting end on
the very edge of a precipice which overlooks the waters
of the great North River near the border of Tibet.
A casual glance at the map will show that the main
wall extending westward from Shanhaikwan seems to
divide itself into five sections of more or less regular
and equal lengths. These are as follows: (1) from the
Yellow Sea to the Eastern Fork of the great Peking loop;
(2) from the Eastern Fork to the first intersection with
the Yellow River; (3) from the Yellow River to the
second intersection with the same River; (4) from the
second intersection with the Yellow River to Liangchow-
fu; and (5) from Liangchowfu to Kiayükwan, at its
Western extremity. We may designate these five divi-

fit for construction purposes or for making lime, might
have convinced the builders that an earthwork barrier
would be the most satisfactory form that the Wall could
take in this region.
In order to understand the real significance of the
Great Wall we must have in mind the background of
the age in which it was built and should recall the
character of that remarkable person who was responsible
for its construction.
The Chou dynasty, which had lasted for eight
centuries, and which had produced China's most venerat-
ed philosophers, Lao Tsu, Confucius and Mencius, had
just come to a close and the great Han dynasty (during
the second century of which Christ was born on the
western edge of the same continent) had not yet raised
its star above the dim horizon. Situated between those
two important eras of Chinese history, was the short,
ill-fated, but powerful, dynasty of Ch'in. The history of
the Chou dynasty is the history of eight hundred years
of petty warfare between feudal States, which waged an

is now known as Shensi, lay the State of Ch'in. Other
members of this feudal family looked upon Ch'in with
grave apprehension. On one occasion the following well-

one all the other feathered and shell-protected creatures.
The two powerful States of Chou and Yen were soon

The age of Ch'in Shih Huang Ti was the age of
magnificence; the age of the massive, the colossal. Great
rulers throughout the world seemed to be seized with
the passion to build on a stupendous scale. This was
true in Babylonia, in Egypt and in Greece. The Marble
Lighthouse at Pharos whose tower was 450 ft. high; the
Colossus of Rhodes (105 ft. in altitude); the mammoth
Tomb of Mausolus; the Statue of Jupiter by Phidias; the
Temple of Diana, and (much earlier) the Pyramid of
Cheops, and the Hanging Gardens of Babylon; all of
these were constructed on a titanic scale and each well
deserved the distinction it received by Grecian writers
as being worthy to be included in the well known list
of the Seven Wonders of the World.

scales surpassing those of other men. Not content with
erecting one or two palaces, we have good reason to

different parts of his realm. It is said that he caused
to be erected in his capital one building with ten thousand

Probably the most execrated name in Chinese history
(at least in the thought of the lettered class) is that of
the great builder. The reason that opprobrium is hurled

"Fên Shu K'êng Ju" which are inseparably associated

When Ch'in Shih Huang Ti conquered and amal-
gamated the warring feudal States, he had more in mind
than his own glory. He had considered the highest well-
being of the whole Chinese race and, by uniting these
States under one central government, he not only
guaranteed their safety from the tribes of the north,
but he reduced to the lowest minimum the age-long
friction which had existed between them. Later, a decree
was issued from the Imperial Court at Hsien Yang, near
the modern Sianfu in Shensi, that all books of classical
literature in the empire were to be burned at the city
gates. It is generally believed that Ch'in Shih Huang
Ti issued this decree in order that history might begin
with himself. That may have been one of his purposes,
but apparently he had a more significant reason, and
that was to destroy if possible all reference that had
been made in writing to the old feudal system. Dis-
affected statesmen, subjugated princes, disloyal officers
and intriguing attendants at the Court, because of larger
opportunities for dishonest acquisition on their own part,
sought to re-establish the ancient feudal system which
had lasted for nearly one thousand years. The classical
literature of the past kept before the minds of the people
former conditions that were incompatible with the
program of a united nation. Certain of the literati, who
were at that time (as they have been for many centuries
since) the ruling class, and who were unable to profit
under a centralized government as they had been able
to do in the past, were the leaders in an opposition
against Ch'in.

astrology and divination, should be openly burned, and

Like most other prominent men of ancient Chinese
history the Emperor was extremely superstitious. His
magnificent Wu Fang Kung palace of ten thousand rooms
was built in order that he might be able to sleep in a
different room each night, for he was told that he was
pursued by spirits that were determined to kill him
under cover of darkness. His only safety lay in sleeping
in a place where he had not previously planned to spend
the night, so that no information of his whereabouts
could reach the malicious creatures of the unseen world.
The expedition of one thousand young men and
women whom he sent off, at the suggestion of his
minister, Shu Tai, to an island in the Eastern Sea as
a tribute to a famous alchemist, was launched in order
to bring back the elixir of life with which he hoped to
outwit the spirits who would do him harm.
After establishing peace within his borders Ch'in
Shih Huang Ti gave himself to the problems of the
protection of his realm. His greatest concern now was
for a defence against the nomadic northern tribes that
roamed over the plains and plateaux of modern Mongolia
and Manchuria, and made frequent and devastating
incursions into the territory of China.

Sectional walls had been built as early as the fifth
century B.C. between rival feudal kingdoms or along
the northern frontiers of China to keep out barbarian
invaders known as the "Hsiung Nu." Among the feudal
States that possessed such walls were those of Yen and
Chao and Ch'in; States which occupied territory cor-

structures finally proved ineffectual and Ch'in Shih
Huang Ti resolved upon a master stroke. He decided
to build a barricade that would border the entire northern
frontier of his empire, one that would span the mountains
and desert from Tibet to the sea, utilizing such lengths
of wall as were already constructed along the course he
planned to follow. It was a tremendous project; but
accustomed as he was to think in terms of tens of
thousands, he was confident that he would succeed.
While Ch'in Shih Huang Ti planned his Wall for
the purpose of protecting his realm other considerations
for its building without doubt entered his mind. Here
was a scheme which would occupy an enormous force of
workers for a long period of years. He was well aware
that there still were in his domain enemies who, although
not publicly opposing his measures, yet were only biding
their time until they should break out in open rebellion.
Why not employ these potential trouble-makers in this
Herculean task? And so it came about that the building
of the Wall served a double purpose; not only was it
constructed to keep out Hsiung Nu hordes, but in its
building there were employed men who otherwise would
have endangered the very existence of the State. We
are told that prisoners of war, criminals from the
Imperial jails, and many dishonest officials were pressed

It is not unlikely that the Emperor had yet other
purposes when he conceived the idea of the great barrier
against foreign invasion. Few men in all history have
been so vain and egotistical. Ch'in Shih Huang Ti
desired history to begin with himself and his reign, and
it is not inconceivable that he regarded this project as
a monument to himself; one that would preserve his
name to countless generations.
The Great Wall, while primarily built to restrain
hostile invasion and to utilize the energy of enemies of
the State and quite possibly also as a monument to
himself, undoubtedly served still other purposes. Sir
George Staunton, the secretary of Lord Macartney's
Mission to China, who in 1797 saw the Wall at Kupehkou,
north-east of Peking, interestingly observed that it
rendered many subsidiary types of service in times of
peace. He wrote: "The Wall had its use in keeping
from the fertile provinces of China the numerous
ferocious beasts that haunt the wilds of Tartary, as well
as to fix the boundary between the two countries, and
to prevent the escape of malefactors out of China and
the emigration of malcontents." And another writer has
observed that the Wall has facilitated the collection of
customs at the official gateways on traffic from the
northern world into China proper.
Work on the Great Wall began about the year 220
B.C. The loyal General Mêng T'ien, who in the previous
year had been sent with an expedition of thirty thousand
men to drive back the encroaching nomads, was placed
in charge of its construction. Historians disagree as to
the length of time required to build the entire wall as
it now stands, but we know from ancient records that
at least ten years were required to complete that section
which extends from the Yellow Sea to the Yellow River
where the latter forms the border between the provinces
of Shansi and Shensi.
It is not improbable that before the Wall was

construction. Some historians relate that three out of
ten men in the Empire were called upon to work on the
stupendous barrier, and tradition has it that as many

building stone walls. Possibly, if the Wall were built

time of Ch'in Shih Huang Ti, the most needed factor

there would probably be little difference between to-day
and the days of Ch'in. A Chinese workman is still

We shall now consider some of the details of con-
struction of the Great Wall, confining ourselves to that
part which lies between the Yellow River and the Yellow
Sea, for that is the section whose appearance is best
known to the outside world. In its form the Eastern half
of the Great Wall resembles very closely the walls of the
average Chinese city. Its height averages about twenty
feet, although in places it rises as high as thirty feet.
The average width is twenty-five feet at the base and
fifteen feet on the paved surface above; a width that
would easily allow for five or six horsemen to ride abreast.
The upper surface of the Wall was always well paved,
either with bricks or stone blocks, and solidly set in
mortar. This care to protect the earthwork beneath is
apparently one of the chief reasons for the longevity of
the structure. In certain reaches of level wall the frosts
of a thousand winters have had their effect in disintegrat-
ing the surface bricks. Aided by driven loess from
northern dust storms, and by seeds carried on the wind,
veritable gardens have appeared and hardy plants of a
score of varieties have made themselves at home upon
its top.

Defence towers have from eight to twenty large ports,

entrance to the watch towers can be had only by means

their tops to defend doubly the exterior foot of the Wall

In spite of the many evidences that the Wall was

In the reign of Emperor Ch’êng Hua, who ruled from
A.D. 1465 to 1488, a general commissioned to defend the
barrier reported that he had twenty-five camps of
soldiers under his command to guard three hundred miles
of wall. Each camp contained only from one to two
hundred men. The General considered that on an average
one warrior could guard two hundred yards of battle-

the effectiveness of the structure in that day when bows
and arrows still were the invaders' chief weapon of
attack. Ch'êng Hua raised a powerful defence force by
promising to give the soldiers of the garrison land behind
the Wall. We are told that this expedient not only
supplied an adequate army, but that it enabled the
soldiers to support themselves and at the same time kept
them contented, although they were far from their own
homes.
From Shanhaikwan to Kiayükwan the Wall is
punctuated with strongly built gateways erected over

from China proper to the lands of the North. Five of

A journey of three weeks along the Wall is ended,
and one turns back after travelling a thousand li along
its course, for one last long look at that great structure,
with a feeling of real regret. As one gazes, the thing of
brick and stone suddenly begins to pulsate with life and
for a moment we see it as a living dragon with towers
for spine. It bends and twists and turns along the
highest ridges; now retreating; now advancing; twisting
up one valley and doubling down the next, giving an
impression of infinite power and at the same time of
infinite peace and poise. It winds and writhes and turns
until its farthest length loses itself in a thin gray thread
on the distant sky line. We turn to leave, and as we go,
we find the writhing saurian has again become a mass of
masonary, and, as our little caravan begins its descent of
a steep defile to the plain below, we are more aware than
ever that we leave behind what is an astounding trophy
of human industry and patience, and, for the age in which
it was built, a triumph of engineering skill. After two
thousand years the Great Wall of China still represents
in the field of architecture the mightiest single accomplish-
ment of human endeavour.
The Great Wall has served its usefulness and is
beginning to decay. Two hundred and eighty years have
passed since it was last employed to check the invaders
from the North, and grass is growing to-day on pave-
ments where once the feet of hard-pressed archers kept
the surface smooth. China cannot longer maintain her
ancient isolation, for foreigners, so long excluded, have
entered through the passes and have come to stay.
Barriers of every kind that nations once set up to keep
out foreign peoples are crumbling now to dust. No
longer can a nation live unto itself. Each is a member
of the universal family and each must face that fact and
bear its burdens for the group. In the new alignment
