​DESCRIPTION OF THE GREAT EXAMINATION HALL
AT CANTΟΝ.
BY J. G. KEER, A.M., M.D.*
THE public buildings of a Chinese city present nothing attractive
or imposing in the way of architectural beauty or proportions.
Official residences consist of a series of apartments with high roofs
supported by brick walls at the ends and wooden pillars in the
middle, with a peculiar kind of frame-work supporting the eaves,
which is the only remarkable feature of the work.
The fronts of temples are generally ornamented with painting or
scenery in relief, and figures of dragons, fish, &c., adorn the roof.
In the construction of the great examination hall there is no
attempt at ornament. The severest simplicity prevails throughout.
The interest which attaches to the place is owing altogether to
the uses for which it is found on examination to be so perfectly
adapted.
The system of competitive literary examinations in China is an
institution peculiar to the country and has been established for
more than 1,000 years. It has had the sanction and support of
every dynasty and of every emperor for that long period of time,
and it has been accepted by the people with universal consent.
Experience has no doubt proved it to be the best thing for the
country under the peculiar circumstances of a semi-civilized, idola-
trous and literary people.
The Examination Hall described in this paper is intended for the
triennial examination, at which all the graduates of the first degree
(秀才 ​Siu-ts'ai) are required to be present. Since literature
is cultivated in every part of the Empire, and the same examina-
tions are required by law to be held in every province, it will be
remembered that there is a hall in every provincial capital, similar
in every respect to the one here described.

A high wall surrounds the whole, and two gates near the south
ends of the east and west walls lead to the enclosure which admits
to the main entrance.
There are two great divisions of the examination hall. - First,
the ranges of cells for the Candidates, and Second, the apartments
for the Officers who superintend the examination.
Entering the main door (頭​門​) No. 2, and passing the Gate
of Equity(儀​門​) No. 3, and the Dragon Gate(龍門​) No. 4,
the visitor is admitted into the great Central Avenue which divides
the ground into two parts nearly equal. The avenue is lined with
trees and there is a stone walk in the centre, and one on each side.
On each side of the avenue are the ranges of Cells, and each range
is marked by a character of collossal proportions, taken from the
Thousand Character classic. They present a most striking appear-
ance to one entering the hall for the first time.
On the east or right hand side there are 75 ranges divided into
4,767 cells. On the west side there are 68 ranges divided into
3,886 cells, making a total of 8,653. Each range is covered by a
roof which slants towards the south, and is six feet high at the
lower edge. A passage 3 ft. 8 in. wide extends the whole length
of every range and thus all the cells in each range communicate
with each other. The cells are 5 ft. 9 in. deep and 3 ft. 8 in. wide.
There are grooves in the side walls which admit planks that answer
for a bed at night, and a seat and table in day time. During the
progress of the examination there are gates placed at the entrance
of each range which are sealed up. The arrangement of the cells
is perfectly represented in the cut, but the number is only about
one-sixteenth of the whole.
In the central avenue is a building No. 5, two stories high,
called the observatory(明遠​樓​) which commands a view of
all the ranges of cells. In the second story is an image of the god
of literature(魁星​).
A little beyond this building an inscription No. 7 is placed over
the central walk, "The opening heavens circulate literature,"

Apartments for Officers.
That part of the hall occupied by the officers who superintend
and conduct the examination is about one-third of the space in-
cluded within the walls. This section is subdivided into two parts
which communicate with each other by only one passage. The
first or outer part(外​簾​) is occupied by officers who superin-

second or inner part (內​簾​) is devoted exclusively to the Im-
perial Commissioners and their assistants, whose business it is to
examine the essays.
At the end of the great avenue is the Hall of Perfect Honesty
(至​公堂​), which is open on its south side towards the Avenue.
The essays are here delivered to seven officers (收​卷​官 ​Show-
kiuen-kwan) who receive them and give in return a ticket which
allows the holder to go out at the Dragon gate. The essays are
examined here, and if there is much blotting, or if certain rules in
writing characters are violated, the writer's name is posted in blue
ink at the outside entrance, and he is not again admitted.
In the rear of the hall of perfect honesty and connected with it
by a covered passage, is the Hall of Restraint(戒慎​堂​)where
the essays are received by three officers (彌​封​官​), whose busi-
ness it is to seal up the outside page of each essay, on which is
written the name, age, residence, ancestors, &c., of the writer.
A list of the names is kept and the essays are then passed to
another officer, (謄錄​官​) under whose direction they are
all copied in red ink. From one to two thousand writers are
employed to this work. The copyists occupy rooms (nn) fitted
up for the purpose on the right of the hall of perfect honesty, and
the chief officer resides in the apartments marked (r).
On the right hand side of the hall of restraint is a passage which
leads to (c) the entrance to the apartments (x) occupied by the
Kien-lin(監​臨​) or chief superintendent of the examination,
who presides over the processes above described and controls every
department. The Governor of the province is ex-officio, Kien-lin
or chief superintendent.

Passing beyond the hall of restraint, the visitor enters the cov-
ered passage way which leds into the inner chamber or green
room (10), called the Hall of Auspicious Stars(聚奎​堂​), where
the essays pass the ordeal of that inspection which determines the
fate of the ten thousand aspirants for literary honours. On the right
and left of the hall of Auspicious Stars, are the apartments (aa)
for the accommodation of the two Imperial commissioners, who are
sent from Peking for the special purpose of conducting the trien-
nial examination. Farther still to the right and left are the rooms
(bb) occupied by ten assistant examiners (同​考試​官​).
When the essays have been copied in red ink, they are sent to
the readers(對​讀​) who compare each copy with the original
to make sure that no mistake has been made. The originals are
then returned to the Governor, and the copies in red ink are deli-
vered to the receiver for the inner chamber (內​收​掌​), who
takes them to the hall of Auspicious Stars. They are there divided
among the ten assistant examiners, who select a certain number of
the best, and these only are submitted to the inspection of the
Imperial Commissioners (主考​) who give the final verdict as
to which possess superior merit.
The commissioners and examiners are required to do their work
in each others presence and also in the presence of a monitor
(內​監​試​). None of them are allowed to have any intercourse
with persons in the other departments, and they are not allowed
to take essays to their private rooms at night. The utmost care
is taken to secure fair and honest decisions.
The hall of Auspicious Stars is regarded by literary men with
mingled feelings of awe, hope and despair, for there it is that the
judgement is passed on them which decides their destiny. If fa-
vorable, the way is opened which leads to fame, wealth and honor,
the sum of earthly bliss. But if unfavorable they are condemned
to years of toil and disappointment.
Beside the principal halls and apartments described above,
there are numerous other rooms for military officers who command
the police force on guard during the examinations, for store-rooms,
for printing, &c., &c., so that the whole forms a labyrinth of
rooms and passage ways, all of which have their appropriate uses.
The police force required for the occasion must be very large.
One military officer has general command and he has under him
thirty-six high-officers, and twenty-four of an inferior rank.

The commissariat department is not a small affair, because sup-
plies are required for all the officers and their attendants, for the
copyists and the police force for about twenty days, during which
time they are not permitted to go out.
Printing is also an important business, since the texts for the
essays must be cut, and eight or nine thousand copies printed in
one night.
The Triennial examination is held in the 8th month, and there
are three sessions. The first is on the 9th, the second on the 12th
and the third on the 15th. The candidates are required to enter
on the day previous, have their names registered, receive their
blank thesis paper, and have their cells assigned to them. They
have one night to arrange their rooms and collect their thoughts.
At daylight the next morning a printed copy of the texts which
have been selected by the Imperial commissioners is placed in the
hands of each one, and instantly ten thousand brains are at work,
and ten thousand pencils commit to paper the learned, eloquent
and poetical thoughts which spring into existence from the magic
influence of the words of Confucius and Mencius. The day passes
swiftly away, and many have finished their tasks before the sun is
hid behind the western hills; many others toil on through the
night, and some have only finished at noon on the succeeding day.
But those who are ready, hand in their compositions before day-
light, and return to their lodgings for a day's rest, and to recruit
their overtaxed energies for another struggle.
On the first day the texts are taken from the Four Books,
(四​書​) and three essays and one piece of poetry are required.
Each essay is to contain about 700 words, and the poetry to con-
sist of 16 lines of 5 words each.
On the second day the texts are taken from the Five Classics,
(五​經​) and five essays are required.
On the third day general subjects, ancient history, &c., are given
for texts. Five essays are required and each one not to exceed 300
words.
The arrangement of the cells is designed to give to each can-
didate a private room, where, with nothing but pen, ink and paper,
he has a fair opportunity of displaying his knowledge of the
classics and his skill in composition. The reward placed before
him and the great numbers competing for the same prize, stimu-
late to the highest exertion.

The arrangement of the apartments occupied by the officers, and
the mode of conducting the examination are designed to prevent
unfair dealing, and to secure an honest and impartial verdict in the
case of every candidate. The several officers who perform the
various duties required, in passing the essays from the hands of
the authors to the examiners, are required to fix their seals at
each step of the process, and to use inks of different colours.
As far as possible each officer is placed under the observation
of all the others, and fines, degradation and other punishments
are prescribed for every wilful or accidental violation of the rules
to be observed in each case. The minutest details are given of the
mode of transacting every part of the business, so that it would
seem impossible to act unfairly except by the connivance or agree-
ment of all the officers who hold responsible positions. The theory
of the institution is admirable if not altogether perfect, and if there
is a failure to accomplish the ends proposed, the fault is not in the
system, but in the corruption of those upon whom devolves the
administration of it.
The interest which attaches to the triennial examination held in
this hall may be estimated, when we consider that the eight or ten
thousand candidates are men of learning of all ages from the youth
of twenty to the gray-haired patriarch, who have already received
the first degree. They also as literary graduates occupy positions
of influence and responsibility in their respective families and
neighbourhoods, and there are thus many who are interested in
their farther advancement.
The importance of the occasion is also evident from the fact that
the highest officers in the province with numerous assistants are
associated with Imperial commissioners from Peking in conducting
the examination.
The examination of essays continues until the 8th or 9th of the
9th month. And on the 10th the names of the successful candidates
must be announced at the entrance of the Governor's Yamun.
Messengers are in waiting to carry the joyful news by express to
all parts of the province. A feast is given by the Governor to the
new graduates; after which they go in state dress to worship the
tablets of their ancestors, and to make calls on relatives and friends.
After each triennial examination a catalogue is published, which
gives the name, title, rank and office of all the officers employed;
and a list of the graduates, with the age, residence and former stand-
