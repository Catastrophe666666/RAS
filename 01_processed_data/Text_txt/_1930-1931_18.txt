By Dr. C. PING
Biological Laboratory of Science Society of China
The Biological Laboratory of the Science Society of
China has been devoting a part of its time and means to
making a biological survey of the Yangtse Valley. The
Natural History of Nanking is a part of the work that
has been undertaken by its staff. Although Nanking
with its vicinity is comparatively limited in area, its
fauna and flora represent practically all those found in the
regions from Wuhu down to Shanghai along the Yangtse
River. Due to the pressure of other work and limited
means, the staff of our Laboratory has not been able to
have the fauna of this locality thoroughly surveyed.
This work is, however, progressing without interruption,
and we are in hopes that some years from now we shall
have a comprehensive report on the local fauna published
from the Laboratory. The account I am presenting here
consists of only a bird's-eye view of our fauna, in which
I have to make many omissions owing to our incomplete
knowledge of this subject.
Before describing to you the fauna which has been
engaging our time and energy for these few years, I
desire first to sketch to you the geographical features of
Nanking. The city is situated on the right bank of the
Yangtse River. The topography around the city is
varied. On the western side a low range of loess-covered
hills, 100 to 200 ft. in height, intervenes between the
city and the Yangtse River. The Purple Mountains,
just outside of the north-eastern corner of the city
wall, dominate the city. Still farther away from the
city, toward the north-east as well as the north, there
is a comparatively long range of hills called Mu Fu
Shan, which runs along the south bank of the Yangtse

River; toward the east, south-east and south there are
also hilly regions, among which the best known are
Tong Shan or Hot Spring Hill, Fong Shan or Table Moun-

Nanking is partly embraced by mountains on its north,

numerous ponds and small and large canals as well as
small prominences. In such varied topography the fauna
deserves our attention.
Secondly, let us consider its climatic features, which
have profound influence on the local organisms. "Nan-
king is situated in the same latitude as San Diego,
California, or Jerusalem, Asia Minor. Its climate is
continental. It has high annual ranges in pressure and
temperature, summer rainfall and monsoon winds."
Having no high mountains in its vicinity serving as
barriers, Nanking, according to the weather report by
Dr. C. C. Chu, is exposed to polar airs in winter and is
apt to be invaded by warm and humid airs in summer.
"Cold waves," brought here by the north-western winds
in winter, may hold their sway for two or three days at a
time, and bring the temperature down to 10° C. below
zero or even less. In summer, "hot waves," with a
temperature of 40.2° C. or over, sometimes occur. These
are, however, the extremes. On an average, there are
seven months in a year, from the middle of March to the
middle of November, which makes the growing season,
when Nanking is free from frost.
There are two rainy seasons a year in Nanking.
The first one is in the month of April and the second in
June and July. In September heavy precipitation some-
times occurs when there is a typhoon passing by
Nanking. From October to March most of the precipi-
tation falls as cyclopic rainfall. The winter in Nanking
is a dry season, and the snowfall never amounts to very
much, with the exception that once in every so many
years it is very heavy. Usually after a snow storm, snow
covers the ground only for a day or two, then disappears.
In comparison with regions situated in the same latitude
along the east coast of North America, Nanking is drier
in winter and wetter in summer.
The relative humidity is comparatively small in

may not appear very conspicuously.

Nanking, as already mentioned, has numerous ponds

Of the fresh water Coelenterata, there is a very

Usually a good number of them could be found on the

can be kept alive for a considerable length of time in
an aquarium placed at the window, and under a moderate
sunshine they become extended, moving around with the
upper parts of their bodies and tentacles, catching food in
the water, and once in a while moving from one place to
another along the stem of the aquatic plant by looping
or dragging.

The earthworms of Nanking consist of nine species
so far as records show. Those which were described
some years ago are Pheritima hupeiensis, Pheritima
heterochaeta, Pheritima pingi and Pheritima hawayana,
and those which have just been described recently
as new species are Pheritima vulgaris, Pheritima
vulgaris agricola, Pheritima kiangsuensis and Pheritima
obscuritopora. Í have been informed that there are two
other genera, for each of which there is a representative
species. They are Allolobophora caliginosa, subsp.
trepozoides and Drawida japonica. Among them Pheri-
tima vulgaris and Pheritima pingi are fairly common.
These creatures are all beneficial to agriculture as Charles
Darwin pointed out. They turn the subsoil to the surface
and frequently make the land more porous and are able
to convert a poor soil into a more fertile one. That
is the reason why the experienced gardeners and vege-
table growers seem to be quite appreciative of their
presence in their gardens, although they may not really
understand how the actual benefit is done by them.
Among these Nanking forms Pheritima kiangsuensis is
the largest and attains when fully grown a length of
350 mm., while all the others are below 300 mm.

whole the Nanking earthworms are comparatively small.

The habitat of these worms varies: some of them live in
comparatively wet and damp places and others live in
comparatively dry places. The very common earthworm
lives in the same nest, with or at least very near to the
nest of the mole cricket, and it could be easily located by
following the sound that the latter produces in the
evening. Common folk often believe that the sound they
have often heard in wet shaded places is the noise
produced by the earthworm and that the earthworm is an
animal which can produce a certain kind of music like
most of the orthopterous insects.
Of the phylum Annelida, we have another group of
interesting animals. Whole species have been largely
surveyed in Nanking. These are the leeches. Most of
the species are aquatic and a few are found in damp places.
There are altogether eight species, namely, Glossiphonia
lata Oka, Glossiphonia (Helobdella) nuda Moore, Hemiclep-
sis geei Oka,, Erporbdella octoculata Oka, Hirudo nipponica
Whitman, Odontobdella blanchardi Oka, Whitmania levis
(Baird), and Haemopis acranulatum (Whitman). Of these
species Whitmania levis is a large sized one and very
commonly found in Nanking. The leeches are very acces-
sible to the zoologist. As Nanking is rich in ponds and
canals, some of the common ones are found in the shallow
water along the shores. Frequently, when a small rock is
turned over, one or two leeches are found. They are
often closely attached to the under surface of the rock
by their sucking apparatus, difficult to be taken off.
This animal was much used by the medical practitioners
in the early days in Western countries for releasing
the blood of patients. It has never, however, been
very much cared for by the old fashioned doctors in
Nanking. Very few land forms are found, and those
living in damp places are all small in size. There is no
such danger of getting sucked by them as is frequently
the case in Hainan, or in the Yen Tong mountains in
the southern part of Chekiang, where, when a person walks
in the bamboo forest, the creature often creeps into the
sleeve or trousers, fastening its sucking dish to his skin.
Except to the zoologist, the members of this group of
animal are regarded as useless forms.
There are quite a number of oligochaetous worms,

water, and these alone will form an interesting group of
study, but our knowledge of them is too scanty. Con-
cerning the flatworms, we know three genera, whose

There is an enormously rich field for the study of
parasitic forms including both the flat and the round
worms. Owing to the fact that our research is confined to
the normal side of zoology and our means are limited, none
of the members on our staff has undertaken the task of
surveying any such group, but this field cannot be too
long neglected, and we are expecting soon a general
systematic study of the helminithology of Nanking to be
started at our laboratory.
The Nanking waters afford an interesting group of
small organisms, the rotifers. They appear in fairly
good numbers in the spring, summer, and autumn. There
are about ten genera which are under investigation at our
laboratory. As they are microscopic, they have escaped
the attention of the zoologists who have only occasionally
visited Nanking and are interested in higher forms.

Coming to the mollusks we have about five or more

The locust, Locusta migratoria L., is the most im-
portant one. Every year immense damage had been
done to crops before the Bureau came to put a check
on it.
Outside of the city of Nanking waste land
is met in every direction besides the cultivated fields.
The locusts lay their eggs in the soil of the unploughed
field, and in the winter, as Nanking very seldom has a heavy
snow, the eggs are well protected from being killed by
frost and snow. When the warm weather comes in the
spring, they are hatched and the nymphs acquire their
growth in the grass fields. At about the second and third
stage of nymphal development they crawl out of the fields,
spreading on to the country roads and climbing up the
stems of reeds, grasses, shrubs and trees. They are easy
to destroy during this period, for their wings have not
been developed and they are not able to fly. I have often
stamped on quite a number of them when taking a walk
along a path in the suburbs. They could be picked up by
hand or driven to any place in masses and crushed with
any mechanical means. The radical method of fighting
this pest is of course to eliminate the waste fields. If the
fields are thoroughly ploughed in the autumn or early
winter, the soil is turned up and becomes much looser, the
eggs of the locusts are mostly exposed to dry weather
and cold temperature and will be unable to avoid being
frozen in the late winter. In so doing, the outbreak of
the locust's pest could be greatly checked next year.
Another remedy in fighting the locust is by spraying
poison. According to the economic entomologist, wheat
bran,, orange juice, water, syrup and white arsenate are
mixed and used as a bait which works most effectively.
The Bureau of Entomology in Nanking has, however, not
resorted to either of these fundamental methods, and the
method they are adopting in checking the pest is a very
simple one and has served a good purpose every year.
Taking advantage of the cheap labour in this country,
the Bureau sends out every year a number of trained field
men, each of whom acts as a leader to a large number of
policemen and farmers, "swatting" the nymphs of locust
by tons. Throughout the season the pest could be
checked to a great extent and a large amount of money
saved by this simple but effective method.

The ways of keeping down mosquitoes and house

The mosquito wrigglers are taken out with an ordinary

By employing a large police force in the city and in the

a solution of potassium cyanide of suitable strength.
While the campaign is going on every year, propaganda
is issued so as to encourage people to report on where
they have discovered pools with mosquito wrigglers and
manure holes with grubs. According to the report issued
by the Bureau the result is quite satisfactory, the
expenses are not particularly great, and the harm produced
by these two kinds of insects is considerably reduced.

The amphibian fauna of Nanking consists of a com-

now goes. There is only one species of salamander,
Triturus orientalis (David), which is neither found in the
city of Nanking nor in its suburb. In collecting this
form for study we have to go to Shih Hsia Shan or Loong
Tan which is about fifteen miles east of Nanking. They
are found in the ponds near the temples. This form of
animal seems to be well adapted to comparatively clear
and still water with plenty of aquatic plants therein.

The salamander is between three and four inches in length

The tailless amphibians of Nanking are represented
by two species of toads, eight species of frogs, and two
species of tree-frogs. The frogs, which are most com-
monly found in our ponds, lakes and rice fields, are the
golden-lined frog, Rana nigromaculata, which has three

times of the year, with two conspicuous yellow lines on its

people in Nanking as a food article. The lake side frog
is a still smaller one, and appears in great numbers.

numerous black blotches of considerable size and with a
buff streak along the median line of its back. In the
autumn or early winter when it is found before its hiber-

Rana chensinensis David, and the wood frog Rana japonica

commonly found in water and the latter two are less
common. We have a burrowing toad in Nanking, Kaloula
borealis (Barbour). It is comparatively stout in shape

many small warts on its upper surface, no longitudinal

warts, its posterior and flanks lighter, its under side
eiither uniformly dirty white or of flesh colour, and its

fierce. It habitually rubs its skin on the trunks of pine
trees,consequently its skin is covered with terpentine here
and there and it is hard to be killed with small shot.
Once it is startled, it runs most wildly and would break
the trunk of a small tree when it hits it. In the Yangtse
River we often get specimens of the finless river
porpoise, Neomeris phocoenoides Cuvier. This animal
can be seen on the river not far from the city. Its
length reaches about four feet when mature. It migrates
up in the river and travels as far as Ichang and
also gets into the Tung Ting Lake. The wolf, Canis
lupus tschiliensis Matsch., still persists in the mountains,
like Pao Hua Shan, etc., but it is no longer found in nearby
localities of Nanking. It looks in many respects like the
European wolf. The mink, Mustela davidiana M.-Edw.,
is found in the old houses and deserted gardens in the city.
Its fur is much in demand by the Chinese pen shops, for the
hairs are used for making brush pens. There are two
badgers, which are once in a while caught in the farm

The above is the faunal outline of Nanking. This is
however a very incomplete one. There is an enormous
field for further studies, many gaps are to be filled up
and many new discoveries to be made by conscientious
students.
In conclusion I may say that Nanking has four
interesting features as far as the study of zoology is
concerned.
1. It has a varied topography. Inside the city
wall and in the immediate suburbs there are hills and
dales and canals, wood lots and bamboo thickets. It
affords excellent accessible collecting fields. The zoologist
does not have to go very far from his laboratory when he
desires to make field observations on the animal forms on
which he is working. Now that both the Purple Mountains
and the Yuan Wu Lake are under the law prohibiting
hunting, both birds and mammals are well protected in
these regions and they can be studied alive in their natural
environment by ecologists and field zoologists.
2. This locality represents the entire lower region of
the Yangtse Valley from Wuhu to Woosung. The faunas
of Nanking are found very similar to those in any other
localities in this section of the Yangtse Valley. If a com-
prehensive study of the faunas could be accomplished, a
large percentage of the animal forms of the lower Yangtse
Valley could be identified without much difficulty.
3. Nanking has the Northern climate in the winter
and high humidity and sub-tropical weather in the spring
and summer, which are different from the north. The
faunas under such influence afford very interesting
phenomena for ecological investigations.
