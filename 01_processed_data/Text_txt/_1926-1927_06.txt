YEN HSI CHAI,
A 17TH CENTURY PHILOSOPHER
By MANSFIELD FREEMAN
The philosophical thought of China during the last
seven or eight hundred years has been largely under the
influence of the teachings of Chu Hsi. Occasionally a
free spirit has revolted, and breaking away from the
restraints of Sung dynasty philosophy, has established
an independent school of thought, but the efforts of such
schools have been for the most part feeble and few
receive much attention from present day scholars. One
exception, however, is the Yen-Li school which flourished
during the closing years of the Ming dynasty and the
opening years of the Chʻing. The teachings of Yen
Yuan, the founder of this school, and Li Shu Ku, his
most important follower, have devotees among the best
thinkers in China to-day. Ex-president Hsu Shih Chʻang
in the introduction to his work on the Yen-Li philosophy,
after lauding these two men as the only true transmitters
of the Confucian philosophy of life concludes, "I know
that the teachings of Yen Hsi Chai and Li Shu Ku must
cover the world, spreading throughout the vast universe,
therefore, being so important, these teachings cannot be
forgotten, being so powerful they can educate the five
continents for ten thousand centuries."1 And Liang Chi
Chao in his History of Chinese Thought of the Last Three
Hundred Years says of these men, "in destructive thinking
their vision was high and their courage strong. I venture
to say that from ancient times down to the present there
is none to compare with them because from the Han
dynasty on every system of thought of these two thousand
years has been thoroughly criticised by them."2 Because
of the high place Yen Yuan holds in the thought of

"I wrote the Essentials of Education to explain clearly the

their doctrine is not only in the sentences and chapters

To

Yen Yuan, or Yen Hsi Chai as he is better known,
was born in 1635, in the eighth year of Ch'ung Cheng,
the last Ming emperor, and died in 1704, in the 43rd year
of K'ang Hsi. His birthplace was Yang Tsun, a small
village halfway between Peking and Tientsin. His father
was the adopted son of a man named Chu Wen Chiu, so
that as a boy Hsi Chai's family name was Chu and not
Yen. Like many famous men there are apocryphal
accounts of strange events taking place at the time of
his birth. His mother was pregnant for eleven months,
and just before he was born the neighbors saw a strange
cloud of smoke covering the home, which first took the
shape of a unicorn, and then changed to the shape of a
phenix. At the age of three, Yen Hsi Chai's father was
captured by soldiers, probably Manchus, who were at that
time overrunning north China, and ten years later his
mother remarried, so his boyhood days were spent in the
home of his adopted grandfather.

of Hsi Chai. Due to a law suit the Chu family was
impoverished and moved to the country. Here he spent

Hsi Chai's education began at the age of eight, under
the tutorship of Wu Tung Yun, a retired scholar. Very
early he showed promise of unusual attainments. Money
given to him was spent on pens and writing material

Ever since the Sung philosophers emphasized the
importance of the Great Learning, the first sentence of
this book, "to illustrate illustrious virtue, renovate the
people, and rest in the highest excellence" has been the
ideal of every philosophy of education. The question
has been how could these accomplishments be secured?
The Great Learning says of the ancients, "Things being
investigated, knowledge became complete. Their know-
ledge being complete their thoughts were sincere. Their
thoughts being sincere, their hearts were then rectified.
Their hearts being rectified, their persons were cultivated.
Their persons being cultivated, their families were
regulated. Their families being regulated, their States
were rightly governed. Their States being rightly
governed, the whole kingdom was made tranquil and
happy." This was the accomplishment of the ancients.
Chu Hsi and the Cheng brothers, Cheng I Chuan and
Cheng Ming Tao, of the Sung dynasty, conceived that to
secure these much desired results in their own day the
most important task was to investigate into things, and
this investigation was to discover the law of things in
order that knowledge might be completed. Wang Yang

Hsi Chai in a negative way opposes the reading and
writing of books because it is injurious to man's health,
it does not serve to combat Buddhistic teachings, and it
is useless. He thus reaches his positive doctrine that
work and not reading and writing is the panacea for the
ills of the world. Why then waste time in reading,
talking or writing? "There are limits to man's age and
spirit," he says, "When a day is spent in talking and
reciting, practice and activity are spoiled for a day. If
more time be spent on writing less will be spent on
work."18
This opposition to reading and writing was not
started by Yen Hsi Chai, for as Liang Chi Chao points
out in his History of Chinese Thought of the Last Three
Hundred Years, 19 the Liu-Wang school had earlier
become alarmed at the multiplication of commentaries
and philosophical treatises and had uttered a protest.
Hsi Chai in his opposition went further, however, than
earlier thinkers had dared to go. He criticised not
reading and writing alone, but also discussions, debates,
and controversies of all sorts, whether good or bad,
whether regarding the doctrines of Confucius or of the
schools of Buddhim and Taoism. He quotes Confucius'
remark, "I wish I could do without speaking," and argues
that the sage's idea was that practice and activity must
come first. Discussions regarding what has been
practiced may follow if necessary, but to begin by
discussing the subject is useless, especially if it is a
discussion of a metaphysical or ethical problem, for the
people will not understand the subject until they have
had some actual practical experience with it.
