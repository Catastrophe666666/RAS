MEI LAN-FANG
(The Man and His Art)
By GEORGE KIN LEUNG
The pleasant sound of the characters, Mei Lan-fang,
(plum, orchid, fragrance), which obsesses the fond
memory of the Chinese public, also offers the European
ear a suggestion of eastern romance. Although the
approach to Chinese drama may be made in a dusty
closet by reading thick books, it is far more interesting
to enjoy the subject through the greatest living epitome
of the art, namely, Mr. Mei Lan-fang. The art of this
actor, being the impersonation of maidens, is a highly
specialised technique; yet, since no other single actor
has done so much to revive what is best on the Chinese
stage, those interested in this branch of expression will
find this artist not only a most suitable medium through
which to enjoy the drama but also one of the most
romantic and brilliant beings the footlights have produced.
This greatest of actors was brought nearer to us by
his recent triumphal engagement in Shanghai, November
15-December 31, 1926. Fully a month before his arrival,
one of the most prominent theatres maintained huge
half-page advertisements, announcing the coming of the
"King of Actors," who, after many months of negotia-
tions, had at last, "condescended to appear on the humble
stage-carpet" of that theatre. Then, came war and
rumours of war with the cutting off of transportation.
The serious illness of the actor's wife was followed by
a controversy as to which theatre would eventually
glean the golden harvest of the mighty dramatic engage-
ment, until most of us thought, "Surely, he will not
reach Shanghai this year." But when we had given up
hope, lo! the newspapers announced that the widely fêted
idol had arrived, had actually been photographed, and
had taken up his quarters on Thibet Road. That the
unbelievable had really come to pass, my friends and I
could hardly believe; yet, after visiting Mr. Mei, we

That this youth should rise to the pinnacles of fame
so quickly as to be known to art lovers the world over, is
indeed interesting; but when one considers the appalling
struggle among actors, hundreds of whom are continually
hovering over the starvation line, Mei's meteoric rise is
the more remarkable. The ancient prejudice against
the acting profession, which has been but partially broken
down by the inrush of democratic ideas, is but one of the
seemingly insurmountable obstacles this artist has over-
come, for he is accepted by the best of society.
Even a newcomer to the Chinese theatre, unless
absolutely indifferent to art, who casually attends a per-
formance by Mei Lan-fang, can hardly believe that the
dainty heroine is not actually a maiden, and a most
charming one. If the drama be a new one, there will be
dances of the softest feminine movement; if the work
be operatic, the actor will maintain the strict but not
unpleasant demeanour of a model woman of former
generations. The action, the expression of the eyes, the
grace of the wrists and fingers, and the faultless manner
of walking, as well as a captivating stage-presence, will
delight the European. But the high, sometimes shrill,
falsetto voice, with its intricate technique of artificial
tones, may prove strangely novel to the unaccustomed
ear, although this style of singing offers much for the
student of music. The beauty of the falsetto depends
on the artificial production of a series of tones, the
rhythm invariably being striking and attractive. An
indefinable something, which endears the actor to the

At this point, a detailed discussion of the training of
an actor and the characteristics of Chinese drama would
not be out of place and would be of no little interest; but
passing mention of these subjects will have to serve our
purpose. In Peking, the traditional producing ground
of best actors, there are special schools for the training
of boy-actors, who range from seven to nineteen years
of age. The strictest discipline is observed, the boy
being placed in the institution with the implicit under-
standing that he is no longer under the care of the parents
but under the guardianship of the head of the school, who,
judging from physique, voice, and intelligence, decides
what type of actor the boy shall be. Actors often train
relatives or aspirants of great promise, passing on secrets
from master to pupil. Mr. Mei has been trained by both
school and private tutorship; but even to-day he spares no
effort to improve his art.
The dramas of the old school, resembling those of
Shakespeare, but generally inferior to them, possess
merits worthy of close study. Considered as a whole, an
old drama is fraught with a keen sense of make-believe;
the stage is severely bare and change of locality may be
indicated by an actor's walking about in circles. Panto-
mime through the empty air may serve to indicate that an
actor has bolted a door or closed a window. The voice, in
singing and declamation, is used in a highly convention-
alized manner. The indisputable fact that the whole
atmosphere of the Chinese stage is created by a brilliant
actor or group of actors once more leads us to the
incomparable Mei.
The versatility of this actor may be more clear when
one considers that of the four hundred dramas in his
répertoire, more than three-fourths were written especial-
ly for him or by him in collaboration with others. He is
the foremost exponent of every conventional feminine
stage type, excepting the lao-tan(老旦​) or type for an old
woman and the chou (丑​) or the type for a comédienne,
both of which are out of his sphere. Of Mr. Mei's
impersonations of the modest beauties of old, the heroic
warrior-maidens, vivacious servants, or dancing celestial

In order to make a clear-cut differentiation between
the conventional feminine stage-types, various dramas

Pavilion of the Royal Monument” (Yü Pei T'ing, 御​碑亭​).
The actor portrays the wrongly suspected wife, whose
husband has gone north to take the imperial examinations.
Meanwhile, Yüeh-hua, the young wife, on her way from a
visit to her parents, is caught in a blinding rainstorm,
which drives her to a pavilion for protection. Here a
youthful scholar also seeks shelter; but mindful of the
timid lady's plight, being trained as she has that no word
should pass between strangers of opposite sex, he gallant-
ly spends the night outside, huddling close to the eaves of
the structure. With the cold dawn he left. So grateful
was Yüeh-hua to the stranger that she told of his courtesy
to her young sister-in-law, who next passed the story on
to the returned husband. Suspecting the worst, the
jealous man sent the young wife to her parents with a
letter placing her aside. When the husband later learns
from the scholar's own lips of his wife's innocence, the
repentant man, in a scene of delightful comedy, begs his
wife's forgiveness.
It is clear that "The Pavilion of the Royal Monu-
ment" is a typical ch'ing-i(青衣​)drama, because the
most important character of the play, Yüeh-hua, is known
as a ch'ing-i, or the conventional type for the delineation
of women of faultless conduct, singing being a foremost
requisite. The long skirt and the blue coat with its
trailing sleeves is the proper costume for this gentle type.
Yüeh-hua's running in the rain, her dainty actions in
brushing off the imaginary water from her clothing, and
her timidity on discovering the approach of a stranger
are characteristic of ch'ing-i technique, as is the intricate
singing with its well modulated tones, striking rhythms,
and brilliant legatos in the lower register. When the
husband returns to be forgiven, Yüeh-hua sits, obstinate,
but with her head turned away as becomes a gentle
woman, while the distracted husband shifts from one
side to the other, finally kneeling. This being too much
for the woman, she smiles forgiveness.

The foregoing drama is a vehicle for the gentle and

The story of the play follows. At the close of the Sui
Dynasty, the seventh century A.D., there followed a
struggle among the feudal lords. When General Hsin
was killed by Wang Pai-tang, Tung-fang Shih, the widow,
swore she would take vengeance on the enemy. Entering
the field of battle, she captured the slayer of her husband,
only to fall violently in love with him. The second
episode reveals Mr. Mei as the maidservant, a hua-tan
type, another actor taking the part of the widow. The
maidservant keeps reminding the infatuated widow that
she must kill Wang Pai-tang; but as the woman is
determined to wed her handsome captive, the maid finally
adds her effort to that of her mistress in order to impress
upon Wang that it is either connubial bliss or the sword
for him. Although at first obdurate, even reviling the
woman, he finally yields and the wedding is planned
for! This is an amazing ending, seeing it is regarded as
both typical and inevitable that the old playwright should
go out of his way to punish evil and reward good. Yet,
the author may have tried to show that love is greater
than vengeance, or that if vengeance is to be taken, it
should not be interfered with by love, but meted out at
once.
In the first part of "the Rainbow Pass," the widow
in her white weeds, brandishing the spear at her hus-
band's murderer, is clearly a military type, or, to put
it in the stage-vernacular, a wu-tan. Mr. Mei's skill
in the handling of weapons equalled the work of actors
who specialise in such technique. The conventional
tableau-effects created by Tung-fang Shih and Wang
Pai-tang were much enjoyed by the audience. With legs
well astride, the hard-pressed man stood while the infatua-

Tung-fang Shih bit off from Wang's coat a silk rosette

she, resourceful to the last, has her men take him captive.

The series deals with the life of the fascinating
Kuei-fei of the T'ang Dynasty, who made a slave of the

presenting her with the rarest of food, jewels, and
embroidered silk, and staging extravagant entertain-
ments. The unhappy end came when the mutinying
soldiers demanded the death of the favourite, to whose
charge they laid all the ills of the land, before they would
fight the enemy. Unwilling to give up his beloved, the
emperor finally allows Kuei-fei to end her own life by
strangulation with a silken girdle. History, fiction, and
