By DR. EVAN MORGAN
The Messrs. Ernest Benn, Limited, are engaged in a
great undertaking in publishing the Catalogues of the
George Eumorfopoulos Collection. A part of the series
has already been published and the first volume of the
final series has been issued recently from the press. The
Catalogue of Chinese, Corean and Persian pottery and
porcelain was prepared by Mr. R. L. Hobson and published
in six volumes. The Catalogue of Chinese frescoes is the
work of Mr. Laurence Binyon and issued in one volume.
The Catalogue of Chinese, Corean and Siamese paintings
is also by Mr. Binyon and in one volume.
All these are of uniform size, Folio, 18 by 12 inches.
There are two editions, one limited to 560 numbered copies,
(the frescoes only 520), price twelve guineas per volume:
and one, an edition de luxe, numbered and signed, limited
to 25 copies, price twenty-six pounds five shillings per
volume.
The volume published this year and prepared by
Dr. W. Perceval Yetts is also uniform with the preceding
volumes. This new series will consist of six volumes, of
which there will be two editions, uniform in number, size
and price with the others.
It will thus be seen that the scope of the whole
Collection is wide and the different Catalogues are intended
to expound and illustrate this wide range of the Collection.
The new volume edited by Dr. Yetts is a very hand-
some one. The 75 plates, some of them in colour, are
beautifully executed and the texts prepared by the editor

Dr. Yetts' work helps to kindle the imagination as he
in discusses the intricate problems of the old bronzes. In
fact it is only as the imagination is active that a true
appreciation is made of these old vessels. It is not without
emotion that we look on an urn that may have been handled
by some ancient worthy and the object of veneration of
some dim historical king as he offered the ancestral
worship in the Ming T'ang. It is not impossible to
imagine that some of the ancient bronzes may have been
touched by the Duke of Chou or may have been used in the
libations, or held the fragrant incense in the worship of
the nine ancestors from Wu Wang down to later times.
But, of course, such vessels are extremely rare and would
be priceless. If there had been no destructive wars and
cataclysmic times to wipe out all traces of past culture by
the burning of books and the melting of all metal such
objects undoubtedly would be more plentiful and not so
rare as they are. But the world must be taken as it is.
d.
It is this imagination that really gives value to any
collection. The brass and the copper that may have come
from pre-Han times are of no more value than their
commercial worth in the markets. The metal itself is
only worth so much per catty. Of course, age and style
do count for much: the beauty of the shape, of the bird,
and the owl in its morning coat is of value. But it is
questionable whether after all any collector would pay
much for these. There must be something besides to
command the high prices that are now given. It may be
granted that there are people ready to pay for reputed
age: let it be proved that an object carries with it the
relics of venerable antiquity and the cheque is forthcoming.
Age and style are factors in all bargains. Yet after all
these are not the greatest factors. It is the human
imagination which plays the most important part.
Imagination in this field does the function of the ear in
sound. Without the ear there can be no sound. Likewise
without imagination that surrounds the figure with the
people and the drama of the past there is but little vertu.
The imagination summons all the ghosts and spirits that
hang over the past: it clothes the old piece of metal covered
with verdigris—a dull and lifeless mass-with the hands

In the discussion of the Inscriptions, Dr. Yetts has
admirably responded to this demand for imagination. In
the unfolding of the development of the script of China he
brings us into contact with the workers in bronze and the
calligraphists of the past. There is beauty in every stroke
of the brush and stylus and the author traces the processes
as the one form passes into the other. We have first the
pictogram and then a development into the seal form and
the later styles.
This much is necessary to the understanding of the
significance of the inscriptions. Dr. Yetts recognized this
and he has given us a valuable dissertation on the inscrip-
tions, and the formation of the language. He has given
us an elaborate and interesting study of the ancient script.
He has made a careful study of this and by comparative
study of ancient authorities and modern commentators he
has succeeded in making clear to a tyro in these matters
the significance of much that is treasured and valued by
the Chinese connoisseur. The figures, of which there
are 46, add much to the value of the explanations in the
letterpress. The Honan bones have been called into
service. The patient work of Mr. L. C. Hopkins in his
continuous service to decipher these as well as the work
of the late Dr. Chalfant have helped the author.
In the course of his investigations, Dr. Yetts brings
out much that is significant. One thing, that the ancient
period of writing ended with the abolition of feudalism.
That is a striking lesson and reminds us that the abolition
of feudalism in our own country gave a spur to liberty.
Moreover, "the divergence in style had something to do
with the social conditions of feudal times. In the old
regime there was a lack of predominant unity, for the
Nine Provinces comprised 1,773 principalities, etc. These
were autonomous practically. Each had its own calendar,
rites and records, and, probably, its own character of
writing and sacral bronzes. Even allowing that the
predominant states and the Suzerain Power influenced
cultural standards nevertheless there must have been wide
differences according to locality in such a divided country."
It is an interesting point and pertinent question to
ask how far the Feudal kings could use the sacral bronzes,
and if they could use them, how these differed from those

In the process of exposition of these inscriptions
reference is made to some other important historical
subjects. For example the riddle of the character chou,
whether it signifies a name or has some reference to a
political event; also the relation of Li Ssu to the Small Seal.
The vandalism of Li Ssŭ in melting the bronzes, like the
burning of the books, was a method in the endeavour to
unify the land and begin the history of the country de novo.
It was deemed necessary to destroy tradition and get a
new style of writing. So books and bronzes went into the
melting-pot. But it failed in its object, and the only
result was the loss of much that was valuable.

Some of these bronzes also show traces of foreign
influence, such as the picture on Plate LXXII. This is a
sword, chien. It is of Scythian type found in various
stages of evolution over a wide area from Hungary to
Japan. As the author suggests, this question is one that
requires more exploration and in time we may get
material which will help us to understand better the
relation of China and the world.
No better example of the author's method of exposi-
tion could be found than the discussion on pages 66 and 67
of the Plate LXX, one of weapons of the type ko. It is
valuable from every point of view. From the picture of
the owl on Plate II, the conclusion is drawn of the existence
of a bird cult in ancient China. Thus many curious
historical hints are offered none of which is not valuable
to the student of ancient literature. Many difficult
problems are discussed, such as the interpretation of the
inscriptions found under Figures 31 to 34, pages 28 to 30.
The chapter on the Classes and Uses of Ancient
Vessels will be found useful by students who study the
ritual and the libations of the sacrifices of earliest times.
They occasioned much difficulty to the writers of an-
tiquity, for, as they said, the symbolical significance had
been lost, only the ritual remained. But in dealing with
them there is one important subject omitted by the author
and that is the question of simplicity and decoration. In
the sacrificial services simplicity was the highest mark of
reverence and sincerity. There was a severe simplicity in
the making of the altar and the vessels.
The instruments were made after the example of
heaven and earth.

Like the cerulean arch we see
Majestic in simplicity.
The simple was the reverent. So in the preparation
of the ritual vessels, in the brewing of the various wines,
and in the vessels of libation the guiding principle was the
obligation to avoid decoration and follow simplicity. So
it was with the ancient Hebrews, no hammer or chisel could
be used on the stones that were to serve the purpose of
worship. But the loss of reverence of the object led to the
increase of decoration.

Dr. Yetts, in the chapter on the Technique of Bronze
Casting, throws much light on this subject. The three
possible methods are given and adequately treated. It is
well worth a careful study by those who are interested in
bronzes.
There are 75 Plates, beautifully executed, some being
in colour. There is a long list explanatory of each and
dates where known, and dimensions are given.
The Bibliographies are valuable. Dr. Yetts has
made a collection of authorities, both European and
Chinese. The notes attached to the Chinese and Japanese
books are most useful. An index and a list of Tzu Hao
complete the contents.
The publishers and author are to be congratulated on
the production of a valuable work. The paper, binding
and type are all on a magnificent scale.
As we read over the text and gaze on one plate after
another in this memorable volume we are transferred
unconsciously to a very old world, different in many
respects from our own. These bells and bowls, the tripods
and the cauldrons, the wine beaker and the sword help us
to understand and appreciate the culture of early times.
These times seem to have emerged into history in full dress
without any account given of a preparatory stage. There
is much that is fabulous, much that is mythical, which may
not be irrational, and reveal a high state of culture. There
is that predominant doctrine of filial piety and ancestral
worship which is beautiful to behold, there are loyalty and
power. We may use the words of the late Marquis Tseng,
the Imperial historiographer, "As we gaze on these
portraits of the sages we are moved to imitate them and
feel the inspiration of their Tao." Similarly, as we look
on these bronzes and read the text, we are led to think of
the culture of the past with all it did for the unity and the
elevation of the Chinese race.
