FLOOD AND FAMINE IN NORTH CHINA
By Rev. F. B. TURNER
Vice-President of China International Famine Relief Commission's
Chihli Committee, and Chairman of Distribution Board.
It is common knowledge that, among China's many
sorrows is the frequent recurrence of flood by which
much destruction is wrought, and vast numbers of people
suffer from famine.
One of the regions most seriously affected in this
way is the North China plain, which has been again and
again inundated; large tracts of country being laid waste,
crops being ruined, homesteads collapsing, and multitudes
being brought to hunger and misery.
The common people regard these calamities as T'ien
Tsai (Visitations of Heaven); but an examination of the
physical conditions in this region shows that it is liable
to these disasters as much through the foolishness of man
as by the will of Heaven. The causes, though complex,
are not obscure: and were there the will, and were
adequate means available, these floods could be averted,
and the North China plain be made a safe, productive,
and habitable region.
CAUSES OF FLOOD.
The writer has been mostly concerned with the work
of relieving people involved in famine; and so is more
familiar with the results of these periodical floods than
with their causes. He is indebted to his colleague, Mr.
O. J. Todd, the Chief Engineer of the China International
Famine Relief Commission, for the following geographi-
cal and physical data, showing the causes of inundation,
and for the accompanying map indicating the parts of
the province of Chihli subject to frequent flood.
To the north of the province of Chihli are the moun-
tains near the Great Wall; and to the west a range of hills
along which runs the border between Chihli and Shansi.

Amongst these hills rise a large number of streams
which quickly become considerable rivers: from every
valley these pour forth their waters upon the Chihli
plain and seek an outlet to the sea.
The Pei Yun Ho rises to the north-east of Peking,
and flows southward across the plain. In the north-
west the Sang Kan Ho and other tributaries unite and
form the Yung Ting Ho. South of this rise the Sha
Ho, Tang Ho, and other rivers, which join and form the
Ta Ching Ho. In the neighbourhood of Pao Ting Fu
rise the Hu To Ho and the Yeh Ho; further south, near
Shun Te Fu, several streams combine in the Fu Yang
Ho, and later unite with others in forming the Tzu Ya
Ho. And towards the extreme south-west of the
province several rivers pour their waters into the Nan
Yun Ho or Grand Canal, which is, strictly speaking, not
a canal but a gathering together of several streams to
form an artificial river flowing north.
RESTRICTED OUTLET.
From the base of the western foothills the land does
not incline eastward towards the coast as might be
expected: the plain slopes vary slightly northwards.
There is an eastward trend, but it is much less than that
towards the north: far too little for the streams to force
their way directly to the sea. Consequently all the rivers
rising in the west bend northwards when they reach the
plain, converge on Tientsin, and seek their outlet to the
sea by the Hai Ho which flows from Tientsin to Taku.
A glance at the map will show that, with the exception
of the Ning Ho which carries to the sea at Pei Tang the
combined waters of the Chien Kan and Chi Yun Rivers,
there is not in all the province of Chihli a river of any
size which finds other outlet than the waterway between
Tientsin and Taku.
It will be evident that, even in normal times, this
single outlet must be taxed to its utmost capacity: and
that, with rains and freshets at all excessive, it cannot
be expected to carry away, with anything like sufficient
speed, the waters flowing into it. Moreover, it is a tidal
river, and every rising tide adds to the difficulty of
outflow.
So that when in the spring the melting snow pours
down from the hills into the rivers, and when in the
summer the streams are swollen by heavy rains, the

waters are banked up in the upper reaches, and overflow
is inevitable. As a consequence a vast area is liable to
be turned into an inland sea, and incalculable ruin is
wrought.
The plain is on such an almost dead level that there
are hardly any ponding areas; low-lying regions which
could hold for a time an access of water until it could
get away. There are two such areas, lying between Pao
Ting Fu and Tientsin, viz., the Hsi Hu or West Lake, and
the Wen An Wa (Wa=Swamp): but these are generally
covered already with water; and only in times of such
protracted drought as caused the famine of 1920 is there
sufficient evaporation to make them available as effective
ponding areas.
So that when flood does come these places are
speedily covered to a still greater extent; and their people
are the first to suffer.
The liability to flood is also increased in that these
rivers, after they have debouched upon the plain, and
throughout their courses, are badly silted up, so that
they cannot carry any great quantity of water without
overflowing.
Attempts were made in earlier years to meet the
difficulty of insufficient outlet for flood waters by the
digging of special channels running eastward to the
coast. Examples of these are the Hsing Chi Chien Ho,
Chieh Ti Chien Ho, and the Ma Chang Canal. But the
fall of the land eastward is insufficient; the channels are
too narrow and too shallow to be effective; and these
artificial waterways have also become so silted up that
their beds are in many places higher than the fields on
either hand.
DENUDATION OF HILLS.
The silting which has rendered all these Chihli
rivers so ineffective as water channels is largely inevit-
able; but it is to a great extent accounted for by the
wholesale, foolish and criminal denudation of the hill
sides. Were these covered with trees, brushwood, under-
growth and grass, much water would be held back, a
considerable quantity would be absorbed, there would be
time for reduction by evaporation, and what ultimately
reached the rivers would be impeded in its flow, and
would work far less havoc.

As it is, trees have disappeared; re-afforestation is
not carried out; brushwood has been ruthlessly cut down
for fuel and even grass roots are raked away. So that
when the rains pour down the hillsides, there is nothing
to impede their flow; the hills are swept bare of every-
thing, and even the earth is carried down in solution and
suspension, and deposited in the river beds. The waters
also descend, thus unhindered, with destructive force,
the violence of the flow doing as much harm as the
volume of water.
Anyone travelling through the country lying be-
tween the Peking-Hankow Railway and the foothills to
the west of it, and seeing the continuous stretch of stark
hillsides, will realise how the regular downpour of rush-
ing water from them is a perpetual menace to the people
of the Chihli plain. It is probable that there will not be
much further silting, for the hills already seem to be
almost wholly denuded of soil.
ATTEMPTS TO OBVIATE FLOOD.
Another attempt to avoid flood has been made by
the building of dykes on or parallel with the banks of
rivers: continuous mounds of earth placed where rivers
are prone to overflow. Examples of these are the 1,000
Li Dyke, which runs from east of Pao Ting Fu, by the
north side of West Lake, along the right bank of the Ta
Ching Ho and the Wen An Wa to the neighbourhood of
Tu Liu. Others lie along the right bank of the Tzu Ya
Ho: on the right and left banks of the Grand Ganal;
and on the right bank of the Upper Tzu Ya Ho and its
tributary the Yang Ho.
In earlier years these dykes are said to have been
effective: probably they were; for originally they were
supplementary to the natural river bank, and needed
only to hold back sudden, brief, and abnormal freshets:
and it was only the few feet at the surface of the swollen
streams which put strain upon them. But as time pass-
ed the rivers have become so silted up that their beds
are now in many places higher than the level of adjacent
land. As the bed of
river has thus risen, the dykes
have been raised also, until in many places the entire
body of water has nothing more than these artificial
banks to hold it in.

The writer does not possess expert knowledge, and
his view may be open to criticism; but, in travelling over
this country during many years, he has seemed to observe
the tendency of local Chinese to make good the facing
and to neglect the backing of these artificial mounds,
and to raise them at the top rather than widen them
throughout. It seems obvious that a mere embankment
cannot, for resisting and holding power, replace river
banks which have behind them on either hand the whole
body of the land.
And these frail barriers have to stand not only the
pressure of a great body of water; the water rushes
along at great speed, and with strong force so that the
dykes are scoured away. When winds rise the impact
of waves is added. It needs only for a bank, thus
thinned, to burst at one place for wide stretches of land
to be inundated.
The same conditions obtain, and these breaks occur,
in many places; with the result that in the recent floods
the whole area shown by shaded lines on the accompany-
ing map became an inland sea some 200 miles long by,
on the average, 60 miles wide, or approximately some
12,000 square miles in extent.
When the freshets have ceased, much of this water
gradually subsides, but the Tientsin-Pukow Railway
embankment hinders its flow eastward, and its escape is
still restricted to the outlet in the north-east of the
province at Tientsin. Much of the flood water, however,
does not for a long time subside; and the winter turns it
into great sheets of ice over which winds, blowing from
whatever quarter, are bitingly keen; the sufferings of
people in their island villages, deprived of the produce

FLOOD OF 1924.
In the summer of 1924 there was in this region of
mid-Chihli such a widespread flood: it was a repetition
of previous experiences on this plain; and it represents
what may be expected to recur if this serious menace to
the public good is not soon and radically dealt with.
Word of the flood and its ill effects having reached
the central office of the China International Famine
Relief Commission, its Chihli Provincial Committee was
formed, and steps were taken immediately to get into
direct touch with missionaries throughout the province,
and to secure their interest and help in the work of
investigating local conditions.
A circular letter was sent out in all directions asking
for such co-operation. With this letter were sent In-
formation Forms enquiring as to each hsien (or county)
where missionaries' work lay, the extent to which the
county was affected by flood, the number of people in-
volved in its consequences, the proportion of these likely
to be destitute during the winter, the number rendered
homeless through the destruction of their homes or vil-
lages, the co-operation to be counted upon from local
missionaries, the railhead or river port to which supplies
might be sent and the possibility of recruiting, from
among the most destitute, labour for a large scheme of
famine relief work.
To these enquiries replies steadily came in, and a fund
of information was obtained, making it possible to form
a judgment of the extent of destitution probable during
the winter then approaching. And from every hand
were received assurances of co-operation in further in-
vestigation and in the administration of relief.

Faced with this mass of destitute people, it was
appalling to the Committee to realise the meagreness of
resources at command, and the impossibility of dealing
adequately with this great distress.

After careful surveys by the Commission's En-

found for from 100,000 to 200,000 men. These were to

nearly a million people.
FLOOD CHANNEL TO THE SEA.
The plan was to dig a channel some 45 miles long,
500 feet wide and 12 feet deep from the Grand Canal
near Tu Liu to the sea (See Map). It would approxi-
mately double the possibility of outflow available in the
Hai Ho, the only considerable outlet; there was in this
project the promise of freeing the Chihli plain from the
constantly threatened danger of flood.
The scheme was estimated to cost $6,000,000. Urgent
application was made to the Government to appeal
to the Foreign Powers for agreement to a Customs
Surtax to be applied to this purpose. The Government
endorsed the idea and made the desired appeal. But,
to the deep concern of the Famine Relief Commission,
the granting of the Surtax hung fire; a certain foreign
Power witholding its assent pending the settlement of
some other matters under negotiation with the Chinese
Government.
The Famine Committee was assured of the practic-
ability of the proposed scheme, and of its value as a
means of immediate relief, and as an effective measure.
Therefore, in view of the hopes entertained, preliminary
steps were taken to obtain the beginnings of necessary
equipment: rope, carrying poles and baskets, digging
tools, etc., for earthwork, and mats with which to provide
shelters for the workmen.

Meanwhile by correspondence with affected districts
throughout the province plans were made for the recruit-

labour were fixed; arrangements for the transport of
labourers from their homes were tentatively made; and
all that could be anticipated was done so that recruiting
could begin, and men could be sent off towards the Tu
Liu end of the proposed channel work, immediately on
word being received that the Surtax was granted.
But winter came and went without that word being
received; and it was at length realised that a golden
opportunity had been lost; the scheme could not but be
abandoned. For it is to be remembered that it was only
while famine labour was abundant, and people on the
verge of starvation were keenly anxious for work, that
such a plan could be effectively and economically carried
out. Not that it could not be carried out at any time
were the needful funds available. The main idea did not
originate with the Famine Relief Commission: it was
contemplated by the River Conservancy Board. But the
Famine Relief Commission could have used the scheme
as a valuable means of relieving existing distress; and it
could have secured labour at the cost of five rations per
day per labourer (one for himself and four for his
dependents at home) at an expenditure many times less
than such a work would need if undertaken on a normal
wage basis, with contractors' profits to be included.
The assurance that home dependents would be re-
gularly fed by local agencies, manned by voluntary
workers, would have attracted men for the necessary
labour, especially from the late autumn to the spring, a
time when support was so badly needed, and no other
work was available. These considerations will show

LOCAL DYKE LABOUR SCHEMES.
Apart from the larger scheme contemplated, the
Committee encouraged in many places local labour
schemes, which would at once afford relief to the needy
and tend towards flood abatement or prevention. The
procedure was to offer loans at low interest to recognised
local authorities towards projects for dyke repair and
extension, to be effected by the employment of destitute
labourers. The services of the Commission's engineering
staff were placed at the disposal of local people; and
careful surveys were made and the lines of needed work
laid down.
Such loans were repayable in easy instalments,
which could readily be met by those whose land, hitherto
constantly laid waste by flood, would be rendered safe and
productive.
There were those who criticised the Famine Relief
Commission, and who thought that the funds it held-in
part contributed, and in part the residue of proceeds of
a former Surtax-should be wholly available for free
relief.
But, as has been indicated, “work relief" was the
considered policy of the Commission: it was held that it
was better to issue funds on a returnable basis, and in a
manner which would both ensure work and support for
the workless, and would put them in the position of being
able to repay, than to expend money upon free relief
with but temporary results. By this policy also it was
hoped that the Commission's funds would be continuously
available, and with little diminution, for relief.
As will be seen, the Chihli Committee, while pursuing
this policy, was not indifferent to the claims of those in
immediate need, for whom work was impossible.

An example may here be given of the working of the
loan policy of the Committee. A very valuable project,
financed by loan to the extent of half the estimated cost,
was a scheme of dyke repair and extension in Ching Hai
Hsien. Its object was, by repair of existing dykes, and
by new and effective embankments, to enclose the area
between the Grand Canal and the Tzu Ya Ho, from the
neighbourhood of Tu Liu southward, and thus to keep
out flood water.
This triangular tract (see Map) having Tu Liu as
its apex; on the east the Grand Canal and Tientsin-
Pukow Railway embankments southward to the Ma
Chang Canal; and on the west the Tzu Ya Ho and the
Wen An Swamp, has been periodically swept by flood
waters: agriculture was impossible. The aim was to
retrieve this land, make it available for agricultural use,
and secure it against flood.
The scheme was carefully elaborated by the Com-
mission's Engineers and was effectively carried out
under their oversight. Many breaks were solidly closed;
inflow of water from the south was blocked; many miles
of dykes were restored and connected up; and some
250,000 mu of land was reclaimed and secured against
flood. This large tract has been brought under cultiva-
tion, and last autumn excellent crops were reaped from it.
The loan of $29,000 was adequately guaranteed,
and the produce of the land, formerly almost worthless,
will make repayment easy.
In the case of many similar schemes, officials and
gentry were able to raise funds locally, and effective
work was done without more than the preliminary help
and surveys of the engineering staff.
In other cases negotiations fell through; local people
being unwilling to commit themselves to the necessary
repayment of loans desired.
Of these proposed schemes, two of much prospective
value, did not materialise in 1925, owing to the disturbed
state of the country at the time. Negotiations have,
however, proceeded, and arrangements have been com-
pleted for their execution during the present year.
These projects are for dyke restoration of consider-
able extent in the south and in the north of An Hsin
County.

NEED FOR DIRECT RELIEF.
But it was felt that labour relief schemes alone
would not meet the situation; for there was great and
immediate need, and poignant appeals were being
continually received. It was evident that in many
districts numbers who could not work, or for whom no
work could be provided, were in dire straits; were cold,
hungry, and homeless. Not only were these people on
the plain, when winter came, in the midst of frozen flood,
without food, and with inadequate shelter; from the
mountainous region of Chihli also there came stories of
great suffering and of the destruction wrought by the
waters rushing down from the hills.
Fu P'ing Hsien may be cited as an example. It is
about 60 li west of Pao Ting Fu. During the summer
a river in the neighbourhood of Wu Tai Shan had
brought down from the hills great quantities of sand, and
this had completely covered the farm lands and buried
the crops. Nothing had been reaped, and nothing could
be sown. In other parts hillside land slowly brought
into cultivation had been swept bare of earth and nothing
but the stony surface was left. When investigation
began the people were already reduced to eating the
bark and leaves of trees, and the husks of the last year's
buckwheat. Many were dying. Little boys were being
bought by the monks of the Wu Tai Shan Monastery to
be made novices. The people were in despair.

Emigration from their stricken land seemed the

The Committee, in spite of its meagre resources
available for free relief, felt that it must do something

GRAIN PURCHASE.
An appeal was issued in Tientsin for subscriptions,
and resulted in contributions of over $60,000: with this
grain was purchased for distribution.
It had been feared that the dislocation of railway
traffic owing to the civil war then raging, would make
the task of obtaining food and carrying it to the destitute
regions very difficult, if not impossible. In previous
famines the grain had been bought in Manchuria, and
transported to the railheads nearest to the various dis-
tressed districts. It was, however, satisfactory to find
that in many large cities and commercial centres grain
could be secured, and at a price comparing favorably
with the cost of cereals during the drought famine of
1920.
The explanation of this is that the Chihli plain is
not an absolute dead level; and that, while all the lowest
land was inundated and remained so, every tract of land

This was transported to local dumps in suitable
places in each group, and from these dumps it was
distributed to the selected destitute in the surrounding
villages. This was done after the issue of tickets show-
ing the number in the family: the people receiving grain
against their tickets at the dump. The whole operation
from the receiving of the grain from sellers to its reach-
ing the hands of the destitute was under the personal
direction of foreign missionaries and their Chinese staffs.
It is this personal and "international” oversight through-
out which assures to donors that their gifts will be well
administered.
The expenses of investigation, local transport, and
distribution to these people of 1808 villages amounted to
$3,814.52.
Direct relief (a month's food) was given in the
course of the winter or spring to 90,451 adults, and
31,826 children or in all 122,277 people. Children re-
ceived half-allowance. A month's ration for an adult
was 15 catties of grain (about 20 lbs); in a few cases this
was slightly exceeded; in some others it was diminished
a little so as to afford a smaller measure of relief to a
larger number. Money was in no case given but always
food alone. A full ration for one adult for one month
cost on the average 5412 dollar cents.
The grain given was kaoliang (sorghum), except in
a few cases where only maize or millet were available at
an economical price. It is desirable that famine diet
should not consist of grain alone, but should include some
fatty ingredient: in the famine 1920 this was supplied by
the inclusion, with the issues of grain, of a small measure
of beans which are rich in fat; or of bean cake which,
under analysis, proved to have considerable food value.
But in the late famine grain alone was obtainable.
There was an immense amount of labour involved in
the repeated investigations and selection; in arranging
for the local purchase, transport and distribution of food
and-though all in vain in the preliminary steps to-
wards recruiting for the proposed channel scheme. This
considerable service was gratuitously rendered by a large
body of missionaries, Catholic and Protestant, and their
Chinese staffs. Without this ready aid, which in time of
stress is always available, from these devoted men, the
Commission would be helpless as a Famine Relief
Organization.

HOW FLOOD MIGHT BE OBVIATED.
But though it is well to help suffering humanity, and
to save life, this benevolent work leaves almost as it was
the constant menace to the people of the North China
plain.
Surely here are conditions which cry aloud to be
dealt with, and which demand the best efforts of those
who have at heart the public good. What has been
squandered in one month upon civil strife would have
gone far towards curing this great evil. For it could be
cured; as has been proved by careful examination of the
affected region on the part of experienced Engineers
both of the Famine Relief Commission and of the River
Conservancies; and by partial but effective works which
have been already carried out by them.
River channels should be deepened that they may
carry more water. The system of dykes should be co-
ordinated: they should be everywhere strengthened that
they may effectively hold in the swollen torrents; the
Tientsin-Pukow Railway embankment, which now hin-
ders the outflow of flood water eastward, towards the
sea, should for many miles be pierced by frequent
culverts or flood passages.
The denudation of the hillsides by fuel gatherers
should be strictly forbidden; thereby would be avoided
the scouring down of the hills, the washing away of soil
from hillside farms, and the clogging of the rivers with
mud.
The present lack of undergrowth does undoubtedly
permit the descending waters to get away too quickly,
so that they concentrate on the plain in such quantity
as to overflow available channels already heavily silted
up. The depth of the water where rivers unite in the
plain is determined by the speed with which it descends
from the hills: the deeper the water for a stream with
any gradient the greater the speed continues to be, and

the stronger the scouring effect on banks and dykes:

So that the holding back, though but temporarily, of
this concentration, by a good growth of brush and trees
would help the situation materially.
Afforestation along the watershed, on a considerable
scale, would have ultimately the effect of holding back
the spring floods in the form of snow in sheltered areas:
much water would also be absorbed by roots of trees and
shrubs: and the reduction by evaporation would be not
inconsiderable.
But afforestation to be really effective would need to
be on a large scale, and to extend over a lengthy period.
Its benefits would only be received by a later generation.
That is, however, no reason why it should not be begun
and carried forward: though it is probably the reason
for the present selfish inaction.
What is of immediate concern, and would quickly
bring results, is the provision of an effective outlet for
water which cannot now get away through the only
available exit before it has worked havoc over vast areas
of agricultural land.
Such a project is that of the proposed Flood Channel
from Tu Liu to the sea. Famine conditions happily no
longer obtain to any serious extent: and to do this work
with famine labour and at the low cost which would
have been incurred in 1924-5 by the Famine Relief
Commission would not for the moment be possible.
But whoever undertakes it, and whatever the cost
entailed, it ought to be undertaken. And it would be
worth all necessary expense for it would rescue the
people of the Chihli plain from the constant danger of
suffering and loss and it would make that region the
safe and productive habitat of a contented and pros-
perous people.
This is a matter which might well be brought to the
notice of the British Boxer Indemnity Commission: how
better could a small proportion of its funds be applied
than by ensuring the safety, comfort and happiness of
half a province?
