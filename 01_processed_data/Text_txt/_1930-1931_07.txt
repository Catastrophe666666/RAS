THE BOOK OF CHANGES
AND GENESIS
By Rev. H. R. WELLS
The Chinese Classic called the Book of Changes is a
very ancient document.
By some it is supposed that its earliest text was made
and arranged by Wen Wang, father of the first Emperor
of the great Chou Dynasty, which began about 1122 B.C.
But many Chinese believe that it was, at least as far as
its figures, which are its basis, are concerned, prepared
by Fuh Hsi who lived about 2900 B.C., perhaps even reign-
ing for nearly one hundred years. It may be that he also
arranged the order of the figures.
If the Book was really the product of Fuh Hsi, it
must be about 4,800 years old.
In his time China had not begun to use characters,
and used various methods to record events. But,
apparently, at that period this new type of record was
invented, and if the figures of the Book of Changes, which
are sixty-four in number, were then made and arranged
in a definite order, they must be one of the earliest records
in the world, perhaps as early as those of ancient Egypt,
or those of the west of Asia, which have been studied and
examined for a very long time.
The method of development of these figures is an
extremely interesting study, but if it can be proved that
the book is really an essay of sixty-four words, or pictures,
or symbols, and that it is really a record of historical
value, it would add very considerably to its interest and
perhaps even its importance. It may even be that the
author wished to leave instruction for after ages, and the
composition may prove to be his contribution to world
knowledge.
It would seem that if this were his intention, that
intention has been hidden for milleniums, and that the
comments of later times on the figures and their lines
have really been, like the sands of Egypt and Western
Asia, the means by which the real contents were buried.

Presuming that Fuh Hsi formed the figures and
arranged their order, the next part of the book, which is
one of the great Classics of China and perhaps the earliest,
was written by Wen Wang about eighteen hundred years
later.
Wen Wang wrote a sentence about each of the figures.
Sometimes his comment consisted of several sentences,
but even when it appears to be only one sentence, each
word may have a great deal of meaning, and some of these
words are discussed at great length even to this very day.
As far as can be seen Wen Wang did not fix the order,
but that is a subject which might be argued. If he did,
the point of what is set forth in this statement would be
somewhat lost, as it would appear that Wen Wang did
not have the knowledge which it is felt that Fuh Hsi might
have had.
If this is so, it is probable that Wen Wang's notes have
misled later commentators, and even Confucius himself.
Wen Wang's son, Chow Kung, went a step further,
and prepared comments on each of the six lines of each
figure, relating his comments to those made by his father.
Confucius and perhaps others wrote certain parts of
what is now used as the Book of Changes. His remarks
are instructive, but he seems never to have found the
true meaning of the author. It is stated that he said,
"If some years were added to his life, and he gave fifty
of them to the study of the Yih (Book of Changes), he
would have no great errors.'
99
It is doubtful whether fifty years of study would have
brought him to the real knowledge of the author's thought,
as probably he did not have the facts on which he might
have based such a study.
During many years of study of this book, efforts have
been made to get back to the original text, and three years
ago it became evident that Chow Kung's part of the text
might be set aside as of no real value in such a study.
The question then arose whether Wen Wang had fixed
the order, and, if so, what principles he adopted in doing
it. This proved a difficult study, and Chinese scholars
seemed wholly unable to shed light on the subject.
Suddenly a year or two ago, as if by an inspiration,
it became evident that a number of the earlier figures
were capable of interpretation on the lines of Genesis,
and further study revealed that at least the first fourteen
figures seemed to be on the same lines as parts of the
earlier Genesis records.

One can easily conceive of other explanations for other

what has been observed seems so plain that it might only
confuse the issue if one were to introduce other material.
Fuh Hsi is credited with being the author of the
famous eight figures which are so prominent in many
Chinese works of art, and in ordinary life.
These eight figures had certain positions, North,

altered later. Whether this may have helped still further
to mislead those seeking to study the work of the sixty-four
figures or not, is not yet clear, but it is quite conceivable
that it might do so.

get the meanings of the eight three-lined figures in mind,
and as these have been evolved from the single lines we
may first observe them.
