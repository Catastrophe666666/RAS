A POSTSCRIPT ON TOMB-DESTRUCTION
By E. T. C. WERNER
In Vol. LVI (1925) of the Journal, I maintained that
the statement made in one of my books that the dynastic
tombs had been destroyed was correct, holding that
"though grave-mounds and images of the guardians of
the tombs, etc., are to be found in many places, the
dynastic tombs were destroyed in the sense that little else
than these-practically only the 'outer shell'-remains,
and that only in a relatively small proportion compared
with the original total number of tombs
that
they had been destroyed in the sense that they were
rifled, emptied of their contents, and obliterated, except
in a comparatively few cases."
I then proceeded to "place on record, as far as
possible, what actually does still exist in the matter of
grave-mounds, etc.," giving in extenso a mass of most
valuable information furnished me by Mr. Moore-Bennett
and illustrated by photographs taken by him, showing
the results of his visits to and surveys, etc., of Ch'in,
Han, Sung, Kin, Wei, and Yüan tombs.
I am now, through the further generosity of Mr.
Moore-Bennett, enabled to give what I think will be
regarded as conclusive proof of my original statement
regarding the destruction, as above defined, of the
dynastic tombs. In fact, Mr. Moore-Bennett's obser-
vations on the sites of these tombs prove even more than
I had at first contended, for he includes in his memoranda
the well-known Ming tombs, which are generally sup-
posed to have been spared and to remain intact to this
day. To repeat the expressive term used by him in
conversation with me, these, too, have been "gone over."
Here are, verbatim, the memoranda furnished by Mr.
Moore-Bennett, to whom, I feel sure, every student of
Chinese archæology will be deeply grateful. They are
particularly valuable in that he has made the dynastic
tombs the subject of special study on the spot during

CH'IN TOMBS.
Tomb of Shih Huang-ti. Lin Toun Prefecture.
During the war between the Ch'u and the Han factions about
206 to 204 B.C. this tomb was opened up, and a circumstantial
account of the rifling is given in the Han Shi. Another account
occurs in the Sung Shi. The sunken East face of the mound, to
this day, bears silent witness to the account given in the Han
dynastic History.
SUNG TOMBS.
Honan, Chêng Chou.
During the Yüan Dynasty, after the death of Temur-Kublai's
grandson, who reigned as Chêng Tsung, the Buddhist Lamas got
entirely out of hand and amongst other crimes broke open and rifled
all of the Sung Tombs, the Chief Abbot of Kiang Nan being the
prime mover in this act of vandalism. See the Yüan Shih, and also
compare the sides of the three larger and western mounds-east
faces-with the rest.
KIN TOMBS.
Fang Shan Hsien, Chihli.
No dynastic reference known to me. But all of these mounds
show that many years ago they were tunnelled into-but roughly,
not systematically-as, for instance, in the case of the Sung Tombs.
WEI TOMBS.
Loyang Plain, Honan (Toba Dynasty).
There are 16 large and 18 small Wei Mounds of pyramidical
shape, much weathered. My notes show that of these 11 large and
either 7 or 8 small (there exists ambiguity as to one), have been
tunnelled into, since they were built.
YUAN TOMBS.
South of Changtu on hill range.
Abbot of Changdu and the Ssu Lama of Dolonor state that all
these Tombs were rifled, first by the Ming Armies under Suta
and/or Fu Yu Ta, and again by the army that Yüan Shih-k'ai sent
to Dolonor, which burnt the one remaining temple at Changtu,
murdered the Abbott, and occupied the territory (1913).
Of the so-called Chou Mounds in Northern Wei-Pei north of
San Yüan, I have not seen one single considerable mound which
does not bear upon its face definite marks of having been tunnelled
into; and many of them show that they were tunnelled before the
lime mortar cement of the layers had entirely hardened.

The dynastic Histories of the various dynasties support
Mr. Werner's contention regarding the rifling of the tombs, and
anyone who had any knowledge of ground-settings and building-
weatherings, would find upon actual examination, support for the
historical references. Personally, as a mathematician, I am not
apt to accept Chinese historical writings on their face value, know-
ing by experience that such have been "edited" to an extent which
robs them, as a rule, of all precise and exact value, but where the
reference is supported by absolutely sure ground-indications of
similar purport, I accept them, but not otherwise.
Let me give a case in point. There are many historical refer-
ences to the tombs of various very interesting petty Chiefs, which
existed in the country of the Loop, i.e., that tract which lies between
the Toba Great Wall which runs from a point on the Great Wall of
China, North-West of Mi-Yün Hsien over the Head of the Nankou
Pass and West to the Shansi border, and the Great Wall which runs
North of Kalgan.
This Loop country was taken by the Chinese and Mongols again
and again, and has been the cockpit of North-east Asia, certainly
for two thousand years, from the days of Bagdur onward.
In it, and especially in that portion of it near Hwailai Hsien
and Hsüan-hua Fu, there are to-day the broken remains of three or
four hundred considerable tombs.
Now there would appear to be no historical references so far
as I can find down to that period when the Ming dynasty was
already suffering decay-Yung Tsung being on the Throne and
Essen, son of Toghan Khan, was again extending the Mongol
dominion at the expense of the short-lived Ming Power-of any
attempt or actual rif riflings of these tombs, but we have in Mongol
mongol
and in Chinese history of the period, definite references to the fact
that Essen, after he had smashed the Ming power at the battle of
Great Tu-Mu, capturing the Emperor and making his triumphal
march down through Northern Chihli, did rifle and break open all
the Chinese tombs encountered in his line of progress; and as to-day
there remains no single tomb of any size within the Loop that has
not been ruthlessly broken-in many cases so destroyed as to be
difficult to recognize as a tomb-I accept the historical reference at
its face value.

now known as "North China" has seldom for any long period of
