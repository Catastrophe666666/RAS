THE INDIAN FRONTIER
By EDWIN HAWARD
Probably there are many who will welcome the
opportunity of studying India apart from the burning
topics of recent political controversy. The story which
I am endeavouring to tell is one which is all too little
known and has features possibly unique in the current
history of the world. The only country which in recent
years has had a similar problem is possibly France with
Morocco, but there conditions lend themselves more
readily to final solutions. The North-West Frontier of
India is India's vulnerable point. Of the six thousand
miles which go to make her borders, three thousand have
the sea for their frontier. Roughly, about two thousand
consist of the impenetrable curtain of the Himalayas and
the wild hilly country of the North-East and extreme East
where Burma marches with China. But there remains,
roughly, a thousand miles from the Pamirs to the mouth
of the Indus, constituting the North-West Frontier line.
Perhaps the best idea can be obtained from a glance at
the map. The North-West Frontier, then, means the
tract of country running from the Hindu Kush in the
north down to Chaman in the south, bounded on the west
by Afghanistan and on the east by the Indus. The
boundary proper between India and Afghanistan is called
the Durand Line, after the British negotiator who
arranged the demarcation in 1893. The Durand Line is
not co-terminous with what is known as the Administrative
Border. In other words, there is between the Administra-
tive Border and the Durand Line a tract of what is called
Independent Tribal Territory, over which the British
administration exercises nominal control and does not
completely administer, as, for instance, in Baluchistan or
in the settled districts of the Frontier Provinces. The
Tribal Territory, sometimes called No Man's Land, is
inhabited by about 500,000 fighting men, about 120,000
of whom are armed with modern rifles. These tribes are
split up into clans, under feudal chiefs. They have a

common kinship with the Afghans in that they are of
Pathan origin, some of them having the Greek strain-
relic of the invasion of India by Alexander the Great.
They are constantly at enmity with each other, and they
can combine in sections for internecine warfare, as also
for warfare against either Afghanistan or against the
British Raj. I will not weary you by attempting, even if
I could, to give you a comprehensive list of the different
tribes. I will merely mention three who have persisted
in securing the limelight during recent years: the
Mahsuds, the Waziris, who gave their name to Waziristan,
out of which they have been practically driven by the
Mahsuds, and the Afridis. These three tribal groups
are split up into different clans whose hostility to each
other is latent or active at various times. They have a
primitive democratic family system of government. Their
chief instrument for conference and for settlement of
tribal disputes is known as the Jirga, and it has been
adopted by the British administrators as a useful means of
obtaining the tribal view and of conducting negotiations
through the political officers. Historically they are all
connected with Afghanistan, and although they perhaps
despise the Afghan as a fighter, they have a great reverence
for the Amir or King of Afghanistan, a reverence which
does not make them necessarily very docile all the time.
In the settled districts the more peaceful Pathan has close
kinship with these tribes, and although he may appreciate
the advantages of being in less turbulent country, there
are times when even in the settled districts the tribes can
count on adherence, as was recently shown during the
outbreak near Peshawar in 1930. It should be remarked,
in parenthesis, here, that further north are very
mountainous tracts, ruled over by independent chieftains,
of whom the most important are the Mehtar of Chitral,
the Nawab of Dir, and the Mian Gul of Swat. These
chiefs fight among themselves, but are consistently at
peace with India, and their country gives very little cause
for anxiety owing to the fact that they have very tight
control over their subjects. It is south of their country,
where the Afridis and also the Mohmands and the
Oraksais, as well as the Mahsuds and Wazirs, have close
connections with the people of the Frontier Provinces,
that is, the Administered Districts, that anxiety and
trouble occur. The number of expeditions conducted by
the Government of India against the tribes has been
on an average of one a year for the last seventy or

In the old days of Anglo-Indian history the frontier
problem gave rise to controversies which produced two
definite schools of thought, and advocacy of these two
different opinions led to most acrimonious discussion
among the British political officers and the British soldiers.
A soldier or political officer who believed in the Close
Border Policy, for example, did not merely disagree with
a man who was in favour of the so-called Forward Policy,
but simply attributed to that man all the vices with which
the human race can be afflicted. So violently was this
controversy apt to develop at times that it may be doubted
whether in the Close Border camp the man who was rash
enough to advocate the Forward Policy would be credited
with being even a good father to his own children. To
define the two policies, it may be stated that the Forward
Policy was that which demanded an advance up to the
Durand Line along its whole length, the disarmament of
all the tribes in the territory thus covered, and the
administration of the area on the lines of that existing in
purely British India. An illustration of the successful
application of the Forward Policy is to be found in
Baluchistan, where Bruce and Sandeman managed to
advance up to the Durand Line and pacify the country, to
which a loose form of administration was applied with
success.
The Close Border School maintains that it is imprac-
ticable to assume responsibility for the rugged and barren
country in which these tribes live without indulging in
expense beyond the dreams of the ordinary finance
members of the Government of India, and they would
leave the tribesmen to stew in their own juice and content
themselves with keeping them out when they raid on
the fertile tracts of administered territory. After the
Waziristan campaign of 1920 it became apparent that
the Close Border Policy led to as large expenditure on ex-
peditions as any member of the Forward School could
well expect to demand. Moreover, it certainly did not
have the effect of keeping the administered districts free
from the unwelcome attentions of the raiders, for the his-
tory of the districts of Dera Ismail Khan, Khohat, and Banu
during 1920 and 1921 was crammed with stories of raids

and lootings which grievously disturbed the peace of the
Frontier Provinces. So, in 1921, Lord Rawlinson being
then Commander-in-Chief, the whole question of frontier
policy was reviewed, and, broadened in vision perhaps by
the experience of the Great War, it was felt that the time
had come to put an end to the interminable quarrels of
the two conflicting schools of thought. Sir Archibald
Montgomery-Massingberd, the present Adjutant-General
in Whitehall, was then Deputy-Chief of the General Staff
to Lord Rawlinson, and he produced a most able review
of frontier history. This review stigmatized as ex-
travagant and ineffective the burn and scuttle policy
which the Close Border school had been able to enforce.
It was pointed out that although an expedition might for
the time being give the tribesmen quietus, it left behind
it memories which inevitably bore bitter fruit after the
tribe had recovered from the physical effects of the
punishment. It was admitted that financial consider-
ations, which then were particularly pressing on the
shoulders of the Government of India, precluded the idea
of an extensive expedition involving, perhaps, as many as
twelve divisions, to carry out the methodical disarmament
of the tribes. The gist of the new plan was the adoption
of the Forward Policy as an ideal and its piecemeal
application where conditions rendered it desirable. As
the campaign then proceeding in Waziristan was coming
to a successful termination, it was obvious that the first
application of this new Half-Forward Policy could be
usefully made in that country. Moreover, Waziristan
presented a special problem of its own. The Mahsuds
and the Waziris, unlike the Afridis, had no territorial
connections with the settled districts. The Afridis, so
the argument went, had land in Peshawar area or Khohat.
There was, therefore, some kind of a hold over them, and
it was assumed that the necessity for applying the
Forward Policy to Afridi territory would not immediately
arise. The Mahsud and Wazir presented quite a different
difficulty. In the summer, when temperatures rise, the
Mahsud found the time between the sowing of his crops
and the gathering of them most convenient for increasing
his slender resources by the process of battening on the
well-fed people of the plains. "He made his raids and then
retired into the mountain fastnesses to profit by the
proceeds. In the winter he made his way, singly as it
were, through British India right down to Calcutta and it
would be quite possible in December in Calcutta to meet

Now the conduct of the operations in 1921-3 led to
the construction of what became a circular road which
penetrated into the heart of Mashud country, and enabled
the military authorities to establish at Razmak, at a
height of 7,000 feet above sea level, a cantonment camp
where British and Indian troops could be healthily located,
and once along the circular road, speedy action could be
taken by armed forces to put down any tribal disturb-
ances. It may be surmised that the framers of this policy
built better than they knew. They were inclined to lay
more stress on the coercive capacity of a road than on its
persuasive qualities. In point of fact, the history of
recent years shows that the road has exercised a great
civilizing influence, and so successful has this policy been
that although the Mahsuds last year undoubtedly were
up in arms when the frontier was ablaze-it would have
been strange if they had not been-they were speedily
induced to revert to their new-won placidity of conduct,
and it is now probable that the construction of an outer
circular road will be shortly made, and thus bring the No
Man's Land of Waziristan still more certainly under the
control of the soldier and political officer.
The immediate problem, however, is not Waziristan,
but Afridi country. The Afridi has been the spoilt child
of the frontier. As already stated, it was thought that
he had too much of a stake in administered territory to
be unduly hostile. Still, even as far back as 1924 he was
not altogether satisfactory, and one of the indications of
this came from the protection given by Afridi tribesmen
to the kidnappers of Miss Ellis and the murderers of her
mother. The construction of the Khyber Railway through
the Khyber Pass kept the Afridi quiet up to a point,
because it gave him plenty of employment, and it may be
remarked that one of the features of the road-making
policy has been to employ local labour as far as possible.
În point of practice, this really resolves itself into paying
blackmail, because from my personal observation of the
road-building in Waziristan, the Mahsud contractor did
as little as he possibly could, and it was the regular
Pioneer Battalions who really put their backs into it
along the road. Nevertheless, the Afridis did very well
out of the Khyber Railway, which, incidentally, cost an

hitherto been enjoyed by the camel caravans. Merchan-

was a menace to their military security. This made for
dissatisfaction, and in spite of severe warnings, the
Afridis were ready to listen to the ill-judged counsels of
emissaries sent up into Afridi country by the India
National Congress. There must have been some serious
miscalculation in the frontier government over this. The
Hindu domination of the Congress and the well-known
hostility of the predominantly Islamic north to Hindu
counsels produced a false sense of security, and, helped
in some degree by Bolshevik agitation, the Afridis had
the idea that some form of revolution was afoot in British
India and that this provided honest tribesmen with a
legitimate opportunity for making hay. When the trouble
was over, the Government of India decided that the
medicine that had been applied so successfully to Waziri-
stan might well be applied to the Afridis. It should be
observed that the ostensible cause of the Afridi truculence
was due to an internal Mohammedan dispute among the
Oraksais, and also to the misrepresentation by agitators
of the Early Marriage Act in India. Owing to the hot
weather all that the Government could do at the time
was to establish a blockade; but in the Autumn it was
decided that to insure against future incursions an exten-
sion would be made of the road from Peshawar to Bara
Fort for ten or twelve miles to the edge of the Afridi
hills. This road is now being constructed, and it should
have the salutary effect of curbing the Afridis. The
operations have involved a certain amount of fighting, as
the telegrams have shown, but nothing as serious as
occurred in Waziristan ten years ago.
This brief discussion of the actual facts of the situa-
tion does not perhaps explain the fundamentals of this
frontier problem. It should be remembered that until
the British came to India, the pathway of the invader
into India has invariably lain through the North-West
Frontier. Three times in the history of India the fate of

India was decided at Panipat, a little village 50 miles

won. Alexander the Great invaded India over the moun-
tain passes of the North-West Frontier, and it will be
realised that the existence of this belt of mountainous
territory, inhabited by hardy tribesmen, well armed and
well accustomed to fighting, constitutes a serious menace
to any force which attempts to keep out the invader. If
there is no control over the tribesmen, an invading army
from Afghanistan would obtain from the tribes assistance
which would be exceedingly serious to India itself. I do
not wish to touch on the Power beyond Afghanistan. We
all know how the Russian menace has loomed large in
past Indian history. Perhaps to-day it would be unwise
to attach much military importance to it. Nevertheless,
the fact remains that in the north of Afghanistan, Soviet
Russia has by infiltration established herself fairly
securely, and there are air concentrations in Russian
territory that could bomb Peshawar from the air within
a few hours. The disarming of the tribes would not
necessarily mean a relaxation of the vigilance of the
frontier wardens, for on the other side of the Durand
Line are tribes over whom even the King of Afghanistan
himself exercises but the loosest control. And the resident
in the frontier districts must always be prepared for the
type of action which takes no account of the look of things
and is only concerned with the primitive law which
impels a man to fight for his existence. And it is a fight
in tribal territory, for the country produces faster than it
feeds, and the hardy Mahsud, accustomed as he is to
having his little precariously perched kach, green with
corn, swept away in a few moments by the sudden spate
of those unruly rivers, is not disposed to be squeamish
about the way in which he keeps himself and his family
going.
The policy of the Government of India in constructing
roads has been supplemented by a decision to bring into
Waziristan some method of irrigation in order to make the
cultivation of the crops less uncertain. It has been
suggested, too, that there must be oil there, but there
has been no geological survey to confirm that suspicion.
It may be doubted whether any such effort will be made in
the immediate future, for although the road policy has
been adopted, the Government seriously hold to the view
that the tribesman should be allowed, as far as possible,
to lead his own life. It is a very simple one. The old

although the conservatively minded may doubt the benefits
of that change. The Ford used by the troops for bringing
up supplies eventually found its way into the possession of
the tribal chieftain. He first conceived the brilliant idea of
using it as a kind of armoured car against his particular
enemies. Then he found that it was most useful in
carrying him down the road to the big city, as he thought,
in British territory. There he managed to go into the
Bazaar to purchase a velvet waistcoat, at times to attend the
local cinema and generally to have a very good time. This
experience changed his outlook on life. But the Mahsud
lady here came into the picture. She did not see why
she should not have the trip in the Ford car. It
certainly meant that the money which her husband spent
would not be diverted entirely from her use, and the latest
account that I have had from Khohat is that the old
Mahsud chieftain was bitterly complaining of the extrava-
gant tastes that his wife had after she had sampled the
delights of the very modest shops in Kohat Bazaar. If this
kind of notion spreads, the development of Waziristan is
likely to proceed at a pace which would astonish the old-

To come to a discussion of the country, the illustra-
tions will indicate characteristics of the country round
the Khyber Pass. In the course of this short lecture any
description must be plainly impressionist in character.
But impressionism is no bad guide to realism, and as I
look back I cannot help ruminating on the realities of the
country I have left behind. I have endeavoured to crowd
as much into a short lecture as memory, observation and
time permitted. Yet there is still much that could be said
to assist in focussing the picture. The skill and devotion
of the engineers on the road, the unflinching endurance by
the troops of the acute discomforts of an active campaign,
without any solace in the shape of the glory of a full-sized
war, the persistence of the spirit of intrigue, the preda-
tory habits of the tribes have been noted. It remains to
record a few final impressions jotted down in 1923 after
a visit to the road and the country it was destined to
dominate:-
Of the central figure in Waziristan-the Mahsud-it is
sufficient to say that he has three virtues: he is brave, he
is thrifty, and his lack of cleanliness is personal rather
than moral. Indeed, compared with tribesmen living
farther north, the Mahsuds are a clean-living set of men.
On the other side of the shield there are all the quick,
unbalanced temper of the savage, matching fully the
destructive whims of the Tak-i-Zam treachery combined
with a primitive adherence to certain arbitrary laws of
good faith, laziness, and "slimness." Blood feuds in
Waziristan are less common than on other parts of the
Frontier; possibly the Mahsud goes out of his way to
avoid them, yet it is computed that 80 per cent. of the
male population die a violent death. There are ways, too,
of escaping from the blood feud. If the murderer or one
of his family can jump into the grave of his victim or of
his victim's relation, or in some way get to the corpse
without being shot, he can claim immunity either for ever
or for a term of years. The story is told of how a Mahsud
girl, the daughter of a murderer, was captured by her
father's enemies. As luck would have it, while she was

standing with her captors, she saw the funeral procession
of one of their family. Swiftly she rushed to the bier and
seized the feet of the corpse, claiming the right to demand
what she would. It was given her and the brave girl,
caring not for her own safety, asked for immunity for her
father. It was reluctantly given, but it is sad to relate
that the heroine was treated badly by her captors
in consequence. Chivalry wins little respect on the
Frontier.
Again a murderer who flees from his village can
become one of another tribe who will take his blood feud
on their shoulders if on arrival at the place of refuge he
cuts the throat of a sheep and thus puts himself under the
"shadow" of the tribal chief. With people of this type
intrigue is a flourishing occupation, and the difficulties
experienced by the engineers in getting the local labour
to do an honest day's work are the constant preoccupation
of the Political Officer. The plain, straightforward ex-
planation of any incident on the Frontier is rarely the
correct one, and the "Political's" task is to get to within
as easy reach of the truth as he possibly can. This tends
to give much power for mischief to men whose very
indolence teaches them to delight in scheming, but the
Political Officer, as a rule, uses his own personality to
prevent misunderstanding coming between him and the
tribes through the malice or stupidity of third parties.
And what of the road when it is "through"? As you
recall the yellow line gradually becoming more marked
against the stern rocks in the heart of Mahsud country
you feel that you have seen there a portent. Sniping and
"shooting up" will not necessarily cease because of the
road, any more than pocket-picking has been abolished
from the streets of London. But the road must stand as
the first sign of civilization, and, if there are any possibil-
ities of developing the country so as to give its inhabitants
more than the present inadequate means of supporting
life, it must be by the road that they will be brought to
light. A geological survey of Waziristan would be in-
teresting. Rock formations in many places seem to be of
the igneous type, and oil appears to be there, too, although
in what quantity no one at present will hazard a guess.
Improved methods of protecting the kachas against the
river floods might also benefit the Mahsud agriculturist.
These are, perhaps, dreams rather than prophetic visions,
but the road is no dream, and where it runs it must bring
with it understanding.

The process may be said to have begun; the return of
the bones of the Mullah Powindah to their old resting-
place at Marobi betokens confidence in the meaning of
the road. As the white flag flies over the shrine at the top
of the green hill it signals that the spirit of Waziristan is
watching the road on the other side of the river. Fancies
like these crowd the mind as we look back on that wonder-
ful beauty spot in the heart of the country. Gazing from
the Indus at the fierce barrenness of the outer walls of
Waziristan, it is difficult to believe that behind them are
grassy hills, plentiful-sometimes too plentiful-streams
and brave attempts at cultivation guarded by precipitous
and rocky cliffs.
Having penetrated the walls and explored the myster-
ies they hold, you feel that you have been to one of those
wonder-countries of which the old-time travel books tell.
Evil sprites there must be in such a tale, but the good
genii cannot be left out, When the history of Waziristan
comes one day to be written, the engineer and the soldier
will, perhaps, figure as the good genii of the plot. And,
as every reader of fairy tales knows, the good genii con-
quer the evil sprites in the end. At present these good genii
hardly recognise themselves in that guise. Theirs is a
hard, practical, everyday life. Thrusting the road along,
guarding it and its workers against the dangers of the
country, they have little time to think of the meaning of
it all. They may vaguely wonder whether their work will
stand, whether it will accord with the spirit of the country;
but their wonder will not deter them from their task.
And at the end the road will emerge complete. What history
will be written on its winding miles only the future can tell.
