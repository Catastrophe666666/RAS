A MAP OF CHINA IN THE MAKING
By LADY HOSIE
The last work of the late Sir Alexander Hosie was the editing
of the "Commercial Map of China" for Messrs. George Philip of
London. This account of the origin, method and purpose of that
work was written for this Journal by Lady Hosie at the suggestion
of the Editor.
Maps of a country are milestones in the history of
its civilization, indications of its progress. Before my
husband was taken with his last illness, he was working
at the two most Ancient Maps of China, rubbings of
engravings on stone tablets, discovered by that tireless
sinologue, Professeur Edouard Chavannes of Paris, many
years ago, at Hsianfu. Graven in the 12th century, they
are amazingly accurate, a tribute to China's acumen in
the past, and a ground of hope for her future. We are
apt to take the maps of our own western countries for
granted: perhaps too much so, as anyone who reads
Anglo-Saxon history will agree. They were brave, in-
deed, those early sailors from English shores who crept
in their small barks, with fearful yet courageous hearts,
through the Cattegat, the Skaggerack and round the
shores of the Baltic; and who came back to draw a chart
of their wanderings. Still more admirable, possibly, was
that Chinese general of the Middle Ages who led his
envoy across Asia to the very banks of the Oxus. One
wonders what sort of a wife, mother, son he had, this
Chinese Ulysses, with a Chinese Penelope and Tele-
machus.
Till very recent years, there has not been possible
any sort of scientific survey of China. How, then, could
one set about making a Map of such a vast area, with
any hope of accuracy? With all the care in the world, it
was necessarily the case that every Map of China was but
a collection of approximations. Yet, when one considers
the very short time in which foreign travellers have been
able to journey to and fro in China, and the blood and
sweat of these travellers, men who set their teeth to

Comparatively few places in China, till lately, have
had their latitudes and longitudes determined. The
taking of a longitude requires a certain amount of train-
ing unusual in the average traveller. Baber, of the
Consular Service, determined a number of places for us
round about 1880, as did Capt. Gill before him-Gill, who
wrote that delightful book, long out of print, The River
of Golden Sand, (meaning the Upper Yangtsze.) A few
missionaries and naval officers have been similarly
attracted to make accurate geographical observations,
and have published their findings. But unless the map-
maker kept a strict and constant watch for these obser-
vations over a period of many years, he was prone to draw
the Liaotung Peninsula of Manchuria five degrees further
east than it ought to be, or set Chengtu, the capital city of
Szechwan, two degrees to the west. Within the last few
years, the Chinese Government have set themselves on
very scientific lines to survey the country, and are much
to be congratulated on their achievements. With the
progress of wireless, and its time-signals, still more will
be accomplished in due course.
When one considers all these handicaps to accuracy,
a Map of China appears a very wonderful thing. Nor
does it lack romance. Many nationalities and professions
have contributed to it as we have it to-day: consuls and
missionaries, merchants and soldiers. Marco Polo,
Venetian merchant, named the cities he passed through,
going east: Fa Hsien, Chinese monk, did the same, going
west to India through Kokonor, seeking Buddhist En-
lightenment. In more modern days, Baber and Margary,
both of the British Consular Service, sent back reports
of their travels which were eagerly read. Their bones
lie on the Burmese borders, Baber dead in his prime,
Margary murdered while seeking a peaceful passage for
a Trade Mission. Then there was Rockhill, the American
diplomat, who travelled the Tibetan border, and who wrote
The Land of the Lamas: and Dr. Susie Reijnhart, the
Canadian missionary, who lost husband and babe within
that cold region. Her book would draw tears from a
stone. Gilmour, the Scotsman, and Prejalvsky, the
Russian, were for long our only authorities on Mongolia.
Indeed, so deep is still our ignorance of that uncharted
land that we can believe true the tale one recent traveller
therein tells of certain present-day maps of Mongolia.

He says that he searched diligently for a city marked on
these maps, only to find at last that it had been razed to
the ground by Kublai Khan in the 13th century! One
looks forward to new light to be shed on this region by
American expeditions.
The botanists have more than done their share in
adding to our knowledge of Chinese geography, from
Bretschneider in Peking to E. H. Wilson from Kew.
Indeed it was Bretschneider's beautiful Map of China,
published originally in Russia for botanical purposes,
with its cities of China marked exquisitely clearly, that
was the foundation of the Commercial Map of China
which my husband edited for Messrs. George Philip of
London, the subject of this paper. Death claimed two
other botanists prematurely, Farrer and Purdom; but
they had done much good work before they, too, went
their way.
The French Catholic church has never been better
represented in geographical research than by the abbé
Huc and his colleague, Gabet. Their accounts of Sze-
chwan and Tibet are unique. The British Army has
had its part. Sir Francis Younghusband, later of
Tibetan fame, and President of the Royal Geographical
Society, began his name by his journey through the heart
of the Continent of Asia. And the tale is still going on.
I think of Sir Aurel Stein, of the Indian Archæological
Survey, discovering the buried temples and camps in far
Kansu; delaying an hour too long to take a longed-for
panoramic photograph of the Karakoram Mountains, and
enduring the tortures of frostbite three weeks in con-
sequence, while his men bore him back to Leh, to a
mission hospital on the Indian border. I think of that
joyous, gallant gentleman, General George Pereira, who,
not content with accomplishing, with a badly damaged
leg, his marvellous trek from Peking to Darjeeling by
way of Lhassa, returned to his self-appointed task on
the marges of Tibet only to die in the wilds. And of
Mr. Teichman, British Consul, overcoming ill-health in
order to make peace betwixt Tibetan and Chinese. At
the back of all the books written by these undaunted
people may be found the little route maps, looking so
dull, meaning so much. These are the maps which have
made our maps of China possible in the first place.

necessary to estimate the personal element in quoting or

to a mountain pass as being 16,000 feet. But unless my
husband knew him and could trust his accuracy, or unless
the writer definitely stated that he had found the
boiling-point of water on that pass, as was my husband's
invariable custom, his figures were put down as not
proven. Similarly, his figures for population could only
be taken as guesswork, although his observation as to
habits and customs, food and dress, might be irre-

The Map of China which my husband edited was the
result of a life's residence and work in China, and of
friendships with Chinese as well as foreigners. The
Viceroy at Cheng-tu was at pains to go into minute details
with him when he, the Viceroy, took the census of that
city. Many Chinese officials gave him copies of the maps
of their localities, maps and histories of their magis-
tracies being part of the old-time dossier handed to an
incoming magistrate. But since maps in China used to
be made mainly for military purposes, it was sometimes
suspected of the foreign traveller that he was travelling
and making maps for purposes of invasion. When my
husband was Opium Commissioner, journeying through
the heat of summer, looking into the matter of the poppy,
he arrived at Lanchow, the capital of Kansu. He was
rather coldly received by the Viceroy. Later his
reception grew warmer; and he heard from his Chinese
writer that the Viceroy had sent for him privately, to ask
if the foreign official were not secretly there to prepare
a map of Kansu for possible British invasion!
But, indeed, the idea of making a Map of China did
not enter my husband's head until he found that he had
done it, almost inadvertently. His first idea had been
simply to superimpose on another map the economic and
commercial products of the country, these being his real
passion in life. It was only as he found cities missing,
or on the north side of a river when he knew that they
should be on the south, that he found that he was making
a new map. The actual drawing was, of course, done
by the publisher, and took many months of highly tech-
nical work. It is of interest to note that the publisher,
Mr. George Philip, has since then himself edited a Com-
mercial Atlas of the World, which is a chef d'œuvre in
its own way and on something of the same lines.

mineral, on the map. He had very rarely to refer to his
diary for these. Such and such a place brought up in-
stantly to his inward vision memories of silk filatures, of
coal mines or blast furnaces, of native paper factories or
quiet market gardening. It appeared to him very little
use merely to know the name and whereabouts of a place.
To him each city was inevitably connected with its vege-
tation, its minerals, its factories, and means of transport-
ation. He had been Commercial Attaché in his time,
moreover, and had witnessed the stupendous and rapid
growth of manufactures on modern lines in China. With
the invaluable China Year Book and the Customs publi-
cations always open beside him, to refresh his memory,
he had these manufactures indicated on the Map. And
you may see, thereon, by an ingenious system of symbols,
devised by Mr. Philip, how China, from Harbin to Canton,
in all her huge cities, has evolved bean-crushing mills,
egg-drying factories, cotton mills, flour mills, engineering
works, and many other signs of the industrial revolution
which, whether for weal or woe, is the unavoidable re-
sultant of the discovery of machinery.
In map-making one owes much to one's predecessors;
and every map has its own special virtue and failing.
Some are too big for certain purposes; others are too
small. Some have too little detail: others too much. It
is hard to strike the happy medium, especially with such
a huge country as China with its very awkward shape.
Many maps omit all Manchuria farther north than
Moukden. But my husband maintained that Manchuria,
ungainly though its shape is, was as much China as
Honan or Kueichow, and must appear in toto. Sinkiang,
the New Dominion, must also be included in a map of
China, he thought. Other map-makers differ. Yet all
lay the foundation for those who follow.
My husband's admiration for the compilers of the
China Inland Mission Atlas was very great. The initial
toil must have been enormous. The China Inland
Mission, alone, out of all the missions, owing to its
scattered areas, was first able to give us a picture of
China Proper. I have spoken of Bretschneider's beauti-
ful little map. Later came the mighty tome of the Postal

Atlas, the summing-up of the work of that marvellous
administration, to which every foreigner in China must
pay homage. The Far Eastern Geographical Atlas, also,
with its attention to detail, laid another stone in the
temple of our knowledge of China. Each map-maker
must carefully compute the knowledge acquired by his
predecessors, sift the evidence, reject and correct inaccu-
racies, add his own researches, and then try to make of
the whole a proportionate and clear picture, with all the
information thereon easily available to the general public:
a mirror of exact truth, as far as in him lies.
The matter of proportion is one of importance.
There are nowadays about two thousand Hsien cities in
China, the terms Fu, Chow, Ting having been technically
done away with. My husband determined that each one
of these two thousand should appear in his Map. On
several occasions he had needed to journey to a Hsien
city, and had been unable to find it on any foreign map;
whereas, he discovered later, a village in its neighbour-
hood might be registered on the map. This was because
a foreign traveller, making a route map of his journeys,
would naturally insert the village he had passed through,
but omit a Hsien city distant perhaps twenty miles, to
which he had not been and of which he only heard by
word of mouth.
Now the names of these two thousand Hsien may be
found in lists issued by the Chinese Government. But
the verifying of their positions, and the correction of
their exact places, was indeed a labour. There were
many complications. In Manchuria, for instance,
Chinese, thanks to railways, are migrating continually
north and west, till Inner Mongolia promises soon to be
the home of nomads no longer, but one waving plain of
wheat or millet. Large new cities in Heilungkiang have
sprung up like the mushroom cities of Canadian Prairies.
On the Korean-Tumen border also, new trade marts are
beginning to flourish. There is always trouble in the
demarcation of a border line. General Feng has lately
set himself to alter the northern borders of Shansi, and
may yet make the loop of the Yellow River a fertile
garden with new towns and villages. Then there are
the great spaces of the Tibetan borderlands. What few
names there were, my husband would frequently, some-
times to my uneasiness, eliminate. He would imper-
turbably bid me look up his old Parliamentary Papers;
and there I would find that most of such places consisted

The spelling of Chinese place-names was another
consideration. The varieties of spelling are legion. He
decided that, for motives of trade and convenience, it was
wisest to adopt that in use by the Chinese Post Office.
But the Chinese Government, in 1912, on the inauguration
of the Republic, altered a large number of the place
names; which is a habit of Chinese Governments at the
end of a dynasty and very disconcerting to cartographers.
Some names were but slightly altered: others were
completely changed, such as Kwangchang to Laiyuan.
We had occasion to visit this Hsien city, which is on the
Chihli-Shansi border. With great difficulty, as neither
its old nor new name appeared on any foreign map
of China, we had learned its whereabouts from a
Chinese friend. When we reached the place in 1919, just
seven years after its name had been altered, we were
surprised to learn that the new name had grown to the
town so well that the inhabitants, in seven years, had
forgotten the old name and hardly recognised it! The
same might be said of Loyang, the headquarters of Wu
Pei Fu. It is almost incredible that, so few years ago, it
was known to all and sundry as Honanfu. On the other
hand, just occasionally, the new name fails and the old
persists, which is another source of embarrassment to the
map-maker. This is, however, rare. Still, one case is
prominent. The new name of Haichow, the sea-port of
Kiangsu, where a branch railway now ends, is Tunghai:
and Tunghai is the name on Philips' Commerical Map of
China. Yet the old name, Haichow, is that in constant
use.
All these changes had to be indicated in some
manner, and they appear at the end of the Handbook.
The Index to such a Map is very important. Four
thousand names are apt to stray into wrong portions of
the alphabet, even though systematically written on
cards, in a curious sheep-like fashion. As for the dotted
lines marking the borders of China, what with wars and
politicians, it is impossible to claim them as exact. They
ought, by rights, to change every few months. The only
feasible method is to draw a conventional line, in the
knowledge that those acutely interested in such border
problems are aware of the facts of the situation. My

But it would be hard to say how often the tide of war

Again, the rivers of China are of the first importance.
It is a story to make us smile that of the British Fleet
watching the mouth of the Yellow River for months,
before they discovered that they were watching the old
mouth, which the river had left for its present outlet long
before they appeared on the shores of China! Yet how
little do we still know of Chinese rivers. And how often
has a traveller in Interior China, with its lack of railways,
so few in mileage compared with India, sighed over its
execrable roads! Therefore my husband, who had
travelled down so many of China's rivers, placed little
anchors, black for junks, blue for steamers, and red for
motor launches and small gunboats, on each navigable
branch of Chinese rivers, to mark the head of navigation.
The Yellow River, with its main length black to denote
its unnavigability all these centuries is truly China's
Sorrow. The great flood of the Yangtsze, on the other
hand, now carries steamers on its bosom, in the right
seasons of the year, far up beyond Suifu, right up to
Ping-shan Hsien, marked with a blue anchor. Even
there one can take junk for a further forty miles to a
spot marked by a black anchor. Similarly its tributaries
are marked; as is also the West River, that rich outlet
for the trade of Southern China, when peace returns.
One of the greatest shortcomings in the the education of
the modern Chinese student seems to me to be his sad
lack of knowledge of his own country. In the West, we
as children learn to draw outline maps of our country,
and we study its resources as a matter or routine. The
modern Chinese student can tell you of the growth of
democracy in Europe or the Constitution of the United
States, but barely knows his own provinces. This seems
disproportionate. He can generalise on the enormous
riches of China, but does not know what these are or
where they may be found. I have made a point of
showing my husband's Map to Chinese students, most of
whom are supposed to have had an exceptionally good
education. Their comments have been pathetic in the
extreme. On this Map, there are large, oval, blue marks
to indicate coal-fields. The position of these invariably
fills them with surprise. For the first time, they begin

to realize the value to China as well as to Japan of the

never to have seen a Map of China before. A Map of
their own country, openly displayed on the wall, is surely
a first essential in a Chinese school. Its presence would
help in the training of reasonable and well-grounded
young men and women. The study of its own Maps is,
in very truth, a milestone in the history of a nation's
progress.
My husband's right foot had been frostbitten during
his travels in China. He had to have it amputated the
day after we despatched the Map, finished, to the pub-
lishers. Exhausted though he was with his toil, in pain
with his foot, he yet said to me that his long journeys
and the succeeding work had been worth while, if he could
thereby add his mite to the fostering of human knowledge
and the furthering of world commerce. He believed
honest trade to be Heaven's gift to man, one of the most
quietly efficient, if sometimes unrecognised, handmaids
of international peace and understanding.
