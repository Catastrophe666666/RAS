A GERMAN TRANSLATION
OF THE HSI HSIANG CHI ()
(A Review)
By Dr. M. E. R. F. MEERKERK
Usually when a book is published it is appraised by
the critics according to its merits and their lights; for a
week or so it occupies a prominent place in the bookshops
and their advertisements; finally some other book is
published and replaces it in our minds-and is forgotten
in its turn. A review about such a book has to be
"actual," if it is meant to be of any value at all; who
cares to hear about the movies of last month? Very
rarely-much more rarely than the publishers want us
to believe a book is published that is of real importance,
not only for the short time that it is "a recent publication,'
but also for years to come. Comments about a book like
that never grow stale; neglect by the critics cannot harm
it.
99
Such a book then, is Das Westzimmer.¹ Still, this is
no reason why this Journal should be as late as this in
reviewing it. Let it be said at once that the Editor is in
no wise to blame for this delay; it is all my fault; and I
offer my best apologies to the Editor and the author,
Dr. Hundhausen.
This is not the first time that the Hsi Hsiang Chi
has attracted the attention of the Western world.
Stanislas Julien and others have made translations of the
whole text or part of it; some of these are scholarly, "with
various notes and copious indexes." On the other hand,
this is the very first time that poetry in the Chinese
language has been rendered into poetry in some foreign
language. In this respect Hundhausen's work is abso-
lutely different from that of his predecessors: their work,

to say, a rendering of the meaning in unrhymed prose, or
rhymed prose, wrongly called verse. Why do translators
hardly ever become famous? Why is a man like Fitz-
gerald an exception? Doubtless there were and are many
professors who could and can make a more exact trans-

The Hsi Hsiang Chi is a Chinese lyrical drama in the
Chinese language; Das Westzimmer is a Chinese lyrical
drama in the German language. It needs to be appre-
ciated as a work of art in German; in order to be able to
do so, one has not to be somewhat of a sinologue; all that
is needed is a taste for poetry and knowledge of the
German language. Here there are no notes to explain
the "typical beauties of the Wên-li"; no explanation how
difficult it is to translate it at all, as Stanislas Julien justly
-and naïvely-points out: and we are far away from
Giles' definition of a Chinese poem, "at its best a hard nut
to crack." The beautiful printing, the decent paper, the
good illustrations are those of a work of art, not of a
scholarly dissertation. And the text, carefully following
the original, is not meant to be the work of a sinologue;
it is meant to be, and is, the work of a German artist.
I even doubt very much if Hundhausen is what we
call a sinologue; he is something I was going to say, far
better, but let me change it into-something quite
different; he is a poet; of a kind that is exceedingly rare
and valuable; the kind of poet who writes rather in-
different verse as long as he depends on his own inspira-
tion, and who rises to sublime heights when he renders
into song in his own language what has struck him in the
art of some other language. In this connection I would
make bold to say that Hundhausen is the best poet of
Chinese verse we have ever had. As it takes only six
lines to prove this assertion, they may follow here:
"Und der tiefe Sinn, der in allem war,
Der wurde mir plötzlich vertraut und klar,
Beinahe wurden es Lieder.
Doch alle die Worte, die ich erdacht,
Vergass ich wieder, und dann kam die Nacht,
Und ich finde die Worte nicht wieder."
There is nothing like this either in French or English
translations that I know of.

The contents of the play I can suppose to be suffi-
ciently known to the readers of this Journal; so all that
remains to be done is to give a few samples of the quality
of the verse. Take this song of Dschang:
"Ich wanderte lernend umher im Land;
Da war keine Fessel, die haltend mich band
Wie Sommerfäden im Windeshauch.
So schwebte, ein fahrender Schüler, ich auch.”
How closely this follows the Chinese text; and still
how German it is! And I cannot think of a higher com-
pliment than this one: it would be easier to translate it
from the Chinese into English, that to translate from the
-so much easier-German into English. The reason is
that Hundhausen's verse gives the full equivalent (as
intricate in rhyme and rhythm as the Chinese original is)
in German poetry of what he found as Chinese poetry.
And take this song of the charming Ying-Ying, “die
versteht zu nähen und zu dichten, zierlich zu schreiben
und zu rechnen":
"Die Weidenranken
Erzittern und schwanken,
Vom Winde berührt;
Und Spinnfäden schwingen
Vom Winde entführt
In zierlichen Tänzen.
Sie sinken und schlingen
Sich zärtlich zu Ringen,
Die Blüten zu kränzen.—
O, schimmert jetzt nicht
Dort hinter der Türe
Durch die Kugeln der Schnüre
Ihr Blumengesicht?"
Or this one, where the syntax is almost broken over the
garland of rhymes:
"Ich erwarte

Seide,

Zitternden
Rauch:
Ying-Ying."

Once in Germany I heard a song that sounded familiar.
I asked what it was and the answer came: "Das? Das ist
ein Volkslied, wie man so singt." (That? O, that's a
folk-song, as they are accustomed to sing them). I was
not satisfied and looked it up and found that it was a song
by Eichendorff: I told my friends and they said: "Yes,
that may be; but now it is a folk-song."
And if there only is a man who will make the right
music to these words, I know that within a few years
from now, the following stanzas will no more be Hund-
hausen's verse, than the best of Eichendorff's songs are
Eichendorff's any longer: they will be a Volkslied.
"Heute tröstet meinen Kummer
Kein verliebtes Lied,
Und mein Auge flieht der Schlummer,
Weil es ihn nicht sieht.
Möcht hinausziehn zu den Bergen:
Doch zu krank und matt;
Möcht im Zimmer mich verbergen:
Doch des Zimmers satt.
Kann nicht schlafen, kann nicht wachen,
Bleiben nicht, nicht fliehn,
Kann nicht weinen, kann nicht lachen,
Denke nur an ihn."
And when we hear that, we shall repeat what we say
now: "Dem Dichter Dank und Heil!"
