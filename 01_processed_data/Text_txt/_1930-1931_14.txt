CHINESE POEMS
Translated by CHARLES BUDD
A SONG OF AUTUMN WINDS
BY THE EMPEROR WU OF THE WESTERN HAN DYNASTY,
Reigned from 140 B.C. to 88 B.C.
The Autumn winds are blowing,
And white clouds fleck the sky;
The leaves are sear and falling,
The wild geese homeward fly.
The lotus flowers are opening,
And asters scent the air;
And midst these scenes I'm thinking
Of you, my Love, so fair!
As o'er the stormy waters
Our high-pooped boat we sail,
And watch the foaming surges
Beat on us till they fail.
To drum and cheery fluting
We sing the boatmen's songs,
Forgetting, while rejoicing,
The world and all its wrongs.
But even in our gladness
We feel that youth is brief,
And age will soon o'ertake us,
With death to end our grief!

CHINESE POEMS
A SNOWSTORM
This poem was written by Shen-Chow, a famous
artist and poet of the Ming Dynasty, on beholding an
ancient picture of a snowstorm which he discovered in
the attic of a monastery.
After ten days in the city,
Mad with glare and scorching gust
And the brain is whirling widly,
Choked my eyes with burning dust.
Pleasant then to see before me
In the attic of the priest
League on league of snow-clad country
To my arid sight a feast.
Small the measure of the canvas,
Which displays the snow in flight
Yet so real the white flakes falling
That I shivered at the sight.
Long, lithe willow branches bending
Laden with the frozen sleet
While through thickset bamboo copses
Whistling blasts of winter beat.
Moored by the farther margin
Of the chill dividing stream
Waits the ferry-boat for travellers
But of man there's not a gleam.
Staggered are the rooks in flying
By the West wind's gusty course,
Far off wild geese cackle loudly
Blinded by the white storm's force.
Light limned are lines and shading,
By the hand of genius bold
Showing countless miles of landscape
As a gem of perfect mould.

Just as if some leagues of Kiangnan
At this moment came in view,
But my aged eyes need rubbing
To perceive the rocks anew.
Painting such as this is genius
Of the highest, rarest kind,
Showing art's profoundest mysteries
Captured by the master's mind.
Through the classic age of T'ang-Chao,
Specially by the men of Wu,
This rare work was closely guarded
With a care and reverence true.
And its value went on growing
Through the Northern, Southern Song
And the wealthy sought to purchase
Showing purses broad and long.
Costly relics oft are hidden
In the mansions of the great,
But a phoenix seldom dwelleth
With the poor of low estate.
Once before I saw a snow scene
On a canvas one-foot square
Marvellous but over-crowded
With a wealth of genius rare.
Now in age I gaze in wonder
On this greater picture scroll
In which the master's mind has let
All its lofty thoughts unroll.
Surely on this priceless relic
Heaven bestowed a special ward,
Teaching Tai-Kiu's sons and grandsons
How to judge it, how to hoard.
So they wrapped the prize in paper
Very tight and very small,
Hiding it within a furrow
Bored deep in the temple wall.

THE SOUTH BANK OF THE BROOK
BY YUEN I-SHAN,
Kin Dynasty, A.D. 1115 to 1234.
On the south bank of the brooklet
Wine was made of generous brew;
On the north side of the brooklet
Groves of flowering plum trees grew.
There I spent a pleasant summer,
Last year and the year before,
Drinking often and admiring
Plum flowers on the farther shore.
This year winter tarries longer,
And the flowers are hid from ken;
For they shrink from such wild weather,
As a maid from riotous men.
Poets love the flowers and mountains,
Shaded vales where spring first peeps,
Where one sees the wind-swept hill-tops
Just emerged from cloudy heaps.
In the deep and quiet gorges,
Safe from boisterous storms above,
Flowers and fragrance dwell together,
As a pair in mutual love.
Now the north wind's howling round me
In my mountain lodge at night,
With a sorrow, I seem stricken,
Of an overwhelming might.
