OF
THE IRRIGATION WORK OF THE
CHENGTU PLAIN
By Rev. T. TORRANCE, F.R.G.S.
All visitors to Szechuan who come as far as Chengtu
are surprised at the productiveness of the Chengtu plain.
The surprise deepens into admiration at Kwanhsien,
() a city at its upper or N.W. edge, when they see how
the rushing waters of the Min river have been divided
and diverted by an artifical side-channel which again
divides and redivides into a fine meshwork system of
irrigation that is nowhere equalled in China. Naturally
they ask who first conceived this big engineering feat, for
of course, the man who made famine here an impossibility
deserves to be retained in the affectionate remembrance
of his fellow-countrymen.
On the answer there verily hangs a tale. Ninety out
of every hundred savants in the West say it was Li Ping¹
who became Prefect of Shuh soon after this part of
China was conquered by the state of Ts'in in B.C. 316.
At first one accepts their word for it, and supported as the
assertion is by local topographical works, there seems no
reason for doubt, until we begin to read something of the
general history of the province. Then it is found that
others had a share in the work both before and after his
day. Li Ping had much to do with the extension and the
perfecting of the working of the system; he indeed was a
man of genius and a great public benefactor, but it is
open to serious doubt if he did all that some nowadays
give him credit for.

The rivers T'o and Ch'ien presumably are the same as
those called in provincial histories by the same names,
though in the latter case the character is differently
written. The Ch'ien () flowed out of U-lui mountains
at Kwanhsien and to the South East flowed into or
became the T'o. Whatever be the particular identi-
fication, the general reference is certain: King Yu,
according to it, gains the credit of being Szechuan's first
conductor of rivers. Local historians frankly admit this,
though one or two think his works lay with the main
stream of the Min below Kwanhsien and not with the
artificial channels on the plain. They seem to base their
opinions on a statement in the Yuen dynasty edition of
the General History of Szechuan.
The second mention of irrigation in Shuh is more
explicit and more interesting. It occurs in the "Shuh Chi"
() compiled by Shang Chu () who lived in the
days of the Tsin dynasty A.D. 265-419. It quotes a book
of the Hsia dynasty B.C. 2205-1818 which says that "the
'Tao Kiang' or conducted river of the Min mountains
parted to the East and became the source of the T'o.
Its rise was distant and great. It was the head of the
'four tuh' () or sluice rivers that, divided, made the
nine rivers. The hills and groves of Shuh with its waters
and fisheries, its gardens and orchards, unremittingly
flourished in their seasons."
The "Tao Kiang," we know, was the Kwanhsien river.
Tao Kiang-hsien was the name of an old city situated
20 "li" from the present Kwanhsien. We know too that
the river to the east did become the T'o. There is no
difficulty here in the identification. If this ancient his-
torian be correct, irrigation works have been established
on the plain for over three and a half milleniums. Shuh
as early as the Hsia dynasty was a land of rare prosperity.
No reference is made to King Yu: no attempt made to
trace the river works to his initiative or skill; the in-
ference is simply that they existed at that time. The
West too had its nine rivers-rivers which fan-like con-
verged into one river, the T'o, which flowed South and
joined the Yang-Tze at Kiangyang the present city of
Luchow.

The third reference to river works in Szechuan is
again in the Shuh-chï when it records that Pieh Ling
or Kai Ming (-) a minister of Shuh several
hundred years B.C. cut a passage through the U-lui
mountain to save the country from inundation. His
undertaking was regarded as so meritorious that Wang
Ti the Emperor of Shuh following the example of Yao
and Hsuen vacated the throne in his favour.
Wang Ti was famed for his zeal in fostering agricul-
ture. Kai Ming's labours doubtless served directly and
indirectly to promote Wang Ti's favourite object and the
Emperor recognising this was glad to have a successor
who would carry on his efforts in the people's behalf.
Now where was the mountain of U-lui that Pieh Ling
cut? On its definite location rests the decision of a very
important question, viz., whether he or Li Ping is to be
given the honour of making the famous cutting through
the ridge of the hill at Kwanhsien that draws off a large
volume of the water of the Min to one side for agricultural
purposes.
Almost all present day Chinese say it was Li Ping
who made the cut. In this the topography of Kwanhsien
and other works copying it support them. Obviously the
two men cannot share the honour of initiation, since the
one lived 200 years after the other.
The Chinese who base their contention in favour of
Li Ping do so because of a passage in Sze-Ma-Ts'ien's
history (Shi-Ki) which says that Li Ping cut the detached
hillock called Li-tui to avert the evils of the Moh river

At Kwanhsien the separated end of the ridge through
which the cut was made now bears this name of Li-tui;
they assume therefore that it was made by Li Ping and
that the name "Moh" was used to signify the turbulent
nature of the Min waters. At the same time they ignore
the statement of the Shuh-chï that it was Kai Ming who
cut U-lui. A prejudice in favour of one of their own
nation seems to warp their judgment here and make
them jump to a very unprovable conclusion.
Re the location of Mount U-lui there is no division
of opinion among Chinese writers. All agree that it was
the U-lui at Kwanhsien. But, since so much depends on
that, it will be best to see for ourselves whether they have
sufficient grounds for their belief.

We learn from the Shuh Chï that U-lui like Mount
Omei formed part of the outer natural ramparts of
ancient Shuh. The Shui-King-Chu (*) says it was
in the district of Mien-sï-Hsien (A). The present
Kwanhsien formed part of that district. The Yuen-Ho-
Chi () says U-lui lay 29 "li" north-west of Tao-
Kiang-Hsien and Tao-Kiang-hsien, the same work gives,
as 26 "li" from Kwang-k'ou-shan (). Kwan-K'ou-
Shan was the original name given to the detached end of
the ridge at Kwan-hsien and only divided from Kwan-
hsien by the breadth of the river, therefore U-lui was
very near Kwan-Hsien. The General History of Szechuan,
quoting the Han Chi () and (E) Huan-U-Chi,
says that the Ch'ien water () flowed out of U-lui.
The Ch'ien mountains was an old general name for the
ranges near Kwan-hsien which adjoined the plain.
The Shui-King-Chu says the Ch'ien water arose in
the U-lui mountain of Mien-Sï-Hsien. This water flowed
to the east of Kwanhsien and gave its name to the Ch'ien
river which joined the Lo river and further East got
the name of the T'o, finally joining the Yang-kiang or
Yang-Tze river at Kiang-yang, the present Luchow. In
the General History of Szechuan, in the Kuan-Ki-Ts'u-
Kao() and in the Kwan-hsien Hsiang-Tu-Chï
(*) it says that the Min river flowed past U-lui
somewhere below the mouth of the Yu-Ch'i gorge (which
is 25 "li" west of Kwan-Hsien) and above the city. We
read again that U-lui was regarded by the people of the
plain as their parent mountain. From it a fine view
could be had of the high peaks beyond. On the shady
side of it stood the Ts'ing-Yuen temple where Chao-Ih,
a Prefect of Kia-chou, was worshipped and the Ts'ong-
temple which the common peoples called the Rï-Wang
temple because in it Li Ping and his son were given
obeisance. Over the river here swung a rope bridge, the
old name of which was Chu-p'u (l) and the Song
name was P'ing-si bridge (*). At the foot of
Mount U-lui was the U-lui-kwan or pass near the camping
hollow of the aborigines.
Now the West Gate pass at Kwanhsien is still called
the U Lui-Kuan; the aborigines camping hollow is still
beside it and the famous Rï-Wang temple still stands at
the river side a little way beyond the pass while a rope

Min. Could more proof be wanted? If so we have it in
the Kwanhsien Hsiang-T'u-Chï which says distinctly that
the Ling-ngai mountain was the ancient U-Lui and that
in all probability the name included all its subsidiary hills
or ranges.
Mt. U-lui is, therefore, the mountain immediately
behind Kwanhsien. The city is built at its foot and the
city wall rising up over the ridge at the west gate is
actually built on it. From Ling-Ngai to the city inter-
venes a distance of 10 li. Even in the alleged mileage
from the ancient Tao-Kiang-hsien there is no discrepancy
if we allow for variation in ancient and modern mea-
surement. Outside of Shuh two other hills came to be
called U-Lui, one at Weichou and one in the far north
but the U-lui-shan of the Shuh-Chi and referred to in
all these works is this one where the Min river emerges
from the mountains.
It follows that it was here that Kai-Ming, many years
before the advent of the Chinese to the West, put through
his famous work which gave him a throne.
A local examination of the ground shows that a long
sloping ridge runs down to the edge of the river, the
river washing the side of the ridge for nearly a mile.
The lower part of the ridge has been steeply scarped for
two or three hundred years and then a cutting made
through it leaving a great rock at the extremity to serve
as a breakwater. It was an admirable plan to prevent
flooding for it drew off a great body of water into other
channels, natural and improved, to the North. Appa-
rently there were even then irrigation streams and these
all served to promote his beneficent scheme.
Other works of reference corroborate the statement
of the Shuh Chï. A dictionary of China's riverways
called the Shui-King-Chu which was written a century
or so later says that the U-Lui mountain of Mien-Sze-
Hsien was the mountain that Kai Ming cut, and identifies
the river he directed as the Ch'ien-kiang () and
U-Lui as at Kwanhsien. Nothing could be more explicit.
Quotations from the Shuh Chi (E), a work I have
so far been unable to obtain, confirm this position. But
one in the topographical History of Chengtu makes it
contradictorally put the scene of Pieh Ling's labours at
U-Shan-Hsien. It says that he cut the U-Shan mountain
and opened the three Yang-tze gorges. It is inconceivable
that the author wrote such arrant nonsense, especially as

U-Shan mountain was hundreds of miles away from Shuh
while elsewhere his statements make it unmistakeable
that it was U-Lui-Shan he cut. The seeming contradic-
tion is clearly due to faulty transcription U () being
written for Ü () and hsiah () for kwan (). U-lui-
shan was also called U-shan, it was easy to confuse the
two characters, seeing the sounds were similar. San-kwan-
K'ou the name for the three main irrigation openings at
Kwanhsien, and San-hsiah-K'ou the name for three large
Yangtze gorges, got likewise mixed. Corrected thus it
reads that the Shuh people suffered often from floods due
to the river becoming obstructed. Pieh Ling therefore
cut the U-lui mountain and opened the irrigation mouths
which let the Shuh people live on dry land. For this he
was made the ruler of Shuh and finally became Emperor
when Wang Ti vacated the throne in his favour. This
rendering is in entire harmony with the author's other
statements and with that of the Shuh-chi.2
The Shuh Chong Ming Sheng Chi (蜀中​名勝​)
quotes old authorities in such a way that it puts Kai
Ming's work first and locates it at Kuan-k'ou-shan, which
was the original name of the detached breakwater rock at
Kwanhsien.
Speaking of Li Ping, it says, that he was able, be-
cause of old courses left after King Yu had dealt with
the waters, to clear them out and broaden them. There
can be no doubt, therefore, that this exactly sums up
Li Ping's irrigation exertions: he did not create a new
system of waterways but improved and extended the
old one in such a way as to confer a lasting and immense
benefit on the dwellers of Shuh. But his labours were
not confined to the Min river. He was a general and
not a local administrator; all parts of Shuh claimed his
sleepless interest. The Shuh-chï gives a fine account of
his widespread energies and it is best to add here a free
translation with notes that we may understand exactly
what they were.
