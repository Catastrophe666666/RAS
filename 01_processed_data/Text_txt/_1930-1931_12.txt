III. SPRING FLOWERS OF THE TSINAN PLAIN
Although there are lists of the flowering plants of
Chefoo, Tsingtao, Tientsin, Peking, and other such local-
ities, the plants involved are seldom described, and when
they are, it is rarely in English. The present paper is
a contribution towards the remedying of this condition.
Non-technical language is used, even though not so well
understood by the systematic botanist. In order to make
the identification of the species as simple as possible, and
at the same time to present a series of pictures of the
flora as it changes from season to season, the order of
presentation is chronological. Thus, with this paper in
hand, one may become acquainted with the wild flowers
one by one as they appear throughout the spring. The
plants may be ecologically divided into the following
associations:
Field weeds (spring: among the wheat, summer
rainfall: among the fall crop plants)
Field margins (narrow herbaceous ridges left
between fields)
Graves and terraces (sloping ground, more or
less permanent)
Water runs, draws, arroyos (some being used
as "road" ways)
Stone dumps, villages and yards.
Arbor Vitae (Thuya orientalis) groves (burial
grounds)

known as the Shepherd's Purse (Capsella bursa-pastoris

A week later, or rather during the next warm pulse,
usually occurring during later March, the characteristic
spring flowers begin to add colour above their unfolding
leaves.
Astragalus scaberrimus Bunge which forms a
greyish-green mat over the ground, sets up its slenderly
pea-like flowers which, although usually white are often
flushed with yellow or pale rose. The greyishness of the
small oval leaflets is due to a covering of closely crowded,
closely appressed, stiff, white, bristle-like hairs. The
flowers are often so crowded together as to form a mat of
white blossoms, a very attractive addition to any "lawn".
Later the greyish-green pods may be found close to the
ground among the leaves. The plant is spreading in
habit and common in burial grounds and on graves.
Gueldenstaedtia giraldii Harms is also a bean plant,
its flower being much more pea-like than the former and
borne in heads of 3 to 6 on a stout, erect stalk (scape).'
When in bud they are rosy, but turn lilac on opening,
with a white flame in the centre of the upstanding petal
(standard). On withering they turn dark blue. The
compound leaves spring from a peg-like root, forming a
tuft of blue-green, silky haired mouse ears. When seen
from above, each flower cluster is seen to be twisted like
a pin-wheel due to the twist of each flower. When
fertilized the scape slowly bends earthward so that the
hand-like cluster of pods lie close enough to the earth to
be safe from browsing sheep.
G. stenophylla Bunge, first described by the early
Russian botanist Bunge, from specimens he found near
Peking, is similar but has very narrow leaflets, and pale
pink, sometimes almost whitish, flowers on much longer
and more slender scapes. It is therefore much less con-
spicuous and not so common, though far more dainty.

G. giraldii glabra Jacot is an absolutely hairless
variety whose leaves are consequently a bright green,
giving the plant a fresher, cleaner look. Although the
species itself loses most of the hair of the leaves
during the summer it never has the same vivid green and
always has hairs about its buds and unfolding leaves.
In the variety the flowers also are more rosy and vivid.
All three of these forms are common on terraces, graves
and burial grounds.
Viola prionantha Bunge, another of Bunge's Peking
plants, is the common violet of this region. It has long
been called V. patrinii, but Becker, the late authority on
Eurasian violets, has shown that V. patrinii, a Siberian
species, never comes south of the Great Wall. Our
species has no beard inside the side petals, and has
elongate oblong leaves when older, though some are
elongate-triangular with an almost straight (truncate)
base. The leaf stalk (petiole) is winged. Terraces and
graves are the preferred habitat.
Euphorbia lunulata Bunge, the north China spurge,
is conspicuous by its height and tuft of green flowers.
Actually the green petal-like parts are better called the
involucre. Above them are the lobed pods crowned, like
the moslem, by a crescent (gland). The strap-shaped
leaves, like the rest of the plant, exude milk on being
bruised or broken. Large individuals may reach 15-18
inches in height. It is common on graves, terraces and
field margins. One may often find specimens crowned
by a cluster of thick leaves whose lower surface is covered
with yellowish "powder" caused by a fungus disease
which arrests the growth.
Accompanying these flowers but waving above them
are the poplar (Populus tomentosa Carrière) whose
catkins become long, drooping red fingers, the willow
(Salix matsudana Koidzumi) whose catkins become erect
and fuzzy, and the elm (Ulmus pumila L.) which puts out
its clusters of tiny green flowers and then showers its
green seed-wafers about their shade area.
Also, if one looks carefully along the roadsides and
pathways for what is usually thought of as grass, one
will find the flower of a cosmopolitan weed, the slender
leaved sedge (Carex stenophylla Wahlenburg). This
flower consists of a tuft of brown "chaff" on a slender,
erect scape, rarely over three inches high in this dry,
sunny north China plain. The upper half of this chaffy

The next warm spell, about the first week of April,
brings out another group of which the first will probably
be the dandelion but not, as has usually been reported,
T. officinale of southern Europe but, as has been shown by
Handel-Mazzetti, Taraxacum mongolicum found abun-
dantly throughout north China. The blossom and leaves
are like the old familiar species but the seeds are very
different, being ornamented by encircling ruffs or tucks
turned upwards, instead of longitudinal ridges (see figure
1). The shape and size of the leaves varies greatly with
age and locality (moisture and shade). Of many speci-
mens examined around Tsinan, Tsingtao, and Chefoo, I
find they are all Chinese species (see contribution IV).
Sisymbrium sofia L. another wheat field annual and
cosmopolitan weed has greyish-green, very much cut up
or compound fern-like but coarsely thick leaves, which
the poorer Chinese use, like the Shepherd's Purse, for
vegetable greens. About this time it sends up its stalk
the tip of which soon bursts, rocket-like, into many, tiny,
yellow, star-like flowers. A count of its four petals
shows it to belong, like the Shepherd's Purse, to the criss-
cross family which gives us our mustard, radishes,
turnips, and such. It may also be found wherever earth
has been newly turned up.
Marrubium incisum Bentham begins growing in the
fall, like winter wheat, and thus is ready for a big spread
in spring. The rosette of roundish, palmately lobed
leaves then sends up a stout four-angled stalk, which is
frequently interrupted by a quartette of leaves which
still higher up become couples embracing in their axils
clusters of velvety white flowers, each one stuck in a
bristly-toothed cup (the calyx). The plant may reach a
foot in height, though usually less, and forms a conspicuous
blue-green tuft on graves and terraces. By May it begins
drying at the base so that by June nothing is left but a
dry, brown stalk studded by crowns of chaffy calices.

apetalum Willd., a species of central Siberia, and com-
monly called Pepper-grass, spreads its petal-less flowers

Three or four weeks later it is more familiar to the eye

Trigonotis peduncularis (Treviranus) the north
China forget-me-not, is often so tiny in plant and flower
as to be usually overlooked. In favourable places it may
attain considerable size and in mass is quite conspicuous.
As, however, there are four kinds of small blue-eyed
Borages in this region one must look closely. In this
species the leaves are almost to quite smooth (hairless),
the blade is shorter than its stalk (petiole) though much
broader, while the flowers are staggered along the elon-
gating axis without subtending leaves. The tip of the
axis unrolls, resembling, like young ferns, a fiddle head.
Robust specimens may reach a length of 15 cm. (6 inches)
with a spread of 2.5-3 dm. (10-12 inches). It is one of
our daintiest spring flowers found chiefly on new land
recently gone "wild" and with some plant covering. It
does best in limestone rockeries developing to its
maximum size in contact with such stones.
Bothriospermum secundum Maximowicz is another
of these small flowered plants with blue petals. The
blossom is at least twice the diameter of the former and
the leaves are long-oval or ob-lanceolate and studded with
coarse, spiny hairs (almost bristles, each with a dilated,
disc-like base, figure 3). As the year advances the
branches keep lengthening, the flowers continuing to
develop, crowded along their tips, each subtended by a
short leaf. The sprouts (figure 5) are erect with
oblanceolate leaves scattered along the axis, but soon lose
their attractiveness because of the large, hairy calyces and
rough, hairy stems. Common about waste land.
Bothriospermum chinense Bunge resembles the
preceding very closely but is less hairy, the hairs more
silky, while the flowers are much less crowded and sub-
tended by leaves which far exceed the flowers. Still

Oxytropis bicolor Bunge is another "bean” plant
found only in the north China area. About Tsinan it is
found chiefly on overgrown, stony dumps, terraces, and
on the limestone hills. It may be distinguished from all
other Tsinan plants by its pointed, sessile leaflets often
grouped four together along the petiole (the leaf being
compound), and by the yellowish-green heart of the
elongate scarlet flowers closely bunched in a roundish
head which terminates a long prostrate scape. The
generic character is a small point standing out from the
tip of the flower keel, while that of the Gueldenstaeditias
is the extremely short keel, shorter, in fact, than the calyx.
Lepidium incisum Roth, another Pepper-wort, which
grows much taller than the above one, and branches only
at the top, leaving a clean, though leaf garnished,
"trunk", has lyrate-pinnate, basal leaves with very fine
pubescence (short, soft hairs) on the upper surface only.
The leaf divisions (pinnae) are strongly and deeply
toothed and the edges slightly turned downward. The
flowers are petal-less, like L. chitungense, or rarely with
small, white petals.
The other spring sedge, Carex heterostachya Bunge,
may also be looked for now. Though its leaves and
colonial habit resembles the earlier one, its flower-fruit
arrangement is quite different. To begin with, coming
later, its scape has time to grow twice as tall as C.
stenophylla. Then instead of one broad head, it has two
or three slender ones, of which the upper are male flowers
bearing pollen and the lower female bearing seed.
Finally, the heads are a much darker, walnut brown.

the city one may find the annual Hypecoum erectum L.,

(13 mm.) and conspicuous yellow flower, borne well above
its tuft of grey-green very finely divided (compoundly
pinnate) leaves, and in clusters (cymose) several on a

After the ensuing blow from the northeast or cold
blast from the north with its accompanying dust storm,
one may go again in quest of a new galaxy.
Conspicuous, in the morning before the sun is high,
is the fringed disc of Lactuca versicolor Schultz-
Bipontius, the wild lettuce, which closes up to a wizened
tube as the sun gets hot. In the evening, however, the
plant is all aglow again. Both flower and leaf resemble
the dandelion but the ray flowers occupy only the outer
edge and the blossoms are usually more than one to a
scape. The leaf is greyer-green and more remotely
toothed (figure 8), to almost untoothed in shady places.
Although the flower is pale yellow about Tsinan, further
south, as at Tenghsien, may be white, or white and
yellow ones may be scattered about without the slightest
relation to soil, shade or other environment factor.

Androsache umbellata Loureiro, better known as A.
saxifragaefolia Bunge is the only spring "primrose". Its
tiny white flowers with yellowish eye borne as an
inverted candelabra on a three inch scape, recall forget-

them along the north foot of the hills, though they are
locally common on the alkaline plain. It is found on the
plains of India (Calcutta to Punjab) to 4,000 feet in the
Himalaya, and throughout China to Japan and Man-
curia. "The distribution of this species is highly
remarkable".
Vicia tridentata, another of Bunge's Peking finds,
is the common vetch of the plain, colouring the ground
with its large violet blue, pea-like blossoms. The name
tridentata is due to the shape of the tips of the leaflets
which are so deeply emarginate as to leave the sides of
the leaflet protruding like horns which, with the protrud-
ing mid-rib, form three prominent teeth (figure 6), as is
the case with Gueldenstaeditia multiflora of the Peking
mountains. These leaflets are opposite and four to six
in number, the terminal one represented by a longer,
branched tendril. There is no more brilliant blue flower
on the plain than this, and the blossoms, though but two
or three per peduncle, are unusually large for this genus
(2.5 cm. long). This, with the colonial habit of growth,
makes the species quite attractive.

During the third warm spell (week) of April one
may go out to meet the following debutantes.
Erodium stephanianum, one of Willdenow's many
children, is the only wild geranium of this region, and
even it cannot be called a true Geranium, because there
are but five stamens and the tail (awn) of the seeds is
bearded along its inner edge. Like many of the humbler
geraniums and several of our wild flowers, the petals are
lilaceous though more bluish than those of Gueldenstaedtia
giraldii, three or four growing on an erect scape so that
later on one is presented with a nest of cranes pensively
looking down their long bills. An interesting_charac-
teristic is the gland tipped stile terminating each sepal.
This species grows from a thick tap-root and sends out
a large rosette of branches lost in a mass of pinnately
divided leaves (figure 9). Such plants may reach 38 cm.
(15 inches) in diameter, forming pleasing, variegated
mats on graves and terraces.
Erysimum cheiranthoides L., a cousin of Sisymbrium
sophia, and growing up like it tall, slender and yellow,
crowned with four petalled flowers which give rise, like
its cousin, to slender, erect, tiny, bean-like pods, has
leaves which are strikingly different in that they are
lance-like but shallowly toothed. This waste-land weed
is garnished with stiff trifid hairs.
Lactuca denticulato, Maximowicz differs from the
earlier species in having a smaller more orange-yellow
flower, on larger plants with more elongated branches,
and in having very large, halberd-like bracts at the base
of each fork. The basal leaves are lyrate (figure 10),
runcinate or obovate and denticulate (especially when
young). The bracts are entire at the top but become
progressively more toothed, first at the broad, eared,
clasping base, then along the slender blade, until near
the base of large plants they have long, toothed spurs,

Potentilla flagellaris Willdenow is in my estimation
the loveliest of the yellow flowered plants. It resembles
very much in the shape and size of its flowers, its
leaves and trailing runners, the strawberry plant,
but its petals are yellow, a clear buttercup yellow,
the blossom exceeding in its clearness, flash and
size (to 3 cm.) any other local, yellow, wild flower.
The runners become adorned by leaves whose axils bear a
single flower and possibly another shoot. About Tsinan
it is always and only found at the foot of graves and
terraces facing north, if steep enough to afford plenty
of shade.
After these last two plants, which usually bloom about
the middle of April, have spread their stars on the green
carpet there is a marked pause in the procession which
some six or seven days later is resumed by the following
two composites:
Aster hispidus Thunberg is the only but quite
common wild Aster. Its leaves are not only undivided
but untoothed and strap-like. Furthermore they are
characterized by being covered with stiff grey hairs
which, along the margin when seen in silhouette, are
seen to be quite large, stiff, and strongly curved, pointing
towards the tip of the leaf. In fact, most of the plant is
rendered hispid by these curved "hooks". The blossoms
are fairly large (to 2.5 cm. in diameter), the rays being
pale blue to almost white. They may be found almost
any place where they are not ploughed or hoed up, form-
ing increasingly large colonies.
Cnicus segetum (Bunge) is the thistle of the region
but mild in every way. Its blossom is lilaceous to rose,
small for a thistle; its leaves are merely scalloped
(denticulate to coarsely toothed), the prickles being quite
weak. Typically its stalk is terminated by a single flower
though smaller ones occasionally occur below it. The
flower head is enclosed by several ranks of pointed
bracts, each successive row upward, longer than the
lower, until in the uppermost, the bracts are lanceolate.
It is found in greatest frequency as a field weed.

It is also at this time that the fourth tree Paulownia
fortunei (Seeman) puts forth its long (6 cm. or more),
tubular, white flowers among the large, long-triangular
leaves (blade to 25 cm. X 16 cm.). These blossoms
demand closer study. The receptacle-like calyx is a
heavy, green cup, with thin, silvery skin (epidermis) and
rusty plush (toment). One is at first struck by the
heavy, sweet scent. On looking down on the blossom one
is surprised to see it is mauve (a lilac blue). Cor-
respondingly the three lower lobes are white, the upper
two mauve, but on the inner (upper) face the three lower
lobes have deep blue points of two sizes, the larger of
which are arranged in rows converging towards the
interior of the flower. Within the tube one finds two
elevated tracks which are a pale yellow, and run to the
honey glands. The arrangement of the reproductive
organs is similar to that of Rehmannia (above), but the
stamen filaments are straight and immaculate. The style
exceeds the stamens by twice the length of the anthers
when fully grown, the tip being bent down as a pointed
nob with a tiny hole at its tip. The filaments are curved
near their base, first downward, then outward to the sides
of the blossom tube, leaving exposed the four honey
openings of which the upper is much the longest. The
two little eggs (ovules) are like flattened wheat grains
with a deep groove but with pebbled surfaces. It is thus
not only the largest wild flower, but one of the most
interesting. The trees about Tsinan are infested with
a fungus which developes witches' brooms.
The last week of April is marked by the flowering
of two trees, one native the other an alien:
Catalpa bungei C. A. Meyers, the columnar tree of
north China becomes crowned in violet. There is no
more glorious sight in the plains of south-central and
north-central Shantung than a mass of these tall trees
towering as a grove above the buff wall of a village, their.
upper region a mass of violaceous bloom, like islands of
rosy spray against a sea of green wheat. The flowers
are thimble-like and about 4 cm. (12 inches) long. The
colour is due to purple spots in the throat, the ground
colour being white but so clear in the sun and the blossoms
so grouped (in corymbs) as to give the general purple
colour to the distant eye. The leaves are deltoid with
two or three prominently projecting teeth about the
widest part.

Robinia pseudoacacia L., the black locust of the
United States, introduced by the Germans especially
along the railroad, is now being used more and more by
the Chinese, as a rapidly growing, freely sprouting, pest
free producer of fuel. Its gracefully pendulous com-
pound leaves and clusters of white blossoms contribute
to its rank as an ornamental shade tree while its stout
thorns tend to keep off the careless.
The wild alfalfa (Medicago sativa L.) now tips its
long, spreading shoots with a cluster of small, whitish
blue to deep blue blossoms. It may be easily recognized
by its deep blue-green foliage spread in large, roundish
mats, and the close clusters of small, pea-like flowers
which have so much the blue of the leaves as to be easily
overlooked. It is most common along roadsides and field
margins.
Polygala tenuifolia Willdenow, another blue flower,
may be found in special localities as it is more of a dry
hillside species than one of the plains. It grows up like
a tiny bush with very slender, strap-like leaves (rarely
exceeding 3 mm. in width) and several small blue flowers
scattered along the upper part of the longer branches.
These flowers are quite unique in appearance for they
are bilaterally symmetrical and are quite unlike a pea-
flower. They may be easily recognized by the stiff
tassel at one end formed by the fimbriated end of the
middle (of three) petal. Two of the sepals function as
petals thus making up a total of five coloured parts, as in

As the violets were formerly believed to be simple,
clearly recognizable species, which are now known to be
represented by many closely related, often freely

writers lumped several species under one name. Such

V. patrini DC. is throughout its range a plant of moist

River region to Vladivostock and Japan. It has not been found
(according to Becker 1916) in Korea.
