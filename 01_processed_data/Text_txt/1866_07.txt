ARTICLE VII.
NOTES ON SOME OUTLYING COAL-FIELDS IN THE
SOUTH-EASTERN PROVINCES OF CHINA..
BY THOS. W. KINGSMILL.*
THE general position of the Great Coal-fields of China, stretching
in a line trending from N.N.E. to S.S.W., from the Si-shan near
Peking along the frontiers of Pe-chi-li and Shan-si, and thence
through Ho-nan and Hu-peh into the great coal and iron district
of Hu-nan, is known to most members of this Society; though
information of their details and lateral extension is much to be
desired. I have therefore endeavoured to throw some light on the
less important fields of Kiang-si, Hu-peh, An-whei, Kiang-su, Cheh-
kiang, Fuh-kien and Kwang-tung; though with the exception of
one or two districts, I do not see much prospect of the produce
ever becoming of more than local service.
The geological position of all these fields seems sufficiently
well marked as belonging to the true "Coal Measures" of the
Carboniferous system; sometimes indeed thin beds of coal occur in
the Carboniferous limestone itself, but these do not appear to be
sufficiently productive to permit of being worked with profit.
To render this description plainer it will be well to give some
account of the rocks anterior to the coal measures, as they are found
to exist in these provinces, marked as they are by a remarkable
similarity throughout the entire area.
Commencing with the primitive formations of the district, we
find enormous masses of unstratified quartzites, clay slates, gneiss
and other metamorphic rocks of uncertain date, much entangled
with igneous protrusions consisting of granite, porphyries, syenites,
and in the southern districts at least, greenstones and hornblende
rocks. These rocks may be said to form the main axis of the
greater mountain chains, and constitute the characteristic rock in
all the tea districts, for the proper growth of which plant indeed
their presence seems absolutely necessary. Resting on these seems
to occur the first sedimentary formation whose position can at

In the absence of fossils which have not to my knowledge been dis-

Carboniferous: it clearly represents in position if not in age the
Carboniferous limestone of Europe, reposing unconformably on
the edges of the Devonian rocks, and being succeeded by the
Coal measures, into which in some localities it seems impercepti-
bly to run.
It differs from the lower rocks in being generally of darker
colour, distinctly stratified, and not mixed with siliceous rocks. In
some localities it exists as a mass of calcareous shales, but generally
the beds are much thicker varying from 1 to 6 or 8 feet. The lime-
stone is not so pure as in the earlier rocks, being mixed to a
greater or less extent with carbonaceous or argillaceous impurities.
Very little metamorphic action seems generally to have taken
place, and the laminæ of bedding are often beautifully distinct, the
slightest ripple mark being well preserved. It exists in enormous
masses in the valley of the Yang-tsze; as between Hankow and
Kiukiang, where it forms the bold scenery of the Pwan-pien-shan
and the well known "Cock's head."

Throughout the entire area mentioned above, containing portions

casional outbursts of trappean formations constitute the dominant
geological features of the country. They are every where much
disturbed and contorted, broken up by igneous protrusions, and
jumbled together in masses of greater or less extent. The strata
are never horizontal, are often vertical, but as a general rule are to
be found dipping at high angles; the later rocks are however much
less contorted than the earlier; the strike generally approaches
W.N.W. to E.S.E. or nearly at right angles with the prevailing
direction of the mountain ranges; at least this is the general result
I have noted for the earlier portions of the series.
The general prevailing characteristics of the formations antece-
dent to the coal measures may thus be easily determined. The
rocks are found flanking the great mountain chains; of which the
great Woo-e-shan between Kiang-si and Fuh-kien, continued in a
N.E. direction into the low plains of Kiang-su is pre-eminently the
most important. Outlying ranges of greater or lesser importance
are however to be found at both sides of these mountains, on the
flanks of which we meet with similar rocks. The most important
of these lesser chains, are the Mei-ling mountains between Kwang-
tung and Kiang-si; the Lu-shan to the south of Kiu-kiang con-

and forming the watershed between Kiang-si and Hu-nan, and

ranges the coal bearing districts I have to mention will be found

At Wu-chang and Han-yang the rocks forming the base of the

The next coal field with which I am acquainted is that of Yi-
shan about sixty miles below Hankow; it lies about five miles
inland from the town of Hwang-shih-kang (Yellow stone ridge)
so called from the color of the prevailing rocks which are composed
of a reddish shale, the representative apparently of the Permian
of Europe; the strike of these rocks is approximately N. & S. at
Whang-shih-kang, the dip is E. about 20°. These rocks seem to
be succeeded by light red sandstones, passing upwards into im-
perfectly conglomerated gravels and sands; the sandstone is soft
in texture is remarkable from containing large numbers of lenticu-
lar argillaceous nodules, and sometimes has distinct remains of
interlacing stems, but no other fossils, that I could discover. This
stone is much used in building operations at Hankow, but unless
carefully selected is apt to disintegrate with exposure to the weather.
These formations extend a considerable distance towards the west,
forming low hills on the south bank of the Yang-tsze, as far as the
small town of Wu-chang-hien, when they may be seen dipping
towards the east from the flank of the quartz hills which here join
the river.
Immediately below these rocks the coal bearing formations are
to be seen in a group of low hills known as the Yi-shan bordering
the San-ko-tang lakes. The rock is composed of Quartzose Grits,
sometimes almost massive, interstratified with argillaceous schists;
the hills are round topped about 200 feet high, the water in the
lakes is decidedly chalybeate, the dip of the strata varies from 5° to
25° towards the west.

I must therefore bring this paper to a close with a resume of
what I have already stated. Coal fields of greater or lesser extent
are known to occur in all the South-eastern provinces; their geo-
logical position throughout appears to be the same, flanking the
mountain chains of earlier date; the mountain chains seem to have
been elevated at a date posterior to the deposition of the coal, but
probably prior to that of the Permian and Triassic rocks.
So much for the position of the coal; my observations up to this
time do not hold up much prospect of its being eventually worked
with profit. The fields cover a considerable area but wherever
worked the coal is scarce and of inferior quality, it is quite possi-
ble of course that the latter would improve at increased depths,
but unless some thicker seams be discovered, these advantages
would be counter-balanced by the increased cost of working.
These remarks are to be distinctly understood as applying only to
the district I have mentioned. I have not learned enough of the
great coal-fields of Hu-nan and the north to be prepared to hazard
an opinion as to their productiveness.
