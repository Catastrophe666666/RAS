THREE SCORE YEARS AND TEN
The Seventieth Anniversary of the North China Branch of the Royal
Asiatic Society.¹
By ISAAC MASON, F.R.G.S.
Vice-President of the Society.
I esteem it a privilege to have the honour of
reading this Paper before the Society on the auspicious
occasion when we commemorate the completion of seventy
years of its existence. There are others with a longer
connection with it than I have had, who might more
acceptably have occupied this position, my only claim to
which consists in a warm interest in the Society, and a
love of delving into its history and of learning what I can
of the honoured and prominent members of bygone days.
Twenty-five years ago, at the forty-fifth anniversary,
on October 16th, 1902, the late Dr. Joseph Edkins, one of
the original members, and the first Secretary of the
Society, gave an interesting retrospect, recalling the early
days, and the men of vision and courage who saw the
need of such an association and became its first members.
For the anniversary referred to, the late Professor Henri
Cordier supplemented much valuable information.
these two prominent authorities I owe much of what
follows, so far as the earlier years are concerned. There
is now no survivor to give us reminiscences of the earliest
days, and we have only fifteen members on our list of
six hundred whose membership covers half the period of
the long life of the Society. Happily our Journals and
other records have preserved a good deal for us, and these
have been consulted, with the result that this chronicle
of our history has been made, which may not only interest
present members, but which will also be found useful to
future chroniclers who may assist at the Centennial and
other celebrations of this Branch of the Royal Asiatic
Society.

The China Branch of the Royal Asiatic Society was
inaugurated in 1847, at Hongkong. A desire had long
existed among some of the foreign residents for the
establishment of a Society for the investigation and
discussion of matters relating to the Arts, Sciences,
Literature, Natural Productions, etc., of China, and there
was launched what was called "The Asiatic Society of
China," with Sir John F. Davis, Bart., F.R.S., as President.
As one of the original members of the Royal Asiatic
Society, founded in 1823, the President was, a valuable
link with the older institution, and was instrumental in
securing the admission, in 1847, of the new Society as a
recognized branch of that distinguished body, so the name
was altered to that of “China Branch of the Royal Asiatic
Society." That Society issued six volumes of Transac-
tions, the last being in 1859.
The North-China Branch of the Royal Asiatic
Society, the seventieth anniversary of which we are now
commemorating, is quite distinct from the one just
mentioned, though doubtless its inception owed something
to the Hongkong undertaking. Our Society may be said
to have commenced on October 16th, 1857, though a
preliminary meeting is mentioned in the original Minute
Book as having been held on September 24th, 1857.
The hour was 4 p.m. and the place, the Reading Room

The first President of this Society, Elijah Coleman
Bridgman, D.D., was a remarkable man, and it is fitting
that some brief account of him should be given here. He
was born in 1801, in Massachusetts, U.S.A., and reached
China in February, 1830, the first American missionary
to this country. He became a distinguished sinologue,
and wrote some works in Chinese, besides translating
Chinese works and sharing in translating the Old and the
New Testaments into Chinese. He commenced, in 1832,
The Chinese Repository, in English, and edited that
monthly magazine for fifteen years, and continued his
contributions until the magazine ceased a few years later.
He also acted for a time as Secretary and Chaplain to
the American Legation. Dr. Joseph Edkins-our first
Honorary Secretary-in his retrospect said, "Dr. Bridg-
man was the first editor of The Chinese Repository, and
held liberal views. He encouraged research. By asking
him to be our President-a man whose memory went
back to 1830 when he was welcomed to Canton by Dr.
Robert Morrison, the first British missionary to set foot
in China-we linked the literary and scientific work to
be done in Shanghai under the auspices of the new
Society, with the work already accomplished at Canton
and elsewhere during the many years of Dr. Bridgman's
missionary life."
Dr. Bridgman was President for two years, and he
died in 1861, nearly thirty-two years after his arrival
in China; his tomb is in the old cemetery in Shantung
Road, about half a mile from where we are now
assembled; I stood beside it to-day, and in thought
connected the two events-separated by 70 years-of the
first President's opening address, and our commemoration
this evening.

An interesting feature of the early Journals, for
about eighteen years, was the Retrospects of current events
in the Far East. We have in these carefully prepared
articles, which began in 1859, much that is of value to

In 1868 the formation of a library was considered,
as there was an opportunity to secure Mr. Wylie's library
on his leaving Shanghai, and a public appeal was issued,
in which it was stated, "The special aim of this scheme
is the creation of a public repository for every book or
paper illustrative of the study of China and the Chinese."
The appeal was successful, and the valuable collection of
books was purchased, and formed the nucleus of our
present splendid library.
The subject of accommodation for these books, and
for a prospective museum, and for the meetings of the
Society, now became an urgent one. Removals had been
frequent, and after having been accommodated in various
locations, the Shanghai Public Library (1864), Ningpo
Road (1865), New Masonic Building (1869), Com-
mercial Bank Building, Nanking Road (1870), the
Society, thanks to the liberality of the British Govern-
ment, and to the public spirit of the Shanghai Commun-
ity, and to the energy and enterprise of its members, at
length found a permanent home in 1871, on its present
site.
A letter addressed in 1868 to the British Government
by Sir Rutherford Alcock, on behalf of the Society, had
resulted in the granting of a strip of land for the purpose
of building a Library, Museum and Lecture Room, "on
condition that it never be diverted from the purpose for
which it is granted; and should the Society be dissolved,
or circumstances occur which might prevent the erection
of any building within three years, that it then revert
to Her Majesty's Government." The required Tls. 2,700
for the building were subscribed in time to take advan-
tage of the generous grant.
The Rev. E. W. Syle was the first Librarian of the
newly-formed library, and was succeeded by Mr. Karl

The idea of a Museum for Shanghai had been enter-
tained for some time before a beginning seemed possible,
the two chief difficulties being lack of proper accommod-
ation, and the expense which would be involved if a
skilled and professional curator had to be secured. The
new building of the Royal Asiatic Society removed the
first of these difficulties, and early in 1874 a good oppor-
tunity offered to secure a useful man at moderate cost.
The celebrated naturalist, Père David, being compelled
by ill-health to give up his researches in China, was
passing through Shanghai on his way to France; he had
with him a Chinese assistant named Wang, a skilled
taxidermist and a painstaking man, who had been
working under, his personal supervision for some years.
At a meeting of the Society on February 13th, 1874, the
matter was brought forward by Mr. Michie, who proposed
"That the Council be recommended to take immediate
steps to organize a Museum and to make arrangements
with the Trustees of the Recreation Fund for a loan of
one thousand five hundred taels." This was seconded by
Mr. Wylie, and carried. In the following month another
meeting decided to urge upon the Council "the establish-
ment of a Museum; and that they be requested to take
steps to engage the services of the Chinese curator, now
with Père David, and to take such other steps as may be
practicable to carry out the resolutions passed at the
Annual Meeting of the Royal Asiatic Society." The
scheme was proceeded with, and a number of gentlemen
became responsible for different departments of the
museum, Mr. W. B. Pryer being appointed Honorary
Curator.

Alexander Wylie received an ordinary education
in Scotland, and afterwards became a printer with a view
to engaging in service in China for the London Mis-
sionary Society. Before leaving England he attained
such proficiency in the study of Chinese as surprised
Dr. James Legge and others, and awakened high expec-
tations of his future course. He arrived at Shanghai
in 1847, and entered at once upon the charge of an
English and Chinese printing office. In the intervals of
his working hours Mr. Wylie gave much time and
attention to the study of the Chinese literary style. For
his own use he translated several of the Chinese classics,
and read widely on other subjects, some results of which
he gave in his famous Notes on Chinese Literature. He
and Dr. Edkins were among the most active in starting
the N.C.B. of the Royal Asiatic Society, and did much
towards giving it success by reading papers and stimu-
lating discussion and research. His valuable library as
has been said above, became the nucleus of our present
magnificent collection. Mr. Wylie became the Agent in

Joseph Edkins, B.A., D.D., might suitably be called
the "Grand Old Man" of this Society, of which he was a
member for nearly fifty years. He was one of the
founders, and the first Hon. Secretary. He contributed
many papers, and took an active part in the discussions
of the Society for very many years. For a short time he
acted as President, and for many years he was Vice-
President, holding that office at the time of his death in
1905, after the unusually long period of 57 years in China.
At his death the Society lost the last link which bound it
to the original members, and it is not too much to say
that Dr. Edkins had no superior in the amount and value
of the work done in the interests of the Society. He was
a wonderful student of languages; besides Chinese and
other languages of Eastern Asia, he combined a know-
ledge of Hebrew, Persian and Sanskrit with his know-
ledge of the languages of Europe, which gave him a
foremost place in his generation. Dr. Edkins patiently
investigated the religions of China, and the published
results are valuable and lasting contributions to the
understanding of Chinese religious thought. He con-
tinued his work until within a few days of his death at
a ripe old age. There will be some present this evening
who remember Dr. Edkins; and all of us can pay tribute
to the memory of this outstanding character in the
history of our Society.

Rev. Alexander Williamson, LL.D., first reached

His Journeys in North China contains many valuable

Society. He joined our Society nearly sixty years ago,
and was an occasional contributor to the Journal.
D. J. Macgowan, M.D., arrived at Ningpo in 1843,
and later lived at Shanghai. He was a gifted man, with.
wide knowledge, and wrote many articles on China which
have been much valued. He contributed much to the
Journal of our Society, which he joined in 1864, and of
which he remained a member nearly thirty years.
Rev. W. A. P. Martin, D.D., first arrived at Ningpo in
1850, as a missionary from the United States. He sub-
sequently became Interpreter to the U.S. Legation, and
Professor of International Law at the T'ung Wên College
at Peking, and later was first President of the Peking
Imperial University. He wrote some very popular
books in Chinese, as well as in English and contributed
to our Journal. He joined our Society in 1864, and was
a member for fifty-two years, until his death at Peking
at nearly ninety years of age.
Rev. William Muirhead, D.D., arrived at Shanghai
in 1847, and joined our Society in 1864. He served on
the Council, and as Vice-President. He had a remarkable
knowledge of the Chinese spoken language, and did much
translation. Besides being a devoted missionary, he is
remembered as being public-spirited in the welfare of
Shanghai.

Sir Everard D. H. Fraser, K.C.M.G., was for many
years President of the Society, which suffered a great
loss by his death in 1922. He was an accomplished
student of the Chinese language, with a wide knowledge
of the literature in it. He had wide and varied exper-

Samuel Couling, M.A., was for many years engaged
in missionary work in North China; he subsequently
resided many years at Shanghai, and was for two years
Honorary Secretary of the Society, and Editor of the
Journal, and put much devotion into these duties. It was
during this period he perceived the need of a general book
of reference as to "Things Chinese," and set to work to
prepare such a book, the result being his Encyclopaedia
Sinica, for which he was awarded the Prix Stanislas
Julien in 1919. He was elected an Honorary Member of
our Society, and was a Vice-President at the time of his
death in 1922.
Prof. Henri Cordier was also one of our Honorary
Members. He came to China in 1869 and soon joined the
Society, of which he became Librarian, as recorded above.
He held this position until his return to Europe in 1876,
preparing our first published catalogue in 1872. M.
Cordier received his appointment to the Paris School of
Oriental Living Languages in 1881, and occupied this
position until his death in 1925. He was a prolific writer
on Chinese subjects, his Bibliotheca Sinica being a work
involving immense labour. He also founded and edited
the review known as the T'oung Pao. His work was of
great value to those who are interested in the Far East.
Prof. Friedrich Hirth, PH.D., will be remembered as
a sinologue of the first rank. From 1870 to 1897 he was
in the Chinese Customs Service. He was President of
our Society for the years 1886-87, after serving as Vice-
President several years. He contributed some valuable
articles to the Journal. On the founding of the Chair of
Chinese at Columbia University in 1902 he became the
first Professor, and held the post more than twenty years.
He died recently, at more than eighty years of age.
Prof. Edward H. Parker, M.A., was one of the most

I wish now to refer to a few who, happily, are still on
our membership roll, though in most cases no longer in
China. I believe our oldest living member is Dr. John
Fryer, who joined the Society in 1866, and at eighty-eight
years of age is still vigorous, and sufficiently interested in
China to write weekly to his son who superintends the
schools for Chinese blind, and deaf and dumb, which
Dr. Fryer has founded. During his thirty-three years in
China, Dr. Fryer was engaged in teaching and translating
work, after which he was made Professor of Oriental
Languages and Literature in the University of California.
He was elected an Honorary Member of our Society
in 1886.
Mention must be made of Prof. Herbert A. Giles,
M.A., LL.D., who is also now more than eighty years of age.
He joined the Society in 1880, and was President in 1885,
and was later elected an Honorary member. He has been
an occasional contributor to our Journals. After his
career in the British Consular Service in China, Professor
Giles retired to England in 1891, and was appointed to
the Chair of Chinese at Cambridge in 1897, which position
he still holds. His fame as a sinologue is world-wide;
his Chinese-English Dictionary, and his many other pub-
lished works are well-known, and are highly valued.
Dr. John C. Ferguson has probably done more for
the Society during the past thirty years than any other
member. He has been at various times Honorary Sec-
retary, Editor of Journal, Vice-President, and President,
sometimes holding two offices at once, and ably shoulder-
ing the responsibilities. He has often contributed to the
Journal, and is ever ready to give a lecture, or to take
helpful part in discussions, when he is at Shanghai. We
value him as one of our Honorary members.

Dr. Arthur Stanley, late Commissioner of Public Health
at Shanghai, was for some sixteen years a most efficient
Curator of our museum and valued member of the Council.
He served as Vice-President, and President of the Society,
and occasionally gave a lecture, and contributed to the
Journal. I heard from him quite recently from his place
of retirement in London, and he expressed his continued
interest in the Society and the Chinese Staff on these
premises. He is an Honorary member.
Florence W. (Mrs. F.) Ayscough left us so recently
that she will be remembered by all here. For fifteen
years she acted as Honorary Librarian, and brought our
library to its present excellent condition. A student of
the Chinese language, and of poetry and art, and a great
lover of books on Chinese subjects generally, she is a
veritable storehouse of information on "Things Chinese."
Her lectures are remembered with great pleasure, and her
contributions to the Journal are of fascinating interest.
She has befriended the Society in many ways, and her
election to Honorary membership was deservedly popular.
We hope to hear and see more of her in the days to come.
I need not mention any of our present officers. They
look upon it as an honour to serve, and to promote the
interests of this old-established and still active Society,
the need for which is fully as great now as it was seventy
years ago. China, and the Chinese people, are still
subjects worthy of earnest and patient investigation and
study, and the operations of this Society will always tend
to promote better understanding and fuller appreciation
of the great peoples of the Far East by those who have
come amongst them from the West, and should result in
increased mutual respect and friendliness.
