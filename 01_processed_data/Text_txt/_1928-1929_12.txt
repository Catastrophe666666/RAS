I. LIFE ZONES IN NORTH-EASTERN CHINA
A review of past researches on the distribution
of the flora, as also the fauna, of China reveals three
distinct life-zones, namely a southern, a central and a
northern, which I will for the sake of clarity refer to as
Kuangtungian, Yangtzian, and Shansian. By Shansian
is to be understood, speaking generally, the tier of nor-
thern provinces. Some of the species of the Shansian
zone range eastward into Japan and south-westward into
Szechuen and even Yünnan (on the high mountain
ridges). Again some of these Shansian species are
confined to north-western China (not reaching Chili).
I find that but very few species of north-eastern China
range into Shensi unless they go right on to Kansu or
Szechuen. Thus the Shansian zone divides naturally
into the north-eastern and north-western divisions.2
The Shansian zone is limited north by the Mongolian
plateau and south as outlined below. By Kuangtungian
is usually meant Kuangtung and Kuangsi (possibly
including the southern edge of the tier of provinces
immediately to the northward). The intervening Yang-
tzian also divides into eastern (or Lower Yangtzian)
and western (or Upper Yangtzian) divisions.

Topographically the province divides readily into two
parts: (1) the eastern, which forms the southern end
of the alkaline Chilu plain; (2) the western, a mountain
region whose east-west backbone, consisting of Funiu
Shan (2,000 m.) at the east end and Hsiungêrh Shan-
Liaokiun Shan (2,200 m.) at the west end, like its major
ridge, the Tsingling Shan of Shensi, acts as the Yellow-
Yangtze watershed. As a result, the streams of the
south slope running south-eastward and those of the
north slope running north-eastward, have acted
troughs for the migration of plants once they have
crossed the central ridge by way of the various gaps
and passes.
The result of this topography on the flora is particu-
larly marked. That of the plain, as might be expected,
is a unit belonging strictly to the Chilu plain. That of
the mountains, however, consists of at least three dis-
tinct floras. Unfortunately for a complete and detailed
analysis, the forty different localities from which Hers
collected, are all located, as for as I have been able to
determine, on the northern slope of the divide. As a
matter of convenience in studying the results of these
collections, the mountainous section of the province was
divided into five parts: (1) the northern, entirely north
of the Yangtze River and an integral part of the Shansi
mountains; (2) the north-western, lying on the north
side of the divide and west of the Yi Ho; (3) the central,
lying on the north slope of Funiu Shan; (4) the south-
eastern; and (5) the south-western, forming part of the
Han River watershed. Since no collecting has been
done in the south-western (Bailey's material being
south-eastern), one may only guess that it comprises a
mingling of the two divisions of the Yangtzian flora with
a sprinkling of the Shansian in favourable localities.

Thus it is evident that about half the trees are
Shansian; the bulk of the remainder are Yangtzian, while
Kuangtungian species are quite few. Furthermore, of
the four oaks which range through south and central
China (Q. cuspidata, Q. fabri, Q. glauca, Q. sclerophylla)
none have been collected on the north side of the divide.
Still more significant is the fact that only one species of
Ficus (a deciduous species of central China) and no
Castaniopsis occur in this zone. A review of the species
listed in Rehder's second list gives similar results. One
finds only Spiraea cantoniensis, Pyrus calleryana and
Rubus parvifolius representing the Kwangtungian-Yang-
tzian flora, and a nearly equal representation of Shansian
and of Yangtzian species.
This is in marked contrast with species from
Chikung Shan, where one finds most of the Kuangtungian-
Yangtzian species and a correspondingly smaller number
of Shansian species.
Thus one may, at least tentatively, consider the
east-west watershed of Honan as marking the southern
limit of the Shansian zone as a unit (there always being
some species that are more tolerant than others) as well
as the northern limit of the Kuangtungian-Yangtzian
flora. As to the sorting of the Shansian and Yangtzian
floras, the ecologist will undoubtedly find that the
Shansian flora in general is confined to the drier
exposures, while the Yangtzian flora is dominant on the
more humid tracts.

In Shantung, the Yangtzian flora extends along the
south coast at least into the Lau Shan area and probably
to the eastern end of the promontory. Some of its
species may also be found along the southern edge of
Shantung and up some of the sour valleys in the hill
area. Pinus massoniana is an excellent example of this
flora (just as P. tabulaeformis [=P. sinensis Mayr. not
Lam.] is of the Shansian), but it is restricted in range
northward. Pterocarya stenoptera and Aphananthe
aspera have extended into Shantung promontory at least
to Tsingtao. Species-like Morus acidosa-which are
more or less drought-resistant, or summer rainfall
annuals of this flora (like the common grass Chloris
virgata), may be found as far north on the plain as
Peking and Peitaiho. These, however, are very few in
number, central China being typically a moist area.
Vanieria (Cudrania) tricuspidata has extended its range
at least to the Yellow River. It should be noted in con-
nection with this northern limit of Yangtzian plants, that
it is also the northern limit of the Indo-Chinese species
which extend over central China, as, for instance, the
Indian Strawberry (Duchesnea indica).
The mountain areas of Shantung divide naturally into
three elements, namely: (1) the south-eastern, fringing
the coast, which, as has already been pointed out,
represents a tongue of the Kuangtungian and more
especially the Lower Yangtzian zones; (2) the north-
eastern, extending from the Laichou mountains to the
eastern tip of the promontory; and (3) the western.
The north-eastern is largely made up of a flora
characteristic of Korea and Japan, while the western is
nearly purely Shansian.
The flora of the Chilu plain, as I expect to point out
in detail in a later paper, is a mixture consisting of the
southern limit of Siberian-Mongolian species and the
northern limit of drought-resistant Yangtzian and Kuang-
tungian plants. Did the winter rainy season of central
China extend on to this plain it would be changed from
alkaline and semi-arid to one resembling the Kiangan
plain, making the limiting factor not temperature (see
Jacot, Life Zones and Temperature Conditions in
Shantung), but rainfall. It is thus possible for the
semi-arid element of the Siberian-Mongolian plants to
bloom and mature seed in the Chilu plain in early
spring when the temperature is still low and the moisture
conditions very low, while the summer rainy season

II. SUMMER FLORA OF BRUCE HILL, PEKING REGION.
A preliminary phytogeographic review of the fringe
of the hill area west of Peking was afforded during the
spring and summer of 1921. The hills are typical razor-
back crests which run from west to east, their steep
southern and northern slopes corrugated by equally steep,
sharply-cut ridges and boulder-choked ravines, resulting in
an enormous run-off of water. This is further increased
by their absolute bareness of trees or higher shrubs except
where temple grounds and monasteries have been planted
with evergreen or other more or less sacred trees, such
as the ginko, poplar, phoenix tree and pagoda tree, or
have allowed indigenous trees to grow. Such groves form
dark patches on the otherwise pale coloured, brilliantly
lighted slopes and serve to convey a partial idea of the
original flora and fauna. The chief of these near
Peking are at Pa Ta Ch'u (A), Chieh T'ai Ssu
(#)¯¯and T'an Chê Ssu (). The hills of this
region are formed almost exclusively of rather massive
sandstones and gabbro which, on being denuded, break
up into large blocks, and in more and more shattered
condition, find their way to the plain down the numerous
rock-choked gullies. As these gullies approach the plain,
they cut more deeply and sharply into the hillside, form-
ing small ravines which become shallower again as they
run on to the plain or stream valleys. The slopes are
so steep and rugged that there is far more shade during
different parts of the day and there are more spots shady
all day long, on the northern than on the southern slopes.

The flora on the southern slope of Bruce Hill (alt.
1,200 ft.) on which the Ch'aoyang An (E) tract is
situated, was alone studied in detail. This tract, once
having been temple grounds, is thinly wooded and there-
fore presents a richer flora than that of the bare hillside
and will therefore be presented last. It is on the ridge
crests (of the main slope) that the soil is thinnest, so that
they best show which species are best fit to survive the
hot, dry, thin-soiled condition prevalent over a large part
of north China. The three dominant species in order
of abundance are:
Andropogon ischaemum L. which covers the hillsides
and is the chief food of the sheep, donkeys, and, rarely,
cows, is a mat-forming grass with long, slender, flowering
stalks crowned by a slender tassel composed of a few
spikes crowded with reddish "flowers" which, on ripening,
become buff and fuzzy. Its colonial habit and dense mats
aid it in becoming a dominant.
Vitex negundo L. an erect shrub with five-fingered
leaves, and panicles of small blue flowers which give rise
to hard, little nutlets, and:
Zizyphus zizyphus L. the thorny, slender, curly
stemmed wild "date," is thickly sprinkled over the hillside,
forming the chief source of "fire-wood", and grows, under
these conditions of continual cutting back, to a height
of two to six feet each season, being the dominant and
characteristic woody plant. This "wood," as well as
herbs of all kinds, are cut in the late summer by the older

The following species (given in their order of
abundance) are not abundant but may be considered
common to fairly common and, like the above, do not
demand special conditions, except perhaps the first.
Wilkstroemia chamaedaphne Meisn. a red-barked
shrub with glabrous, oblong-lanceolate leaves and termin-
al clusters of small, lilac-like yellow flowers, is found,
especially higher up, next to large boulders where the
soil is deeper. Its flowering season is chiefly after that
of Vitex.
Cynanchum sibiricum (L.) with its tuft of slender,
almost grasslike but basally truncate leaves crowded along
the fine, often long and curling twigs and its meager,
axillary clusters of tiny flowers scarcely bring to mind
a milkweed, but later in the year the two to three inch
pods will dispel all doubts. It is fairly common about
rocks generally and beneath Wilkstroemia and other
shrubs.
Salsola kali L. with its scale-like leaves and harsh,
tortuous, prostrate branches, is common in places, other-
wise occasional. The variety S. k. tenuifolia is found
in shadier places.
Themeda triandra Forsk. the coarsest, tallest grass
of the hillside, is common higher up and locally. After-
a rain, its dull coppery colour having become enhanced to
salmonaceous, it forms bright patches on the hill-side.
It is much used for fuel. Hackel, in his Monograph,
calls it T. forskalii imberbis.

Cotyledon fimbriata Turcz. (hen and chickens) usu-
ally seen as a dense cushion of thick, grey-green leaves
with comb-toothed tips, is fairly common about boulders
and ledges, especially higher up. Its white flowers form
a flame against the dark rock in late summer.
Astragalus scaberrimus Bunge, an early blooming
legume forming grey mats, is rather rare on these slopes
and quite inconspicuous because of its humble habit.
The leaves are oval and, like the stems and calyx,
scabrous because of their thick covering of very stiff,
white hairs. The flowers are whitish with flushes of
yellowish or pinkish, forming mats of white in the spring.
It is abundant on the plain.
Potentilla sischanensis Bunge has umbels of some-
what small, yellow flowers and pinnate leaves with the
pinnae cut into sharp teeth. The under surface of the
leaf is white-woolly. It should be carefully discriminated
from P. chinensis which has more than five pairs of
uncrowded pinnae with minute ones interposed, while the
present species has but four or five rather crowded pairs.
It forms bright, scattered splotches of yellow when in
bloom, or greyish-green rosettes.
Arthraxon lanceolatus Hochst. is a grass which looks
very much like the common Andropogon but has wide
(1 cm.) leaves whose base is finely denticulate, each
denticle bearing a fine hair. It is typical of shady spots
on hillsides and usually forms colonies, but is also found
on face of rocks and cliffs springing from narrow ledges
and crannies on the shady side.
Molinia (Diplachne) serotina (L.), another grass,
is very readily recognized by the narrow, alternate leaves
which invariably stand out at right angles to and on
opposite sides of the stalk, giving it a very characteristic
appearance. The flower heads consist of small spikes
separated from each other on slender stalklets. The
plants usually grow crowded together to form dense,
round clumps.

leaves of the branchlets. These are cut up into four
pairs of thread-like lobes and a central one which is
usually at least three-pronged. The lobes spring from

Bidens pilosa L. This slender seeded beggar-tick

Tragus aliena (Spreng.) is a low, appressed grass

On the eastern side of the ridges, higher up, where
the rocks and cliffs afford plenty of shade, some moisture,

Grewia parviflora Bunge, a handsome shrub with

Scorzonera austriaca Willd. in the spring, by reason

be distinguished at all times by its long, entire, curled,

suit of black hair-like fibres. It is uncommon, occurring

Cheilanthes (Aleuritopteris) argentea obscura (Chr.)
the only fern collected on this hillside and the fern of
the lower, especially limestone hills of north-eastern China,
has a small (two to three inch), triangular, deeply lobed

has the medio-posterior lobes much lengthened and is
also deeply lobed. The fruiting bodies are borne all along
the edge of the lobes and partly covered by a narrow
continuous skin which looks like the inturned leaf edge,
which is denticulate. Here found against overhanging
rock masses where practically no sunlight can reach it.
Panicum miliaceum L. is a tall grass whose large,
roundish, frail-looking, filmy heads, fall off in the early
fall and are blown about by the wind. The much
branched stalklets of the head are extremely fine though
stiff and angular and are terminated by plump little
seeds. Not common.
Ampelopsis humulifolia Bunge resembles the grape-

thick and whitish-green on the lower surface with usually
long petioles at least equalling the blade. The pedicels
are still longer and opposite the leaves. The fruits are
at first green, then turn violaceous and finally indigo
blue. A very few plants were found festooning the
boulders of the rock slides with their roots in the cool
shade of the north side of the boulders.

Broussonetia papyrifera (L.), the paper mulberry,

is a common, wild shrub of north-east China. Here found

Periploca sepium Bunge very much resembles the
willow, but is far more slender and is twining. More-
over, the leaves are long acuminate so that the edge
of the terminal half is concave in outline and not convex
as in the willow; they also are without teeth and
the veins all join close to the edge to form a strong,
continuous vein all along and close to it. The flowers
are brown and fuzzy and immediately show it to belong
to the milkweeds! Common in this habitat, twining
about the rocks.
Andrachne chinensis Bunge is a low, dioecious bush
with slender branchlets crowded with alternate, ovate
leaves which are truncate to cordate at base, much paler
beneath, sometimes sparingly pubescent about midrib.
In the axils of the petioles are tiny buds covered by
reddish, fimbriate to shaggy scales. The flowers are
axillary and borne on very slender, nodding pedicels so
that they all face the earth. On turning them upward,
one is charmed by the lacy and symmetrical design form-
ed by the slenderly obovate, buffy petals alternating with
the broad, rounded, green sepals framing the central
group (in staminate flowers) of yellow, rather deeply
bifurcate scales, one opposite each petal and alternating
with the delicate, upward curving stamens. Common:
an underplant, preferring the shade of the others.

The Ch'aoyang An tract readily divides into two
distinct areas: the upper, a steep slope characterized by
a sprinkling of Arbor Vitae (Thuya orientalis L.), and
the lower, a gentle slope characterized by deciduous trees.
Through its centre runs a ravine bordered and shaded
chiefly by the Arbor Vitae. Needless to say, this wooded
tract harbors many of the above-mentioned species,
besides several which would only be found on the north
slope and several which are distinctly adventive from
the plain. Due to the gentle slope of the lower area
some of it is kept under cultivation by the keeper. This
undoubtedly aids in the maintenance of some of the
plains species. Altogether sixty-five species of vascular
plants (including Selaginella mongolica) were collected.
