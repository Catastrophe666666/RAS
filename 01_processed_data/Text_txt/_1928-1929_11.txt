THE ORIGIN OF THE CHINESE
PRIESTHOOD
By E. T. C. WERNER
We cannot observe the Chinese priesthood in its
beginnings, differentiating from the priest, because the
earliest records show clearly that the differentiation
had then already taken place. The distinction is impor-
tant, for though a priesthood implies priests, priests do
not imply a priesthood. This distinction has not, I
believe, hitherto been made; yet it is not only legitimate
and essential, but helps to place an obscure subject in a
clearer light.
At the beginning of things, and for some time after,
the priest is individual and unincorporated, and if, later,
we find him at the head, or a member, of a priesthood,
the situation is similar to that of a housewife engaging
servants to assist her in duties grown too onerous for her
to discharge single-handed. It is in this way that one
type of priesthood arises. We may call it the patriarchal
or royal type. But there is another and very (perhaps
more) important type. The two have the same origin,
but only in the sense that they both have their root in
the fear of the spirits of the dead. Otherwise they differ
as an oak and a willow, though growing in the same
ground, differ from each other. Which is to be compared
with the oak and which with the willow, will depend upon
the circumstances of each case.
It is only in the very earliest ages, and for no great
length of time, that priests of the first type remain
unincorporated. In the most primitive times and among
the least developed races, where each family has a god
unto itself, the father of the family is the priest. We
have here not only the proximate origin (the ultimate
origin being the fear of the dead above-mentioned) of

the first type of priest, but also the origin of the name,
presbyter, or elder, the intermediary between the people
of this and the spirits of the other world. He may be
called the unofficial chaplain of the tombs. As families
coalesce into clans, clans into tribes, and tribes into
nations, the fathers or patriarchs of these are also their
priests. At first there is little division of labour. As
the father of the family combines domestic duties with
those of defence against enemies and propitiation of the
gods, so, in the nation, the king is simultaneously high
priest, chief ceremonialist, commander-in-chief, etc.
And when, individual capacities being limited, it becomes
necessary to delegate a portion of these multifarious
duties, the delegation of the religious duties initiates a
priesthood properly so-called.
This delegation is at first temporary only, to a
brother or other member of the family. With further
increase of business the delegation becomes permanent,
but only complete in the most civilized nations. Gods
being apotheosized ancestors, prayers and sacrifices to
them are normally made by descendants, who thus
constitute at first a royal priesthood, which, where royal
descendants or relatives are wanting, or in the rare cases
where disestablishment has taken place, develops into a
purely official or an unofficial priesthood.
Thus as the godhead early evolves into a godhood,
so also does the priesthead evolve into a priesthood.
In the case of China, this type of priesthood and its
origin are too obvious to need emphasizing. The kings
and emperors have always been also the high priests of
the nation, attendant upon the spirits on high, and inter-
mediaries between the gods (their ancestors) and the
people (their "children"). It was this relation which
naturally gave them great influence with the supernatural
beings.
A further general rule is illustrated by the case of
China. It cannot truthfully be said that China has ever
been priest-ridden. Where there is strong personal
government the priest-class does not gain the ascendancy.
And so it has been here.
Before going on to observe the second type of priest-
hood it may be as well to notice two points. Regarding
the type of priesthood just described, it is stated in at
least one standard work that "this priesthood was in its
inception an official creation, not a spontaneous develop-
ment of the animistic substratum of Chinese religion."

There are here two errors. While it is true that the
royal type of priesthood may be termed an official
creation, the writer does not note the stage where it is
not so much official as domestic; and further, following
De Groot, he posits the substratum of Chinese religion
as animistic. Next, he does not see that the priesthood,
being a delegation by the high-priest, finds through him
its ultimate origin in the fear of the spirits of the dead
(which origin is not animistic).
Regarding this fear of the dead, it is not at first
obvious why mankind in general should have feared the
spirits of their ancestors at least to the extent to which
they are generally supposed to have done. The fact is,
that they have feared them very little, if at all. The
deceased parents and ancestors, when in this world,
supplied their descendants with shelter, food, and cloth-
ing, and at times may have been angry with and punished
them for negligence or disobedience. But that can
hardly be the complete explanation of this widespread
belief. In this life it was more the duty of the children,
assisted by domestic retainers, to attend upon their
parents than to provide for them. That relation is now
inverted, and from being the purveyors for their
dependants the ancestors have now become literally the
"dependants" (kuei) of their descendants. It is
reasonable to suppose that, though quite a number of
domestic servants may have been despatched to attend
upon the ancestors in the next life, if the descendants
neglected them in any way, by failing to provide them
with all the necessaries and even luxuries they had been
accustomed to in the previous one, the latter would be
wroth with them and visit them with calamity. In fact,
expecting the dead to return quite soon, as the primitive
man does, or later and later (as at some remote judg-
ment-day), as the relatively civilized man does, the
descendants may regard themselves merely as tenants or
trustees of his property, which he will reclaim for his
own use.
Nevertheless, a complete explanation would seem to
be found only in the fact that the ancestors, when in this
life, were not only the caterers for their descendants, but
also their protectors against hostile individuals or tribes,
or even against ferocious attacks of the dog in the next
courtyard. Therefore, if angered in any way, they would
fail or cease to exercise their influence or power against
the evil machinations of the kind of spirits most dreaded.

were not superstitions, but living beings; and we seem

Chinese literature of an exorcist who was originally an
ordinary military man who carried weapons, and who,
perhaps, went mad. This was, apparently, the origin
of the fang-hsiang shih, or inspector of localities,
mentioned in the Chou-li and the Li-chi; for

Chinese funeral processions, and exorcism of the grave-
pit before the coffin is lowered into it (see infra).

The fang-hsiang shih's official equipment and duties

evil; they are, in fact, the good spirits of the four quarters
of, or under, heaven. But when they descend to lurking
in the dark corners of grave-pits, it becomes a case of
not touching pitch without being defiled, and they are
supposed to be tainted with) the yin-ness of their

None of these functionaries, however, is a priest.
They are all concerned with the negative, the evil, the
yin side, and go before to prepare a place for the yang
to be established in its stead. In the person who under-
takes this latter duty we see the priest proper. He is
the wu, a simple character reduced from its original
elaborate pictogram representing gesticulating hands,
waving sleeves, and much talk. He does not seem origin-
ally to have had the bad character now generally asso-
ciated with wu and wu-ism. The I-shih-chi-shih places
his origin in the misty ages of Huang Ti (as it does also
that of the fang-hsiang shih); which is at least safe, for
good and evil, and the necessity of substituting yang for
yin, must be pretty well coeval with the birth of mankind.
And, as with the fang-hsiang shih, so with the wu-he
acquires his status through his own innate or acquired
ability of influencing or controlling-in his case, the
beneficent-spirits of the other world. The wu already
occupied an important place in the funereal and eccle-
siastical functioning of the earliest monarchs, whom they
accompanied on public occasions as State officials, but
seem quite soon to have fallen into disrepute, though the

The wu were priests of both sexes, but there was
also a class called hsi, composed of male priests only.
Giles (Chinese-English Dic.) gives only the sound chi,
but I think this is an error. A hsi was a man who, by
fasting and other rites presently to be noted, served or
worshipped spirits, to procure their aid, and who could
see spiritual existences and put them on, as it were, like
a garment, thus resembling them and acting as they
would. A wu was a man or a woman who, by certain
gesticulations and incantations, could cause beneficent
spirits to appear and descend, and by their presence
prevent the approach and counteract the influence or
actions of demons and other maleficent beings or things.
They became wu and hsi in a similar manner to that in
which others had become fang-hsiang shih-by proving
that they could foretell future events, heal the sick, bring
rain in times of drought, and generally that they
possessed power or influence over the spirits, but in their
case over the good yang kind, who would accordingly
imbue them with yang, or even themselves take up their
abode in their corporeal bodies (for they were human
beings, and not shên, like the yin kind), and thus enable
them to achieve the desired results. Shên settling in a
male person made him a hsi or a wu; in a female person,
made her a wu only. It may seem strange that so yin a
person as a woman could become predominantly yang,
but we have a parallel case in the enormously swollen
yang-ness of the modern Western woman.
The greatest influence of the hsi and the wu arose
from the belief that they could communicate with and
control the spirits of the other world and convey their
wishes to the living, but their services were also requisi-
tioned as medicine-men to chase away disease-conveying
creatures, such as foxes, rats, snakes, lizards, toads,
insects, and many others. They established a hereditary
priesthood of their own, but as Taoism grew they attach-
ed themselves to it, and regarded themselves as members
of its borrowed priesthood, in later times becoming
identified with it. They, however, had temples and
images and paid officers of their own, and offered sacri-

As with all similar cults in China, their fortunes
varied from time to time. Occasionally they became a
danger to the State, by plotting to overthrow the govern-
ment. A notorious instance of a check being put to the

to be thrown into the river to inform the river-god that
his fiancée would be sent to him on another day. How
dangerous they could be in ordinary life, and the great
risks they themselves ran, is shown by the kinds of
demons they had to deal with:-those of the ground,
water, trees, foxes, wolves, tigers, dogs, reptiles, birds,

by the practice of preparing a poison-cauldron (#

which devoured one another, until the last survivor,
containing the super-poison-quintessence of them all,
was as dangerous and fatal a weapon as the TNT of
modern civilized warfare.
They also had much social influence, and succeeded
in defying the Confuciist rule against women appearing
in public in the presence of men. Wu-ism prevailed far
more in the southern than in the northern provinces, and
was more in favour with women than with men.
Those results were achieved through the exercise of
those powers (gifts supposed to manifest themselves
spontaneously) over the spirits of the unseen world, and
the office of the wu and hsi was to put those powers into
action on occasions when the road, grave, house, body,
etc., having been duly prepared by expulsion of the yin
(the purge before the tonic), it was necessary forthwith
to supplant the latter by the more auspicious influence.
Consultation with the spirits (wên hsi) was obtain-
ed by burning incense, putting some rice into a cup of
wine, hanging down the head and muttering until the
mouth foamed, or jumping and dancing (as enraged
people often do), after which the will or message of the
spirit was supposed to have been received and could be
revealed to the parties concerned. The uncanny nature
of the spirits is indicated by their sometimes "soughing
like the wind and settling behind the curtains."

Note that the wu and hsi were believed to have the
power of compelling the spirits to obey them. Should,
however, the spirits be indisposed to descend from on
high or grant the request, more drastic measures
might be resorted to, such as in case of a drought placing
his image in the burning sun, or a Ewang might be used
for the same purpose. A wang is an infirm and dodder-
ing old hag, deformed, idiotic, or crazy, whose unusual
behaviour is ascribed to possession by demons. Wang
were useful for exposing to the rays of the burning sun
in cases of drought. Or a young boy, who was taken
along in case his services might be required, and stood
by the side of the wu, might be substituted; for boys are
yang, and the younger they are the better the yang, so
that their utterances on those occasions are spiritual
revelations, even the ditties (t'ung-yao) hummed
or sung by them being listened to by passers-by and inter-
preted as messages or commands from the other world.
But if the spirits were still deaf, obdurate, dilatory,
etc., it was necessary to adopt a different attitude. Con-
siderable knowledge of spirit-nature was shown. Force
having failed, persuasion was tried. But since for the
same person to adopt the latter method after the former
had failed would indicate weakness, the services of a
chu, or invoker, were requisitioned. He was usually
brought along with the wu, first in case his intercession
might be needed, and later as an established institution.
He also had acquired much yang, but he influenced the
spirits by request, pleading, sympathy, conciliation,
prayer, and not by the relatively antagonistic or despotic
methods of the wu and the hsi, though each occasionally
adopted the policy of the other.
Thus it was in the beginning; but the duties of the
different classes, engaged in this dangerous occupation,
soon became mixed or interchangeable, and we find the
wu and the hsi performing exorcizing duties properly
belonging to the fang-hsiang shih, and using his imple-
ments, the branches of the peach-tree and the bundles
of reeds the former being by nature so impregnated
with yang that merely by waving a branch the evil spirits
were sent scampering off, rendered innocuous, or killed,
when the reeds (also saturated with yang) would be used
to sweep up the remains of the spirits or other evil influ-
ences, in the same way that a Chinese coolie would sweep
up dead cockroaches, flies, and other yin things which
afflict mankind.

In the title of this brief sketch of the origin of the
Chinese priesthood, using the term in the singular, I
include the royal and quasi-royal and the quasi-servile
priesthoods, because in China they have been comple-
mentary to each other, at times practically one and the
same, and because the militant (or to avoid ambiguity,
let us say soldier) priest, and the battle-prompting and
warrior-applauding priest have been inconspicuous, while
the priest-class has never set its foot on the neck of or
subjugated the political government, nor in any notice-
able degree usurped civil functions, such as those of
judges, etc. As, in social developments, archaic forms
frequently survive and function alongside newer forms,
so, not only do we find the priest existing and functioning
individually after a priesthood has become an established
institution, but we also find the latter functioning simul-
taneously with priesthoods of another kind having sepa-
rate though related origins. In China, the two types-
quasi-royal and quasi-servile-existed together, at times
overlapping, though Confuciist priests were not seen at
private funerals. We here see the marked difference in
character between the two, for it is difficult to imagine
the emperor or his high-priest performing, for example,
the duties of the grave-hopper as herein described.
This double priesthood-the vast body of exorcists
and the much smaller body of official (one might almost
say Confuciist) priests-is all that can be considered
under the title of the origin of the Chinese priesthood.
I have said nothing about Buddhist and Taoist priest-
hoods, because these were not in their origin Chinese:
they were borrowed, one might rightly say stolen, for
priesthood-stealing has been by no means uncommon in
the history of religions. The rule seems to have been,
if you have none, borrow or steal one; and priesthoods,
like umbrellas, were regarded as legitimate loot. This
stealing and grafting is illustrated, among innumerable

Yet, once borrowed, we see the same cause producing

in the West as in the East. Religions, though growing
in far distant lands, are all planted in the same earth.
If we really believed that our departed loved ones were in
need of food or clothing, we should do as the Chinese do.
Only this week I read in a London paper of an English
woman who habitually places food for the dead on her
husband's grave.
Originally separate, the functions of exorcist, in-
voker, and priest in the West are now generally united
in one person, as we saw they have largely become in
China. As, after invocatory prayers over the dead in
the chapel, he precedes the coffin in the funeral proces-
sion, exorcizing evil influences by flattering and endea-
vouring to appease the wrath of the holy ghost, by
reading passages from the sacred books (the sacred
classics in China are regarded as potent exorcizing in-
fluences), and by beseeching the great spirit to "take the
plague away" and "hold not his peace at his tears," the
Western robed priest or clergyman is a k'ai-lu shên, a
chu and wu all in one. In modern, as in ancient and
mediaeval times, in all parts of the world, the skeleton
hand of the Dead keeps its firm grasp on the human mind.
