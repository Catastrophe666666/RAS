LANGUAGE AND NEW THOUGHT
or, New Words for New Ideas
By Dr. EVAN MORGAN
We are living in an epoch of exceptional weight in the
creation of new words. It is an age full of perplexity
and difficulty, of disaster and change. It is an age of
transition and cataclysmic events; nevertheless, it is an
age rich in new thought and words, which are finding their
way into the new dictionaries. "Boycott," "Bolsheviks"
(Ti chih and Chin lieh tang), new ideas and new words,
both in English and Chinese, which will be the interesting
study of some future historian as he tries to expound the
catastrophic age of the 20th century-for the rumblings
and reverberations of the present will pass through many
future centuries, and be recorded, partly, in the words the
present is creating. We may even imagine the scholar in
some distant age trying to reconstruct the idea of mo t'o
k'a in some ancient script, wondering what it could have
been; just as we are trying to-day to know of the self-
propelling machine invented by the Duke of Chou about
3,000 years ago.
This generation in the midst of the hurly-burly of
new experiences is in the condition governed, as the
Chinese say, by the subjective point of view and is like
the cicada and the young dove, which see things from
their own point of view. (Chuang Tzu: Giles, p. 306).
We are meeting with new experiences, feeling after new
expressions, finding new things and making fresh dis-
coveries, and human-like, endeavouring to put these into
signs and symbols that can be understood. Like every-
thing else, language is in the process of development.
Whilst it would not be correct to say the language is new,
nevertheless, there are many new things in it. And the
new things are finding much favour. But the law of
order must be remembered. Dean Inge has said: "It is
most significant that the Gospel at once introduced a new
ethical terminology. The Greek words which we translate
love (or charity), joy, peace, hope, humility, were no part
of the stock-in-trade of Greek moralists before Christ.

Some Chinese writers and speakers greatly affect new
terms. They think it elegant: they imagine it creates
more impression to use them. They exist in every age.
Bruyère, you remember, wrote one of his essays on such
people, and critics here have also pointed the finger of
scorn at these lovers of new words and doctrines.
THE SOURCES OF THE NEW WORDS
The editors of the New Edition of Hillier's dictionary
say in their Preface: "The eight years which have
elapsed since the first edition of this dictionary was
published have witnessed the advent of a Republican
regime in China, followed by a development of parlia-
mentary and legal institutions, and of the press, all of
which events have had a marked effect on the language.
New terms have been found necessary in order to enable
public and private speakers and writers on the events of the
day to convey to their audiences the new ideas connoted by
revolution and progress. It was inevitable under the
circumstances that, in order to meet this need, recourse
should be had in the first instance to the kindred
language of the neighbour Japan, where large stocks of
expressions coined in recent years to give currency in the
East to the ideas of the West were ready at hand. So
marked has been the Japonisation of the modern Chinese
vocabulary as a result of this borrowing that it is hardly
too much to say that a Japanese dictionary has become
almost an essential in the study of the language in its
present form."

There is truth and exaggeration in this. It is true
that Japan has been a factor in the creation of new words,
or rather, in the resurrection of old words: but it is an
exaggeration to say that there has been a Japonisation of
the modern Chinese vocabulary. In English the influx of
French words during the Norman conquest was considera-
ble but the language was still Anglo-Saxon and maintained
its identity. It was in no sense Gallicized. The example is
a suitable parallel to the present tincture of words from
Japan. Chinese remains Chinese, and the language is in
no sense Japonised. Even most of the words that bear the
Japanese complexion are old Chinese terms. The words
of purely new Japanese creation are very few. Another
apt illustration of the case may be found in the expressive
slang of America. These can be traced largely to old
English, as is pointed out by Mr. Lowell in that interesting
disquisition that forms an introduction to the Bigelow
papers. Further it is questionable how far this influx
has affected the colloquial. It mostly affects literary
compositions. It is true not a few new terms have been
coined by the Japanese, like the words "police station" and
"police beat" (*), many legal terms, political, chemical
terms and terms in physics have been adopted and will
stay in the new family. But it cannot be maintained that
all of the Japanese terms that have been introduced and
found currency in the Chinese language will remain, for
the simple reason that the storehouse of Chinese words is
full, and well stored with a great variety of words that
can be used to meet new conditions.
How NEW PHRASES ARE MADE
Let us consider more definitely how new terms are
created. They are created in many ways:
1. New combinations.
2. New contents and thought given to old terms.
3. New dress.
4.
New formations.
5. New phrases introduced by transliteration.
Generally speaking the Genius of Language hunts
amongst the piles of old materials for suitable words to
new ideas, and in this respect it may be pointed out that
there is a difference between the method used in English
and that used in Chinese. English has borrowed largely
from outside. Its structure helps it to do so. Chinese on
the other hand borrows largely from within in the creation

Thus, then, for the most part the language develops
by a new shuffling, new combinations, new arrangement
of old words. Very often a new content is given to an old
phrase. The word for "prayer" is tao kao, but the custom
and the experience of the Christian church has given a
new content to the ancient word so that the phrase is
considered to be a new one.
But the great way is one of new alliances: new
partners. These are being constantly formed. Just as a
family grows by new alliances begetting new children, so
with language. Take for example, "railway," t'ieh lu or
t'ieh tao. T'ieh, "iron" and tao, "road" are old words.
But, until recently, they were never in alliance. There
have been many roads beside the railroad. But the
experience of the Chinese was limited until the thing
itself came upon them-they had not heretofore passed
over this, but when it did come, they were ready with a
name, “iron-road." I believe that was also the first name
given to the invention in English: they called it the
"iron-road" and the "iron-horse." The Chinese have done
much the same. It will be known that the Chinese also
have the railroad, kuei tao. In both languages we thus
have the road, the characteristic of which is rails, or iron
bars in line. In connection with this railway enterprise,
it will be noticed that the Chinese have hovered between
two words, i.e., tao and lu. The words themselves you
know in an old combination tao lo, "road"-and both are
used in the new terms. T'ieh tao kung sso, "railway," but
we also have t'ieh lu tse lieh, "railway by-laws." Possibly,
the hovering between t'ieh lu and t'ieh tao is a matter of
provinces. In Shantung t'ieh tao is more common: in
Shansi, t'ieh lu. T'ieh lu possibly is the more Wenli-ish-
and t'ieh tao is more Mandarin.
With regard to the "iron road" and its corresponding

It might be advisable at this point to introduce a
little caution as to the use of new words. We might very
well recall to mind the imperfection of words. This
applies equally to new words and old, and to every
language. John Locke laid down certain general ideas
on these lines. First of all, "by recalling to mind what
the primary use of words is, we shall be ready for certain
imperfections and inconsistencies. What philologists
and users of language should be on their guard is not to
perpetuate a wrong use of a new term, but rather help to
recover its proper and lawful use.
"Words are used for recording and communicating
ideas for our own use, and to communicate with others.
In a sense they are only symbols. Now if a man uses the
same sign always for the same idea there will be no
difficulty or confusion. But where identity of use is lost
uncertainty of meaning creeps in. Words in common
use, in conversation and commerce are easily stereotyped
and come to have a fixed meaning to all. In such subjects
as philosophy and theology, where the ideas are more
recondite and the meanings not quite so clear, since
they are not so frequently used, it is easy to see how
imperfections arise. The speaker and the hearer again
may understand different things by the terms used.
Hence there is a degree of doubtfulness very often."
New names in English such as "shun," "wheedle,
"banter," were created by the inventor to indicate certain
ideas he had in his mind. At first possibly these did not
convey quite the same idea to those who heard. So it
required time and use for them to gather round them the
same definite meaning. So it is with new terms in every

The Chinese theory of unity is really a profound one.
Its doctrine is very deep-rooted and its fruit widespread.
It has entered into every department of their life, politi-

of view, and you must remember they, too, were the cicada
and the young dove looking at things subjectively, the

Think of the phrase Tsung t'ung, "President." Only a
few years ago what struggles there were to get into this
position of honour: what millions were squandered, what
trampling of liberty to step into this coveted position-
the throne of power as people supposed. We think of the
good-natured Ts'ao Kun and the loyal Wu Pei-fu. But the
honour was fleeting. Ts'ao is a private person to-day-

THE CREATORS OF NEW TERMS.
Who are the creators of the new terms? It is very
difficult to say really. For the most part they are un-
known-mostly journalists and writers of books. Stealth-
ily have these new members crept into the family of
language. They have only sought a place for their
offspring, they have coveted no name or glory for them-
selves. It is so with most of the operators and forces of
creation. And even where the author is known it was
only so by chance and not design, just similar to Apelles,
the great painter. You know he is the author of a famous
phrase, "Cobbler stick to your own last." It arose in this
wise: Apelles was in the habit of exhibiting any new
painting in front of his house and to hide behind a door
to catch people's criticisms. One day a cobbler came by
and criticised a new picture: the next day he came again
and made somewhat uncomplimentary remarks which
made Apelles quite angry so that he cried out, "Cobbler,
stick to your own last." This has become current coin;
in such chance ways many new phrases have arisen to
enrich the vocabularies of men.
At the beginning of the paper it was said this was an
age for the creation of a great number of new terms.
There is an overflowing addition to every language.
Particularly is this the case with China. At the close of
the paper we might well ask what do these new terms
indicate in the evolution of human welfare. Do the terms
that we have passed in review imply optimistic view or

Words are nothing in themselves. Their only value
is to serve as symbols of communication: to stir the soul
of others by that which quickens one's own: that is to say
they are valuable only as indicators to the state of thought
that lie behind them. And we may say that of the new
terms in China they are an indication of a growing
national life, they indicate a swelling tide of new currents
that are sweeping through this ancient people. If we
look at their hands their fingers are none the less long and
