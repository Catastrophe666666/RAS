LITTLE LU-FU
(Translated from the Original Chinese Ballad)
When the roseate hue of dawn
Flushes pink the cottage wall,
And the first rays of the sun
Waken up the inmates all,
Then rises little Lu-fu.
Silkworms are her gentle care.
Every day she walks for miles
Gathering tender mulberry leaves
For her pets. All joy and smiles
Is the little Lu-fu.
Silk cords tie her basket neat
To the branch of cassia tree
That is on her shoulder poised;
No one dainty is as she,
None like little Lu-fu.
Down upon her graceful neck
Fall the locks of raven hair,
And like moonbeams in her ears
Shine the pearls. Like spirit fair
Is the little Lu-fu.
Her long skirt of gauzy silk
Falls in pale green folds, and close
To her shoulders clings her robe
Tinted in the richest rose;
Charming little Lu-fu.
Passers-by who see her stop,
Stroke their beards and drop their load.
Little children lift their caps,
Bow with reverence on the road
To the little Lu-fu.

LITTLE LU-FU
83
Ploughmen in the fields forget
To guide their ploughs when she goes by,
And the toilers with the hoe
Slight their task whene'er they spy
Pretty little Lu-fu.
The Governor of the south rides by
Splendid in his five-horse car,
Stops to rest his steeds and sees
The little maiden from afar.
He calls the little Lu-fu.
And when asked she answers bright,
"Not quite twenty years am I,
But my fifteenth birthday's past.
That's our cottage, just close by.
I am little Lu-fu."
"Wealth and honour shall be thine,
Pretty maid, but come with me."
"No, Your Grace has wife at home;
My betrothèd, none but he
Shall take little Lu-fu."
ELFRIDA HUDSON.
