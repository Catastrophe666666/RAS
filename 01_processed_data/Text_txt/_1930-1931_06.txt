CAN CHINESE WRITING BE
SIMPLIFIED?
By HERBERT CHATLEY, D. SC. (Lond.)
There is an urgent need for some simplification of
the method of writing Chinese. With all its elegance,
suggestiveness and historic character, the established
method is a burden which weighs extremely heavily on
the Chinese brain. It is even tenable that many of the
idiosyncrasies of the people are due to the cumbrous
system of recording ideas. Modern psychology shows
quite clearly that thought, speech and writing are so
closely associated that they react on each other to a re-
markable extent. Precision and simplicity in language
and writing are paralleled by clarity of thought.
PRESENT SYSTEM
The standard system of Chinese characters (also
called, not quite correctly, "ideographs") has one charac-
ter for each word and there are therefore as many
characters as words, not sounds. In ordinary practice
some 5,000 or so are employed (the Classics have about
this number but many of their characters are obsolete),
good literary custom uses about 10,000 and a complete
dictionary has about 50,000. Of the latter number many
are variants and rare or obsolete forms.
The writing of the characters has been convention-
alized into a number of strokes which vary from 1 to 36
in number in any one character. The average number
of strokes in a character is about 12.
There is naturally great difficulty in learning even a
few thousand characters and the Chinese student must
spend many years in thoroughly mastering them. In this
way a great deal of mental energy is consumed and an
unnecessarily tremendous burden is impressed on the
growing brain. Much wasted space is occupied in re-
cording ideas and the loss of time in acquiring the medium
of writing is colossal. The mental training involved has
some use in developing memory and notions of form, but

the results are out of all proportion to the effort. One
of the practical results is that the art of reading and
still more that of writing is confined to quite a small
minority. Apologists will naturally emphasize the historic
interest of the characters, the beauty of Chinese calligra-
phy and the aesthetic features of such a script, but all
this is again much overdone unless one is convinced that
reading and writing should be confined to the leisured
classes of exceptional intelligence. The elegancies of
style, the nuances of meanings in the characters, the
necessity of continuing to interpret old records, are all
points in favour of retention but cannot weigh seriously
against the use of a medium which will be much easier to
acquire and use.
The characters during their 4,000 years of evolution
have lost to a great extent their ideographic character
and many are really phonetics. Various schemes of
classification have been employed. Undoubtedly the
earliest characters (say 1800 B.C.) were purely picture
writing, but stereotyped forms developed and about 1,000
reputedly primitive elements can be perceived.
Since Kang Hsi's time the characters have been
arranged under 214 radicals, each of which form part
or the whole of every character. The remaining parts,
some 900 or so in number, are more or less phonetic but
are not consistently so, except in the light of an abstruse
theory of phonetic evolution.
To add to the difficulties there is a distinct difference
between the vernacular and the written language even in
its most modern journalistic forms.
Literary composition abounds in metaphor and elliptic
formulae which obscure the meaning, but this does not
affect the question of writing except in so far as it leads
to the introduction of rare characters. Poetry is almost
unintelligible to the poorly educated for this reason.
ALTERNATIVES
The need for simplification has long been felt. A
reduction in the number of current characters has actual-
ly occurred. Chinese, like all other forms of writing, has
a tendency to become formal and more simple but this
tendency has not proceeded far enough. There is a new
school of thought which aims to produce a standard
vocabulary of 1,000 characters for work-people but it has
already surrendered to the extent of using 1,300 and, as
it has no principle of reduction except that of frequency

The Chinese have made certain experiments towards
a phonetic system. The phonetic element in compound
characters has been referred to already. In the 3rd
century the Buddhists introduced the Fan Ch'ieh (
system consisting of pairs of standard characters making
initials and finals. The Japanese more than 1,000 years
ago developed the Kana system of syllabaries which is
fully adequate for purely Japanese words but breaks
down when applied to the enormous number of Chinese
words which Japan has borrowed. Various missionaries
(such as those of Wenchow) have attempted to
romanize Chinese and a few years ago an alphabet of 40
signs was invented in Peking, but all these attempts
have so far proved almost valueless.
Nevertheless this is the only direction in which pro-
gress can be made and it is therefore necessary to consider
what the difficulties really are.
PROBLEMS OF PHONETICIZATION
The fundamental difficulty is that the language is
monosyllabic, so that there is a paucity of actual word
sounds (800 or 2,000 in number according as tones are
disregarded or not) and a consequent complete un-
certainty as to the meaning of individual sounds. Thus
a sound such as Chi may have over a hundred meanings
and correspond to a hundred different characters so that
phoneticization per se deprives the characters of meaning.
A second difficulty is dialectic variation. In different
parts of the country the changes in the sound attached

A further difficulty is that the Phoenician alphabet
and its derivatives are deficient in some sounds and
redundant in others, while "scientific" alphabets invented
by philologists are unfamiliar and pedantic in character.
Thus the English (really Latin) variant of the Phoeni-
cian alphabet has the following useless letters:-C, Q, W,
X and Y, and lacks several vowels, aspirates, nasals and
and diphthongs. Nevertheless the Latin alphabet is used
by about half the world's population and is obviously the
only basis for a system of "Romanization." What is
then the solution?
To the writer's mind, no sudden solution is probable
or even possible, but there is a line of development which
may finally lead to a solution. This is the systematic in-
troduction of polysyllables. If we take a di-syllable such
as Ti Fang, there is little ambiguity in the meaning as
compared with complete uncertainty in the meanings of
the separate sounds Ti and Fang. With a tri-syllable such
as Huo-che-chan ambiguity almost entirely disappears,
except in respect to proper names. Incidentally it should
be observed that in all languages proper names tend to
lose meaning.
In Japan the Kana systems of romanization fully
represent true Japanese polysyllables and the same would
be true in China for polysyllables. As to which alphabet
should be used, as remarked above the Latin one seems
preferable in spite of its defects, simply because it is
easy to learn and is already extremely widely used. New
diphthongs are preferable to dieresis. Aspirates are
perhaps best expressed by the Wenchow system of Hard
and Soft Consonants (P, B, T, D, Ch, J) which are much
easier to remember and less liable to error than the
apostrophes of the Wade system.
Doubtless ardent patriots would prefer a less foreign
method, but it should be observed that the Latin alphabet
was foreign to almost all the nations that now use it and
is the nearest approach to a really international set of
sound signs in existence.
There will be enormous passive resistance by the
million or so literates of China to any drastic change of
writing and it will take many years to put into general use
a new system. In the meantime if a simple romanization
is used in conjunction with polysyllabization a step will
have been taken towards the end. Vernacular forms
