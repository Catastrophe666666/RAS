These notes are based on my observations ever since
arrival in China, in 1894. Continuing the habit formed
in youth when making reports on bird migration to the go-
vernment Bureau on Biological Survey, under Prof. Wells
W. Cooke, Assistant Biologist, the writer kept a record
of all birds seen and places visited each day of the year.
Unfortunately the habit was not resumed in China until
March 1896 and was allowed to be broken during the
Boxer cataclysm, May 1900 to January 1903, and again
from April 1904 to March 1913, so that we have records
for only some 17 or 18 years.
For the last ten years Mr. Hubbard has interested
himself in bird study, making a collection of skins, and
keeping migration notes. While the main body of our
notes is based on my own work, I have had access to his
collection and he has made additions from his records.
In important cases we differentiate by our initials.
Where no initial is given the W is to be understood.
We cannot lay claim to scientific accuracy in covering
our field as a professional ornithologist might, for these
notes are simply made in passing while occupied with
regular mission work in school, church or Y. M. C. A.,
while travelling, doing famine relief or taking vacation.
Systematic daily studies have been impossible, in the
busiest migration seasons, as those were also our busiest
seasons. While we have been able to observe superficially
at all seasons on the Peking plain where we live, we have
not been able to make all the year studies of the seacoast,
the Mongol border, or the mountain region. These have
had but occasional short visits in summer, or winter, and
still briefer visits in spring and fall.
The Region. We have had no opportunity to work
the extreme southern tail of the province. The spring
of 1921 was spent in famine relief as far south as Tz'u
Chou and Tai Ming Fu; and one summer month was spent
at Tabool, the extreme northern limit of our province,
designated in the notes as the Mongol plain or Mongol
border. So the area we study runs from 36° to 42°
North latitude. Covering six degrees of latitude, our
field also covers about the same number of degrees of
longitude from Shanhaikuan on the east to the Shansi
border on the west. This is an area approximately the
size of New York state.
There are four distinctly marked bird areas. 1.

"Along these rivers which shift to lower levels,
laterally, each time the old course is built up with

region. Many sections of these rivers and their outlying

the incessant chorus of reed warblers, (and the calls of
their parasitic cuckoos), fill the neighborhood.

wrens, warblers, shrikes, drongos, orioles, and other
groups too numerous to mention.

The Seacoast.-The seacoast is the western shore
of the Gulf of Pechihli, from Shanhaikuan on the north,
southwest past the mouth of the Lüan River to the mouth
of the Peiho at Taku and around the bend to the Shantung
border, a total of some 200 miles. The waterfowl and
shore birds, as Mr. La Touche has pointed out follow this
shore line, doubtless attracted by the great marshes and
mudflats at the mouths of the rivers. We have studied it
only at Peitaiho, when we have had summer vacations
with a few week end visits at the other seasons. At
Peitaiho there is a rocky promontory and beach more
extensive than at Chingwangtao and for that reason
perhaps not quite so favorable for migration study. The
birds are detained there more among the trees of the
settlement and of the little "Lotus Hills," and by the more
extensive pools and marshes, and sand flats to the north
of the summer resort, so that observation of migrants and
distinguishing of them from sojourners is not so easy.
The "sand flats" locally called "shell beach," reaching
back a mile from the sea up the arm of a small stream,
often covered by the tides, are a splendid assembly ground
for ducks, geese and waders in migration times, the more
or less grassed sand dunes and the pools interspersed
among them with fairly luxuriant willows are very attrac-
tive and the larger fresh water ponds back two or three
miles and along the railway embankments furnish a
variation from the sea food which the wild fowl seem
to appreciate. The rugged and barren mountains ten or
fifteen miles north at this point seem to compress the
wildfowl migrants into a narrow highway here between
the mountains and the sea. Our references to Peitaiho,
and the sand flats refer to this section. For a fine account
of the region and the climate we refer to Mr. J. D. D. La
Touche's article in the Ibis for Oct. 1914 on "The Spring
Migration at Chingwangtao."
At Peitaiho the sea does not appear to freeze so
steadily as the bay at Chingwangtao "from the middle to
the end of Dec." until the middle of February, but on one
occasion it was a sea of ice as far as could be seen for a
few days. Usually the gulf is open for navigation all
winter. The climate, however, is of course the same as
that described by Mr. La Touche who says "The minimum
temperature in winter rarely falls below 7° Fahrenheit,
the mean winter temperature being between 18° in cold
winters and 26° for mild seasons, while in summer the
mean temperature of July and August ranges from 72°
to 75.5°, seldom reaching above 90°."

The Hills. The hill and mountain region is typified
by the Western Hills, which rise from one thousand to
five thousand feet above the sea-level plain, and are for
the most part bare of trees and scant of water. But in
the western part 15 miles east of Yu Chou there is an area
about 15 miles in diameter with the five peaks of Hsiao Wu
T'ai Shan, which rise to within a few feet of ten thousand
feet above sea level. The broad valley of the Sang Kan
to the north of it is about three thousand feet.
This area
has a Siberian flora and the avifauna, so far as we can
judge, corresponds, in that a few species of birds, that
regularly go farther north to breed, stop here in the small
growth of white birch, larch and spruce, so characteristic
of, say, the Daurian region in Siberia, east of Lake Baikal.
The remoter parts of the mountains west and north have
young forest, in places growing thick with saplings the
size of ones wrist or arm and fifteen to twenty-five feet
high, and from the Western tombs northwards, some of
the northern slopes, and here and there deep cool ravines
have a considerable bushy growth. It is sufficient to
afford a home and breeding resort for some species of
sparrows, shrikes, thrushes and warblers that are found
on the plain only as spring and fall migrants, and also a
winter shelter for others that pass on south as a rule. At
Hupeik'ou and beyond it furnishes a home for the black
cock and capercaillie which otherwise would not be found
this side of Siberia.
Where the clear mountain streams have cut their
gorges down to the lower valleys or plain, and there are
pools, waterfalls, huge boulders and frequent shade of
cliffs or trees, we have the haunts of the rock creeper, the
water thrush, water redstart, water ousel, hair crested
drongo, etc. Such are found at the Eastern tombs, east of
Nank'ow pass, Western Tombs, Yen Ch'ing Chou, Hsiao³
Wu T'ai Shan¹ and particularly above Ch'i Chung
K'ou³, and northwest of Chai T'ang some 10 miles, Lung
Men Chien.
These same streams lower down, where still protected
by the hills and warm enough to remain unfrozen all
winter, harbor many water birds, ducks, grebes, snipe,
kingfishers, etc., that we ordinarily consider too tender for
the cold. P'ing² Ku³ hsien, and the Western Tombs and the
stream west from Paotingfu are notable examples of this.

we have the very considerable pine groves planted around

still harbors a few Reeves' pheasants, black, white-backed

forest of the province, brings great sorrow of heart to all
nature-lovers who are aware of it. The streams flowing

still clear, and stocked with trout, in amazing and delight-

Mongol Border.-The Mongol plain is about 5,000 feet
above the sea, the most beautiful prairie, with enough of
broken, rocky ridges a few hundred feet high, and rounded
knolls to adorn the landscape, without cutting off the
infinite vistas of green. These brush-covered hills, and
the grassy expanse, give two kinds of bird habitat while
the great lakes and alkali stretches, like that of Angol Nor
near the border, with rivers continually flowing in to form
a seeping marsh, give another habitat where we have the
summer resorts of wild-fowl, gulls and terns.

Climate, etc.-Here we have coolness always in the
shade and cold nights when the shepherds need sheepskins
all summer, as on the top of the higher mountains. Snow
flurries may occur in August and September, and October
becomes cold. The water in wells a few feet from the
surface is icy. In winter extreme cold, far below zero is
common, and when accompanied by snow it drives even the
hardy sand grouse to the lower plain for the winter.
Mr. La Touche's description of the climate at Ch'ing-
wangtao as "extremely dry and bracing" applies still
more in Mongolia, and much less on the Peking Plain than
it does to the sea coast. The interior is much hotter in
summer than on the coast, and with a smaller rainfall.
The characteristic summer birds of the plain, like the
oriole, drongo, great reed warbler, are found in the valleys,
where trees and water suit, up into the mountain regions
to an elevation of about 3,000 feet above sea level. From
that point upwards to the tops of the many peaks that rise
to over 5,000 feet the avifauna changes again, probably
corresponding more to that of the Mongolian plain. From
about 4,000 feet to the top of Hsiao Tu Tai Shan we seem
to have a sub-arctic fauna and flora, and the breeding birds
correspond. But the ecology of the region and the deter-
mination of habitats and boundaries of life zones needs
much more study. In the list that follows we are impressed
with the number of Yangtzu birds, or such as have been
given the Chin Ling mountains, 34° N., as their northern
limit, that come to this northern edge of the great plain,
40° N., to breed. They may have been overlooked here-
tofore owing to the fact that many of them cannot nest on
the plain where the crow and magpie reign supreme, and
many of them can find suitable habitats only here and
there in remote places that have not been easily accessible
to students of bird life.

The Bearded Partridge is said to be common in the grassy
hills but I have stumbled across it but twice, a single bird once
and again a small flock at the Western Tombs. It is always
plentiful in the markets all winter. Three eggs were brought
me that were found freshly laid at Hsiling, July 26th, 1923.
It must have been a very late clutch. The eggs are 37mm by
29mm of a highly polished creamy gray color.
467.—COTURNIX COTURNIX JAPONICA Temminck & Schlegel.
H.3117, D.959, L.191, M.211.
DSMLWH
Quail.an'-ch'un', P., Paot.
The quail is found in the foothills near the Western Tombs
all the year but is most common in October. It is met occasion-
ally on the plain and is common in fall at Peitaiho. Found in
market all winter. We have specimens that we had supposed to
be the migratory quail C. coturnix (L.) but we now understand
that Dr. Hartert pronounces the North China birds all to be
C. japonica.

Manchurian Snow-pheasant. ho-chi', P., P.Ts.
This pheasant has occurred in the Peking market very
rarely. Probably the birds that have been sold there are from
Shansi but our hunter friends say that they used to be found
at the Western Tombs and are still to be found 200 li from there
in the more wooded mountains.

Mongolian Pucras. Sung' chi.
This bird is found in the markets every winter from early
November but is not nearly so common as the Daurian
Partridge, Mongolian Pheasant, Chukar, etc. It is said to be
found in the Eastern Tombs, but I have never met it. Mr. R. C.
Andrews shot it there.

REEVESII (Gray). H.3190, M.204.
Reeve's Pheasant. chih'-chi', P., Paot.
DSMWH
Before the game preserves connected with the Eastern
Tombs were thrown open to the Manchus for farming and
lumbering, in 1915, Reeve's Pheasant was fairly common in those
magnificent forested mountains but they are nearly exter-
minated now. A few fine specimens of the males have been
brought in this past year. In January 1903, I had two days
fine sport shooting among two or three flocks of males and
again in 1908 they were the most common pheasant there.
