ARTICLE I.
NOTES OF A JOURNEY FROM PEKING TO CHEFOO VIA
GRAND CANAL, YEN-CHOW-FOO, ETC.
BY REV. A. WILLIAMSON.*
On the 18th October, 1865, I set off from Peking in company
with a native teacher: at Tung-chow-foo we obtained a boat to
take us on to Lin-tsing-chow the spot where the Grand Canal
bifucrates, the main branch leading to Hang-chow-foo and the
River Yang-tze, the other called the Wei-ho to Ho-nan and the
west. Two days took us to Tien-tsin, and our journey may be
said to have begun.
Travelling along this portion of the Canal we found it in excel-
lent repair, from 80 to 100 feet wide and from 8 to 10 feet deep,
with the exception of a spot near Pau-t'ou where the banks were
out of order, and the water shallow. After travelling some distance
the winding of the stream showed its constructors had taken ad-
vantage of the bed of an ancient river, though the raised embank-
ments at some places, proved that where necessary it had been
constructed by human labor. The trade on the Canal did not come
up to my anticipations, not amounting nearly to that which was
carried on in prosperous times in the lower portions of Kiang-su.
The towns also along the banks were less flourishing than I had
anticipated, many being little better than heaps of ruins; the only
towns of real importance on the way to Lin-tsing being Tsau-chow
and Yüh-chow spelt Yi-chow in the Admiralty maps; the former
encloses a large space of ground but there does not appear to be
extensive business carried on. The latter which lies on the high-

The people appeared much poorer and less refined than south of
the Yang-tze-kiang; everywhere we met with Mahomedans; they
often saluted us from the banks, and in the streets of the cities,
with the cry "Mussulman," and claimed kindred with us: they
sometimes called upon us and said that they believed in the same
God, and were not like these stupid idolators among whom they
lived. They appear much less bigoted than their brethren in India;
and on enquiry we found that they had little or no connection now
with the west: in former times Pilgrims used to go to Mecca, and
in this way kept alive the spark of intolerance, but for many years
I could hear of no one having undertaken the journey. We found
numerous Mosques in this district, sometimes 3 and 4 in one city.
The scenery all along the banks from the capital, onwards to
Lin-tsing-chow was extremely uninteresting; it was one extended
level, swampy now and then, but for the most part excellent rich
soil, without a hill to relieve the landscape and few trees.
Rows of the castor oil plant almost covered both banks from near
Tien-tsin onwards to Lin-tsing. The towns and villages from
being built of the dried bricks, owing to the absence of stone, had
a dull monotonous appearance; the country was generally destitute
of game of any sort, indeed with the exception of domestic animals
scarcely a living creature was to be seen.
Arriving at Lin-tsing we found it to be an extensive market for
all kinds of goods; the city had been burned down by the Taiping
rebels several years ago, and had not been rebuilt; it presented a
sad sight, the walls, which were intact, enclosed only piles of bricks
now covered with weeds, and a few poor buildings where beggars
and semi-beggars lived. The inhabitants had rebuilt their houses
on the banks of the canals, and formed a large town, the main
street of which had a most animated appearance, crowded with

large shops and warehouses, and nearly two miles long; in it great
quantities of furs were exposed for sale. Here the Canal branched
off in two directions, one to Ho-nan and south-westward, and the
other and formerly the principal one proceeding south to Soochow
and Hang-chow. Here the famous locks commenced, but they were
now all out of repair and the Canal all but dry; accordingly we
had to leave our boat and hire carts. The road ran nearly parallel
with the Canal, and so we had the pain of seeing its delapidated
appearance, every now and then; we were told that it was useless
for between 60 and 70 miles, but receiving a supply from the
Yellow River, it again became navigable, and continued so on to
its ancient termination.
Proceeding onwards, we found cotton growing in great abun-
dance, and whole families and especially the female portion of the
households, busy picking the wool. Here for the first time I met
with numbers of those extraordinary wheelbarrows propelled by
sails, familiar to most of us in written accounts of China, but seen
by so few; at first sight we hardly knew what to make of them-
something moving along on dry ground with a sail set. Much in-
genuity was shown in the rig, some being handled in much the
same style as in native boats, the driver being able to reef or set
the sail at pleasure: fourteen bean-cakes (three ordinary mule loads
in this country) was no unusual load for one barrow, and sometimes
we met them with six bales of cotton, two being considered a good
load for a mule; there is however a considerable difference in point
of speed, a man with his barrow can only make on an average 70 li
per day, while 100 li, or upwards of 30 miles, is the ordinary task
for the mule. In some instances two men were attached to a
barrow, but in this case the load was increased.
The next morning we came in sight of the fine bell tower of
Tung-chang-foo, and at least reached the city about 8 o'clock A.M.
We found it to be a most important place; the city was in good
order, well fortified, and with a fine Bell Tower in the centre. But
the eastern suburbs far exceeded the city proper in importance;
they were most extensive and the trade appeared enormous, I had
seen nothing equal to them, unless it be the eastern suburbs of
Shanghai, or the great north street of Tien-tsin. The city was
nearly surrounded by water, partly by canal and the Yellow River.
In summer time pleasure boats ply for hire all round the place. In
the suburbs especially, as well as all over the city there was a great

Up to this point of our journey we found it somewhat difficult to

Having spent some hours in the city, we again set out, few
objects of interest presenting themselves. We examined the sluices
of the Canal and their construction; the canal was all but dry, and
the sluices entirely out of order. I found many of what seemed to
me to be sea-shells, in the mud of which the banks were composed;
the country appearing to have at one time been the bed of the sea.
Passing a village called Wo-chung, hills appeared in sight; we
knew that the famous Yellow River skirted their bases, and so our
enthusiasm began to rise. The road became extremely soft and
clayey, and we were nearly stuck fast; this part of the country
having evidently been recently overflowed by "China's Sorrow."
Slowly the river dawned upon our vision, like a mighty yellow
dragon lying at rest on the level soil: at two o'clock we reached
the ferry; there also we found a military post, for it was said there
were bands of mounted robbers in the neighbourhood; the soldiers
were very civil.
We found the river broad, rapid, and muddy; though not so
broad as we anticipated, much wider than the Thames at London
bridge. There were multitudes of ferry boats of all sizes plying,
and having selected one of the largest, had our cart and mules and
ourselves quickly placed on board, as we wished to travel 40 li
further that afternoon. When everything was arranged, a number
of men in single file dragged us up the side of the river a good
distance; this was the more easily accomplished that there was a
strong eddy. Being let go eight strong men seized two tremendous

Whilst the boatmen were thus engaged, I had a good illustra-
tion af the manner in which this wilful river forces its way; as the
current flowed swiftly on, the banks falling into the rapid stream,
which continually undermined their bases, reminded me of cattle
browsing or a mower cutting hay. In proportion as the banks
were carried away on this side, they were continually renewed on
the opposite; the river thus constantly changing its course. After
landing and viewing the country I obtained still further insight
into the way in which it did its work, and chased the people on its
banks; the mountains here were not in ranges but separate circular
hills; entering the country I found it marshy with a raised bank
which served as a road; enquiring the reason, I was told that the
Yellow River only four years before, had forced its way in between
two hills a mile to the S.W., had flooded the whole district, and
made its egress a little to the east of the village we were in.
I fathomed the river as we crossed it, immediately after we were
let go I found it 15 ft.: in the centre I could find no bottom, not
because of the depth, but because of the force of the current and
the rudeness of the means employed, -a long pole; I also tried to
estimate the average force of the current, I do not think it is near-
ly so swift as the tide at Shanghai, a gig could easily pull against
it, and so I suppose it may be set down at 3 or 4 knots per hour.
The question naturally presented itself to my mind whether or not
the river was navigable for steamers; I made as many enquiries as
I could relative to this point, and came to the conclusion, that
there is no obstacle. The first sight of the river gave me the idea
of shallowness, but this illusion was speedily dispelled; inde-
dendently of sounding, it occurred to me that such a body of water
flowing so quickly must have a deep bed; it may be at this side
at one time, and that side at another, but it cannot but always
have a deep channel somewhere. And this has been confirmed re-
cently by actual investigation. Mr. Davis, of Messrs. Fergusson
& Co., of Chefoo, sailed down the river from Tsi-nan-foo to the
sea, and sounded it all the way; he found the depth of the chan-
nel averaging from 18 to 26 ft., and the bar not so formidable as
was anticipated; at low water he obtained 3 or 4 ft. on the bar,

I made very particular enquiries as to the course of the river,
the cities on its banks, and how far it was navigable; and give
the information I received, which I think can be depended upon.
At the ferry there lay a good many junks of the 2nd and 3rd
class as to size, on their way up the river; we found some were
bound to Tsau-chow-foo, others to Tsz-kow and others to Ta-
ming-foo. Thus the course of the river appears to be from Ta-
ming-foo, Lat. 36° 28′ N., Long. 115° 18' E., nearly due south to
Tsau-chow-foo, Lat. 35° 25′ N., Long. 115° 35' E., then north-east
to Tung-oh-hien, Lat. 36° 24' N., Long. 116° 23′ E., where it
takes possession of the bed of the Ta-tsing river, and through it
flows on towards Tung-chang-foo, Lat. 36° 39′ N., Long. 116°
12' E., past Tsi-nan-foo to Tieh-men-kwan ("The Iron-gate
Pass") about Lat. 37° 35' N., Long. 118° 12′ E., and there pours
itself into the sea through an estuary about 20 miles long.
Thus the river opens a way far into the interior, and to a part
of China quite new to us, and rich and populous. I am somewhat
dubious as to the truth of the statement, that navigation is impos-
sible beyond Ta-ming-foo, but supposing it to be correct, a glance
at the map will convince any one of the great importance of the
river. The city of Tsi-nan-foo is the capital of Shan-tung, while
Tung-chang-foo, Tsau-chow-foo and Ta-ming-foo are little less im-
portant than the first in a commercial point of view. Moreover it
will be seen, the river crosses the Grand Canal and also the Wei-
ho or Shang Canal, and other minor water roads, as the Chinese
call them; so that by the first the whole of the south-west of Shan-
tung and the entire province of Kiang-su is thrown open to us,
while by the latter we have easy access to Kai-fung-foo and the
whole of Ho-nan and Shan-si. In view of these facts, I have no
doubt the river will not be forgotten at the revisal of the Treaty
of Tien-tsin in 1868.

There are several important seaports on the north coast of Shan-

Our route lying direct through the scene of Great Yu's labours,
I endeavored to pierce the mystery of that flood which has so
long interested Chinese scholars, and which is graphically describ-

the hills and the rivers operated upon by that indefatigable minis-
ter. The emperor Yaou thus speaks of the flood :- "Destructive
is the overflowing of the waters, in their vast extent they overtop
the hills, threatening the heavens." And Mencius enters more into
detail and says:----"The waters flowing out of their channels
inundated the middle kingdom; snakes and dragons abounded,
and the people had no place to settle." Some have referred this
to the flood of Noah, but I am inclined to think that Mencius
hints at the true reason. Consulting old maps of China we find

considering the nature of the country which we have just described

little if any above the level of the sea. This river is not only
one of the largest in the world, but the most wayward; it is con-

has been a perpetual source of anxiety to every dynasty; and
great the labour, and fabulous the sums which have been expend-

20 years to remedy. In the Han dynasty there were repeated floods.
In A.D. 1344 all the districts of Wun-shang, Kea-tseang and Jin-
ching were overflowed. Indeed so repeated and disastrous have
there been that this river has got the name of "China's Sor-
row." Now looking at the tremendous amount of water pour-
ed out by it, and at the flat and swampy nature of the country
all through, we can easily conceive of an inundation such as
that described; the few hills, or rather mounds, would certain
ly be overtopped, or appear like insignificant islands here and
there upon the surface of the waters; thus the flood can be
accounted for by natural causes. Again having traversed this
region, the extraordinary labours of Yu do not appear so super-
human as they once did; I used to think, that the accounts
given of his travelling over so vast a region in his four kinds
of carriages, a cart for dry grouud, a sledge for the mud, a boat
for the rivers, and huge spikes in his shoes and staves for the
hills; of his incessant toil, passing his own door so often and
hearing his infant child, he had never seen, cry, and refusing to
enter, &c., &c., were to be set down as romance. But now I do not
think his labours impossible; he had the best men in the kingdom
to assist him, he had the full sanction of the government, and so
all the resources of the kingdom at his disposal; the country ex-
tensive though it be is not impracticable, in a month he might go
from one end of his field of action to another, and thus by travel-
ling about, and having the command of all kinds of labor, could
easily complete his work. No doubt he must have been one of the

most extraordinary of men, clear headed, active, faithful to the
empire and philanthropic, strong both in mind and body; we see
this even in the brief description we have of his toil. He did not
like his predecessor K'wan, who had been first appointed and had
failed in the works, dabble away at the banks and details; he
placed his finger at once upon the sore point, saw the cause of the
disaster, and set to work there. He first fixed upon and mapped
the high hills and great rivers, defined the ranges of the former,
and found out the courses of the latter. This done, he divided the
country into nine provinces, and arranged how he could most effi-
ciently drain off the waters. He then set to work, commencing
at the hills on the north-west of China, at a place then called
Hoo-kow, Lat. 36° 15′ N., Long. 6° 5' W. of Peking, in the
present department of Seih, where the river was manageable; and
here he cut off the force of the Yellow River. He then, to use his
own words, "cleared out the nine rivers and led them to the sea,
and deepened the canals and led their waters into the rivers." Thus
in the course of a few years, some say three, others eight, he
drained the country, and the people returned to their employments.
I also during the journey tried to identify the mountains and rivers
mentioned in the Shoo-king, as having been operated upon by Yu.
The mountains still stand; but the rivers are less easily identified,
and no wonder, for the soil is flat and yielding, and nearly 4,400
years have elapsed since then. Some of the rivers however are
clear enough. The Hang river, and the Wei river in the Kei pro-
vince, mentioned in the Shoo-king, Bk. II. Sec. I., still follow
their old course; the former is the river which flows from Pau-ting-
foo to Tien-tsin; and the latter is that which comes from the
neighbourhood of Ho-kien-fu, the first is now called the Kow
River, and the latter the Hoo-to river; they unite a little to the
west of the Pei-ho, then fall into it, and flow in its channel on to
the sea past Tien-tsin: we marked the place where they entered
the Pei-ho, as we sailed past it on our way to that city. The
river called the Yih-shui must be an old name for the Pei-ho.

Having gained the level country again, we stopped at Tung-
ping-chow. This city lies on a river and is a place of considerable
trade. The neighbouring farmers were engaged in sowing wheat
at the time of my visit, large quantities of arrow-root grow in this
city and suburbs.
Here we again stood on historical ground. This is the district
anciently called Tung-yuen, referred to in the Shoo-king as having
been successfully brought under cultivation by the operations of the
Great Yu. The soil and trees still corresponded with the des-
cription given more than 4000 years ago. It is still clayey, red,
and rich, and the trees and grass strong and bushy. (Shoo-king,
Bk. I. Ch. V.)
The city had evidently taken its name from that same passage
in the Shoo-king, Tung-ping, the Eastern plain, and hence called
Tung-ping-chow. The city is famous in history. After the mur-
der of the Emperor Seang, 5th of the Hea Dynasty, the Bam-
boo Books tell us that his Empress Min fled to Yew-jing
the old name for this place; it would almost appear as if this was
also the birth-place of Shaou-k'ang, the succeeding Emperor.
Again, in this neighborhood was fought the great battle which put
an end to the Hea Dynasty, and introduced the Dynasty of
Shang. History informs us that several times the inhabitants of
this district have been driven from their homes by inundations of
the Yellow River. One flood more disastrous in its effects than the
others on record occurred A.D. 1344.

Beyond Tung-ping-chow, the country was covered with forts
owing to the nearness of the rebels, who within a week of our
visit had approached within 100 li of the place; but fortunately
news had come that they had divided their forces and were de-
parting-one division due south, and the other to Ho-nan. The
burning villages could be seen from where we were.
Continuing towards Yen-chow-fu we crossed the classic river
the Wên-ho; it was broad, full of water and well supplied with
ferry boats. All Chinese scholars, know of this stream; it is one
of the rivers mentioned in the tribute of Yu, as that by which
produce of the north country was conveyed to the river Tsi,
and thence to the Capital. It is also mentioned in the Lung-yü
Bk. XI. Ch. VII. where Min-tse-k'een not wishing to serve the
K'e family says "decline for me the honor positively. If any one
come again to me with a second invitation, I shall go and live on
the banks of the Wên."
This river was taken advantage of in the time of Yuen dynasty
to supply the Grand Canal; it was made to strike the canal at
right angles, at its summit level, part of its waters flowing north
and the rest south. Since the recent change in the Yellow River,
it is of little service and I believe now flows entirely to the south.
Having crossed the river, we reached the city of Wen-shang-
hien, the gates were closed through dread of the rebels, and we
had some difficulty in obtaining admission. We remained but a
short time and drove on for Yen-chow-fu which we reached the
same afternoon. As we approached this city we had still more
painful proofs of the misery caused by an ill ruled country; we met
whole families in carts, some returning to their homes, having
heard that the rebels had left their district, and others from a great
distance hurrying to the city for safety. It was most painful: the
males were walking, and the carts crowded with the female portion
of the families, who sat on sacks of flour and corn, which were also
piled up in front and back of the cart. All their live stock were
yoked to the cart, and the produce of their farm, as much as could
be carried away, had been brought but everything else left. Our
attention was arrested by a fine grove of tall cypress trees, with a
tall grave in front. We found that it was the burial place of
Hwuy of Lew-new mentioned in the books of Confucius and
Mencius; he was an officer of the kingdom of Loo, was a man of
virtue and talents, and holds a conspicuous place in the pre-

Soon the pagoda and walls of Yen-chow-fu came in sight: I
was now approaching the district rendered classic as being the
birth place of the two great sages of China, and the scene of many
of the more important events in their lives. This city takes its
name from one of the nine divisions of ancient China, into which
we are informed the Supreme Ruler in a vision told Yu to
divide the empire. It stands in the centre of the old kingdom or
dukedom called Loo so often referred to in the classics. This is
certified among other things by an inscription of four large
characters over the top of the West Gate, telling us that to the west
lay Chau and Wei, and we know that Loo lay to the east of these
places. The father of Confucius is said, on a tablet in the temple
of his son, to have ruled over this place in his time. The city lies
on the river called the Sze-shui, of which I shall again have occa-
sion to speak; it does not appear to be very rich, and bears a
strong resemblance to Tung-chang-fu.
Having passed the night here we started for Tsiu-hien the city

Leaving it the ground begins to ascend, and becomes gradually
more and more rugged. Hard stony ground, bleak hills and
marshes now prevailed on either hand, and we found herds of black
cattle feeding on the scanty pastures. We passed several villages
and reached Woo-tai just after the sun had gone down; spending
the night here amidst discomforts, we in the morning set off for
the city of Mung-yin-hien. Our route now lay through a beauti-
ful hilly district; many of the hills were covered with the stunted
oak shrub, on which they feed the so-called silkworm of the district,
numerous baskets of the cocoons of which were exposed for sale in
the villages through which we passed. The next day these oak
plantations became more numerous. In the days of the Emperor
Shun, more than two thousand years before Christ, silk is mentioned
as one of the articles of tribute brought from the old province of
Tsing-chow, our present locality.
Passing on we came in sight of another historical object, the
eastern Mung hill, which is mentioned as having been brought
under cultivation after the waters had been carried off by Yu, and
on which in ancient time sacrifices had been offered, as we infer
from the remarks of Confucius in the Lun-yu. It is also famous
as having in modern time called forth the poetical genius of Kien-
lung, who composed a piece of poetry in view of its snow clad
summit, on one of his journeys to the southern parts of his king-
dom. Not far from this hill is another called Yu, where Shun
kept Kwan the father of the great engineer till he died. It was also
from the vallies in the neighbourhood that the famous variegated
pheasants' feathers came, which are also mentioned in that sort of
dooms-day book and which were, even in these times, used for
military decorations. In the afternoon we reached the city of
Mung-yin, not a very important place, except as a depôt for salt;
we found large salt warehouses here and a great trade in that
commodity: the salt is carried on mules from the north of the city
of Tsing-chow-foo. Leaving in the morning, we found the country
thinly peopled, and goitre very prevalent in the vallies; the country
shortly grew less hilly, and we at last reached the River E spoken
of in the Tribute of Yu, as one of the rivers regulated by him. It
is a small shallow river without bridges, but fordable at the spot
where we reached it; it flows to the south and then south-east
falling into the Sze River.

On the succeeding days our party passed through the Hien city
of Wei, the country undulating and gravelly; the city itself the
head quarters of a large business. We crossed the river Wei by a
ferry; this river rises near E-chow-foo, flows past the busy city of
Chu-ching, and thence northward to the sea. It is a stream of
some importance, navigable for forty or fifty miles; it also came
under the care of the great Yu, who we are told conducted it to its
proper channel. Advancing we came in sight of the Lai-chow
hills, where lived the wild tribes, whom Yu instructed in the art
of tillage and pasturage, nor does his teaching seem to have been
ineffectual.
These tribes continued down to comparatively modern times; the
tombs of their princes are pointed out on the sea side between
Tung-chow and Che-foo, and I have often passed them, large
mounds of earth, enclosed by what originally had been a high mud
wall. The present Chinese inhabitants of this eastern part of
Shan-tung disclaim being the original occupiers of the land; they
trace their origin from the west, many families having come from
Ho-nan and Shan-si as settlers.
Lai-chow-foo is famous for it's beautiful soap-stones of various
colours and finely veined. This stone is cut into idols, pagodas and
all kinds of ornaments. We procured several specimens of it, very
like it that which is referred to in the Shoo-king as the strange
stones, which formed part of the tribute of this district; if not this,
it may the beautiful pebbles and also amber, which are to be found
in abundance at the eastern point of the promontory. Lai-chow-
foo is now faded in its glory and is a comparatively poor city to be
the capital of a foo.
