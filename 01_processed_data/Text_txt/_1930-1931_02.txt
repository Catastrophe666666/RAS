SOME POINTS ON THE HISTORY
OF SCIENCE IN CHINA
By Prof. GIOVANNI VACCA
University of Rome
I. Matteo Ricci, the first European who was able to
read and write Chinese books, was the first to acquire
some knowledge of the scientific ideas of the ancient
Chinese (¹) and to see clearly the extent of their astro-
nomical and geometrical conceptions.
Among the first successors of P. Matteo Ricci, was
P. Joannes Terrentius, one of the first members of the
Accademia dei Lincei. He wrote in 1623 a letter from
China, which reached Kepler in 1627, and was published
by him, together with his important answer, in 1630 (2).
Kepler suspects with reason the astronomical obser-
vations of the ancient Chinese to be not real observations,
but the result of later and much more modern calcula-
tions. He supposes that the whole astronomical science
of the Chinese came from the Arabs.
As their studies developed, the Jesuit missionaries in
China were impressed by its imposing literature, and
especially by the wealth of historical tradition, and came
to have a too favourable impression of the value and age
of Chinese science. The best known studies are those of
P. Gaubil, which he condensed in a short Histoire de
l'astronomie chinoise (3). The writings of P. Gaubil
form the foundation of the studies on Chinese astronomy
by J. B. Biot (*), and lately of a long series of studies by
L. de Saussure (5).
A careful examination of the Chinese texts used by
Gaubil and after him by all the supporters of an ancient
Chinese astronomical science, brings to the conclusion
that their opinion was groundless.
This has appeared clearly in the case of Schlegel's
Uranographie Chinese (). The supposition that astron-
omers existed in China 170 centuries before Christ was
too fantastic to be believed.

The first serious studies upon ancient China, which
give a just idea of its science, are those of H. Maspero (7).
The most elementary astronomical knowledge sup-
poses the use of some astronomical instruments. J. B. Biot
claims that in the Chou-pi there is a description of the
most exact form of a perforated gnomon. He translates
the Chinese text as follows: "Prenez un bambou, percez-y
un trou dont le dimètre soit 1/10 de pied, à la longueur
de 8 pieds. Cherchez l'ombre et observez-la. Le trou, en
droite ligne, couvrira le soleil, et le soleil, correspondra à
l'ouverture du trou." (8)
The Chinese text says much less; it does not say that
a hole should be pierced at the length of 8 feet, nor does
it say that the sun's image on the ground is the object to
be observed. The Chinese commentators have pointed
out the obscurity of the description ("). In my opinion
the Chinese writer seems not to have any idea that the
end of the point of the gnomon gives a shadow corres-
ponding not to the centre of the sun, but to the superior
limb of the sun (10), but he seems to have paid attention
to the necessity that the body of the gnomon should be
sufficiently thick, so that also its end should cast a true
shadow, not a penumbra. The conditions of the Chinese
observation require that the gnomon should have the
thickness of a Chinese inch. This only reasonable ex-
planation seems sure, because it is quite impossible to
make a hole in a bamboo cane in such a way that the
image of the sun should be equally clear at the summer
solstice and the winter solstice. J. B. Biot has seen this
difficulty, but his opinion that the gnomon described is a
rudimental instrument, seems to me unacceptable, because
I think such a cane with a hole would have been useless
as a gnomon.
Further, astronomical calculation supposes the meas-
ure of angles, the use of graduated circles, and some
notion of plane geometry. Now, the existence of a celestial
globe, used for astronomical purposes is based on un-
founded interpretation of two Chinese characters (11).
Only late commentators have given these two characters
the meaning of celestial globe, or some other similar
astronomical instrument. But the first Chinese who have
given this interpretation, had learned from the West, even
if we do not know how, the first notions of spherical
astronomy, and had tried to find in their own ancient
literature something corresponding. But such a research
was absolutely useless.

The most ancient Chinese work from which could be
ascertained the science of the Chinese about 1000 B.C. is
the Chou Pi already mentioned. The first translator,
P. Gaubil, recognized that the second part of this work is
recent. But even the first part and its dialogical form
seem to point to an echo of western, and ultimately of
Greek, science.
The calculation of the magnitude of the sun and its
distance from the earth, founded upon the shortening of
the gnomon's shadow as one goes towards the south, and
on the hypothesis of the plane earth and the measure of
the sun's apparent diameter, conduces to state that the sun
is as large as Peloponnesus, and its distance from the
earth equal to the earth's radius. This same calculation
had been made by first Greek mathematicians and
astronomers.
But while we can give the names, places and dates of
these calculations in Greece, and their gradual develop-
ment, in China there is no evidence that the scholars of
the Chou dynasty knew the properties of similar triangles
and the theory of proportions.
The wonder expressed in the Chou-pi that it should
be possible to measure the sun's distance without climbing
to it with a ladder, but with simple calculations, a wonder
which is not even indirectly reflected in the flourishing
Chinese literature, proves its derivation from some western
tale, which never made any lasting impression on the
Chinese mind.
It is on the other hand sufficient to think about the
abundant harvest of information on the astronomy and
mathematics of the Greeks, which can be found not only
in the works of a single philosopher, but even only in the
ironical allusions of one of Aristophanes' comedies.
Henri Maspero has clearly shown the real origin of
the mythological legends of the Sun King about the ancient
Chinese astronomers Hsi and Ho (12). From these re-
flections it seems that we must reject the conclusion not
only of Schlegel's old Uranographie Chinoise (a book
which has some interest only for sinologists on account of
the information it contains), but also the recent writings
of L. de Saussure, which, though made under better
conditions by a sincere and enthusiastic scholar, are not in
any way historically true. De Saussure himself, in one of
his last papers (13), not satisfied by his reconstruction of an
ancient Chinese science, has tried to find elsewhere, in the
Iran, conceptions like those of the Chinese authors of the

third and fourth centuries B.C. (theory of five elements,
denary cycle, etc.). But his conclusions on the propagation
from Iran to China in the fourth millennium B.C., contain
perhaps some truth if applied to the fourth century B.C.
To prove this conclusion we can observe that there is
only one way to confirm, if possible, an historical account
in which are recorded eclipses or other astronomical
phenomena. We can make astronomical calculations to
determine the date of an ancient eclipse only when it is
known within a few years; but if the historical period in
question is too long, we can always find an eclipse con-
cording with the requirements of the historical event.
The mathematical calculation is naturally correct, es-
pecially if calculated by Laplace, as has happened, but
the historical deduction is inconsistent. In fact the cata-
logue of the eclipses recorded by Chinese historians begins
with some regularity only after 776 B.C., and the doubt
remains that the most ancient eclipses of the series have
not been really observed, but interpolated later by Chinese
astronomers (14). The scarcity of astronomical and
astrological notions of the Chinese, as they appear from
the historians, beginning from Ssu-ma Ch'ien, is in con-
trast with the abundance and exactness of the statements
on the points to which the Chinese mind has turned its
attention in an original way.
Take, for instance, the records of periods of drought
and following famine given by the Chinese historians. In
an interesting article Alexander Hosie (15) has collected
many hundred cases for a period of more than a thousand
years, described exactly and with abundance of geograph-
ical data the important phenomenon. European historians
have certainly been unable to collect a series of such
complete data for as long a period. These observations
are an extremely important document for the history of
meteorology, and we can expect from them a better know-
ledge of the history of the climate of Asia.
II. The first Chinese astronomer is undoubtedly the
Buddhist saint I Hang, of the T'ang dynasty. He studied
the Indian mathematicians, from whom he learnt impor-
tant mathematical and astronomical notions. A critical
edition of his works and of such fragments as have been
preserved would be desirable. Certain contributions to
arithmetic contained in his mathematical commentary on
the system used by soothsayers of divining with rods
according to the I Ching, are perhaps his original work (16).

I have purposely omitted mentioning the calculations
made by Chinese mathematicians of the ratio of the
diameter to its circumference, which they are said to
have calculated in the form of a fraction 355/113. This
result, which is quite correct, seems to be an importation
from the West. It is natural to think that about the third
or fourth century A.D., in a way that we cannot yet define,
some results found in the works of Heron found their
way to China through Central Asia, when we consider
that under the Tsin dynasty, together with the above
fraction, several other results contained in Heron's works
made their appearance in China, such as, for instance,
the complicated taximeter described by Prof. H. A. Giles.
We come to the same results when we inquire into
the origin of the arithmetic of great numbers represented
with the decimal system grouping the figures eight by
eight, as Archimedes did in his Arenarium. This same
result is found in a biography of Buddha, contained in
the Lalita Vistara.
Of two Chinese translations, one of the fourth, the other
of the seventh century (Bunju Nanjo, 159, 160), only the
second, attributed to Arjuna, Buddha's master, contains
calculations much like those of Archimedes.
The pos-
sibility of an Indian origin of Archimedes' ideas, which
is unlikely for many other reasons, is thus absolutely
removed, as E. Blochet has well understood (17)
It seems probable that this interpretation of the Lalita
Vistara should have been made in Central Asia, in the
fourth or fifth century A.D., by someone who had a more or
less direct knowledge of Archimedes' method. F. Woepke's
hypothesis appears therefore quite baseless. At the end
of the thirteenth century an algebraical work by the
Chinese mathematician Chu Shih-chieh was published in
China; it has been analyzed by A. Wylie (Jottings on the
Science of the Chinese Arithmetic). It contains the table
of the binomial coefficients, which appears in Europe
over two centuries later, in the works of Petrus Apianus
(1527), Stifel (1544), etc.
Neither the Chinese nor the European mathemati-
cians claim to have discovered this important table. But
the great mathematician Omar Khayyam says clearly in
his works that he was the first to find the general method of
extracting roots from numbers of any order; it seems
therefore highly probable that his discovery should have
been transmitted, in what way we do not yet know, from
Persia to the East and to the West.

The method of giving an approximate solution to
equations of a high grade, found in Chu Shih-chieh's
works, calls forth the same operations. The extreme
simplicity of the examples he uses in explaining the
method, avoiding irrational numbers, shows that he had
never made any application of it to real problems. This
hypothesis may explain why this Chinese mathematician
had no disciples, and why his works did not attract the
least attention in China.
When in 1582 Matteo Ricci came to China, he aroused
great admiration by teaching and translating the first
elements of Euclid, the application of arithmetic to
geometry and Sacrobosco's sphere. Notwithstanding his
efforts and those of his disciples Giulio Aleni and Sabatino
de Ursis, and of his successors in the eighteenth century,
Schall and Verbiest, no real mathematical school was
formed in China.
There were, it is true, in various periods, learned and
active Chinese scholars, but none who was able to have an
original conception of Western science, or to reconstruct it.
The efforts of Protestant missionaries in the nine-
teenth century were not more successful. The most
prominent of them was certainly A. Wylie. His attempt
to translate with symbols, half European and half Chinese,
the elements of differential and integral calculus found
no followers.
The works of Li Shan-lan show an exceptional mind,
but did not contribute to the diffusion of Western science
in China.
I do not think the small development of astronomy
and mathematics in China is to be ascribed to deficiency
of the aptitude for study in the race, or better races,
which make up the Chinese people. The analysis of a
prelogical mentality in primitive peoples, made by Levy-
Bruhl and his school, seem inapplicable to the Chinese.
Our exact sciences are based upon an evolution of
Greek science for many centuries. It is impossible
without long efforts, and not worth trying, to change,
even slightly, the form of notation and the symbols. The
mathematical formulas are difficult to read where there
is no distinction between italic characters (sloping type)
and ordinary (straight type). The absence of this dis-
tinction makes it difficult to read Leibniz's printed works.
The formulas become nearly illegible if the alphabet
only is changed: Germans and Russians have had to
set aside, in formulas and calculations, their national

alphabet, and to accept the symbols of other European
mathematicians. In the seventeenth century Galileo found
it simpler to write in Latin his geometrical discoveries,
though he became one of the creators of scientific language
in Italian.
In the eighteenth century Germans and Russians
wrote in Latin and in French, because they lacked a suit-
able scientific prose style, which they produced only in the
nineteenth century.
Those who know China well have never doubted that
the Chinese are capable of assimilating Western science;
they have done so when, like the Japanese, they have
taken the right course of careful study before attempting
to write original expositions. The most subtle theories
can be expressed by the Chinese symbols as well as in any
European language. But it is possible that only in the
second half of the twentieth century Chinese literature
will achieve a scientific prose formed on the works of
Chinese scholars who have studied science in a European
language.
Chinese civilization is not that of an old, tired and
conservative people, but rather that of a young people,
eager to learn, reflective and quick. The ability of the
Chinese as mechanics, sailors, pilots, skilled workmen in
all sorts of industries is well known.
Chinese civilisation, while it is to be considered young-
er than ours, has contributed, in a way that seems almost
necessary, to our progress. Some fundamental industries
(silk, paper, porcelain, varnishes) come from China.
That the Chinese in the eighth century knew the chemistry
of gases, as Klaproth claimed, is now considered in-
credible (18), and the discovery by the Chinese of oxygen
and of the composition of air and water must be removed
from the history of chemistry. The chemical manipula-
tions of the Chinese deserve however to be examined more
attentively than has yet been done. The question of the
origin of alchemy is still unsolved, and future studies may
prove that it comes from the West (19), but Chinese
contributions to metallurgy and pharmacopea have un-
doubtedly been very useful.
More important are the Chinese contributions to the
study of the vegetable and animal world. I shall only
refer to the interesting researches by the U. S. Govern-
ment on the Chinese Materia Medica. The first study of
the only known remedy against leprosy, the use of
chaulmoogra, comes from the Chinese pharmacopea, and

the study of an ephedra, a gnetacea, may give a real cure
of influenza.
Another proof of the Chinese aptitude for scientific
research is their geographical work. The accounts of
Chinese travellers from the first centuries A.D. to our
times are remarkable for the accuracy of their observa-
tions. While we cannot speak of great Chinese mathe-
maticians, the list of great Chinese travellers is long, and
can reasonably be compared to that of western travel-
lers. China, however, has no great navigators, because
navigation is an art, or rather a science, born and bred
first in Greece, then in Italy, and next in the rest of the
world.
The Chinese had an original school of cartography, or
rather topography; its maps of China and neighbouring
countries are really important. The earliest documents
of Chinese cartography, published by E. Chavannes,
compare favourably with contemporary European maps,
or rather surpass them. I wish to add to these maps a
printed map, perhaps the first one printed in China, the
work of a great Chinese poet and author, Su T'ung-po.
He drew an interesting historical map of China under
the Chou Dynasty, of which I can give only two recent
reproductions (20). Copies of the original impression
may still be extant in some Chinese, Japanese, American,
or even European library.
This new merit of a great poet is a new proof that the
Chinese people, who have already given China and all the
world great historians, poets and painters, will be capable
in the future of giving original contributions to science.
