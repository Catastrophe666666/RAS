SOME NOTES ON THE REMAINS OF
A PHALLIC SHRINE IN JAPAN¹.
The shrine, of which a photograph is shown, is
situated in the Nikko District some three miles from
Yumoto. The actual site is close to the path leading
from Yumoto over the Konseitung Pass and is at an
elevation of some 1,000 feet above Yumoto, on the side of
the mountain. The slopes are thickly wooded.
The shrine now contains only a single Phallic emblem
which stands on what are evidently the remains of a small
wooden shelter-hardly describable as a Temple. As
indicated in the photograph the emblem is draped with
a piece of white cotton cloth.
During the latter part of the Nineteenth Century the
practice of Phallism in Japan was proscribed by law and
since that time has almost entirely died out, until at the
present day the shrine here described is believed to be
almost the last remaining in the country.
At the present time the Yumoto shrine is visited
fairly frequently by peasants, especially women, and
when being photographed by the writer several arrived
and prostrated themselves. Originally world-wide in its
existence as a form of worship, Phallism seems to have
formed the religion of the earlier inhabitants of Japan
and from it, with the advance of civilisation there appears
to have gradually evolved the worship now termed
Shintoism. In Phallism we have the worship of the
procreative organs, while in Shintoism we would seem to
have a kindred idea brought out in the form of ancestor
worship, with which is coupled the sanctity of the Royal
family from the originator of which is popularly supposed
to have sprung the Japanese nation.

therefore cruder form of Shintoism, the continuance of
the former was possibly considered to have a contaminat-

At the present day Phallism actively exists as the
main principle of the Hindu worship of Siva, and in
Hindu temples are to be seen the male emblems termed
linga and the female yoni. The symbols are represented
in the stupas and dagobas of Buddhist India, Burma and
Ceylon though the original meaning of the symbolism has
been lost to the mass of the people. The writer ventures
to bring forward the suggestion that Phallism may have
been the underlying idea in the ancestor worship of
China, while by some the Pagoda is thought to be derived
from the same source.
In the past it was practised by the Assyrians (Baal
and Astarte); Egyptians (Isis); Phoenicians; Greeks
(Dionysic and Orphic); and Romans (Aphrodite); while
traces are found in Mexico, Peru and other parts of
America; in primitive African religions, in the totem
poles and other symbols of Polynesia-and, in fact,
throughout the world.
Early man found the need for some supreme power
to worship and he began by deifying the phenomena of
nature. From the sun and moon he turned to the worship
of the principle of life as instanced in the trees and
creatures around him, and from this to the worship of the
symbols of life-renewal seems but a short step.
