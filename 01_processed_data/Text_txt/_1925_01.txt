By HARLEY FARNSWORTH MACNAIR, Ph.D.
(Professor of History and Government in St. John's University.)
As a result of the publication by the writer of a
volume on modern Chinese History¹ some two years ago,
he has been invited by the editor of the Journal of the
N.C.B.R.A.S. to attempt the formulation of certain con-
clusions to which one is likely to arrive after a study of
the foreign relations of China. The research student
of an exact natural science may find little difficulty in
enumerating the results of his experiments and in stating
his conclusions; not so, however, with the student of the
social sciences whose facts change with conditions and
whose laboratory is a nation, a race, or perhaps the
round earth itself. The tourist who puts in at the ports
of the Far East, or even spends a few weeks in a rapid
tour of China, finds little difficulty in turning out a
volume of observations, as any one may testify who
meets such travellers or reads publishers' advertisements.
The desire to generalize is paralleled only by the dangers
of giving way to it. The perils of generalization were
clearly realized by George Wingrove Cooke, a corres-
pondent for the London Times in 1857-8, when he wrote:
"I have, in these letters, introduced no elaborate essay
upon Chinese character. It is a great omission. No
theme could be more tempting, no subject could afford
wider scope for ingenious hypothesis, profound genera-
lization, and triumphant dogmatism. Every small critic
will, probably, utterly despise me for not having made
something out of such opportunities
I may add

I.
To understand Far Eastern history and the relations
of the nations of eastern Asia with each other as well
as with western countries some knowledge of the geo-
graphical background is necessary. Asia, the largest of
the continents, contains one-third of the world's total
land area. This great land mass may be divided into
four main spheres of influence. These are (1) Western
Asia, whose contributions to the development of Western
civilization have within recent years only been fully
appreciated; (2) Northern Asia, including Siberia, the
exploration and colonization of which by Russia have
caused it to be studied from the point of view of that
country, a European power; (3) India, a continent and
a civilization in itself, the influence of which has been so
widespread in all directions as to bring it from an early
period into limited contact with (4) China and the lands
under its direct influence. China, like India, is in itself
to all intents and purposes both a continent and a civili-
zation which is but slowly being welded into a nation.
Eastern Siberia and China with its present or former
dependencies plus the island groups of Japan and the
Philippines constitute the area generally covered by the
term "Far East."
The history of China, as well as its relations with
foreign nations, has been controlled by its geography.
With its dependencies China forms a great triangle, the
apex being at the Pamirs in central Asia and the base
extending from the Amur-Ussuri rivers on the north-east
to the island of Hainan on the south-east. The base of the
triangle is the sea while its two sides are marked by moun-
tain ranges. On the north are the Tien Shan or Mountains
of Heaven, the Altai, and the Yablonoi; on the south are

the mighty Himalayas, the rough plateau of the province
of Yunnan, and the rugged hills which form the water-
shed of the Si-kiang. China proper which lies within the
eastern part of the triangle has been likened to a fan the
handle of which is the long north-western extension of
Kansu province. The great triangle of China and its
dependencies is on the eastern edge of the continent of
Eurasia, and slopes gently to the seas on the east.
Although China is so much larger than Egypt the
geographical conditions and the historical beginnings
of the two countries are in some ways similar. In the
former civilization began at a much later period than in
Egypt; the authentic history of China can hardly be
considered to antedate the end of the third millenium B.C.
Chinese civilization began in a river valley, that of the
Hwang-Ho, as did Egypt's in the Nile. The sea in both
cases contributed to the growth of empire and of trade
although in the case of China not to as great degree com-
paratively speaking. In both countries early civilization
was protected in the river valley in which it developed.
Although China is not as completely protected as is Egypt
by the Sahara, nevertheless in that part of it in which
civilization originated, where the Wei river flows into the
Yellow river, the surroundings are semi-desert in their
general condition. In China the degree of protection was
not too great, nor was the climate so salubrious as to be
lacking in stimulation for the early inhabitants.
As the Chinese spread through the Eighteen Pro-
vinces in the course of centuries, they overcame the
difficulties presented by the highlands not by road-build-
ing, as did the Romans, but by making use of rivers,
with which the country is rather plentifully provided,
and by the construction of canals of all sizes which have
turned parts of the country into areas which remind one
of the Netherlands. There are three great rivers-the
Hwang-Ho, the Yangtse-Kiang, and the Si-Kiang-which,
with the canals just mentioned, serve to bind the country
together and to produce cultural homogeneity among the
people. China's history has been controlled by its water-
ways, and by the great plateau lands to the north and
west. The fact that the country has depended largely
on waterways partially explains the lack, until quite re-
cently, of cart and motor roads and railroads throughout
the country, and the tardiness with which the leaders of
the country have taken up the idea of developing the
country along such lines. Such incidents as the taking

the holding up by Consul Alcock of the tribute rice junks
in the harbor of Shanghai in connection with the Tsingpu

waterways in its relations with foreign nations. On such
highways as do exist carriage costs are ordinarily from
twenty to forty times as great as on a river. Roads are
spoken of as "dry" ways in contrast to the water way
which is the way to the historically minded Chinese.
The waterways with the mountains also have had a
determining influence upon the boundaries of the pro-

such a part in China's history and international relations
is to be traced to these.
During the summer months there is a very low
pressure in the interior regions of eastern Asia while in
winter the pressure is high. As a consequence during
the summer all of south-eastern Asia receives ocean winds
from the south, south-east, and south-west. During the
winter months there are land winds from the north,
north-east and north-west. These winds, known as
monsoons, have had an important influence not only upon
China's temperature and rainfall, but upon her earlier
voyages of exploration and commerce in southern and
western Asia, and her later trading relations with other
Asiatics and Europeans. The south-west monsoon, blow-
ing during the summer from the sea, brought sailing
vessels to Canton, by way of the straits of Malacca,
Sunda, and Macassar, before the first of November about
which time it changes. These vessels were then carried
on their homeward journey down the China Sea by the
north-east monsoon during the spring months. American
sailing vessels in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries
which did not arrive or depart in time to avail themselves
of the monsoons often made use of the Pacific Ocean
route.
The Chinese began the exploration of the seas along
their coast at least fifteen hundred years ago, although, as
far as can be shown at present, the great ocean itself
proved a permanent barrier. Despite the fact that the
Chinese as a people are not sea-farers they have not been
as closely bound by the sea as might be inferred from
certain writings. Communication with India was not
unknown as early at least as the beginning of the fourth
century of the Christian era. Several embassies from
India reached China by sea during the sixth century, and

the Tang dynasty annals of the seventh and eighth
centuries describe the sea route of Chinese junks to the
Euphrates river from Canton. As late also as the
fifteenth century Chinese vessels went to India, Ceylon,
the Persian Gulf, Aden, and the Red Sea. The first
meeting between the Chinese and the Portuguese occurred
at Malacca, while intercourse with the Spanish first took
place at Manila. In the modern age, since the sixteenth
century, Western influences have largely approached
China from the south by way of the sea. Russia has,
however, not only threatened the north-western and
north-eastern land routes, but the sea route by the Gulf of
Pe-chi-li. In the nineteenth century the ports to the
south assumed a new strategical, as well as commercial,
significance. Clearly then the sea has played a notable
part in China's history through the ages.
Passing to the land boundaries of China proper it is
to be noted that their effect upon the history and inter-
national relations of the Far East has been significant.
It has been said that the Roman empire collapsed because
of direct and indirect attacks from men of the plains to
the north and east. China likewise has suffered time and
again from attacks of a similar nature from the men of
the semi-arid plateau lands on the north and west. To
ward off these Shih Huang Ti built the Great Wall in the
early part of the third century B.C. This wall, fifteen
hundred miles in length connecting earlier walls and
extending from Kansu to the Gulf of Liao-tung, is still
the marvel of travellers.
On the west China is guarded by the militarily
impassible land of Tibet, and the difficult land of Sin-
kiang. The former is a plateau from two to three miles
high; the latter while lower is half desert and for the
most part arid and stony. Lhasa, the capital of Tibet,
is approached by important trade routes from both China
and India. Its position has made it an object of interest
to Russia and Great Britain as well as to China. No
army has ever crossed Tibet into either India or China.
Down the Wei and the Hwang-Ho valleys, however,
pressed the plateau landsmen. Deciding to defend them-
selves by attacking their enemies, the Han dynasty rulers
in the last two centuries B.C. and the first century A.D.,
sent armies to conquer the men of the central Asian
plateau; this was the brilliant period during which Chang
Kien, Pan Chʻao and Kan Ying immortalized themselves as
travellers and news bearers of China's power and culture.

Because of the great distances involved and the
difficulties of the desert, China found it as difficult to
maintain control of its central and western Asiatic terri-
tories as did later travellers to cross them in seeking the
imperial court of China. The invasions of the Mongols
and the Tatars in the medieval period are later aspects
of influences upon China from the north. Although the
Yueh Chi, the Hiung Nu, the Tatars, and the Mongols
have all pressed in at times and ruled part, or all, of
China and, although the Chinese themselves have spread
their power far in all directions, it yet remains true that
the country is surrounded by land barriers which have
largely cut it off until recently from any considerable
intellectual and cultural intercourse with most of the
world's other great civilizations. The result has been
the development over a period of more than four thousand
years of a civilization of a distinct type which, passing
through many vicissitudes, has lasted to the present day.
Tibet, Mongolia, Manchuria, Korea, Japan, the Philip-
pines, the East Indies, Malaya, Indo-China, Siam, and
Burma-all have felt the influence of this civilization.
Much of the best in the culture of these lands is to be
traced directly to the civilization of the Middle Kingdom.
A country as well supplied with minerals and other
raw materials as is China must long play a distinguished
part in international relations. Of coal there is enough,
according to the estimate of Baron von Richthofen, to
supply the whole world for several thousands of years.
Iron too is to be found in quantities almost as great.
Copper, tin, antimony, and salt are of importance. The
manufactures of the country have been in demand for
more than twenty centuries. In the Ming period, for
example, Chinese and foreign ships carried overseas great
cargoes of raw silk, rolls of finished silks, brocades, and
satins of divers kinds and colors, porcelains, musk,
rhubarb, iron, saltpetre, silver, pearls large and small,
chests and carved woods, and fans which are valued to
the present day. The Chinese mariners who traded to
the east coast of Africa did not circumnavigate that
continent as did the Portuguese for the reason chiefly
that they had not the motive for discovery and explora-
tion which encouraged the latter.
Cut off from ease of intercourse with other great
centers of civilization, the originator of the one culture
in the sphere of the Far East, its moral and material
support sought by all other lands of the Orient, little

II.
An understanding of the geographical background of
China's international relations must lead inevitably to a
comprehension of the fact that many of the misunder-
standings which have arisen between China and other
countries from the earliest period to the present time
have an almost purely psychological basis. The fact that
China was powerful and surrounded by a group of satel-
lite states, and that there was no civilization to compete
with her's nearer than that of India, separated from
her by thousands of miles, resulted in an assumption of
superiority which was no mere conceit on a grandiose
scale, as it appeared to Westerners in the nineteenth
century, but an altogether natural and sincere
albeit
mistaken-belief that China among all the nations on
earth was supreme in culture and in power. Nearer than
India there were no palaces temples and other monuments
to equal those of Peking. To China for hundreds of years
came tribute-bearing embassies; the fact that these em-
bassies actually came quite as much to trade as to pay
respect to the rulers of China detracted no whit from the
respect required and received by these same rulers, from
the members of the embassies.
The attitude of superiority assumed by China was
caused by the backwardness in civilization of the tribes
by which the country was surrounded. It is to be traced
as far back, apparently, as the period of the emperors
Shun and Yu in the twenty-third century B.C. when the
term Yu or barbarian was applied to foreigners. Various
references to the barbarians on the mainland and the
islands of the sea are to be found in the Shoo King or
Historical Classic. In the Canon of Shun of the Book of
Yu, (Translation of W. H. Medhurst, Sen.) the emperor

Into the manifold aspects of the age-long period of
intercourse between China and foreign countries which
preceded that of the modern period we cannot enter here,
but whatever discrimination may have been shown in
dividing foreigners into barbarians and civilized peoples
in an earlier age appears largely to have been forgotten
by the sixteenth century. As already mentioned the first
contact of the Europeans and Chinese in the modern era
took place in the islands south of China. The treatment
meted out to the Chinese by the natives and Spaniards in
the Philippine Islands, and by the natives and the Dutch
in the East Indies while slightly irritating to the Manchu
rulers of China were not regarded by them as serious,
inasmuch as the Chinese massacred in those islands were
of little importance in the eyes of their Manchu rulers;
nevertheless the co-operation of the Europeans with the
natives of these barbarous places in persecuting the
Chinese lowered the Europeans in the estimation of the
haughty Manchu and the cultured Chinese officials. The
precedent for the treatment of the Europeans at Canton
and the restrictions placed upon them prior to the middle
of the nineteenth century are supposed largely to have
had their origin in the treatment of the Chinese in Manila
by the Spaniards. A writer in the Chinese Repository
in 1834 says, "Pwankequa, the father of the late well-
known senior Hong merchant and grandfather of him
who bears the same name now, saw there the harsh treat-
ment inflicted on the Chinese in order to keep them in
subjection, and marked it as a 'model and motive' to be
acted upon, after his return to China."
The greater number of the merchants and sailors of
Portugal, Spain, Holland and England who appeared on
the coast of China itself during the course of the sixteenth
and seventeenth centuries were of an aggressive type
little likely to raise the Europeans to the rank of the
highly civilized and cultured in the mind of the Chinese.
Before reaching China these hardy explorers and traders
had come into touch with less able or less civilized peoples
than the Chinese. The Europeans expected to be treated
with at least the respect due an equal, if not with the
humility displayed by an inferior before a superior.
Consider the shock to their self-esteem when they were
met with demands for tribute for the Son of Heaven, and

III.
The difference in the viewpoints of the Europeans
in general and of the Americans in approaching the
Chinese, and of their respective attitudes toward the
government of the country, are worthy of careful study.
Disregarding exceptions on the part of individuals on
both sides, it may be mentioned that the attitude of
superiority assumed by China in dealing with Westerners
was met by one of equal pride on the part of the Euro-
peans in dealing with China. The Portuguese reached
China after touching Africa and India; the Spanish after
reaching Mexico and the Philippine Islands; the Dutch
after touching the East Indies; and the English after
seeking and obtaining a foothold in India. In the days
of the Polos there had been, apparently, not the slightest
assumption of superiority on the part of the few Euro-
peans who were in Cathay. Marco Polo himself evinced
the most respectful, not to say enthusiastic, admiration
for the civilization of the land. The early missionaries-
Franciscans, Dominicans, and Jesuits were most cir-
cumspect toward the emperor and his court in their com-
parisons of the civilizations of China and Europe. Pere
Ricci drew a map of the "Ten Thousand Kingdoms," i.e.,
the world, to show the imperial court that the earth was
greater than they had been led to believe-but, for the
sake of courtesy, the great missionary placed the Middle
Kingdom in the center of the map. In this way the winds
of Western knowledge were tempered to the imperial
shorn lamb.

In the later period, however, contact with or conquest
of uncultured or effete peoples on the fringe of Asia
created among Europeans a quite different attitude
toward the Chinese. That which had fostered an assump-
tion of superiority in the minds of the Chinese developed
a like attitude in the minds of the Europeans; and, mixed
with the belief in their inherent superiority, there came
to be an element of fear in the minds of the Chinese and
of their rulers, when it became common knowledge that
the Europeans were pursuing a policy of conquest and
absorption of territory in the Orient such as China itself
had pursued from the period of the Han dynasty onward
both by land and sea-and was still pursuing in the vast
stretches of central Asia.
The Americans, however, did not approach China
after conquering lands or peoples in the East. Having
more land at home than they needed during the early part
of the nineteenth century they abstained from the joys
of extra-continental conquest; consequently Americans
did not approach China by the route of India and may be
said to have been almost completely lacking in an Indian
"complex." This fact is clear from the beginning and
accounts largely for the attitude assumed by the
Americans and the Chinese in their dealings with each
other. The American Government has at no time shown
even the suspicion of a desire for Chinese territory, and
alone among the nations has pursued a policy of support-
ing China in defending its sovereignty and territorial
integrity. As Mr. Tyler Dennett has clearly shown in
his recent studies of American policy in the Far East,
for more than three-quarters of a century the American
policy has been to aid the nations of the Far East to
maintain their sovereignty and territorial integrity so
that all nations may have an equal opportunity in the
eastern marts of trade. Whenever it has been possible
for the American Government to co-operate with the
other powers in carrying out a constructive policy by
which the integrity of the eastern nations might be
maintained it has so acted; at other times it has with-
drawn in order to act independently.

IV.
Among casual students of the Eastern Question, both
occidental and oriental, there has been observable a
tendency to consider the East as a unit against the West,
and to think of the Chinese as a group facing Westerners
as a group. No idea could be more erroneous. To the
rulers of China in the early days all Westerners were the
same; they appeared on the China coast from countries
"far to the west" or "across the sea," and they were
supposed to be similar physically and mentally. Little
or no distinction was perceived as far as their intentions
were concerned. Years passed before it became in any
way clear that foreigners should be divided geographi-
cally and occupationally. To the present time many
ill-informed Chinese still believe and act upon the suppo-
sition that all missionaries are in reality agents of their
respective governments, and that they are little better
than spies seeking out the weakness of the land. Un-
happily there is just enough occasional connection
between missionaries and their home governments to lend
color to an otherwise baseless charge which constitutes a
source of grave misunderstanding. Calls made by consuls
upon their nationals indiscriminately in various parts of

Proud and self-satisfied in her isolated culture, China
denied equality to foreign nations practically throughout
the past century. But arrested development in her
culture, and a forging ahead on the part of the West-
and of Japan following in the footsteps of the West-as
a result of the discovery of the new world, and of changes
brought about by the mechanical, commercial, and
industrial revolutions placed the nations of the West, and
also Japan, in the dominant position in reference to China.
And then, unhappily, the nations of the West, for the
most part, and Japan-became as arbitrary as China had
earlier been. They treated China as China had previously
treated those whom she had considered her inferiors.
Japan and China were treated in much the same way
by the nations of Europe; but Japan, by demonstrating
evidence of both military and political competence in the
setting of her house in order, was able in less than half
a century to revise unequal treaties. At the opening of
the twentieth century she met the West on a basis of
equality-having a few years earlier demonstrated her
ability by assuming superiority over her neighbor China.
The latter country, however, laboring under a burden of
vast territories and population, a decaying though rich
culture, and almost inexhaustible but undeveloped natural
wealth, gave evidence of small ability to govern and put
its house in order in spite of the fact that noteworthy
progress had been made along religious, educational,
economic and social lines.

What is the outcome to be? Few students of history
would be rash enough to prophesy. It would seem that
conciliation on the part of foreign governments and
especially of foreigners in China must be a sine qua non
of any peaceful and constructive settlement, but concili-
ation on the part of foreign governments has generally
been attributed by the Chinese people and rulers either
to fear or to inability on the part of these governments
to carry out their policies without conciliation, which
robs conciliation of its true meaning. But conciliation
alone is the medium between force and sentimentality, and
