KINGSHIP IN CHINA
By Prof. W. E. SOOTHILL, M.A.
The idea of sovereign power associated with ritual is
clearly seen in the regulations for the monthly sacrifices
to be performed by the emperor. Without him they lost
their force. By the day of Confucius this force had almost
disappeared, a fact deplored by him. Only by strict atten-
tion to ritual could it be restored. The importance of this
State Ritual may be seen in the Yueh Ling, or Monthly
Ordinances, where complete directions are found for the
rites connected with the new moon. Take the following
as a sample of one month. It is taken from the Li Chi
which, though it took its present form early in the Chris-
tian era, very evidently was compiled from existing
material, some of it very old.
"In the first month of Spring the Sun is in Shih
(alpha Markab Pegasi) the Star culminating at dusk
being Ts'an (in Orion), and that culminating at dawn
Wei (in Scorpio). Its days are Chia and I (the two first
of the Ten Celestial Stems). Its Ti, or Divine Ruler, is
T'ai Hao (the deified first traditional emperor Fu Hsi,
who was made divine ruler of the three months of Spring);
and its Shen, or associate minister-spirit, is Kao Mang.
Its creatures are those with scales (i.e., reptiles and fish).
Its note in music is Chio and its pitch-pipe the T'ai Ts'ao.
Its number is eight; its taste is sour; its smell is rank.
Its sacrifice is at the door, and the spleen of the victim
has precedence over the other parts.
"The Son of Heaven occupies the room on the left of
the Ch'ing Yang (a hall of the Ming T'ang); rides (there)
in the carriage with the phoenix decoration, drawn by
(horses representing) azure dragons, carrying the green
flag. He wears green robes and green jade. He eats
wheat and mutton. The vessels he uses are slightly
carved with shoots (of plants).

"In this month occurs the inauguration of Spring.
Three days before the ceremony, the Chief Recorder
informs the Son of Heaven saying: 'On such and such a
day is the inauguration of Spring.' That season's virtue
(or power) is seen in (or represented by) wood. There-
upon the Son of Heaven devotes himself to self-purifica-
tion, and on the day appointed he leads in person the
three ducal ministers, his nine high ministers, the feudal
princes, and his great officers, to meet the Spring in the
eastern suburb. On their return he rewards them all in
Court (i.e., gives largess with mimetic purpose). He
charges his assistants to disseminate (similar) virtue to
the myriad people in harmony with royal order and in
expression of his pleasure and the bestowal of his favours.
Such largess is thereupon bestowed on all in due order.
"He also commands the Chief Recorder to be careful
of the statutes and maintain the laws and (especially) to
observe the motions in the heavens of the sun and moon,
and of the zodiacal stars in which the conjunctions of those
bodies take place, so that there should be no error as to
where they rest and what they pass over; that there
should be no failure in the record of all these things,
according to the regular practice of early times.
"In this month the Son of Heaven, on the first hsin
day, prays to Shang Ti for a good year; and afterwards,
when the day of the first conjunction of the sun and moon
has been determined, he takes the plough-handle and
share in the carriage, placed between the man-at-arms,
who is its third occupant, and the driver, and conducts his
three ducal ministers, his nine high ministers, the feudal
princes and his great officers, all with their own hands to
plough the field of Shang Ti. The Son of Heaven turns
up three furrows, each of the ducal ministers and feudal
princes nine. When they return, he takes in his hand a
cup in the great chamber, all the others being in atten-
dance on him and the great officers and says, 'Drink this
cup of comfort after your toil.'
"The king gives orders to set forth the business of
husbandry. (Here follow instructions of an ordinary
character).
"In this month orders are given to the Chief Music-
master to enter the College for practising the dances.

"Prohibitions are issued against the cutting down of
trees, (for cutting down would interfere with the spirit of
growing up, or growth).
"Nests should not be thrown down; unformed insects
should not be killed, nor creatures in the womb, nor very
young creatures, nor birds just taking to the wing, nor
should eggs be destroyed. (There was, of course, nothing
humane, or any idea of game laws in all this. It was
merely a form of sympathetic magic.)
"No congregating of multitudes should be allowed
(possibly assembling of armies is meant, though it may
have another meaning); nor any rearing of fortifications.
(Dr. Legge adds a note that "such operations would
interfere with the labours of husbandry." That is true,
but the real reason lay in sympathetic magic).
"Skeletons should be covered up, and bones with the
flesh attached to them buried." (This was the season of
life, not of death, and it must not be discouraged by
suggestion).
"In this month no warlike operations should be
undertaken; the undertaking of such is sure to be followed
by calamities from Heaven. The not undertaking warlike
operations means that they should not commence on our
side. (The last sentence is probably editorial. The season
was one for life and growth, which must mimetically
be encouraged, not discouraged. The "calamities from
Heaven" were not in vengeance, but part of natural law).
"The ways of Heaven may not be interfered with;
nor the laws of Earth be nullified; nor any breach be
made in human society.
"If in the first month of Spring the State proceedings
proper to summer were carried out, the rain would fall
unseasonably, plants and trees would decay prematurely,
and the various states would be in constant anxiety. If
the proceedings proper to autumn were carried out, there
would be great pestilence among the people; boisterous
winds would work violence, rains would descend in
torrents; (autumn greens and herbs) would flourish.
If the proceedings proper to winter were carried out,
pools of water would produce their destructive effects,
snow and frost would prove very injurious, and the first
sown seeds would not enter the ground."
Each month of the year is dealt with in similar
fashion.
The most important point from the standpoint of
mimetic magic is that the emperor must always be in tone

with the season. In Spring he wore green robes and
ornaments; in Summer, red; in the interposed section,
associated with Earth, yellow; in Autumn, white, or
probably straw colour; and in Winter, black. His
carriages and horses, in like manner, as well as all their
accoutrements, must accord with the season. His diet,
probably only during the two days associated with the
ceremonial, was regulated, but on what grounds is not
clear. The order according to the seasons was wheat,
beans, panicled millet, hemp-seed and millet; and the flesh
foods were in the order of mutton, fowl, beef, dog, and
sucking pig (one cereal and one flesh food to each season);
they probably had some relation with the five elements.
The vessels for his food must agree with the seasons; in
Spring they were carved but little, with sprouts and
shoots, and apparently were low and shallow; in Summer
they were tall, representing large growth; in the inter-
posed section they were round; in Autumn, rectangular
and somewhat deep; in Winter, large and deep. Thus in
shape, size and ornamentation they were a kind of
suggestive prayer, all was en rapport with the seasons,
and was meant to have the force of mimetic magic.
In the first month, as already shewn, the emperor
went forth to meet the Spring. In the second, inter alia,
he went to sacrifice to the Founder of Marriage, attended
by his ladies, special honour being paid to those he had
lately approached. In the third month of Spring, animals
were torn in pieces at the nine gates of the capital to
ward off epidemics and develop the influence of Spring.
In the fifth month, or midsummer, the longest day
arrived, when the influences in nature of darkness and
decay struggled with those of light and growth; and
death and life were in equipoise. Men of the upper
classes must keep vigil and fast. They must remain
secluded in their houses, avoid violent exertion, not
indulge in music or the beautiful, avoid sexual indulgence,
eat sparingly without condiments, control themselves and
be free from all agitation. The magistrates also should
avoid punishments. All this was done in order to aid the
forces of darkness and decay to overcome those of light
and growth. At first sight this may seem very singular,
but the reason clearly is that Nature must no longer be
stimulated to further growth, but must settle down to the
ripening of the harvest.
In the second month of winter, in which occurs the
autumn solstice, no digging should be done, and all doors

should be kept as closely shut as possible, otherwise
the heat of the earth would escape, or be stirred to
unseasonable activity, when insect life would perish and
pestilence ensue. On the shortest day, a day of equipoise,
men of the upper classes must act as on the longest day.
It was one of the taboo days. The noble class must aid
the forces of darkness and decay to overcome those of
light and growth.
Judicial and military affairs were regulated. In the
first month of Spring, warlike operations which involved
cutting and chopping down should-such as cutting down
trees-be avoided, as they would disturb the springing
up and natural growth now so much desired. In the
second month, litigation should be discouraged and
prisoners freed from their fetters, that is, in order to
aid in setting free Nature's expansion. The time for
repression of crime is the autumn; for as Heaven and
Earth then begin to be severe, so the judges must aid in
strictly carrying out justice, but not in excess, or as is
implied, the weather also would become too severe. It is
also the proper time for martial exercises.
These are some out of many such regulations in the
Li Chi. I do not mean to say that we have these
regulations in their original form. Changes were no
doubt made in the process of the centuries both in theory
and practice, but I see no reason to doubt the validity of
the theory recorded, whatsoever may be said of the
practice, and that the records are a valuable contribution
to Comparative Anthropology. It is evident that the
practices arose out of a primitive belief in China, as
elsewhere, in man's union with Nature, in unseen forces
to which he only gradually gave personification, and in
his own power, spiritual power, to bend these unseen
forces to his service; perhaps it would be more in keeping
with Chinese philosophy to say to blend himself, Man, in
a harmony with Heaven and Earth, each of the ternion,
Heaven, Earth, Man being essential to the operations of
Nature. To me, one of the most impressive features of
the discoveries of Anthropology is that Man so early saw
the clod as more than clod, and by this fact raised himself
from the clod to the concept of spiritual powers which he
could not define, but like himself dwelling in a realm of
Mind as well as a realm of Matter.

Another interesting point arises from this ritual,
namely, the evidence it provides why the Calendar was
of such extreme importance to government.
Such
importance did not only rest, did not indeed chiefly rest,
in the knowledge it gave of the proper time for ploughing,
sowing and reaping. Important though this information
might be, especially as the sovereign must take the lead,
it was far more urgent to know when and how he, as
representative Man, could correctly align himself and his
people with the unseen forces of Nature so as to aid
them. To be out of date with the seasons and perform
the ritual of, say, the last month of winter in the first
month of spring, would throw all the Powers above and
below out of gear and bring woe on the nation. The
Calendar from the earliest rise of civilization, was a
matter of first rate ritual importance as also of profound
difficulty and it was one of the main sources of the rise
of the royal power. It is also possible to see that the
acceptance of the Calendar by the feudal lords gave
proof of their loyalty, for with different Calendars in
different States everything in Nature would be turned to
confusion. It strengthened the concept of one Heaven,
one Earth, and the one representative Man, the emperor.
Moreover, the main ritual must be performed by the
emperor himself. That was of the very essence of re-
ligion. The "virtue" attributed by Confucius to the
throne was something far more than ethical, or at least in
origin it was more than this. It is inferential that, if we
had the necessary material for tracing this idea back to
the earliest days of the tribes who united to form the
Chinese nation, we should find the king, who provided the
nucleus for this union, to be early "medicine man", the

Though the rites were maintained and enlarged, the
peculiar virtue itself ceased, for after a long period the
king in China was no longer a man of outstanding personal
power, who had won his way by his own force, or virtue,
to the throne. This decadence apparently set in with the
advent of the first dynasty and, along with it, the system
of rulers hereditary in the paternal line. This hereditary
system seems to have been the chief cause of the decay,
for little beyond formality of ritual remained in later
generations. The magnetic personal force and wisdom of
the earlier Sheng Jen was dead. The system associated
with the Priest of Nemi may not have been without its
value!
It is impossible to discuss the subject of Kingship
without a brief reference to the centre from which it may
have arisen, and in which for ages lay its power. I refer
to the Ming T'ang, the centre, and to some extent the
source, of China's religious and cultural development. As
I hope to publish a study of this subject ere long I will
here but draw attention to it. The Ming T'ang had its
origin in the remotest past of Chinese history and trad-
ition. By the days of Confucius it had already sunk, along
with the royal power, whose mainstay it had been, into
powerlessness, and though a Ming T'ang as one of the
imperial buildings was from time to time revived until the
Ming dynasty, it had lost much of its early significance.
What was the Ming T'ang? That is a question which
has long puzzled Chinese scholars. Briefly I may say that
they trace it under different names back to Yao and Shun,
to Huang Ti and even to Fu Hsi. It is evident that the
building was not the emperor's palace, nor was it in the
palace grounds. It was situated, some say three li, others
up to thirty li from the Court, and in the open country to
the south-east.
There are traditions which indicate that the original
of the Ming T'ang was a very simple thatched hut, sur-
rounded by water, away from the haunt of men. There
are also traditions which imply that its early, and perhaps
primary, use was for astronomical and meteorological
observation, with a view to the regulation of the Calendar,
and the determination of the proper days for the ritual.

Far down in its history the primitive character of the
building was still maintained. Long after the invention
of tiles, the Ming T'ang still was thatched, indeed in even
later times thatch was retained under the tiles. Its
primitive use then, seems to have been astronomical and
meteorological, with the national ritual as its object.
In course of time other institutions gathered around
it. It became the centre where the Shang Ti were served.
These Five Ti and their Spirit-ministers ruled five seasons,
and with them were associated all the subjects of Nature-
worship, until, at a later period, these were separated
to other altars. It has been mistakenly assumed that the
Ming T'ang was a royal ancestral temple. That is only
true in the sense that certain ancestors were invited to
share the banquet spread for the Shang Ti and the Nature-
Spirits. As time passed, a Hall of Audience was connected
with it, for the transmission of royal commands, probably
at first calendrical and ritual, to the barons. A School
or College of princes also grew up with it, thus making it
the origin of culture. The imperial Archery Court for
selecting military officers formed part of the enclosure,
and so on.
It would not serve our immediate purpose to go further
into the detail of this ancient and royal institution, its
foundation and influence of the magic square of nine, which
played so important a part in the government of China.
Suffice it now that I think there is sufficient traditional
indication-evidence is hardly the word that the Ming
T'ang of later ages, with its temples and elaborate ritual,
grew out of an earlier building for the observation of the
heavens, not least of Ursa Major. Such observation I
think, had a ritual objective, and was, at first, creative of
royalty and later its mainstay. It existed to provide a
general calendar for the chiefs and rulers of the people,
but chiefly for the determination of the times for per-
forming the necessary ritual, which was of a mimetic,
or homoeopathic character, for aiding the Powers of
Nature to do their duty. Further, I think that earlier
still, all this elaboration arose out of a very simple
thatched hut, which existed long before the days of an
inherited throne, in a period when the Wise Man, the
Sheng Jen, the reader of the stars, perhaps the so-called
Medicine-man, was by his magnetic personality and his
occult powers, the royal man of Virtue.
