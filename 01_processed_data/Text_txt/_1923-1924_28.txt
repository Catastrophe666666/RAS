ŚRAMANA-SHAMAN.
ETYMOLOGY OF THE WORD "SHAMAN".
By N. D. MIRONOV and S. M. SHIROKOGOROFF.
The word "shaman" is used in different forms by
various tribes of N.E. Asia to denote a kind of in-
termediary between man and the world of spirits; as he
acts in a highly nervous state near to the trance of a
medium of spiritualists, the name "shaman" has been
adapted to similar phenomena in other parts of the globe.
So the primitive religion of Tibet (Bon-pa) is
sometimes called "shamanism"; a distinguished scholar¹
believed to have found the same features in the worship
of Ancient India. The term has been applied to the
beliefs of some tribes of natives of the New World (N.
American Indians).
3
No wonder, the etymology of this word has attracted
the attention of many scholars from A. Georgi (XVIIIth
Cent.) up to our contemporaries A. Meillet and B.
Laufer.
I believe that we have not a matter of vain curiosity,
but a really momentous problem before us. For the science
of Comparative Religion it is of great importance to
know whether this term was borrowed from outside
or originated from native sources. As man under
similar conditions comes to similar ideas, in many cases
we cannot go farther than accept a parallelism.

Now the question arises how old is shamanism?
The animistic theory underlying shamanism is, of
course, as old as mankind; the method of influence over
the spirit by sacrifice, prayers and frightening is also an

true that the shamanism in its present form is one of the conse-

ethnical groups. It is very suggestive to consider it as a product
of interethnical struggle resulting from the ethnical growth of the
