CORRECTIONS AND ADDITIONS

LIST OF THE BIRDS OF CHIHLI PROVINCE
By G. D. WILDER AND H. W. HUBBARD.
(Printed in the Journal. Vol. LV.—1924.)
We take advantage of this opportunity for correcting
our List of the Birds of Chihli Province, to also make
additions from our field notes of the last two years. For
corrections made in the following paper our thanks are
due to Dr. E. Hartert, Mr. J. D. La Touche, Pere Courtois
and to Dr. E. Streseman of Berlin. The corrections are
mainly changes in spelling or names made since the appear-
ance of the various parts of "Die Vögel der paläarktischen
Fauna" from which we drew the scientific names. We
would apologize to the author, Dr. Hartert, for overlooking
corrections he had made in his supplements.
We are also indebted to the very careful studies of
Mr. Peter B. Sushkin, results of which are given in his
"Notes on Systematics and Distribution of certain Palae-
arctic Birds," in the Proceedings of the Boston Society of
Natural History, 1925.
Numbers in this paper refer to those in the "List"
except where the page is indicated. For the convenience
of users of our original paper we offer a table of Contents.
Page 157, first line, "some" read "names".
2. CORVUS CORONE ORIENTALIS Eversman. The Black
Crow winters in mountain valleys in company with the Thick-billed
or Jungle Crow, and in some localities on the plain where it also
nests occasionally. We have not yet met it in the city of Peking.
where the Thick-billed Crow, Corvus coronoides hassi, is common.
3. CORVUS CORONOIDES HASSI Reichenow. D. 601
should be D. 603. In some parts of Chihli the Chinese use the
appropriate name of 大嘴​老​鵡​tatsui lao kua¹ for this Thick-billed
Crow.

83.-МOТACILLA FLAVA MACRONYX Streseman. This sub-
species is erected mainly on the basis of a longer hind claw than
No. 84 M. f. borealis, and other subspecies.-11 mm. as com-
pared with 8-10 mm. Sushkin, however, finds color differences,
in absence of eyebrow-stripe in macronyx, earcoverts less black-
ish, borders of the wing coverts light, of a purer color and broad-
er, sometimes twice as broad, as in borealis. No dusky collar
around throat and back a trifle brighter.
Most of our specimens supposed to be borealis are macronyx
according to the length of hind claw, but on separating them by
this characteristic, the eyebrow-stripe and dusky collar charac-
teristics do not seem to co-exist with the shorter claw, consist-
ently.

104.-PARUS ATER PEKINENSIS David. The English name
should be The Peking Coal Tit. Our note, from the name in
Chinese to the words "Peking market", should be applied to the
Marsh Tit, No. 107, which we have observed for years under
the name of the Peking Tit. This led us to confuse the Marsh
Tit with the Peking Coal Tit. It is distinguished from the
Marsh Tit chiefly by its crest and a white spot on the nape
from which it gets its name in Chinese子​點兒​tzu-tien-erh.
It is known, but not common, in the song-bird market. Wefound
it in the Eastern Tombs in January, 1925, but have not been
able to find it anywhere else since then.
The synonym given, Parus communis hellmayri Bianchi
should be deleted, as it is the synonym of No. 107, the Marsh
Titmouse.

(433).-STERNA HIRUNDO TIBETANA Saunders. In June,
1925, at the Hsieh Chia Ying marshes, we found a few pairs of
the Common Tern breeding, and shot one specimen, which seems
to have the characteristics assigned to this supposed subspecies,
i.e., sides of neck, shoulders, flanks, clear gray, inclining to
vinous on breast and belly; but it seems very doubtful if this
darker form is a geographically distinct subspecies. Mr.
Sowerby found the same differences in British specimens as in
these of the Far East compared with European birds.

462, (463.), (464.) - Mr. Sowerby questions the presence of any of the
family Tetraonidae in Chihli on the strong grounds that market
specimens are brought to Tientsin, and presumably also to
Peking by rail from Siberia; also that the forests of Chihli are
no longer of such an extent and nature as is suitable for them.
There is on the other hand only their presence in Peking coupled
with the statements of the dealers that the they come through
Hupeik'ou, a pass in the mountains north of Peking, but
scarcely a route for game to be brought all the way from
Siberia. Dr. Miles, of the Peking Union Medical College, while
hunting in the mountains north of Kuei Hua Ch'eng, in North
Shansi, saw two large gallinaceous birds that he thought to be
Capercailly. In spite of David's record of both Capercailly and
Hazel Hen in 1877, when the forests both east and west of
Peking were far more extensive than now, these birds should
probably be stricken from the present day Chihli list, as Mr.
Sowerby says. These three species should be sought for, if
anywhere, in the unexplored forests north of Peking between
the Great Wall and the Manchurian border.
