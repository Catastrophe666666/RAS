II. If art be mere imitation only, then it follows
logically that the imitations will have a similar effect
upon men to the originals. Therefore to represent an
immoral content is only to double the examples of im-
morality; but to create works of art with high moral
content will be one of the best means of true education.
The moralistic principle, then, is one in which the prac-
tical interest predominates over the æsthetic interest.
According to this theory, beauty is valued not primarily
as the adequate expression of reality, but as an effective
mode and means in education. Under this discussion of
practical interest in moral education we may very pro-
perly include a very brief summary of Plato's position."

Confucius said, "I learn without satiety; I teach
without weariness."16 So insatiable was that hunger for
knowledge, so unswerving his desire to impart what he
believed, that a later sage declared, "My purpose, it is to
learn Confucius !" The ages have felt the splendour of
this passionate allegiance to moral character. For we
shall see that Confucius made all pursuit subservient to
this one aim. Elsewhere he said, "I examine the multi-
plicity of things between heaven and earth." At first
glance one would say, Surely this is the true scientific
spirit! But further investigation discloses the fact that
the examination of nature was not in the interests of
science, or of purely disinterested truth as such, but
rather for the sake of morality. Is this why many are
saying that there never has been any true science in
China? I trust that the subsequent discussion, in the
instance of music, will throw light on the question
whether the fields of morality, æsthetics, and truth are
absolutely co-ordinate, and that to make one subserve
another is a violation of reality; or whether, and to what
extent, an art such as music can or should be used for
the purpose of moral improvement. Or further, is music
as a means of moral education legitimate for a youth,

of eight notes from D to D, of which Bh was the sixth,
and it had its semitones between the second and third and
the fifth and sixth degrees counting upward. The Phry-

low. The Dorian base was majestic, the Lydian tenor
tender, and so forth. More specifically, the Greek modes

The views of Plato and Confucius as to the purpose
and uses of music in moral education have many points
of similarity. Like the Chinese sages, Plato determined
the ethical and symbolic affinities of metres and melodies,
and other features 38 of the vocal and instrumental music
of his day.39 Plato preferred stringed to wind instru-
ments. (The "Panharmonion" was to be excluded, on
which all the modes could be played). The Greeks
associated wind instruments with excitement and emo-
tional effects, and stringed instruments with the sense
of form and precision. The stringed instruments in use
were varieties of the harp, and not like the modern
violin. 10
