them the guardian outposts of T'ien-p'en. Their ap-

three altars by the river side and thereon presented the
three sacrifices with offerings of jade and pure water.
Even after the rise of the Han dynasty officers were
several times sent to continue these sacrifices.

Kai Ming, Li Ping and Wen Ong divide the honour

Wen Ong is more remembered as the father of learn-
ing in Szechuan than as a great administrator. Not a
little is recorded of his stone school and stone classics but
scarcely anything written of his irrigation works, great
though they were.
For a thousand odd years the irrigation system
seems to have been kept in a high state of efficiency.
The first extensive repairs we read of, come in the T'ang
Dynasty when Kao Ping the governor of the province,
to his other great services of State, added an overhaul
of these artificial waterways.
During the Song Dynasty the only extra thing done
was to deepen the courses: no new methods were adopted
because it was realised the old were better. Li Ping's
plan of making dykes and lining the banks by means of
bamboo basket sacks filled with cobble stones was faith-
fully carried out. These were 100 feet long, 3 feet in
diameter and laid one above another seven deep.
About the close of the Song dynasty the embank-
ments for some reason or other had fallen into bad repair.
By the time of the Yuen (A.D. 1367) the people owing to
this were in great distress but the right man appeared
in the person of a high official named Kieh Tang-Pu, who
set himself to remedy things. At the divide of the Min
and the Tukiang channel he used 66,000 catties of iron to
mould into position a wedge-like tortoise. He transfixed
this by an iron pole to assist in resisting and splitting
the dash of the current. The banks of the dam and Tu-
kiang he endeavoured to render permanent by lining them

During the Ming dynasty (A.D. 1368–1643) the olden
time bamboo basket plan was re-adopted. We read of
repairs during the reigns of Ch'eng Hua, Hong Chï,
Chen Teh, Chia Ch'ing and Wan Lih. The Ming officials
were diligent builders, and naturally the needs of the
irrigation channels did not escape their attention.
It was during the reign of the Emperor Chia Ch'ing
(A.D. 1522-1562) that two iron oxen were substituted in
the place of the tortoise at the dividing of the water.
They each measured over ten Chinese feet in height and
totalled 67,000 catties in weight. The heads were united
but the bodies spread apart like the shape of the character
"ren" or man A" or our inverted "V." Hence its name
of REN-Tsze-T'i (A). It was the old idea repeated
of capping the head of the dam with an immoveable
wedge like figure. The position of the oxen was so related
to the height of the current that they registered at a
glance its rise or fall. An inscription on their heads read
that when it reached the head it betokened fulness; when
it covered the horns abundance. The uncovering of the
feet disclosed the bottom, and the width between the tails
gave the required breadth of the dam.
These repairs lasted till the reign of Wan Li (A.D.
1573-1619). The dykes then had again to be reinforced.
This time tree stakes were used and the proper depth to
which the gravel should be yearly removed clearly in-
dictated by placing thirty iron poles at intervals in the
bed. The rush of the Min carries down such great
quantities of sand and cobble stones that even in one
season the mouths of the irrigation channels become half
choked. And their restoration to the necessary depth is

At the close of the Ming dynasty when the Sze-

rebel Chang Hsien-Chong () the dykes fell into
great neglect. The Manchu officials on their arrival had
to see to their entire renewal. Every reign on to the
Revolution witnessed its renovations and repairs. The
Chinese compare the irrigation works to the respiratory
system. Any choking of the channels they reckon to be
as fatal to the prosperity of the plain as an inflammation
of the bronchial tubes to the health of the body; conse-
quently all through the Manchu regime we find the most
scrupulous care taken to maintain the system at its fullest
efficiency. The old method of Li Ping of bunding the
banks with bamboo basket-sacks filled with stones was
strictly carried out together with the annual clearing of
the main entrances. The sacks were not so long or so
bulky but the method was the same. No iron tortoises,
iron oxen or other more ornamental than useful figures
were used; two iron cylinders only were laid in the river
bed above the Pao-p'ing-k'ou or Li-tui opening as a guide
to excavation. Opposite Li-tui a water gauge was painted
on the rocks.
To-day the only change is that the annual repairs
are not discharged so concientiously as formerly. The
bare minimum of work seems to be the rule. Yet the
workmen dare not lessen their excavations or there would
be a riot. Once, it is said, there was a surreptitious
attempt to raise the iron poles slightly, and the outcry
was so loud and threatening that never since has any
one attempted it.
Visitors to Kwanhsien should see the irrigation chan-
nels when they are empty in the spring, and again in the
summer when they are in full flood. The contrast will
help them to understand better the ingenuity of this great
engineering feat and appreciate its success. They will not
wonder then that the Chinese boast so much about it.
But a good guide is needed: otherwise local descriptions
may be misleading.

There is an early name for this cut that has been

opening. The "hai-yen" is therefore the Pao-Ping-K'ou

waters.
Leaving the Fu-Long-Kuan and coming round to the

to be explained as "yah-shui-kuai" i.e., for the repression

buffalo set up here had some sort of queer power to keep
down floods.

a distance of about 1,500 yards. It is thus much longer
than formerly, a fact evidently due to the right bank

Six or seven feet of water at the beginning of the
rice planting season is enough. Later, nine feet is re-
quired. More than that is too much. By means of this
side canal the flow can be regulated according to need.
Behind the dam there has grown up an island of
gravel. Every Spring adds to it. It as an interesting
sight watching the small army of workmen clearing out
the courses. Only two iron poles now mark the bottom
of the bed. They are 12 Chinese feet and 7 inches thick.
They lie opposite an inscription on the bank which is
Li Ping's adage. (E) to dig the beds deep
and keep the banks low. Forty thousand bamboos are
said to be used annually in the repairs, and the sum spent
over $10,000.
The money is trifling compared to the wide area sup-
plied with water. Eleven "Hsiens" or districts profit by
the system. It makes possible in them a population of
1,200 to the square mile. Who can estimate the thousands
of millions in the past that have owed to it their suste-
nance and material happiness? And could the same
long continued benefits be said of any other irrigation
works the world over? Probably not. It is a strange
mystery that a people so highly favoured should remember
with gratitude only Li Ping, one of the human agents, in
their prosperity, and almost forget his great predecessor.
For they give Kai Ming very scant recognition. Yet it
remains that to this minister of the aboriginal kingdom
of Shuh, and not to Li Ping the Prefect of the Chinese
State of Ts'in, must be given the main honour of creating
the Kwan-Hsien waterworks.
