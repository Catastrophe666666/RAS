THE MURDER OF ACHMACH BAILO
Note by The Rev. A. C. MOULE, M.A.
In my article printed in last year's Journal there
was a very careless mistake and some omissions for which
apologies are due to my readers. First, while it is true
that there are no pictures of Catapults or Guns in c. 6 of
San ts'ai t'u hui, yet c. 7 is full of such pictures, some of
which are actually reproduced in Yule's Marco Polo! so
that my mistake was doubly inexcusable. In the second
place, more exact details of the carrying out of the post-
humous sentence on Ahmad might have been given from
c. 12, fol. 2vº-5rº, of the Yüan shih. Thus on June 4,
1282, "those who had obtained office by offering their
wives, daughters, or sisters to Ahmad" were degraded
and land which had been seized by him was restored to
its owners. On June 7 the remaining 581 of Ahmad's
clan () of 714 were punished, an inventory of Ahmad's
"horses, camels, oxen, sheep, and donkeys" (3,758 head)
was made, and Ahmad himself was disinterred and
exposed outside the T'ung-hsüan Gate. On June 17 an
inventory of the property of his wives and sons was made
and his slaves were set free. On July 12 the remaining
33 of the 204 superfluous Offices which Ahmad had set up
were abolished. On July 19 another inventory was made
of the slaves and property of his wives, sons, and sons-in-
law. On July 27 Ahmad's private house was granted to
Ho-li-ho-sun. On October 3 the Metropolitan
Board was ordered finally to suppress Ahmad's clan.
On October 4 his third son, A-san was put to death
and flayed as a public warning. On October 7 the fourth
son was executed. On October 19 Ahmad's nephew,
T Tsai-nu-ting, was executed, and Ma-lin of
the clan of Hu-hsin was deprived of office. In
October 18 a list of the names of the members of Ahmad's
clan was ordered to be made in a black register ().
On November 30 Ahmad's eldest son, Hu-hsin, and second
son, Mo-su-hu, were executed and pickled ()

The "recent discoveries" mentioned on p. 28 of last
year's Journal have now been published in Professor
Benedetto's Il Milione, 1928, and unfortunately they
throw no light at all on this particular chapter, for which
Ramusio's printed text of 1559 remains the sole authority
as far as European writers are concerned.
With regard to the identification of the actors in the
story, further reflection makes me feel very much inclined
to identify the Mock Prince (7) with the monk Kao,
and to suppose that Rashid either made two men ("the
saint" and the Vizier Kau Finchan) out of one monk Kao,
or accidentally transferred the name and part of the
deeds of the monk to the Vizier. The fact that Kau
Finchan was killed on the spot and that the monk Kao
escaped at the time to be captured later may reasonably,
I think, be explained as I suggested on p. 27 last year.
The execution of Chang I, which may seem at first sight
harsh, may to some extent be accounted for by the fact
that he had been responsible for the employment of the
monk Kao in the army two years before (Yüan shih c. 11,
fol. 1r) and had perhaps made himself unpopular with
some by procuring an edict for the burning of Taoist
books only a few months earlier.
