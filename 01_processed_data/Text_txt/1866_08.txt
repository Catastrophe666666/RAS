A SHORT SKETCH OF THE CHINESE GAME OF CHESS,
CALLED KH'E 棋 ​ALSO CALLED SEANG-KHE 象棋​, TO

GAME PLAYED BY THE CHINESE,
BY H. G. HOLLINGWORTH.*
THE Chinese Game of Chess is played by two persons, each having
sixteen pieces, upon a board divided into sixty-four squares, eight
on each of the four sides, but separated in the middle between the
players by an oblong division called the River河​界​,which
extends from one side of the board to the other.

The squares are all of the same colour.
The pieces are placed upon the points where the lines forming
the squares intersect each other.
The foregoing diagram represents the board with the pieces
arranged as at the commencement of a game.
From this it will be seen, that, though there are only sixty-four
squares there are ninety positions for the pieces.
The four squares through which the diagonal lines pass form the
"Head Quarters," and the General and his Secretaries, are not per-
mitted, to move beyond the lines bounding these four squares.
The pieces are all of the same size and colour, distinguishable
from each other by an inscription, which is not only of a different
colour, on the pieces used by each player, but the characters used
on the one side, are generally different from those used on the other.
The pieces are termed Red and Black, and the characters on
them are generally of these colours.
The moves are played by each player alternately.
The General is placed on the centre line, and has on each side
four pieces, those next to him right and left are his Secretaries,
then the Elephants, next to them the Horses, then the Chariots.
In front of each Horse is placed a Cannon at a distance of two
points; and in front of the General, Elephants and Chariots, are
placed the five Soldiers, at a distance of three points.
MOVEMENTS OF THE PIECES.
Tseang.
Sae.
The General's movements are confined to the nine points in
"Head Quarters," in which he moves the length or breadth of one
square only at a time, backwards, forwards and sideways; he can
take any of the adversary's pieces which stand unprotected in
"Head Quarters" on the points adjoining that which he occupies,
excepting however the diagonal points, as he has not the privilege
of moving diagonally; he cannot be left on the same line as the
opposite General without a piece intervening. He is exempt from
capture, but he is not permitted to move into check, that is, on to
any point guarded by one of the adversary's pieces.

The soldiers move one point at a time, but only forwards, until

CAPTURING ADVERSE PIECES.
This consists in removing the adverse piece and placing the
captor on the point which the former occupied.
This movement is common to all the pieces, but the Cannon
captures only when a piece intervenes, over which it leaps; all
other pieces capture as they move.
NOTATION.
The longitudinal lines are numbered from Right to Left of each
player, one to nine.
The transverse lines are not numbered.
The pieces are described by their characters in full, and the ex-
pressions, forwards, backwards, and sideways, are made use of
thus,-

the board.

to its former position.
