By Dr. JOHN C. FERGUSON
In my Outlines of Chinese Art, pp. 120-121, I have
called attention to the counterparts in clay of ancient
bronze vessels, both those designed for domestic use, and
also those used on ceremonial occasions. I pointed out
that clay vessels were often substituted for bronzes in
burial rites, and this fact has been recently confirmed by
the discovery of clay vessels in Han Dynasty graves on
the Ta-tung river in Korea, investigated by a joint
committee composed of professors from Tokyo and Kyoto
Universities.
The use of clay vessels for domestic purposes has also
been confirmed by the discoveries of the Geological Survey
of China in the culture of Yang-shao, situated in the
Mien-chih district of Honan province. (Bulletin of The
Geological Survey of China, Number 5, 1920. "An Early
Chinese Culture," by J. S. Andersson.)
Several years earlier than the appearance of my book,
Dr. Laufer in his Chinese Pottery of the Han Dynasty,
p. 137, had noted that all the vases which he had described
had been "modelled after, bronze vases," and on p. 141 he
refers again to "the dependence of pottery vases on the
bronze type."
This fundamental principle is not mentioned by
Hobson in Chaps. I and II of his Chinese Pottery and
Porcelain, published in 1925, in which he gives a good
account of the early evolution of the potter's art in China,
but does not connect it with the imitation of the shapes of
bronze vessels. In Vol. II of The Religious System of
China, p. 401, De Groot, on the authority of written
records, gives a list of earthenware objects used in the
burial of an Emperor of the Hou Han Dynasty (A.D. 25-
220), but, like Hobson, did not connect them with the
shapes or decoration of similar bronze objects in earlier
burials.

Investigations which I have been able to continue
since the publication of my Outlines make it possible to
trace from actual specimens the development of the
fabrication of clay vessels from ancient times down to
the present. Among the specimens are several from the
collection of H. E. the late Tuan Fang, notably a Chou
Dynasty tripod, and a stoneware glazed vase of the Six
Dynasties period. A pottery house taken from the grave
of Li Hsien, of the Northern Wei dynasty, furnishes a
good example of the glaze used in that period. Through-
out the long period of development no one fact is more
apparent than the dependence of clay upon bronze as
to shapes, the poorer imitating and following the lead
of the more valuable. Bronze was a difficult material
to handle, but the moulds in which products from its
melting were cast could be shaped leisurely, whereas
clay required more rapid manipulation. Hobson pointed
out (Chin. P. and P. I. 2), the lack of literary record
as to the first use in China of the potter's wheel, but
actual specimens in the Yang-shao culture of the third
millenium B. C. suggest wheel technique, according to
Dr. Andersson ("An Early Chinese Culture"-The Geolo-
gical Survey of China, 1923, No. 5, p. 49). "It is safe to
say that the overwhelming majority of the painted vessels
are turned upon the potter's wheel." (Ibid. p. 53).
Some of the specimens described had been fired and
decorated as well as painted, but they have little artistic
interest.
It is not surprising, however, that up to the present
time there have not been found clay specimens which
have any more than antiquarian interest, for all available
evidence indicates that the artistic medium of ancient
China was bronze, not clay. The earliest extant objects
wrought in bronze are probably chariot decorations, of
which there are examples in the Study Museum of the
Government University, Peking, and also in my own
collection; and these show good artistic taste in decora-
tion. In this respect they are in marked contrast with
the clay vessels of the Yang-shao culture. But the most
conspicuous mark of the early dominance of bronze over
clay is in the variety and beauty of shapes. Down to
the time of highest development of porcelain manufacture
during the Yung Chêng period, the ambition of potters
was to produce shapes which could equal in grace and
beauty those of early bronzes. The plastic medium made
it possible to perfect many adaptations and refinements

The provenance of the earliest earthenware speci-
mens which have been discovered is the same as that of
our earliest bronzes. Yang-shao, in Mien-chih district,
Honan province, with its deposits of clay objects, adjoins
Lo-yang and Mêng-ching, from which places many of our
best excavated finds of bronze vessels have come.
In
general, the country lying to the west of the Yellow
River, as it flows southward from Mongolia to Honan,
belonged to the Ch'in tribes who established the first
Empire under Shih Huang, with its capital at Ch'ang-an
(modern Hsi-an in Shensi province). On the east of
the river was Chin with its capital at modern Tai-yüan.
After the river turned eastward toward the sea the
country on the south bank where Mien-chih, Lo-yang and
Mêng-ching are located, was the royal domain of Chou,
to the east of which, further along the river, were Sung,
Ts'ao, Lu and Ch'i. On the north bank were Chin, Wei,
Yü and Yen. In modern geographical names, these
districts are the provinces of Shensi, Shansi, the northern
part of Honan, Chihli, and the northern part of Shantung.
This belt was the cradle of Chinese civilization, from
which came our earliest examples of pottery and bronzes.
In this belt the district most fertile in modern finds is
that which surrounds the bend where the Yellow River
changes its southward into an eastward course, though
many valuable things have come from the district
immediately eastward which included Yin-hsü, i.e., Chao-
ko (modern Chi-hsien, the first station on the Peking-
Hankow railway north of Wei-huei, Honan).
Among the specimens obtained from Tuan Fang I
have mentioned above a Chou tripod. This is the period

and the ascription agreed with all the facts known

similar vessel (Chinese Pottery of the Han Dynasty, Pt.
1, Fig. 2) to the Chou dynasty. It would now appear as
if these ascriptions had been too modest as to age, but
I am inclined to allow the name to stand. Yang-shao
was in the district which was governed by the Chou

greater importance than a supposititious age. It is my

felt able, in 1735 (thirteenth year of Yung Cheng), to
undertake imitations, not only of the shapes of ancient
bronzes, but also of their colouring. He made many
pieces and numbered them. During the first year of
Ch'ien Lung, 1736, he made ten pieces which he
considered good. These included shapes modelled after
bronze tsun, lei, ting, and i, but the one vessel which he
considered of the greatest excellence was the Hsi-êrh Tsun.
I have seen this wonderful specimen of the potter's art,
and consider it deserving of the praise bestowed upon
it by T'ang Yin. This record of T'ang Yin's work at
Ching-tê-chên is valuable for the purposes of this paper in
showing that it was the highest ambition of the greatest
master of porcelain production in China to produce
successful imitations of the shapes of ancient bronzes.
The names of the shapes of porcelain vessels, both in
Chinese and in foreign languages, have been assigned,
to a large extent, by dealers and collectors. They show
a lack of uniformity and in the case of some shapes,
notably in that of vases, there is not a little confusion.
In the hope of providing a guide, I have compiled the
following description of shapes with accompanying plates.

TSUN.
This shape is after the general pattern of an ancient
bronze wine-jar called tsun (), but under this heading
