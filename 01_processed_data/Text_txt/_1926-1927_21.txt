CHOU-KUNG; THE DUKE OF CHOU.
By THE REV. EVAN MORGAN, D.D.
In an age when there is a dearth of great men in
China, it will refresh our minds to turn back to other
times and in the spirit of Carlyle's hero-worship, consider
the eminent men that have made their age and country
illustrious. In this way our thoughts may be lifted from
the sordid present to the brilliancy of the past, and be
provided with some of the ideals which push forward
humankind in the path of true progress, and without
which the race must inevitably deteriorate. One such
hero we shall find in the Duke of Chou, whose example
has inspired his countrymen through the ages, and whose
life is not without value and instruction for the present
age. All that is known of him is brought together in the
following pages.
The Duke was the fourth son of Wên Wang, the
founder of the Chou dynasty, and the younger brother of
Wu Wang. To Confucius these two were the exemplary
kings, the models of all monarchs. In Chinese the duke
is known under the names of Chi Tan姬​旦​, Chi being
the family name: Duke of Chi姬​公​: Duke of Chou 周公​:
and, posthumously as Yuan Shêng 元​聖​, The great sage.
He is renowned as a most filial son and affectionate
brother. In these virtues he was pre-eminent and su-
perior to all others. In love, loyalty and generosity he
was very different from his two brothers, Kuan Shu and
Ts'ai Shu. The two latter were narrow-minded, jealous
and disloyal. When Wu Wang died, Kuan Shu schemed
to become king and dispossess the rightful heir, Chêng,
son of Wu Wang. In this he was frustrated by Chou
Kung, and the young nephew succeeded to the throne.
Kuan Shu was executed by order of his brother. The
new king being young, his uncle, Chou Kung, became
regent during the minority, which office he discharged
most loyally both to the advantage of the state and the
young monarch.

The Duke proved himself a great statesman, distin-
guished in civil administration and military matters.
He composed the Chou Li, included in the Thirteen
Classics. It is a comprehensive and exhaustive work on
all administrative affairs. It offers guidance and direc-
tion on the selection and appointment of all officers of
state: on the division of the country into administrative
units and the relationship of one office to another, as well
as of each to the central unity; principles are laid down
for political and economic matters, and regulations are
given for the ordering of society in all its various
functions. The whole work of organizing a state is out-
lined in this book and it is the foundation on which was
built the government of China and on which in the main
it has been conducted through long ages. He thus not
only helped in the consolidation of the power of his
brother and the House of Chou, which lasted longer than
any other dynasty in the annals of China, but he also
did much to establish the unity of the nation. Torn as
the country has been by constant internecine conflicts
throughout its long history, yet, the ideals and practices
established by the Duke kept intact the bond of union;
the continuity of institutions perpetuated the unity of
national life.
It was one of his cherished desires to uplift the
masses. By his legal code he endeavoured to provide
justice between man and man: by the enunciation of
moral principles he hoped to purify the morals of the
people. He was wise enough to see that without education
in moral truths the state could never be properly gov-
erned. He held the principle, so much advocated by the
Salvation Army, that the people must have food before
they can be converted. So the first thing in his pro-
gramme was to provide them with an adequate livelihood
and afterwards to educate and teach them. His whole
life and great abilities were devoted to the interests of
the state and people. It is not surprising that for these
distinguished merits and unselfish services posterity
named him the First Sage: first in time and first in
degree.
He was not only famous as a statesman and adminis-
trator, but he was also renowned in war. After a change
of dynasty the work of pacification takes a long time, for
usually there are loyal remnants of the old House who
refuse submission to the new power. It took many years
to clear the country of the soldiers of the last monarch

He also appointed the son of the last monarch to a small
feudatory state, thus carrying out an aspect of Chinese
humanity which forbids that the scions of the former

this prince lest he should rebel. By the appointment of
the last scion of the late dynasty the ancestral worship of
Yin was also continued. This also is counted an act of
great merit in Chinese thought. After the complete
pacification of the country Wu Wang advised that all
meritorious ministers be created feudatory dukes. He
made his brother the Duke of Lu, with residence in the
palace at Ch'ü fu. He thus became the Prince of Lu.
But he did not proceed to this appointment, as the empire
demanded his services: so his son was sent in his place.
The Duke was 'retained to be the prime minister' of the
empire. This is the reason for his being called Chou
Kung, Duke of Chou.
One of the secrets of his success as an administrator
was his care in the selection of worthy men to be his
coadjutors in the work of government. This is seen in
the advice he gave his son Pei Ch'in on his departure to
take up his duties in Lu. He said to him, "I am the son
of Wên Wang, brother of Wu Wang and uncle of Ch'êng
Wang, and thus a distinguished man in the country; yet
if any worthy man comes to see me when I am dressing
my hair or taking my bath or eating my food, I stop in
the middle to see the caller at once, lest I miss an
opportunity of getting good men. Therefore, when you
go to Lu never make the fact of your being a prince a
reason for looking down on men."

After these preliminary observations we come to the
most important events in his life, events which are
recorded with great detail and which won for him the
affection of the nation. The one is his love for his brother
Wu and the other is his care over the youthful monarch,
his nephew.
In the second year of the reign of Wu, when the

me as hostage," he prayed at the altars of his father,
grandfather and great-grandfather. Thus he gave ex-

the jade kuei, he made the divination. The prayer and

At the death of the emperor Wu, his son Ch'êng was
a minor and the care of his nephew devolved on the
Duke. The affection he had had for his brother was
bestowed on the nephew. The Duke was appointed re-
gent, possibly on his own initiative, and the welfare of
the child became the chief concern of the uncle. The
Duke looked after his morals; Shao Kung looked after his
physical training, and T'ai Kung took care of his educa-
tion. The Duke's brother thought he saw some sinister
motive in his being regent: at any rate made it an excuse
to stir up a rebellion. The Duke gave it as his reason for
remaining in Chou rather than proceeding to his office as
Prince of Lu that he feared the people of Chou might
rebel, "In which case," he said, "I could not face the
three ancestors. I act for the family in looking after the
interests of the child." His brothers spread false and
defamatory reports. His brothers Kuan and Ts'ai led
part of the empire in revolt. For three years the Duke
pursued them, after having first issued a proclamation to
the nation explaining why the punitive expedition was
put in motion. Kuan Shu and and Wu Kêng were captur-
ed and executed and the other brother Ts'ai was deported.

When the King grew to manhood Duke Chou handed
him all the affairs of State, affirming he no longer need
act. This year the Duke instead of the emperor received
the feudal princes, who came to pay court.
In spite of the happy condition of the country there
were still malcontents who tried to undermine the author-
ity of the Duke and throw doubt on his loyalty. They
got the ear of the emperor and evidently influenced him,
for the Duke fled to Ch'u, in Hupeh. It occurred to
Ch'êng to examine the records in the dynastic archives

found ample evidence of his love and loyalty. For when
Ch'êng had an illness his uncle went to the temple and
prayed for him (as he had done for his brother). When
the King found this prayer in the archives he wept and
immediately recalled him. (Ssu-Ma Chi'en's history).
