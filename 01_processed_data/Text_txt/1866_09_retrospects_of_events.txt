ARTICLE IX.
RETROSPECT OF EVENTS IN THE NORTH OF CHINA
DURING THE YEAR 1866.
COMPILED BY CHAS. H. BUTCHER, M.Α.*
THE year which has just concluded has been comparatively barren
of events and the writer of a Retrospect sees few salient points on
which to fix his attention. The contrast between East and West
cannot be more vividly presented to us than when we look at the
rapid march of stirring incidents which has signalized the year 1866
in Europe and then turn to the monotonous picture presented by
the field of Asiatic Politics.
In the north of China very little of interest is to be observed.
The embers of the rebellion are being gradually stamped out, but
the Nien-fei have taken the place of the Taipings and though not so
formidable a foe they have occasioned bloodshed and trouble to the
Government in several places.
Early in the year we were assured that they had gained a series
of successes in the country to the north of the Yellow River, they
subsequently obtained possession of the district of Chu-chow and
established their head quarters at a place called Hsiao-hsien.
In the south of Shan-tung they were successfully resisted by the
Imperialists under Puan, a mandarin who had held a high post
under Gordon. In the spring they pushed their way with con-
siderable energy in the Kiang-su Province and assembled in im-
posing numbers at Tung-tai, which place they approached by a
circuitous route and forced marches along the seaboard. Their
leaders deserve some credit for the ingenious manner in which they
evaded a conflict with the army of Tseng-kwo-fân who had con-
centrated his braves around Tsing-kiang-fu.
These brigands however have not come into actual contact with
the Foreigners except in the neighbourhood of Hankow and in that
city as well as in the settlement they certainly excited in the month
of February considerable alarm. Their vast numbers were aug-
mented by several regiments of the Imperialists who revolted be-

Newchwang too has been the scene of much trouble owing to the
menacing attitude assumed by bands of mounted Banditti. In
December 1865 the attention of the whole community was roused
by a circular issued by Mr. T. T. Meadows, H. B. M.'s Consul, in
which he stated that Chinese Commandant of the District had
called upon him and stated that a body of robbers or rebels 1,000
strong had taken Hing-king and had detached a body for the
capture of Tung-hwang which force about 300 strong had since
advanced in the direction of Newchwang. The foreign residents
met and organized measures for defence, as the officials candidly
declared they were utterly powerless. Indeed a more unhappy
state of affairs than that which exists still in this part of the coun-
try cannot be conceived. The Imperialists have given up all idea
of opposing the rebels by force of arms and their only plan is to
attempt to induce them to join their standard by offering the bribe
of Official Rank. Both oppressing brigands and unprotected peo-
ple must unite in despising a government which can neither punish
the refractory nor guard the innocent. Amidst these evidences
of the weakness of the existing regime it is satisfactory to be able
to chronicle any circumstance which indicates a desire on the part
of the Chinese Government to obtain information as to the institu-
tions of other countries, for hitherto the Peking Officials have
steadily refused to entertain the idea that it is possible for them to
gain instruction from Europe, and indeed have persevered in as-
suming the attitude of teachers instead of that of learners. China
is still to the Mandarin "The Middle Kingdom" and "All under
Heaven." But it is to be hoped that the sense of their own fee-
bleness has awakened the Heads of departments to doubt the abso-
lute perfection of their time honoured system. At least the con-
stant contemplation of incompetency, failure and disaster, seems
in some measure to have opened the eyes of Prince Kung and
his confreres. We judge this from an event, the importance of
which cannot easily be overestimated which occurred towards
the close of March. Mr. Hart and a part of Chinese emissaries
left for Europe on a semi-official errand. Though the native
members of the Party did not receive any formal commission from
Peking, their journey was suggested by the Tsun-le Yamen.
Rumour of course pointed at their step as one likely to lead ulti-
mately to the Establishment of an Embassy at Home, and Mr. Geo.

The foreign communities at the various Ports will probably re-
gard the subject technically known as "The navigation of Inland
Waters Question" as the matter of most interest in the past year.
It had been the custom for some time for small steamers to ply on
the creeks which intersect the silk districts for the purpose of bring-
ing forward that article of merchandize with regularity and dis-
patch. The local mandarins suddenly and without preliminary
warning issued a notice prohibitory of this traffic. The Chamber
of Commerce at Shanghai forwarded through Dr. C. A. Winchester,
H. B. M.'s Consul at that port, an urgent letter of remonstrance in
which they alleged that the action taken by the Chinese was in
direct violation of the Article IX. of the Treaty of Tientsin. Sir
Rutherford Alcock, however, after mature deliberation refused "to
take any decisive step to realize the wishes of the Chamber of Com-
merce," and expressed his opinion that the *two arguments used
by the complainants to wit "that a prescriptive right to navigate
the creeks in the craft referred to had been established by use," and
"that these small steamers only differed from House-boats (which
were not prohibited) by a difference of motive power" were utterly
untenable.
The year has been signalized by a yet more revolutionary at-
tempt to "open up" the country, viz., the publication of a com-
prehensive scheme for the introduction of Railways into the Pro-
vince of Kiang-su. It appeared to the projectors that three
circumstances specially conduced to the success of their plan-
1st, the level character of the soil; 2ndly, the political events of the
last four years, which have inclined the Authorities and People to
look with greater favour than elsewhere on the introduction of
foreign innovations and improvements; and 3rdly, the density of
the population which occupies the numerous and flourishing cities
of the District. The plan and estimated cost of the Shanghai and

Soochow Railway were laid before the Shanghai Chamber of Com-
merce and a communication on the subject was addressed to the
British Minister. Formidable difficulties however appear to have
been thrown in the way of the scheme by the Native Authorities and
it will probably not be pressed until a more favourable opportunity.
Telegraphs, the latest discovery of Western Science, seem likely
to find their way into China even before the earlier invention of
steam. Messrs. Russell & Co. have constructed a line which will
serve to demonstrate to the local authorities the merit of their in-
vention between their Hong at Shanghai and the godowns of S. S.
N. Company, while in the South, an effort is being made to gain
sanction for the construction of a Telegraph between Hongkong
and the local capital which the Futai of Kwang-tung has appа-
rently been induced to approve.
Though not strictly speaking a subject connected with the North
of China this retrospect would be incomplete without an allusion
to the important events in the Corea.
Early in the year news arrived of the murder of some French
Missionaries by the natives of that country. Vice-Admiral Roze
promptly proceeded to the Seoul but seeing that the Corean diplo-
matists were endeavouring to protract the negotiations until the
river of Kang-hoa should be closed by the ice, weighed anchor and
brought his squadron into the Chinese Ports safely. Several little
things had been observed which showed there was no time to lose
if a disaster were to be escaped. The flower of the Corean Army
had come against the French forces and in an attack upon a forti-
fied pagoda it was perceived that the enemy fought well and re-
pulsed the assault with so much vigour that common prudence
dictated the retirement of the French. There can be little doubt
however that the initiative has been taken and that before long
Foreigners will have the means of becoming acquainted with a
region now almost unknown.
We have now mentioned in order the chief Political events of
the year. It remains to add a list of the Literary Productions
bearing on subjects interesting to the Asiatic Society which are
announced as in active preparation. First we may mention the
valuable rudimentary work of Mr. Wade, H. B. M.'s Secretary
of Legation at Peking which is expected to be of great utility to
Chinese Students and is now in a forward state of preparation at
the Shanghai Presses.

A Dictionary of the Foochow Dialect by the Rev. R. T. Maclay
D.D. and the Rev. C. C. Baldwin is commenced printing, and a
Dictionary of the Amoy Dialect is about to be issued at Hongkong.
The Presbyterian Mission Press at Shanghai is printing Dr. Hep-
burn's Japanese Dictionary, and the Learned Author of "The
Middle Kingdom" is soon to issue a Dictionary of The Court
Dialect.
Two periodicals which are likely to be of great service have also
commenced, one is published at Foochow and is called the "Mis-
sionary Recorder" and the other emanates from Hongkong and is
called "Notes and Queries." It is intended to serve as a medium
of intercommunication for persons interested in Chinese Philology,
Geography or History.
These indications of literary activity are perhaps the most im-
portant "signs of the times" it has been our province to record
for they are all means to facilitate that mutual understanding one
of the other, which seems so hard to establish between Europe and
China. Therefore though we cannot close the Retrospect of the
year 1866 with a *vivid picture of the Genius of the Flowery Land
rousing herself "noble and puissant," "like a strong man after
sleep, we are not without hope that the rulers of this vast Empire
are slowly but surely learning lessons of the West concerning "the
true glory of Kingdoms and Estates." As for vast and substantial
improvements in military organization and efficiency these are not
to be expected until the finances of the country are properly ad-
justed for in the words of Lord Bacon, "The blessing of Judah
and Issachar will never meet; that the same people or nation
should be both the lion's whelp and the ass between burdens;
neither will it be that a people overlaid with taxes should ever be-
come valiant and martial."
