ARTICLE VI.
POLITICAL INTERCOURSE BETWEEN CHINA AND LEWCHEW.
BY S. WELLS WILLIAMS, L.L.D.*
THE recent visit of a deputation of Lewchewans to Peking to
tender the salutations of their Sovereign to the Emperor of China,
has suggested an inquiry as to the real nature of the relationship
which exists between these rulers. It has sometimes been called
a feudal subjection, and these islands in consequence have been
considered as tributaries to China, but if the privileges and duties
of the two parties be compared with those which existed in Europe
during the tenth century it will be seen that these terms are inap-
plicable. No protection or aid has ever been granted to the rulers
of the Lewchewan Group either against internal rebels or foreign
enemies by the Emperor; who on his part requires no services,
collects no revenue, gives no orders to the local officials, and leaves
the time for complimentary visits and amounts of presents brought
entirely to the pleasure of his so-called fief. The tendency of the
European system of feudalism was to render both the sovereign and
villain powerless; for the former could not maintain his acknow-
ledged power against the rebellious spirits, nor the latter defend
his person or property against the avaricious cruelty, of the feudal
lords, who stood between them, desirous to encroach on the rights
of both. Military force was the essential condition of such feudal-
ism, and support and fidelity the principal relations between lord
and vassal. "The vassal owed service to his lord, and the lord
"protection to his vassal; if the vassal failed in his obligation his
"land was forfeited; if the lord failed, he lost his seigniory." This
remark of Guizot, descriptive of the relationship between the parties
in Mediæval Europe has no application to that existing between
China and Lewchew which is neither military nor feudal and in
fact is like nothing which ever existed in Europe nor even in India,
where vassals sometimes held their lands by military service.

influence of their monarch in remote parts where his actual power
had not reached; and also of the beneficial effects likely to result
to these peoples who were wise enough thus to range themselves
on the side of civilization and good order.
The annals of China contain a long series of notices of these visits,
with accounts of the presents brought and given in return, all of
which has done much to foster the national pride down to the
present day, when these so-called "savages and unlettered people"
have learned more than the Chinese ever knew, and the positions
between these two parties, have entirely changed. The only two
nations who now come as tribute bearers are Corea and Lewchew,
and whose isolation from all external influences still retains them
in their old notions that China is the Middle Flowery Land, where
civilization and letters by right do dwell, and who take pleasure
in manifesting their respect and reverence by periodical visits to
its capital. The relationship between China and all these outlying
states, including Siam, Annam, Birmah and other petty nations
has usually been a peaceable one and its maintenance depended
chiefly upon the inferior party. The Emperor on his part was
pleased to receive the envoys and acknowledge their presents by
equivalent or greater ones in return; but the idea that reciprocal
obligations were involved was not thought of by the Chinese; -it
even required a spice of audacity to hint that it would be well for
the Emperor to send envoys in return. As trade was always a
part of this international intercourse, these visits afforded facilities
for traffic which were encouraged in every way, and the foreign
commerce now carried on with China may be regarded as a deve-
lopment of these visits of homage.
But if the inferior nations brought no presents and sent no
envoys, they alone suffered therefrom and it was not worth while
to follow them up. China and her Emperor could still serenely
repose in complacent grandeur, that needed no such adventitious

These remarks will enable us the better to understand the rela-
tionship which now subsists between China and the little kingdom
of Lewchew, as set forth in the following papers. It is the respect-
ful homage of an inferior to a superior, and not that of a fief to a
suzerain; it is the sincere regard of a disciple to the teacher from
whom he has learned every thing that has given him character,
position, safety, and stability at home and abroad. Such a rela-
tionship is creditable to both parties and I have no doubt has
been beneficial to the latter as it seems always to have been
transacted with the patronising consideration which the Chinese
Government knows so well to exhibit. The documents which
passed between the two Governments when the King of Lewchew

The history of Lewchew that tells the Prince of Satsuma conquer-
ed all the islands lying between Kogosima and Formosa during the
reign of Sio-neï 尙​寧 ​in A.D. 1609, who was carried prisoner to
Japan and kept there four years before he was restored to his throne.
The Princes of Satsuma have maintained their sway over this
archipelago ever since, but have allowed the Lewchewans to retain
the management of their internal affairs. One of the privileges
allowed to them was to keep up the visits to Peking, and maintain
the factory at Fuhchau, through which considerable trade was
carried on with Na-pa. This traffic has doubtless been advanta-
geous to their Japanese masters, though no data are accessible as
to its amount or articles. It is possible perhaps, that we may be
able in time to learn more about a trade and intercourse which has
continued without change for nearly five hundred years - a source
of gratification to one party as an homage to its civilization and
power, and of profit and improvement to the other.
From a little sketch of the chronology of Lewchew, we learn
that there were twenty-five reigns from Ten-son 天​孫​,the
founder of the state and a relative of the Dairi of Japan down to
the year A.D. 1176; but neither their names nor the styles of
the reigns have been recorded. Another family called Soun-ten
舜天 ​then obtained the throne; but after the third reign ab-
dicated in favor of Yeï-so 英​祖​, a descendant of Ten-son,

mentioned as having sent the first embassy to Hung-wu in A.D.
1373; but his family only retained the power during three years,
when it gave way to Sio-pa尙​巴​. This prince only held the

arrange your robes in solitary gardens, and immediately propriety, music,
and spiritual influences spread themselves for the instruction and reforma-
tion of all. The firmament rejoices in such virtue, and even to this edge of
the horizon the sea drinks in your goodness.
I your servant Shang Hau, having now succeeded to the oversight of this,
frontier inheritance, shall be able to send up my proper tribute. I acknow-
ledge the dragon-bordered missive which has come from far, and caused our
national Gods to revive from their lethargy; I thank you for the phenix
command to install my father in his shrine, causing the ancestral hall to
glow with fresh lustre. I tremblingly come into heaven's awful presence to
make the nine prostrations; I look towards the ivory gate and give the trine
commands to bow to it.
I have carefully selected my household officers Mau Kwang-kwoh, Ching
Chang-kwan and others with them to reverently take a few articles (lit.
hairs from our rivulets) and arrange them properly for your acceptance.
I humbly implore heaven's ceaseless blessing upon you, and that the
reckoning of your years may be immeasurable. May you like the sun rising
in his splendor gather around you for myriads of years continual blessings;
like streamlets running to aid their source, may your reign embody all

I, your servant, having no right to look up to your heaven-like Sacred-

BETWEEN CHINA AND LEWCHEW.
93
The Lewchewans were first prominently brought to the notice
of Europeans by the visit of the British frigate "Alceste" and her
tender the "Lyra" in 1817, when Captain Basil Hall and Lieut.
Clifford gave such glowing accounts of the treatment they received.
Their published writings placed the Ruler and his subjects in the
most pleasing light. The long continued existence of Lewchew,
with a population of less than two hundred thousand inhabitants, as
an entirely independent government lying between two such power-
ful nations as Japan and China might perhaps be impossible; but
their attainments in self government and civilization during six or
seven centuries past, have doubtless been owing to their relations
with China. Taking her language and literature as their guides
and models in education, and her political system as the best they
knew, they elevated themselves above the Mongols and Manchus
at a period when those nations were mere nomads. The Japanese
sway over them, and the dialect of that language which they speak,
have indeed brought them into closer relations with their nearer
neighbors, but have never been able to overcome their respect for
China. They teach the classics of Confucius in their schools, and
prepare their leading men for the triennial visit to Peking by teach-
ing them to speak its dialect, and sending young men to travel
across the provinces, on their way thither.
If we compare the attainments and orderly sway of the Lew-
chewans, with the inhabitants of Formosa, New Zealand, Hawaü,
or Luzon, before Europeans visited them, we shall better appre-
ciate the immense advantage that a literature confers upon a people
in preserving, elevating and consolidating it. The Lewchewans
have pressing need, however, of other influences to raise them
higher, for by degrees the power of the literary aristocracy has be-
come consolidated into one of the most severe tyrannies that now
exists on the face of the earth, one from whose terrible exactions
their insular position opens no remedy or hope of escape, to the
mass of the people.
