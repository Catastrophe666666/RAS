VI. AUTUMN FLOWERS OF THE TSINAN PLAIN.
The period of rains may begin in early June-or not
until August in a drought year. The June rains are
usually few, but markedly heavier than those of spring,
which are the fag end of north-eastern storms. In fact
they are drenching enough to cause the summer rainfall
seeds to take notice and bestir themselves. The whole
period is one of rapid development. Grey-greens change
to bright greens, grass-greens, and even blue-greens where
the soil is rich in humus. There is no such marked
sequence as occurred through the springtime. This is
largely due to the rainfall flora's consisting chiefly of
annuals-thus resembling a desert flora which awaits the
heavy though infrequent downpours of such arid regions.
This fact, coupled with the custom of being elsewhere
during the summer, leads me to treat of the summer flora
not chronologically but by family and other convenient
groups and to include with them the few autumn flowers
which are but a lagging element of the summer rains.
Howbeit, this is certain, when one returns from the coast
or mountain side, one is confronted by a new host of plants,
most of them tall and rank in growth, forming a dense
vegetation which, with the crops, covers everything.
Such a mass can only be dealt with by comparisons. The
spring flora, though present usually as old leafage, is
here omitted.

Of these groups, the most conspicuous is that of the
grasses (and sedges). The simplest introduction to this

group is by way of the crop plants. By their fruit we
shall know them. In the grasses the fruit is a seed (like
a wheat seed) enclosed in a chaffy pocket, so we may divide
the crop plants by the arrangement of their seed pockets
(or flowers): whether (1) in a single head like the wheat
or common millet, or (2) in slender, diverging heads like
the maize head (not the long-haired, female cob), or (3)
in a diffuse panicle like the sorgum (kao-liang). The
millet (Italian millet) is a Setaria. All the grains are
arranged in a dense head which, in cultures, is so heavy as
to droop. Three other grasses have this arrangement:
Setaria viridis, one of the most abundant annual
grasses, springing up anywhere it finds bare earth, varies
much in appearance but always has its single erect head
of coarse, bearded grains. It may be only six inches high
in poor, dry soil or a foot and a half in rich soil among
other weeds. In this semi-arid plain, the head may be two
to four centimeters long. The colour may be quite reddish
to a vivid grass green.
Tragus aliena is chiefly a wayside annual, spreading
laterally from its root mass rather than upward. It is
at once distinguishable because its seed pockets are
ornamented by about five, slender, raised ribs which
bristle with recurved, hook-like barbs, giving each pocket
the appearance of a burr. In fact they are burry enough
to stick to woollens and would thus be disseminated along
the pathways if it were not for their lowly state (eight
to twelve centimeters high).
Imperata cylindrica is at once the king of this group
and the farmers' pest. A perennial by reason of its long,
horizontal, rooting, underground stems (rhizomes), it
pushes its way out of sight of the farmers' eyes all through
his fields and absorbs the food and water of his crops by
under "handed" means. It can only be eradicated by
digging out its every underground bit-including those of
the roadside or fieldside-which is usually neglected by
the farmer and therefore the plant is never absent. For
us it is a thing of beauty, for it raises its large, silky-silvery
heads, quite free from leaves, high (six decimeters) above
the surrounding plants. To add to its shining aspect,
the heads are so large that they are usually gently or
gracefully bent and wave about on their slender culms
before every breeze. They may be seen already "in
flower" in late spring, being nourished that early by their
copious underground absorbing and storage system.

Chloris virgata is the most abundant and ubiquitous
of this group. An annual which crops out everywhere in
unoccupied territory, after the first heavy rains, it greets
us in September with hundreds and thousands of greenish
to ruddy heads composed of seven to eight (4-15) closely
held, hairy fingers which together look very much like a
point brush, each mounted on its own stiff culm.
Andropogon ischaemum is the most abundant on un-
disturbed land, as on the hillsides, graves, field margins,
but never on plowed or frequently disturbed land. This is
due to its being a perennial which forms dense mats. The
leaves are short and almost entirely confined to the mats,
while the long, very slender culms rise at least five
decimeters, to sway their slender, not wide-spreading,
usually ruddy, five (2-13) fingers, well above the other
plants. The flowers or seed pockets, rather loosely
scattered along the hairy rhachises, are quite slender and
have one, sharply bent, modest bristle. Thus this plant
is far more slender and graceful than its stiff, stout culmed,
shaggy headed, predecessor.
Syntherisma sanguinalis is an annual, sprawling
about on the cultivated fields or disturbed, uncovered earth.
Its flower fingers are usually three (2-9) in number, wide
spread and always green. The seed pockets are almost
always distant from each other, leaving bare spaces
between them on the rhachises which are thus seen to be
undulate. Since in our region the seed pockets are gray-
bearded, we recognize it as the subspecies S. s. ciliaris.
Capriola dactylon is to be found only in special places
where it forms carpets or good sized rugs (colonies) due
to its copiously branching, tough, almost woody, above
ground runners (stolons). Its fruiting heads are rarely
held three decimeters high and the fingers are so much
spread out as to often curve backwards. The seed
pockets are fine, hard, shining, furry edged, pointed and

Eleusine indica has wide (to 5 mm.), flat, green

Molinia (Diplachne) serotina belonging to subgroup
B, is at once recognized by the large dense clumps or stools
it forms, and by the position of the leaves along the stalk
which is at right angles, making a very striking rake-
like pattern when a culm is held out by itself, and giving
the clump itself a very characteristic appearance of
geometric airiness. Not only do the leaves grow out at
right angles to the culm but the fingers of the head as well.
These fingers moreover are very few flowered and fine, so
that this species has the skimpiest head of these grasses.
In the late fall, winter and spring it is conspicuous by
reason of its roundish clumps (to two decimeters in
diameter) of very fine, decurved, leaves, no other grass
forming any such leaf heads in this region.
Echinochloa crusgalli has a similarly arranged fruit-
ing head but the seed pockets are large and coarse, often
ruddy (mahogany green), and the coarse, leafy, often
branching and reclining culms grow singly or a few
together. This coarse, annual, "barn-yard grass," about
the Tsinan region, grows in the arroyo bottoms, especially
where pools formed during summer, and other moist, waste
ground where animals have trampled. It is much com-
moner north of the city.
Themeda triandra is the coarsest, tallest of the grasses
of the region. Although a typical hill species it is some-
times found on the plain, at least near the hills, and will
make a good growth if given the chance. Its chief
characteristics are the arrangement of the seed pockets,
a few together in a flattened tassel or fan-like cluster,
mounted on a slender (two centimeter or so) "pedicel."
The upper edge of this fan bristles with long, angled awns.

The third group consists of plants in which the seed
pockets are so spaced about as to give the plant a shaggy
or tufted appearance entirely lacking the definite head
and orderly arrangement of the preceding. The first
three of these have the seed pockets without awns or
bristles, the last two with.
Eragrostis minor can be easily recognized because
the seed-pockets are arranged in tiny, flattened cones
whose scales (the seeds) are lined up in two rows. These
little seed cones are mounted on very fine, hairlike
rhachises which are arranged alternately along the main
rhachis so that the many cones (100-150 or even more)
form a panicled head. Thus the plant combines neat
solidarity with fineness and airiness. It is a common,
annual weed of the plowed fields.
Eragrostis megastachya, less common, is very similar
but has no silky hairs on the base of the leaf, though the
tongue of the leaf (behind the culm) has them, and the
cones are about three millimeters broad, as well as being
generally bigger.
Eragrostis pilosa, found chiefly in the arroyos or
dry stream beds, has these cones much smaller, unflat-
tened, ragged, and arranged in a closer, more broom-like
head.
Aristida adscensionis forms broom-like growths on
the graves, and on newly opened earth. It can be easily
recognized by its long, slender seeds crowned by three,
longer, stiff awns which are always ready to stick into
ones clothes, if the sharp pointed seed does not.
Stipa bungeana is the most graceful and finest of the
grasses of the plain when in flower-fruit. The leaves are
short and when dry not only curve about but fold up
lengthwise to form a slender trough. The clums stand
up far above the meagre leaf cluster bearing along half its
length a sprinkling of seed pockets whose single awn is
so long and curved (besides being twice, angularly bent)
as to give the heads a very soft and graceful air as they
hang over with their weight of awns, these awns usually
eight centimeters long (the seeds but five millimeters)
being sixteen times longer than their seeds! One may
well say that this species has mostly gone to whiskers.

Cyperus rotundus is a plant that might be mistaken
for a grass. Thanks to its triangled (three sided)
"culms" it is easily recognized as a sedge. As it is a
common cultivated field weed (reaching a height of four
to six decimeters) it represents an exception to the usual
habitat of the sedges. The seed pockets are arranged
along fingers much as in maize but on a much smaller
scale and without the central axis. But most different,
many tassels (or panicles) spring from a common point,
making a sort of double panicle. At this point of union,
as in most sedges, there are (one to) several leaf blades.
Having a root system which sends out long, plant-forming
runners, it is as much of a pest and even more difficult to
eradicate (because of the fineness and darkness of these
runners) than is Imperata cylindrica (above).
The other two species of sedge (Carex) have already
been described as spring plants. This therefore completes
the grasses and sedges, the most graceful, the daintiest
of the flowering plants.
Composites.
Another fairly large and easily recognizable group is
that of the composites, which have aster-like flowers.
These divide into three types or subgroups: (1) those with
a fringe of coloured petals as in the asters; (2) those
without petals at all; (3) those in which the whole flower
is made up of petals as the dandelion.
Bidens pilosa bipinnata, better known as beggar ticks
or Spanish needles because of the way their long, stiff
seeds terminated by two or three barbed prongs or spines,
attach themselves and stick to one's clothes, has aster-like
flowers with but two to our or five, short, yellow petals.
The leaves are compound and neatly cut up (see this
Journal, Vol. LIX, p. 201, figs. 7-8). The plant forms
a stiff, almost bushy, annual growth about two feet high
in waste places, especially on stone dumps.
Inula britannica may not be a plant of the plains but
is a fairly common perennial on terraces between fields
south of Tsinan, and last year appeared in my garden.
It also has an aster-like flower of yellow petals but unlike
Aster hispidus, it forms stiff, upright stems with elongate-
lanceolate, pubescent to silky haired leaves averaging
seventeen millimeters wide and nine centimeters long.

Erigeron canadensis is a tall, columnar plant growing
in waste places, especially abandoned fields. The single,
tall stalk is lined all around with lanceolate leaves, except
for its upper third or fourth which is strewn with the
tiny, apparently petal-less, flowers, arranged in panicles.
For all its height, often two meters, it is an annual.
Artemisia scoparia is another bearer of petal-less
flowers which are even smaller than those of the preceding.
The plant is low and very branching with the twigs tough
and quite reddish, verging on mahogany red. The leaves
have already been described in this Journal (Vol. LIX,
p. 208, figs. 14-21). Very common in Arbor vitae groves,
on terraces and undisturbed land.
Artemisia annua has slightly larger flowers, a much
greater stature (to one and a half or even two meters),
and well developed, crysanthemum-like but very thin,
bright green leaves. Its outstanding characteristic, how-
ever, is its fragrance or pungent aroma, especially notice-
able on being bruised or crushed in the hand. An annual
of waste lands, it may also be found on the north side of
buildings and in out-of-the-way corners. These last two
species may be responsible for hay fever in some persons.
Trailing and "Climbing.”
A group which is easily recognized but has no
botanical or racial affinities, are those which are so flabby
stemmed as to lie about over the ground or, by propinquity,
grow (usually but erroneously referred to as climbing)
over erect objects. Of these, the first three have harsh
stems and leaves while the remainder are smooth and even
hairless.
Humulus japonicus or wild hops, has harsh, palmately
cut, five pointed-lobed, coarsely toothed leaves with long

Rubia cordifolia is a brittle plant growing in shady
places, usually under other plants. The stem is four-
angled, and these edges as also the leaf edges are lined
with barbs or minute prickles all turned downward so that
it can be rubbed or stroked one way only. Its chief
characteristic is that its leaves, which are elongately
heartshaped and fairly long stalked, are arranged in
whorls along the stems. The flowers are very small, usu-
ally five pointed starlets arranged in axillary cymes. Late
in the fall, green and later red berries enclose the black,
somewhat depressed, two-ridged, shagreen-surfaced seeds.
Trichosanthes kirilowii is the third harsh (though
much smoother than the preceding), trailing plant. It is
the wild melon of this area. The leaves are very variable
in shape (see figs. 1 and 2), with a hair-spring, three-
branched tendril on the opposite side. From the axils of
the leaves spring the long petioled flowers, a male and a
female, side by side. The female is single and leafless,
the males grouped two or three (or more) together, each
with his covering leaf. The female is slender and yellow,
sitting in a long gown (the calyx-pedicel), with very fuzzy
(fimbriate) petal ends. The males are much smaller,
stouter, green, with unexposed petals. The fruit is early
gathered by the early risers (ovoid to ovoid-oblong, 9-10
em. long by 5-7 cm. in diameter).
Cynanchum chinense is a trailing milk-weed, as any
one plucking its elegantly formed, heart-shaped, whitish-
green leaves will find. It bleeds milk at every fracture.
The pods are very slender (to ten centimeters long), and
usually three or four together, spreading widely from each
other. Occasionally on terraces and graves.
Columella japonica is at once recognizable by its
pedate leaves (fig. 3), duplicated by no other plant of
this plain. As a herbaceous grape, it bears on its axillary,
stout, stiff peduncle, a flattened cluster of small, green,
later blackish berries. Frequently bifurcate tendrils
spring from opposite the leaves. It may be found at the

Strikingly Differentiated.
The plants in this group have no one outstanding
characteristic in common, but each has its own striking
character by which it may be differentiated from any other
within this group or the following group.
Salsola kali is at once recognizable because all its
leaves are reduced to awl-like or scale-like points, making
the deviously curved but roundishly compacted stalks very
harsh, even prickly to the touch. If it has flowers, one
would never guess it, so inconspicuous are they-and their
fruit. It is common along roadsides and waste places.
Portulaca oleracea is easily identified by its very thick,
juicy, fleshy stems and leaves. Its pale yellow, five petaled
flowers give place to a lozenge-shaped capsule which breaks
open about its waist, doffing its upper, conical half to
reveal its jet black, shining seeds. The leaves are obovate,
fat and perfectly simple. Springing anew after each
downpour from seeds of the previous seeding, it may
easily be encountered in fields and gardens on bare earth,
as a lace-like, somewhat ruddy mat.
Cynanchum sibiricum is conspicuous only in late
October, especially after it has lost some of its leaves
because of the large pods. If one is broken open it is
found to be packed with sticky glistening material while
the edges of the pod wall also exude milk. It is a milk-
weed, like one of the above trailers, but it forms a bushy

Galium verum is at once recognizable because of its
very harsh leaves which are arranged in whorls along the
stems. These leaves, moreover, are very slender, with a

thick and longitudinally ribbed. It forms a stiff, bushy

foot of the hills, but also occurs on the plain.
Xanthium strumarium is immediately recognizable by
its green, oval burrs (12-15 mm. long), which grow in
clusters about the axils of the leaves and branch tips. The
harshly pubescent leaves are heart-shaped but with the
edges very much cut up by shallow lobes and pointed teeth.
The stiff bush-like plant with rather large leaves for this
region, may be found erecting its two to three feet of
coarseness along roadsides, at dumps, in waste nooks and
corners frequented by man.
Solanum nigrum is recognizable by its white, star-like

growing sparsely in shady places, usually on the north side
of houses, walls, and such sun shade.

Unattractives.
The remaining plants may be grouped as unattractive
because they lack showy flowers or parts and are often
coarse, common, wayside weeds. By their fruits we shall
know them. The first three bear their fruit in conspicu-
ous, terminal, leafless panicles; the first four have broad,
the last two slender leaves.
Rumex obtusifolius has its brown triangular seeds
hanging quite separately and independently on nodding
pedicels longer than the seed with its three, broad, fringed,
clasping, leaf-like "petals." A closer examination will
show these peculiar leaf-like "petals" to be coarsely
reticulate-veined and to have, when green, a short, stout,
reddish midrib. Later the whole turns brown and is then
known to children as coffee-grounds. The leaves are two
to three inches long, shaped very much like a human
tongue. Found in waste places, especially sheltered
corners; not common; perennial.
Amaranthus retroflexus has its shiny black, elongate
seeds set in a crown of five, tiny petals and protected by
(two to) three, slender, sharply pointed bracts which make
the whole panicle quite rough and harsh to the touch.
The rough leaves are angularly oval, somewhat lozenge-
like, with a blunt tip and protruding midrib, and a base
which runs gradually down onto the long, pubescent
petiole. Waysides, waste land, dumps, very common;
annual.
Chenopodium opulifolium has its dull black, flattened
seeds set in a recovered, flattened cup, with no armament
whatever, so that the slender panicle is quite smooth and

strap-shaped leaf. The whole plant forms a dense, oval
mass with copious, stiff, erect branches. For all its
bushy appearance it is an annual of waste places, possibly

Polygonum aviculare is a prostrate to semi-erect
annual of the arroyo bottoms. Its oval leaves, rarely two
centimeters long hereabouts, are scattered alternately
along the tough stems, each with a cluster of two or three,
tiny, red tipped flowers, encompassed in a two-parted,
lacerated, brownish stipule. The stems are roundish and
striate, but the young tips are squarish, branching at wide
angles with each other.
Omissions.
The following should have been included in the spring
flora for early May.
Echinospermum lappula anisacanthum is a forget-
me-not flowered annual of waste places, whose pale and
inconspicuous flowers give rise to rugose little nutlets
longitudinally lined with minute, almost soft spines,
terminated by two recurved barbs. These spinules are

Fall Recrudescence.
An interesting phenomenon of the fall flora, is that
as the temperatures approach those of the spring, certain
flowers which bloomed in the springtime at a certain
temperature, bloom again in the fall, when their favourite
temperature is reached. Thus a part of the spring flora
reappears, but in reversed order. For instance on
November 21st the following were in bloom: Astragalus
scaberrimus, Oxytropis bicolor, Erodium stephanianum,
Viola prionantha, Lactuca denticulata, Euphorbia lunulata,
Taraxacum, Aster altaicus; November 14th, Potentilla
flagellaris, Lactuca versicolor; November 30th, in addition
to most of the above, Gueldenstaedtia multiflora.
