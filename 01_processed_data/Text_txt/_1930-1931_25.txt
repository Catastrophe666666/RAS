THE WESTERN TRANSCRIPTION OF
CHINESE
By CHARLES S. GARDNER
The adoption of a single unified system of English
transcription of Chinese sounds for use by all writers
save professional phoneticians offers such manifold and
obvious advantages as scarcely to justify re-statement.
The principles upon which the necessary agreement
among sinologists might be obtained do, however, require
careful consideration.
Is agreement upon the Wade system as it stands an
attainable objective? It embodies brief clear forms of
international character, more wisely and consistently
combined than in any other of the numerous and often
rather grotesque efforts of the period of early experi-
ment. It has received widespread recognition for its
genuine merits, and has accordingly been more often
employed than any other in books of reference.
Notable as were the advantages of "Wade" its
defects are no less serious. From the standpoint of most
Chinese speakers it is incomplete at one important point:
by representing as ch- ch'-, and hs- (before -i and -ü)
three mongrel initials of recent local Pekingese dialect,
it makes impossible differentiation of their proper com-
ponents (j- vs. dz-, ch- vs. ts-, hs- vs. s-) of which the
identity has been preserved distinct in most of the vast
area where some "Mandarin" dialect is spoken, and even
in Peking before -a, -e, -o, and -u. Again, while the
Wade system enables the student initiated into its
mysteries to read with assurance the other sounds
transcribed, its assumed pronunciations are so arbitrary
as to render well-nigh hopeless any attempt to read it with-
out special instruction. More than this, the frequent use
of the apostrophe-to say nothing of a peculiar diacritical

fusion of the skilled proof-reader and of the sinologist.
Finally, despite the wide vogue of the Wade system,
it has so far failed of obtaining universal acceptance that
many of the most careful and conscientious scholars in
all countries resolutely avoid it. It seems no exaggera-
tion to say that the Wade system has been tried for
seventy years and has been found wanting. Let us then
forthright recognize that in continuing the old propa-
ganda for "Wade" we are struggling for an object
impossible of realization; and let us instead commence an
organized search for the bases of desirable modification.
It has been suggested that a system adapted to every
use other than phonetic dissertation is desirable. It may
assist in the solution of very real difficulties to divide
them, and to consider first what may be the essential
requirements of the professed sinologist. These may be
truthfully compressed into a brief and simple formula:
consistency in completeness.
The need for completeness requires no argument.
It is obvious that no important element in the standard
Chinese "Mandarin" syllabary- whatever phonetic
variations local pronunciation may give it-can properly
be ignored. The Wade system is complete for Pekingese,
but for Pekingese only; and therefore requires supple-
ment of the three forms dz-, ts-, and s- before -i and -ü,
as already suggested, to be fit for general use. On the
other hand, there is no reason for multiplying transcrip-
tions of a single standard syllable to meet local variations
in pronunciation. For example, if in some parts of the
lower Long River valley and of Sz Chuan an initial nasal
is prefixed to certain words, such variation is regular
and requires no written expression; just as, were it
instead represented by ng-, the symbol could as regularly
be ignored as superfluous by the Pekingese.
Consistency clearly demands that aspiration be
uniformly represented, whether by insertion of an
apostrophe, a diacritical mark, or supplementary letter
(h); or by variation of initial consonants. Only con-
fusion can result from failure to apply some one of these
methods consistently. Each letter employed should,
moreover, have a single constant value in a given
position, whether initial or final, although that value may
properly change with change of position. Thus initial
h- cannot advantageously be employed to represent

The professed sinologist, who knows the pronuncia-
tion of the whole Chinese syllabary independently of all
transcription, can use upon necessity any system which
embodies this minimum formula, consistency in com-
pleteness, however bizarre may be the particular
symbols selected: most scholars of the present day are
familiar with at least three systems. This does not,
however, by any means imply that all are equally
convenient. Convenience for the scholar as for others
depends in large measure upon intelligent application of
two further principles: simplicity, and avoidance of
forms liable to confusion.
The serious student of Chinese, as of any other
language, can never hope to acquire correct pronunciation
by reading: oral instruction is for him evidently
essential. But the public is not open to instruction of
any kind, and it becomes accordingly desirable to repre-
sent the Chinese sounds by letters which as nearly as
possible suggest them directly. Employment of elaborate
combinations of vowels or consonants, of strange or
inverted letters, of diacritical marks wholly unknown or
but partly understood, only serves to repel the general
reader and to exaggerate the already too prevalent
misapprehension that Chinese is difficult to pronounce.
Strange letters and diacritical signs are
absolutely ruled out by their absence from most fonts of
type in Britain, America, and the Far East.

plus the equally serious ones that it is even more liable
to mistake than the apostrophe, and that it does not

It has been often stated that any acceptable system
must be "international" in character. This proposition
demands careful analysis. Is our aim to be a sort of
Chinese Esperanto which can really satisfy no one?
Must we discard all letters of the alphabet which are
differently pronounced by different nations? Obviously
this is impossible, unless we be prepared to create a
wholly new alphabet.
There are in the world to-day seven conspicuously
important groups of sinologists: the French, Russians,
Germans, Britons, Americans, Japanese, and Chinese
(other important but smaller groups-Swedes, Dutch,
etc.—are linguistically versatile). The French have
consistently employed for the past three decades a
national system of their own; and if they in time
adopt any other, it will be because they recognize
the advantages of international coöperation (as now
with Japanese and Sanskrit), not because an inter-
national system is more convenient for French speakers.
To the Russians it is matter of little consequence what
symbols the other western nations agree to adopt;
for the benefit of Russian readers they employ different
ones in any event. The interests of the Germans are
closely identified with our own, and those of the
British and Americans are identical. The great major-
ity of Japanese and Chinese who employ the western
alphabet owe their principal acquaintance with it to
the English language: their interests too are identical
with ours.
Thus, to re-capitulate briefly: four of the seven
present groups of sinologists are English-speaking
groups; the fifth, the German, has few divergent

As guiding principles for modification of the Wade
system we have then only four: completeness, consis-
tency, simplicity, and avoidance of confusion. In
seeking to apply them it is important to remember that
numerous reference works (however few in proportion
to those of future generations) have employed that
system, and that their use should not be hampered more
than necessary by any radical changes.
It has already been suggested that the use of the
apostrophe or of a diacritical sign of any sort to mark
aspiration, while acceptable upon phonetic grounds, is
not satisfactory for typographical reasons. Insertion of
an h, the recognized sign of aspiration in transcription
of Sanskrit, is another phonetically satisfactory solution
which is practically disadvantageous: chhi, chha, and
chhu are clumsy forms; and if tchi, tcha, and tchu be
substituted, their inaspirate counter-parts tci, ica, and
tcu appear still more barbarous.
In many ways the most satisfactory method of
separating inaspirates from aspirate is the simple and
direct one of using distinctive initial consonants:
inaspirate b-, d-, g-, j-, and dz- conveniently balance
aspirate p-, t-, k-, ch-, and ts-. Complete elimination of
uncertainty which these alternates afford is of equal
advantage to layman and to scholar. To the layman,
also, the forms suggested approach more closely to
actual pronunciation than do those of the Wade system.

Those phoneticians who complain that b-, d-, g-, j-, and

are voiceless or surd, prefer not to dwell upon the fact
that in English simple p-, t-, k-, ch-, and ts- are usually
aspirate. To students it is surely as easy to teach that
the former Chinese sounds should be pronounced without
"voice" as that Wade's series of aspirates are presumed
to be without aspiration. For the professional phoneti-
cian likewise, details of any system for general use are
relatively inconsequential: for his own research and
publication he must use another in any event.
If any
modifications are to be adopted, let them give help where
help is most greatly needed.
Wade adopted initial j- as a symbol for what, to the
English ear, is rather an initial r-. In so doing, he
adopted the French approach to one of the few difficult
sounds of the language, hoping thus to win over the
French to his system of romanization-without, however,
any success whatever, since the French are the one
people who have not made any use of it. Under the
circumstances, and in view of the obvious fact that we
cannot label the j- as "French" for the benefit of general
readers, it seems sensible to avail ourselves of the
nearest equivalent possible and to reinstate r- in its
natural place. It should be noted that by so doing a
fruitful source of confusion is eliminated, since failure
to recognize Wade's j- as something other than it seems
results in pronunciation of a common, but totally
different series of words. Those formed from initial r-,
on the other hand, are not liable to any confusion
whatever.
Completion of the standard Chinese syllabary
requires, as already stated, recognition of the various
sounds j- and dz-, ch- and ts-, hs- and s- before
-i and -ü, in consistency with the first two pairs
recognized by Wade (in conformity to present Peking
dialect) before the other vowels. Because dictionaries
based on the Wade system do not indicate these proper
distinctions before -i and -ü, students whose training is
confined to Peking and who rely solely on English
dictionaries will at the outset find it difficult to maintain
them. It should not, however, be very difficult to
re-arrange any standard dictionary to represent them.
Meanwhile no serious confusion results from continuing
to reflect their Pekingese fusion as j-, ch-, and hs-

There are in Chinese eight rather widely different
vowel sounds which require clear-cut differentiation.
For a, e, i, o, u we must, with Wade, adopt the Italian
values for the simple reason that in English each one
varies widely. The French u, again with him, we can
conveniently represent in its German guise as ü, a
symbol which is widely recogized, and which fortunately
exists in many fonts of type. Substitution of w or v
results in uncouth forms which would require explana-
tion, and which must cause particular and needless
difficulties for German readers.
It has been suggested that neither Wade's -ih nor -ï
is a satisfactory transcription for the supradental vowel
which in Peking has the value of ir in English "shirt.”
The former is distinctive in print, but suggests to the
tongue no sound other than simple -i. It seems that the
best and simplest solution is to substitute Pekingese -ir
for -ih: the former may be as grossly mispronounced as
the latter, but unlike it, can lead to no confusion
whatever, a consideration of utmost importance even for
those areas in China where -ir is less suggestive of local
dialect than -ih. Unlike -ih moreover, it does not appear
an insuperable obstacle to the uninitiated: the veriest
tyro can be sure of being understood by other westerners
at least, a consideration of manifest importance to the
Chinese government since it favours public discussion and
formation of intelligent western opinion upon Chinese
affairs.
The last of the Chinese vowels, and the only one
which offers serious difficulty to us foreigners, is
pronounced with the tongue in almost the same position
as the foregoing one. The sound produced is merely a
sonant -z, a sort of buzz terminating in the closing of
the throat with a faint vocalic sound not identified with
any one of our recognized "five vowels," but rather a
cross between a and u. Various have been the attempts
to circumvent this nuisance, to represent the final vocalic
resonance with an ï, or with a u doctored with cryptic
signs or with extra consonants: but none has been
satisfactory. Not even consistency has been maintained
in representing within a single system the three forms

Serious discrepancy in actual pronunciation between
the three great branches of the “Mandarin” dialect
exists at only a single point. The initial unvoiced
palatal j- (e.g., Jiang, river) of Peking is in Nanking a
gutteral g- (giang) only before -i and -ü. Conversely,
the gutteral g- of Nanking is in Peking an unvoiced
palatal j- only before these same vowels. No matter
whether j- or g- be employed, distinction must be made one
place or the other between palatal and gutteral position.
Inasmuch as the Wade system has made forms based on
the Peking dialect familiar, it seems on the whole the part
of prudence to proceed conservatively, and to defer
abandonment of j- for g- until such time as there may be
further proof that that dialect is no longer standard.
It would manifestly be ridiculous to suggest that
because the great seal characters marked a striking
advance over any medium of communication the Chinese
had hitherto known, they ought never to have proceeded
to evolve their modern script. Is it less ridiculous to
suggest that because the Wade system and Giles'
modifications of it marked a great advance over earlier
methods of alphabetic transcription, we should be
content to receive it, and recognizing its imperfections
to pass it on without attempt at improvement to
the numberless generations to come? Shall we permit
a system which has received but grudging adherence
over a paltry seventy years to cast a permanent shadow
over the limitless future? Or if we find the road to a
partial improvement, shall we hesitate and invite others
to precede us, lest agreement and perfection be not
instantly attained?
