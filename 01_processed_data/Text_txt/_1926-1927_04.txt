A LEAGUE OF NATIONS IN
ANCIENT CHINA
By DR. EVAN MORGAN
Dr. Herbert A. Giles, professor of Chinese in Cam-
bridge University, has disturbed the minds of those who
thought that Europe was the first to attempt the creation
of such a beneficent institution as the League of Nations.
In a recent letter to the Press he informed the public that
China tried to create such a league about 546 B.C. To be
quite correct, the league that the princes attempted to
create in China was a League of States rather than a
League of Nations. For this reason, that a nation is a
distinct race or people having common descent, language,
etc.: whereas in China the league was formed of the same
race and people having the same language, descent, history
and so on. Therefore, it would be more correct to call it a
League of States. It was a league constituted of different
parts of the same empire. Europeans may, therefore,
still feel that the honor of creating a League of Nations
belongs to them. They are attempting a much more
difficult task in creating a league of nations, made up of
peoples with different languages, diverse traditions and
histories, than it would be by bringing together different
principalities of the same country.
Readers of Chinese history know that at many periods
in its history, the country was divided into several states
each owning allegiance to one overlord the sovereign of the
empire. The allegiance was generally a fiction and the
tributary often flouted the suzerain power. The alleg-
iance was more often than not nominal: the real power
was in the hands of some vassal. There were constant
and sanguinary wars between the vassals as each strug-
gled for supremacy. It is the memory of these perpetual
wars between one state and another that creats a feeling
of pessimism concerning the future peace of this country.
Its history is not encouraging. To those unacquainted with
the ancient history of the country, the present condition
