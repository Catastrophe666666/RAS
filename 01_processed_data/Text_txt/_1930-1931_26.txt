CONFUCIANISM, DEMOCRACY
AND NATIONALISM
By YEN-YING LU, M.A.
In these days of changes in China the teachings of
Confucius may well appear to be relics of the conserva-
tive past.
Time was when those teachings held a
supreme sway in controlling Chinese thought, but
China has since progressed a far cry towards moderni-
zation. New things have come to stay. The "Three
Principles" of Dr Sun Yat-sen are in the ascendant.
His memory commands the weekly memorial service,
whereas that of Confucius seems to have been relegated
to limbo.
It is, indeed, the belief of many that Confucianism
has been an embodiment of reactionary tendencies. With
its indorsement of antiquity, ritual and ancestor worship,
it has not only identified itself with a stationary society,
but helped to reinforce an omnipotent autocracy.
But, after all, time has dissolved the Confucian
tradition. From those outworn beliefs and practices all
that is best in Confucianism has been freed at last. It
may be advanced that its sane and wholesome teachings
in their purity may yet deserve a place of useful service.
Already we can discover therefrom kernels of vital truth
and wisdom, and we would be all the richer for their
possession. Highly significant of this is the exhortation
of Dr. Sun Yat-sen, urging the Chinese people to continue
the cultivation of their old virtues, such as loyalty, filial
piety, truthfulness, righteousness, benevolence, fraternity
and pacifism. Needless to say, all these are attributable
to Confucianism.
And more than that. Some of the Confucian teach-
ings present strikingly modern features. Setting aside
Confucius' pragmatic attitude in regard to truth, his
belief in progress and adaptability to circumstances, it is

the purpose of this essay to concentrate attention upon
two aspects of his teachings: Democracy and Nationalism.
I.
An attempt to reconstruct the ethical and socio-
political ideas of Confucius may bring out their demo-
cratic tendencies. Taking as their basis his noble, whole-
hearted humanity and devotion to mankind, those tenden-
cies are cosmopolitan. For we learn from his "Analects"
that he loved and honoured all men, and that he valued
human life above all things.
Besides, Confucius recognized in the individual an
unsubduable will and volition to control his own destiny.
Yet man has his duties as well as his freedom. Individual
freedom of action is in good order only in so far as it is
compatible with the general good and what Confucius
called the "harmony" of society.
The estimating of the worth of man should be inde-
pendent of his outward circumstances. His social status
or material wealth should neither reflect upon his
character, nor be a bar to his aspirations. For poverty
cannot debase, nor can riches elevate, a person; riches
and honours as acquired by unrighteous means are in-
defensible and untenable. A man of virtue should help
the distressed, but should not increase the wealth of the
rich. There is no question that Confucius believed in the
securing of equal opportunities and rights for all. His
teaching career was marked by his earnest plea for
education without class distinction.
As regards the political ideas of Confucius, they are
at once moral and democratic. First of all, government
is to be instituted on a moral basis. True, Confucius
envisaged in his political outlook a kind of patriarchal
monarchy, but that was incident to his own time and
should not discredit his system in general. Moreover,
such government is to be limited not only by moral
principles but by considerations for the welfare of the
people. The latter should be the ultimate test of a
government. In the last analysis, its existence depends
upon the confidence of the people.
A good government ought to be primarily concerned
with the morals of the people rather than with their
subsistence. Next to this in importance comes the pro-
moting of their welfare rather than the maintenance of
armaments. The national happiness, as well as strength,

lies in making the people contented with ample and
permanent means of livelihood. The best way to realize
this is an equitable distribution, rather than an accumu-
lation, of wealth among the people. Yet the duty of
government should be just to carry out certain funda-
mental principles for the maintenance of order and the
correction of morals, without interfering with what
concerns the people themselves.
The selection of persons for government services
should be impartial, yet judicious. It is to be made only
by the test of virtue and talent. Persons of action, with
independent yet broad views, are preferable to talkers
and zealots. Yet in his political vision Confucius
cherished the cultivation of a fellowship and association
of like minds among the good and virtuous for the
furtherance of their principles. He thus intimated
centuries ago what a political party ought to be.
As to participation in government by the people,
Confucius was cautious and reticent. Under the circum-
stances of his own time, he naturally had misgivings
about their ability and judgment to govern themselves.
For he was well aware of what they can, and what they
cannot, attain to. His well-considered view was that the
general public can be made to follow a course of action,
but hardly to understand it. Therefore, unless their
characteristics were thoroughly known, their "likes and
dislikes" should be admitted with all reserve, for these
can furnish no unerring criterion for the choice of
officials or the advocacy of a governmental policy. But
Confucius had an insight of the value of educating the
people, and he considered this, even in his own time, the
most important thing. He predicted, for example, the
possibility of preventing litigations and abolishing capital
punishment, when the people are well educated.
It is superfluous to attempt here an appraisal of
these Confucian teachings as set forth in the foregoing.
There is no question that they have been for centuries
important factors in infusing the democratic spirit into a
paternal monarchy. Owing to their influence, absolutism
has been tempered with justice and checked by the
code of "government for the people." Indeed, they have
contributed to make Chinese social democracy what it
has been. Even nowadays, if we view these teachings
in the light of the political trends in different countries,
they may yet serve to exert their salutary influence in
the development of democracy.

II.
As to whether Confucius was a nationalist or an
internationalist, there seems to be a difference of opinions.
But a balanced view of this may be attempted, if
Confucius's own environment and life experience are
taken into account.
Throughout his life, Confucius saw China divided
into diverse kingdoms and the people were attached to a
narrow provincialism. What he strove after was to
make China unified into one nation in which one ideal
government and culture were to be in force. For we
find him constantly speaking of his own aim as being
to "govern a state well first and then to order and pacify
the universe next." Now what is the universe in his
mind? Owing to the limited and imperfect geographical
knowledge of his time, we may naturally venture the
opinion that he actually referred to the whole, or even
just a part, of China as the universe then. For China
was about all the world then known to him, and we learn
I that his outlook and interests were confined therein.
Other countries were beyond his mental horizon, except
some barbarian tribes on the borders of China, towards
which he sometimes even expressed contempt. Under
those conditions, therefore, what Confucius aimed at may
well appear to us at the present time to be nationalism,
in contradistinction to the localism that was current in
his time.
But the Confucian nationalism would be neither
chauvinistic nor imperialistic, and, viewed from another
› angle, it presents an international aspect. If we
remember that the warring kingdoms of Confucius's
time were actually as separate countries, we may be
warranted to interpret some of his concepts as having
been intended to apply internationally. For example,
Confucius advocated indulgent treatment of "foreigners."
He also enjoined liberal and righteous dealings of states
with one another. States in need or distress are to be
assisted, extinguished nationalities revived. Barbarians
should be civilized by the influence of virtue, but not by
force. Now, if Confucius predicated these principles
about the relations of those states in his own times, then
by analogy they may be applied with equal cogency to
international relations of the present day. For in them-
selves they are truths of no particular period and
transcend national boundaries the world over.

Thus nationalism and internationalism are two
aspects of Confucianism at its best. They are not
mutually exclusive, and the one is the mainstay and the
preparatory stage of the other. The two together mark
the course of progress which a sound nation is to follow.
Firstly, we have a nation strong in peace and sane in
ideals, and then it must develop its international outlook
and interests by promoting universal peace and the
"brotherhood of man."
