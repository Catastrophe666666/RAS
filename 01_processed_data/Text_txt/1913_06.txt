Perhaps there is no part of Mongol history less clearly
elucidated than the period 1368-1644; that is, the space of time
during which the native Chinese dynasty of Ming (明​) ruled over
the "Eighteen Provinces”(十八​省​) (then numbering fifteen) of
China proper, leaving the nominal vassal states of Japan, Corea,
Manchuria, Mongolia, Turkestan, Tibet, India, Burma, Indo-
China, and even Formosa and Loochoo, to look after themselves.
What happened in Mongolia during these 275 years, after the
Genghizides 元朝 ​had disappeared, and before the Manchus took
their place at Peking? Our chief authority for reply is the
retrospective history of the Ming dynasty, chapters 327, 328, and
some further casual allusions to Tartar events scattered through
the chronicles of the Emperors, chapters 1 to 24.
The Manchu Emperor ordered the compilation of the Ming
dynasty history in 1679, and it was completed about fifty years
later. It opens the account of the Genghizide Mongols (whom
the Ming dynasty drove completely out of China) by calling them
Tata 韃靼​, a very old word for a tribe of Turks in the Kerulon
valley 臚​朐​河​or 克魯倫河​;this word appears in every dynastic
history beginning with A.D. 880, and always in connection with
Turk or Turkoid tribes between the River Onon and the River
Kerulon号​諾​or斡​難​河​.
The last Mongol Emperor, Toghon Timur 安​懂​帖木兒​, fled
from Peking to Mongolia in the summer of 1367: the new Ming
dynasty begins officially with the year 1368, and during the
interval Toghon Timur did his best to harry the Chinese
frontiers: his headquarters were at the old shang-tu or "upper
capital" of Kublai Khan 忽必烈​,whose hunting expeditions
there have been so graphically described by Marco Polo: it is
situated a little to the north-west of Dolonor 多倫諾爾 ​on the
northern bend of the River Lwan (Lat. 42) N., Long. 116 Ε.)
which passes Jehol 熱河​兒 ​and enters the sea to the south-west
of the new treaty-port of Ts'in-wang-tao 秦王​島​. This
"Xanadu" of the poets was officially known as K'ai-pʻing Fu
開平​:in 1369 the victorious Ming troops took this place,
together with several highly important Mongol prisoners. Toghun
Timur himself moved further north to Dalai Nor (Lat. 43,

almost nothing of the Western Mongols, but along the line of
the great wall of what we now call Chih Li province, and outside
of it, were now definitely established the three wei (三​衛​) or
military marches: so far as we can gather, these were as follows:
(1) the To-yen military district roughly corresponded with Jêhol,
and extended from the Hi-Fèng K'ou (喜峯口​) Pass to Süan-hwa
宣化 ​Fu and Kalgan 張家口​; (2) the Tai-ning district
began with the territory of Kin-chou 錦 ​and I-chou 義​, and
extended to the Liao River ; (3) the Fuh-yü福​餘 ​district began
with the Hwang-ni Wa (黃​泥窪 ​= yellow mud flats) embracing
Mukden, T'ieh-ling, and K'ai-yüan. The last of the three names
comes from old Corea朝鮮​, which once included what we now
call South Manchuria, a misnomer, for the Manchus only descended
into it 270 years ago when they marched south from their native
haunts to the conquest of China. At the time of which we are
treating these three semi-independent hybrid-Mongol districts
were together placed by the founder of the Ming dynasty under
one of his sons 惠​寧王 ​called the tu-sz都​司 ​or military
proconsul of To-ning 大​寧​; but the real Mongols, or Tata, from
time to time swept over the place, which thus did double "buffer"
service between the Tata and the Chinese. Later on two other
imperial princes, those of Tsin (Shan Si) and Yen 燕 ​(Peking),
were created semi-independent satraps in the north of China,
having each the duty of inspecting Uliangha兀​夏哈​,for so the
three Wei were called as a whole-alternately each year. The
Emperor's own capital was at Nanking: the Prince of Yen
(afterwards himself emperor) was the Emperor's fourth son, and
Peking was the metropolis of his satrapy: when he became
Emperor, Peking became the capital of China.

name of Ussakhal, and describes him as the brother of Aiyushlitala.
