Neoliodes concentricus (Say) 1821
The data entered under this species designation in

Neoliodes vermiculatus sp. n.
Figures 1-5, 8–13.
Diagnosis.-Body large, 1.1 to 1.4mm. long, fairly
slender, .7 to .9mm. wide, robust, about .7mm. high;
blackish brown to dull black; cephalothorax broad, short,
with two angular rostral hairs and a scalloped ridge along
antero-lateral edge, pseudostigmatic organs long, clavate,
setulose; abdomen ovate, mammiform behind, girdles
heavily corrugated, anterior area roughened by fine ver-
miculations and tuberosities, the basal girdle with two
clavate setae on each side of posterior knob, anterior rim
of girdle finely corrugated; venter roughened by irregular
vermiculations, genital plate bristles 5/2, and bristles 3
per plate, labium rugose.

Color.-In life: Although this species appears dull
black, under closer examination the girdle has a coppery
bronze lustre and there is a triangular area behind and to
the sides of the pseudostigmata which has a bronze-green
sheen. The anterior area of the girdle seems smooth
under the hand lens. In balsam mounts the color is
opaque to a light reddish brown, the legs being much
lighter.

Form.-The general shape of the body, when viewed
from above (figures 2 & 3) is pyriform with a coarsely
scalloped rim, mammiform behind; rostrum angular,
rectahedral; when viewed from the side (figure 1) it
is roughly that of a parallelogram, roughened above and
behind.
Cephalothorax (figure 2) with a prominent ridge
anterior to pseudostigmata and at posterior edge of
rostrum which is formed of a mass of fine ridges each
side of median line; anterior edge bordered by a thin
scalloped and more or less corrugated keel which is
interrupted anteriorly by point of rostrum; rostrum with
several transverse, parallel ridges; pseudostigmata large,
irregularly ovate, more or less crenulate or rough edged,
opercle finely vermiculate striate, a finely tuberculate
area between them, often with a more or less clearly
defined undulating ridge anterior to this tuberculate area.
Anterior to this ridge is a broad groove or trough running
down between the pair of vermiculate areas. Rostral
hairs heavy, angularly bent back toward each other;
other hairs absent; pseudostigmatic organs (figure 4)
long, reaching well beyond rim of rostrum, clavate, un-
dulated (or double) at tip, caused by a groove running
down the superior face of the organ. This grooved area
is armed with short, blunt, crowded setae, often giving
this part of the organ a fimbriated appearance (figure 5),
remainder of organ relatively smooth, with crenulate
longitudinal lines, tip more dense and darker. Under
ordinary magnification in an examination of whole
mounts, the organs appear to be thickly covered with
fine hairs (as indicated in figures 2 & 4).
Abdomen (figure 3) above: covered by the four
notogastral plates of which the fourth usually projects
further posteriorly and the first is most included (figure
1). The band of each plate is strongly corrugated along
the sides, the ridges of the corrugations (about 19-21-23
in number) sharp, double angled at the periphery (see
fourth skin in figures 1 & 2), the hollows rounded. The
angles of the ridges when seen from above give a cordate
appearance to the periphery of each girdle. These
girdles are not the peripheral girdle of a complete skin
but are, in themselves the complete new skin, thus each
skin (after the first) consists, not of a disc or saucer,
or cap-like covering, but of a girdle beginning where the
previous one terminated. The removal of any of the skins
leaves a clear, smooth area. Lower rim of notogastral

Ventral plate (figure 3) vermiculated with irregular
ridges of two classes: strong and weak, the outer rim
formed of finely corrugated ridges running out at right
angles to the periphery. About eight pairs of short stiff
hairs disposed as indicated in figure 3. Genital and anal
covers large, contiguous, the former of two parts con-
nected by a flexible joint. Ridge of genital covers few,
diagonal, directed toward outer ends of flexible joint,
anterior section with five pairs of setae of which the
posterior-most are widely separated from the next pair,
posterior section with two pairs of bristles, well separated
from each other; anal covers with about five undulating,
more or less longitudinal to slightly bowed ridges, three
pairs of bristles, of which the middle and posterior pair
are more widely spaced than are the anterior and middle
pair; thoracic-abdominal suture well marked; cameros-
tomal collar broad, angular.
Camerostome (figure 3) elongate, covered by a large,
full plate which is roughened by fine tubercules and more
or less vermiculated ridges, overhung antero-laterally by
a fine, more or less crenulate, transversely ridged plate.
Palp (figure 13) of five segments, of which the basal one
is short and triangular; the next one is short and broad
with one slender bristle at top near distal edge; ante-
penultimate segment very much smaller, more or less
triangular, with one slender bristle at top near distal
edge; penultimate segment still smaller, longer than
broad, with two slender bristles on dorsal face; ultimate
segment the longest, tapering but with a truncate finger
on dorsal face, bearing one bristle at its tip, another
posterior to it, and at least two bristles near tip of the
segment.
Legs (figure 1 & 2) fairly long, the posterior longest,

reticulated by fine, raised ridges which form between
them polyhedral, sunken, shallow depressions. Ungues

kindness of Professor Nathan Banks, received some of
the types from his own collection, and having carefully

and well marked species. N. concentricus may be cha-

not roughened by one or two strong diagonal ribs but
by many, broad, anastomosing ribs (figure 7). The
American form is, furthermore, considerably larger.

Neoliodes kornhuberi Karpelles ?
Three specimens from Tsingtao taken with the
former are provisionally referred to this species whose
status is extremely uncertain due to the fact that it was
insufficiently described from an imperfect specimen.
Not until fresh material can be secured from the type
locality, Hakodate, Yezzo, Japan, and redescribed, will
it be possible to definitely dispose of this rarer Chinese
species.

Oribotritia n.n.
As the generic name Tritia was used in 1826 by
Risso in his "Histoire Naturelle de l'Europe Meridional,"
vol. 4, p. 172, for a group of Mollusca of the family
Alectrionidae (Nassidae) it is not available for use in
the Oribatoidea. I therefore, suggest the designation
Oribotritia to take its place, using for its type the type
of Tritia of Berlese 1883.
