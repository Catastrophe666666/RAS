THE TEACHING OF MICIUS
By THE REV. F. L. HAWKS POTT, D.D.
The philosopher Moh-Tih (墨翟​) or Moh-Tzŭ(墨子​)
is now generally referred to as Micius, a word recently
coined to correspond with Confucius and Mencius. In
accordance with the usage of other writers, I have
adopted that name, and will refer to the school founded
by him as Mohism, and to his disciples as Mohists.
My reasons for making this study are as follows:
First, there has been a great revival of interest in
his philosophical, ethical and social theories. Many of
you will remember, as I do, when in your study of Chinese
literature, your teacher dismissed the consideration of
Micius with the one word "heretical." You were told
that he did not belong to the orthodox Confucian School,
and therefore was unworthy of attention.
Modern scholars like Hu Shih (胡適​) and Liang
Chi-tsao(梁啟超​) have been pioneers in a movement to
resurrect him from oblivion, and now this teacher who
lived over 2,000 years ago is held up as one of China's
most important philosophers and most original thinkers.
Western sinologues have also turned their attention
to him, and a very extensive study has been made by
A. Focke in his "Mê Ti des Sozialthikers und Seiner
Schüler Philosophische Werke."
Secondly, in looking over the annals of our Society,
I find that my subject has only been treated once, and
that was in a short monograph written many years ago
by the late eminent scholar, Dr. Joseph Edkins.
And thirdly, my own very inadequate research has
convinced me that Micius is well worthy of study, espec-
ially as in many ways his thought is similar to that of
Western philosophers of a much later date.

Micius have been taken from The Development of the Logical
Method in Ancient China by Hu Shih and from A Comparative
Study of Life Ideals by Yu-lan Fung.

As to the man himself, we know him only through
the books that bear his name. Sze-ma Chien(司馬遷​)
the great historian, refers to him very briefly in his
"Historical Records," and he never had a biographer in
the full sense of the word until the twentieth century.
There even seems to be some uncertainty as to the
exact dates of his birth and death. According to Liang
Chi-tsao, he was a native of the State of Lu (魯國​), a
compatriot of Confucius, and was born somewhere
between 468 and 459 в.с., about ten years after the death
of Confucius (478 B.C.); and died between 390 and 382
B.C., about ten years before the birth of Mencius. Thus
chronologically he would come between China's two great
sages. According to Hu Shih, he lived a little earlier
than this, and may have come into very close connection
with the disciples of Confucius.
There is a collection of fifty-three books under the
title of Moh-Tzŭ(墨子​), the best edition available being
the one published with notes and commentaries by Sun
I-jang (孫詒讓​) in 1907. None of these books were
written by Micius himself. Books 8-26 and 28-30 consist
of records written by his early disciples, and give his
essential doctrines. Books 38, 39 and 40 contain his
occasional sayings, reports of his conversations and
anecdotes about him. Books 32-37 are in all likelihood
works of the later Neo-Mohist School.
He lived in the latter part of the Chow (周​) Dynasty
in the period known as that of the Contending States
(戰國​). Originally there were some eight hundred small
vassal principalities, but at the close of the fifth century
B.C. these had been reduced by constant fighting to Seven
Kingdoms and a few buffer states. War was waged among
them until finally the State of Chin (泰國​) gained the
mastery, and a new dynasty was established. Thus it
was a time of great political confusion.
Confucius, living in the latter part of the Chow
Dynasty, was a transmitter and not an originator, and thus
did not aim at abolishing the feudal system but at reform-
ing its abuses, by re-establishing the moral teaching of
the ancients. Micius desired to do away with the old
feudal regime and to set up a new political, social and
economic system. He was ra radical where Confucius and
his School were conservative.

following order:

(1) Method of reasoning. The importance of this
lies in the fact that he was not as subservient to authority
as the teachers of the Confucian School. To Micius a
thing was not necessarily good because it was endorsed
by the ancients.
We are familiar at the present day with the philo-
sophy of Pragmatism. William James, one of the leading
exponents of this School, when asked to explain what was
meant by Pragmatism, replied that it was "the cash value
of ideas." One of the chief tests of truth, according to
Pragmatists, is the practical or working value of an idea.
Now Micius seems to have grasped this thought.
Confucius, like Socrates, taught that the most im-
portant thing was the rectification of names, that is a
clear conception of what was meant by abstract general
terms or universals, especially in the field of ethics, and
he wanted to establish a system of li(禮​), ora code of
ideal relations for regulating individual conduct and
social intercourse.
Micius in true pragmatic fashion held that in regard
to everything we must apply three tests: we must trace
it, examine it, and use it, and thus will discover its real
value.
By tracing it, he meant that we should not disregard
the principle of authority altogether, but must see what
sanction it has from the teaching of ancient philosopher
kings. By examining it, he meant we must see whether
it is in accord with facts or experience. By using it, he
meant putting it to the test to see whether it served a
practical purpose.

All through his teaching he applies this method of
reasoning. Let us take one or two concrete examples.
He is strongly adverse to music. It would seem that
his own aesthetic nature was not highly developed, and
that he had small regard for the fine arts. In regard to
music he argues as follows:
"I asked the Confucians wherefore they should have
music, and they answered: 'Music is an amusement.' I said
to them: 'You have not answered my question. If I asked
you why you should build a house and you said it was built
for protection against cold in winter, and heat in summer,
and for the separate dwelling of persons of different sexes,
you would then tell me why you built the house. Now I
asked why you should have music, and you said, 'Music is
an amusement.' That is equivalent to saying that a house
is a house." - Book XLVIII.
The second illustration is his argument for the
existence of spirits and ghosts. Here he appeals to the
three tests. Beginning with the second, experience, he
holds that many have seen and heard spirits, and have had
the evidence of their own eyes and ears. Then he employs
the first test. "If you think the facts of the ears and
eyes of the masses are not sufficient evidence, why not
search the records of the wisest men of the past?" He
quotes from ancient decrees, ordinances, laws, institutes,
and hymns to show that they assumed the existence of
spirits. Finally he applies the third test. The belief in
spirits is a good and useful one. If the spirits exist,
they will be pleased with our offerings. If they do not
exist, the gathering together of relatives and neighbors
in the sacrificial feast is nevertheless pleasurable and
beneficial.
(2) Let us pass on now to a consideration of his
economic and social theories.
It is a complicated subject, and one that has often
been treated in a superficial manner. The emphasis has
been placed on the fact that he taught universal benevo-
lence or love, but the main emphasis, it seems to me,
should be placed on the fact that he was a utilitarian and
that his humanitarianism is based on utilitarian princi-
ples. Micius wished to bring about a new social order
characterized by strength and prosperity, and the aim of
his teaching is to show how a strong and prosperous
people may be developed.
He held that the strength of a people depends largely
on the size of the population. He lived of course long
before the Malthusian theory had been propounded and

But a large population without prosperity would be

As to the creation of wealth, he was an earnest
apostle of labor. Life should be strenuous, and all should

only because it diminished the population but because it
interfered with industry and with the production of

He was strictly practical in regard to the sort of

His practical turn of mind was so strong that it
overcome entirely the natural love of his countrymen for
ceremonialism.
Turning now to the distribution of wealth, Micius is
often referred to as a socialist. In a sense this is true,
as he wanted to bring about a more equal distribution of
wealth, so that there would be no poverty and so that all
the people would be able to take part in industry and
become creators of wealth. He did not rely on force to
bring this about, but on his great principle of benevolence
(相愛​)(chien hsiang ai), which we must now consider.
The greatest harm to the increase of wealth and
population is hatred and variance. Men do not regard
others as of equal value with themselves and hence come
wars, oppressions, violence, and theft. If we hold the
welfare of others as of equal importance with our own,
these things would cease.
This principle of love or benevolence will remove
distinctions, according to the saying:
绥​以​易​別​(chien i i pieh) “By loving, remove distinctions."

people so that there should be no conflict of duties as to
what is due to those who are closely related to us and to

Kung-Shu-Pan, the state engineer of Chu, had just

his undertaking. If unsuccessful, he hurried back to
the state that was to be attacked and helped its ruler
put his country into a state of defense.

the animal instincts predominate. This brings about a
condition of anarchy, in which there is no security for
life and property. In order to escape from this undesir-
able situation, men enter into a social contract, and agree
to appoint some one who shall have absolute authority to
make laws and to rule over them.

The question arises: Did Micius, in keeping with
pragmatic thought, postulate the belief in Heaven's will,
because it was a useful belief and one that buttressed his
utilitarianism, or was he by nature religious, and one

The eclipse of the influence of Mohism on Chinese
thought may be accounted for in several ways.
It may be due to the fact that the doctrine of univ-
ersal love and anti-militarism were incompatible with the
needs of the age. As we have already noted, the third
century B.C. was one of constant warfare between Con-
tending States, which ended in the triumph of the State
of Chin. This philosophy was not found to be practical
by those who were striving for military and political
domination.
Another reason was the influence of the teaching of
Mencius. That sage was scathing in his criticism of the
doctrine of universal benevolence, and referred to it as a
doctrine that would undermine the foundations of society.
He argued, if there was to be this universal love and the
removal of all distinctions, then we would have a con-
dition of communism, and the family would cease to be
the social unit. If we love other men's wives as our
own and other men's children as our own, surely family
bonds are immediately dissolved. Mencius came to be
regarded as the orthodox interpreter of the teaching of
Confucius, and hence any new teaching was looked upon
as dangerous and heretical in the extreme.
