THE MOTIVES OF CHINESE ART
By R. D. ABRAHAM
The manifestation of art in a people proclaims its
degree of civilization. Chinese civilization was a growth
of the study of life and its enigmas, and of earnest and
honest attempts to solve the mysteries, and if that were
not possible, then, to find a way to be in harmony with the
inexorable laws of nature. The basis of all great Art is
the expression of the soul, and the peculiar philosophy
of a people develops its own distinct Art. Environment
plays a great part in shaping and in influencing that
philosophy.
Though documentary records are so profuse in China,
yet the mere study of these records does not give the
key to our quest; in fact, were we only to look to them
for guidance we should be led astray. It is by the
historical and other records that we have, together with
the result of scientific missions of various European
Institutions, assisted by the erudition of Chinese Sino-
logues at home, that we can arrive at a true appreciation
of Chinese Art.
I believe that the result of such study brings us to
one definite decision, and that is, that the Art of China
is indigenous. Many will disagree with this statement
as some have elaborated on the outside influences on
the Art of the Middle Kingdom, and yet, except for
certain distinct influences in religious art, it can in no
way be proved. The question of foreign influence must
be made clear; the majority of writers will cite example
after example and say that such and such a painting
shows distinct Graeco-Buddhist influence, and it cannot
be gainsaid that it does; but this influence only affected
a certain section of Chinese religious art. Such a form
of foreign influence may be called the influx of a religious
or social wave and may or may not be permanent. It is
not the distinct art of the people. There is another
form of foreign influence which may be called the

"immigration complex", that is, when a tribe or people
have been living by themselves and having their own
civilization, but gradually their neighbours by the increase
of population or in search of "pastures new" superimpose
themselves on the secluded tribe. The result in time
will be a merging of both civilizations and the influence
will become permanent. But when the immigration is
of a small number of people into a greater mass, absorp-
tion is usually the result, with no particular influence
in evidence.
Those interested in this subject can with great profit
read Professor Shirokogoroff's various books and articles
which deal with similar subjects. Now let us see if one
can discover in ancient China any foreign influence, and
it seems that we can do no better than look at their
script, which is undoubtedly very ancient, some ascribing
it to two and three millenniums B.C. and which with
many changes has continued to this day. In dealing
with the script of China it must be borne in mind that
it is admitted that it influenced the most important art
of China-its painting, and if it can be established that
the script is indigenous, then it can be proved that the
art of China is her own. I will quote a few passages
from Karlgren's "Sound and Symbol in Chinese".
"Thus, though Chinese traditions give no hint whatever
of an immigration from any foreign country, and though
there consequently is no external chronological point
d'appui, we are none the less able to state, from internal
evidence, that the Chinese tradition which places the
reign of the Emperor Yao in the 24th century B.C. is
correct; that the Chinese even in those remote times
were skilled astronomers; that they put down in writing
and in the Chinese language records of memorable events,
and in all probability wrote their accounts soon after the
events; in short, that a well-developed Chinese civilization
-resting undoubtedly on foundations many centuries
old-together with the Chinese language, existed on
Chinese soil two thousand years before Christ". Else-
where he says, "The Chinese script is so wonderfully
well adapted to the linguistic conditions of China that
it is indispensable; the day the Chinese discard it they
will surrender the very foundation of their culture";
and again, Chinese se tradition places the genesis of the
script in the middle of the third millennium B.C; and
there is not the slightest hint of foreign origin. Sinologi-
cal science has no difficulty in accepting the Chinese

We learn on the authority of this great scholar that

been well known and has never been disputed.

manifestation of the people of China, we can now turn
to study the art-impulses. Religion and philosophy have
always either encouraged or restricted art. In China
we have Confucianism and Taoism as the religious,

The first real foreign influence which made its mark
on Chinese art was Buddhism. The first contact of
Buddhism with China was when Buddhist missionaries
came from India to preach their doctrines. Okakura says
that Buddhist teachings had first to absorb Taoist ideals
before taking hold of the Chinese mind. Buddhism did
take root and develop in China, but what did that art
consist of more than the depiction of Buddhist figures
and legends? New themes were given, but these were
limited to purely religious subjects. It would be wrong
not to give this art its true value. Look at a well-authen-
ticated painting of Sakyamuni and you will be enthralled
with its beauty and calmness. The benign face of Buddha
looks at you and almost speaks to you of his enlighten-
ment. The Buddha having given up all the ties of this life
and all material pleasures, and having schooled himself
so that no sorrow or disappointment can touch him,

Another form of archaic Chinese art will be found
in bronzes, and here one sees the art of the moulder of the
ancient Chou dynasty. Here again many will give
instances of outside influences, but the ancient bronzes
were made for ritual use and were unquestionably the
result of the genius of Chinese artificers. The majority
of the bronzes were used for religious purposes. The
shapes, graceful and unique, with embossed designs
peculiar to the Chou dynasty, have no parallel elsewhere.
One marvels at the simplicity of the design, the perfect
balance and proportion of these vessels.
Pottery was almost as ancient as bronzes in China,
but was made more for utilitarian than for religious
purposes. Many of the shapes were copied from the
bronzes.
But the beauty of Pottery in the beginning lay, not
in any cunning preparation of clay such as the light
eggshell of modern days, nor in wonderful enamel colours,
but in the nature of its glaze. The glaze was rich and
palpitating. When one looked into the depth of a bowl,
though empty, it should appear full, for Taoism taught
extremes and in emptiness was true fulness; this the
artist-potter took to heart. The glazes had many poetic
names from natural phenomena. For example a certain
glaze was known as "Clair-de-lune," another "Blue of
the sky after rain," and these charmingly fanciful names
were the result of Taoist philosophy. The love of nature
was so great that ancient pottery would sometimes for a
design have an actual leaf impressed on its surface.
Thus we see that the art of China was permeated by
its ancient religion and philosophy.
