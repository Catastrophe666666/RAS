ARTICLE IV. a
NOTES ON THE OPINIONS OF THE CHINESE WITH REGARD
TO ECLIPSES.
BY REV. A. WYLIE.*
I do not intend to give a detail of the popular customs and super-
stitions of the Chinese in regard to eclipses. That will be found
with much more minuteness than I could give it in Mr. Doolittle's
recent work, and other books of that class. But it is scarcely fair
to estimate the attainments of a nation in such matters, by the
standard of the uninstructed masses. Otherwise our own country
might cut a poor figure in the annals of science.
It may be interesting to know how many lunar eclipses are re-
corded. The question would take a considerable to answer. I have
most of the material necessary for such an investigation, and were
I not just on the point of starting for the interior, I would direct
a Chinaman to go over the books and collect them. The thing is
most simply; it would merely take time. From the earliest period
of Chinese history, a government establishment has been maintained,
whose special duty it was to attend to this business, and the regu-
lar register of the eclipses solar and lunar begins about eight centu-
ries before Christ. The record, with short intermissions during the
times of trouble and anarchy, is continuous to the present day; so
that without having examined the matter at all, I should guess
there must be several thousands noticed.
Not only have they genuine records at that early date, which
have been verified by Laplace, Cassini, and other eminent astrono-
mers, but there is good reason to believe that they were then able
to foretell lunar eclipses, although not solar ones. This could
scarcely be otherwise with a people who had a perfect knowledge
(as I believe, though I must defer the proof) of the Metonic and
Calippic cycles, long before Meton or Calippus were born. The
predictions were naturally very faulty, and often wide of the mark,
the errors of calculation being frequently recorded. But with the

The great importance of this subject in regard to the state ritual
and the calendar, has ever made it a matter of solicitude with the
government, but their chief failing has been in the want of instru-
ments. They have been perfectly familiar with the theory, we may
say, for nearly two thousand years at least, and had they possessed
proper instruments, with the same skill in observation, that they
have shewn in computation, the Jesuit missionaries would proba-
bly have found them in possession of a much more highly perfected
system.
As it is they have not been much given to speculations on the
physical constitution of the universe, having contented themselves
with devising schemes for calculating the various recurring pheno-
mena of the heavens. We do find some few incidental notices
however in books written long before the arrival of the missionaries,
in which it is stated that an eclipse of the moon is caused by the
earth coming between it and the sun. One of these is as far back
as the 2nd century of the Christian era; but I lay no stress on
them, as I feel very doubtful about their representing any general
or established belief on the subject. They appear to me to be rather
the mere speculations of individual writers.
In such circumstances, Ricci and his colleagues found the native
mind well prepared to receive their theories. During the Ming
dynasty, astronomy and mathematics had been in a particularly
languishing state, and the officers of the Astronomical Board were
working on with Ko Show-king's formulæ, which after a lapse of
more than three hundred years, had accumulated such an amount
of error, as to render them almost useless in the calculation of an
eclipse. On the other hand the Jesuits were picked men, of high
attainments, who had specially graduated in that department of
science; and bringing with them the newest instruments and latest
discoveries from Europe, had no difficulty in demonstrating the
superiority of their science, before the eyes of such a matter-of-fact
people as the Chinese. They were soon installed supreme in the
mathematical department of the state service; and it is to be re-
gretted that along with their new formulæ and improved tables,

they took advantage of their position to foist upon the Chinese the
antiquated system of Ptolemy, which had already for many years
been exploded in Europe, in favour of that propounded by Coper-
nicus. Schaal and others wrote a large work by the emperor's

taught by them. Early in the present dynasty this was supplanted
by a magnificent work drawn up by an imperial commission, all
natives, giving the whole system theoretical and practical, con-
tained in the other, but more methodically digested, including much
additional matter which had been more recently obtained from the
Europeans. A supplement was written at a later date by two of
the missionaries containing a quantity of new discoveries and rules,
including Evection, Variation and Annual Equation of the Moon.
These are now the text books in the Astronomical Board, according
to which eclipses are still calculated. A still farther development
of the European science was laid before the emperor by one of the
missionaries about 1770, including the theory of gravitation, but
it was not put to press. The rapid advance that has been made in
Europe during a century past, especially in the improvement of the
solar and lunar tables, naturally leaves the Chinese considerably
behind the age; and officially at least, they cling to a modification
of the Ptolemaic system that was engrafted on them with so much
pains. There is however a yearly increasing class of inquirers,
who eagerly grasp at the paltry scraps of scientific information,
detailed in some missionary publications from time to time; but
the mental pabulum is far from sufficient to satisfy their cravings.
It would be an object well worth the patronage and assistance of
the Asiatic Society, to multiply works of this class of a thoroughly
standard character. A scientific Annual embodying all new dis-
coveries and improvements, in addition to the outlines of the various
sciences, would be a most valuable contribution to European Chi-
nese literature. It would tell powerfully on the native mind, and
be a lasting memento of the usefulness of the Society.
To return to the subject of Lunar Eclipses; there are four lines
in which the matter may be treated.---1st, the Astronomical, which
my remarks bear upon. 2nd, the Astrological, which is another
department of the state service, as old, but kept quite distinct from
the Astronomical. 3rd, the Ritual of an equally persistent anti-
quity at least, with the other two. 4th, Popular superstitions and
customs.

Although the eclipses are not mentioned in the imperial Almanac,
yet the Board is required always to lay a full statement before the
emperor several months in advance of the event. The particulars
are printed and circulated in the capital in the Chinese and Manchu
languages, sometimes with diagrams. The calculation is made for
the principal city in each of the eighteen provinces, and forwarded
to the officers there, who circulate them among the several cities
of the province, to be received by the proper authorities in good
time for the observances of the accustomed rites.
I need hardly say that while the state of ritual absolutely re-
quires the ancient forms of more than two thousand years standing
to be religiously continued; and the mass of the population wil-
lingly join in the hubbub, believing that they are thus scaring
away the wild moon that has ventured to intrude upon the esta-
blished luminary; yet it is a delusion to think that the more in-
telligent portion have any such faith.

Some years ago, the children of one of my neighbors while at
play in the garden, found the cocoon of a wild silkworm, which was
brought to me as a curiosity. When the moth made its appear-
ance, I recognized it as a female of the Arrindy moth, figured in
Jardine's Library. I placed it in a small bird-cage which I put
in an open window, and the next morning I found that it had been
visited by a male of the same species which still remained upon the
bars of the cage. From their union resulted a numerous crop of
eggs, which I carefully watched, hoping to be able to introduce
the manufacture of the silk, into Ningpo. The eggs duly hatched
in two weeks time, but notwithstanding I supplied the worms
plentifully with the leaves of the Ricinus, and, indeed, with every
other kind of leaf I could find in the garden, I had the mortifica-
tion to see them obstinately refuse to eat, and all ultimately perish
from starvation.
The smaller cocoons sent to me by my Chinese friend, from some
unexplained cause, remained torpid for a long time, so that I des-
paired of being able to identify the species. Subsequently, how-
ever, the moths made their appearance, and proved to be the
Saturnia Cynthia, just described.

Those who have read the accounts of the mode of rearing the wild
silkworm in India, in the works to which I have referred, cannot
fail to remark the great similarity which exists between the method
in use there and that pursued in China. Indeed I am quite con-
scious that I have added little, if any thing, to what is already
known. Nevertheless, having heard that difficulties have been
experienced by those who have attempted to rear the Chinese wild
silkworms in Europe and the United States, as well as in spinning
or reeling the silk from the cocoons, I am not without hope that
through the medium of the Society, I may be able to give publicity
to these accounts and possibly bring valuable information to the
notice of some to whom it may prove of material assistance.
