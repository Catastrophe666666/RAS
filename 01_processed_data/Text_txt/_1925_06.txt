SHANTUNG FORAMINIFERA
By ARTHUR PAUL JACOT
(Shantung Christian University).
Foraminifera are simple one-celled animals much
like the well-known Amoeba but placed in a separate
group by virtue of their ability to secrete or build about
themselves a chamber in which to live, protected from
such mechanical injuries as abrasion, direct contact with
their medium and other forms of life, especially preda-
cious animals which might otherwise find them to be easily
eaten. When this house or room becomes too large for
them they slip out of their front door and on to their roof
or floor bottom, and secrete another room about them-
selves. This they repeat as often as necessary so that
the house, shell or test continues increasing in size, the
largest species becoming as much as three millimeters
long. According to the shape of the shell on which the
new room is built and how often the animal changes its
orientation on exit, so will the plan of the house be. For
instance some houses increase in one direction while
others are built in two or three planes. On this factor
and others, the different families and sub-families are
based.
All of the species reported below belong to the
Miliolidae, a family abundantly represented on the shores
of tropical seas. The material was collected from tide
rifts on the sandy beach east of the Tengchowfu (Penglai)
promontory, north coast of Shantung. The material has
been deposited in the Collections of the Department of
Biology of the Shantung Christian University.
Genus Spiroloculina d'Orbigny 1826.
This group consists of individuals who build more
than five chambers which are grouped in one plane each
successive one on the opposite side of the central axis.
The first chamber has the form of a spiral in one plane
and of at least one coil in length. The aperture is usually
produced, more or less circular, and provided with a bifid
tooth.

Spiroloculina canaliculata d'Orbigny.
Shell shining, white, elliptical, with protruding ends,
of nearly the same diameter throughout, the edges of the
chambers being cemented together so as to make the
whole face quite smooth and continuous; outer edge of
peripheral chambers concave, the edges flange-like; neck
produced; aperture simple, rounded, with a well developed
bifid tooth and a small one on the opposite side.
One small specimen was found. It is generally dis-
tributed in Japanese and Philippine benthal (continental
shelf) waters and is also found in the Hawaiian archi-
pelago.
Spiroloculina impressa Terquem.
Shell similar to the above but with the area between
the flanges flat instead of concave.
Four specimens of this species were found.
Spiroloculina excavata d'Orbigny.
Shell somewhat granular, white, elongate-oval with
protruding ends, thin in the center, becoming rapidly
thicker to the periphery; outer edge of peripheral cham-
bers obliquely convex, the edges more or less sharp but
not flanged; neck prominent; aperture circular with a
prominent bifid tooth.
Several specimens may be placed under this name.
In our specimens the outer edges of the peripheral cham-
ber are semi-angular to broadly rounded.
This species is characteristic of warm shallow seas
and is fossil in the Pliocene of Florida. In the Philippine
area (tropical shallow seas) it is replaced by S. grateloupi
d'Orbigny the rim of whose peripheral chambers are
provided with a strong bead or rim and are not obliquely
convex.
Spiroloculina planissima (Lamarck).
Shell up to 2 mm. in diameter, smooth, very flat, oval
with protruding ends, sides of chambers slightly convex,
edges neatly joined, periphery carinate; neck and aper-
ture elliptical, with a bifid tooth.
Three small specimens were secured. This also is a
shallow water species heretofore recorded from tropical
waters.

Spiroloculina penglaiensis sp. n.
FIGURE 2.
Shell mat, chalky, rough, very flat, almost circular,
nearly as broad as long, peripheral margin rounded, with
neck distinctly protruding and erect; chambers broad,
flat, and very shallow; the earlier chambers truncate, the
later ones doubly truncate giving the base of last chamber
two appearances, one being indicated by the dotted line;
aperture formed by a thin, flaring lip, perfectly circular,
with a single tooth capped by a concave plate giving a
bifurcate appearance. Length up to 2 mm.
Many specimens of varying sizes. Types in Univ-
ersity Collection No. 203.
This species is closely related to S. planissima differ-
ing by its texture, breadth, the flatness of the chamber
walls and their distinctness. In S. planissima the cham-
bers are joined by a porcelanous pointing while in S.
penglaiensis the chambers have no such pointing, the
contact being marked by an incised suture.
Spiroloculina penglaiensis obliqua subsp. n.
FIGURE 1.
Similar to S. penglaiensis but test lopsided, caused
by irregular curving of chambers whose distal half is
almost straight; aperture not so retrorse nor so protrud-
ing; length averaging less than S. penglaiensis.
Several specimens. Types in University Collection
No. 204.
Genus Quinqueloculina d'Orbigny.
Adult shell with chambers added about a single axis,
five chambers to each complete turn but each added 144°
apart, thus intercalating a previously formed chamber
between the last two formed; aperture circular to
elongate, usually with a single tooth.

This species varies so much as regards to plumpness
and consequently angularity that d'Orbigny treated it as
constituting two species. These have since been united
by Cushman. Our material consists entirely of the
angular form and as it is the result of shore collecting
and as the more plump material gathered by the steamer
"Albatross" in the Pacific is chiefly of the plump type it
is suggested that this difference in bulk may be due to
difference in depth.
Found to be common at Tengchowfu, mostly small or
partially grown specimens. Also found throughout the
warm temperate and tropical Pacific, West Indies, etc.
Quinqueloculina chiliensis sp. n.
FIGURES 3-5.
Shell glossy, white, finely striated, oval, lenticular;
chambers broad, sides gently curved, very finely spiro-
longitudinally combed, the lines more or less discon-
tinuous and difficult to trace, basal end of chambers
smooth; edge of peripheral chambers with distinct, non-
protruding, carinal seam; aperture narrowly horse-shoe-
shaped, somewhat angular, the front side longer than the
rear, and descending slightly on the penultimate chamber,
with a thick collar; tooth short, thickened at apex and
truncate; length up to .8 mm.
This species is most closely related to Cushman's
figures 3 under the name Q. dutemplei, but differs in
being wider, more compressed, less angular, in having
less protruding chambers, and a shorter, thicker lipped,
shorter toothed aperture which has the appearance of
wearing a broad, yellow, starched collar.
Four specimens. Types in the University Collection
No. 206.
