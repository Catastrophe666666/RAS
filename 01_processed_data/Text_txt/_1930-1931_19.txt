THE HISTORY OF THE DAYS
OF THE WEEK
By HERBERT CHATLEY, D.Sc. (Lond.)
This particular subject is neither Asiatic nor Chinese
in first aspect, but there are enough Asiatic and Chinese
elements in it to make it of interest to some members of
the Society, and it is one of the few subjects in which
there are some early connexions between Occidental and
Chinese cultures.
*
*
Just in the same manner as the geologist glimpses
in the fossils discovered in rocks the ancient history of
life on earth, so the historian can glimpse the ancient
history of man and his mental life in the fossil fragments
which exist in the speech and the customs of the present
day. The week itself and the names of the days of the
week are such fossil fragments, with the most intricate
and interesting history.
First of all, with regard to the word "Week." The
meaning of the word is said to be "Change." There are
various related words in the different forms of "Wachs,"
to grow, which occur in the German languages, and it
occurs in the Latin form "Vix," an obsolete word which is
the root in the English word "Vicissitude." "Week" occurs
in all Teutonic languages as applied to a period of seven
days. It appears first in Gothic in the gospels of Ulphilas
in the fourth century.
The institution of the week is however most decidedly
taken from the Jews. It is true that its universal observ-
ance arose in Europe with Christianity, but, as I shall
endeavour to show, it was in general use in many parts
of Europe slightly before Christianity came. Before
that time it is not very easy to show that it was in use by
any nation other than the Jews. According to the
accepted traditions of the church and Judaism, it is a
definitely Jewish thing. The reputed origin of it is given
in the Old Testament, but biblical scholars and orienta-
lists are inclined to push it back a little further, and are

generally of opinion that it has something to do with the
worship of the moon in Babylonia. The biblical associa-
tion of "New Moons" and "Sabbaths" is a pertinent fact
in this connexion.
It is rather an interesting fact that the two cities of
Abraham, Ur of the Chaldees and Harran, in the north
of Mesopotamia, were both closely associated with stellar
worship. The city of Ur was famous for the worship of
the goddess of the moon, "Sin." Harran was, and
persisted for many centuries as, an important centre of
Sabaeanism, the worship of the stars. I shall have more
to say of this phase of the question presently. For the
moment all I would say is that the week is probably of
lunar origin. Its association with the moon is very
obvious; in fact a little too obvious. Two real periods
associated with the moon are: (1) That which we ordin-
arily call the Lunar Month, of 2912 days, the period from
new moon to new moon, or from full moon to full moon,
which varies a little from month to month; (2) The
period in which the moon travels round the heavens, from
one star back to the same star. That period is 27 days.
A quarter of it is not quite seven days, and a quarter of
the other period is 734 days; the approximate average
between the two quarters is 714 days. Even a quarter of
a day's difference in a short period like that is of tremen-
dous importance, because it means that after a very short
time, about three weeks, the error is enough to put it
quite out of step with the moon and in actual fact the
week is practically useless as a lunar period.
Various hypotheses have been discussed as to this
discrepancy, one of the principal being that a week was
not originally as it is now, a fixed period of seven days,
but simply the interval between the 7th, 14th, 21st, and
28th days of the moon, the result being an unequal in-
terval at the end of the lunar month, with 8 or 9 days.
However that may be, it is almost certain that the week is
derived in some such manner from the moon, and that
we certainly got it from the Jews. As to it having
existed amongst other peoples, I shall discuss that point
presently.
My main theme is as to the names of the days of the
week. In this connexion I would point out, first of all,
that the Jews did not have any names for the days of
the week. There is no reference in the Bible to the
individual days of the week except the Sabbath (Satur-
day), although there is a reference to the day before the

Sabbath as the eve of the Sabbath. Individual days went
by number. The same feature occurs in some other
countries (Portugal, Russia, Basques). In Russia the
days of the week are arranged similarly to ours, the
Saturday being called the Sabbath but the Sunday is "the
day of no work," and the Monday "the day after," and so
on by numbers. The Basques have an analogous system
with two half-weeks. In Portugal Sunday is called
Domingo and the other days, the first, second, third,
fourth, fifth and Sabado (Saturday). Apart from these
instances we have generally the names of the seven days
with which you are all familiar, but with certain varia-
tions and translations.
Now when do these days first occur, what do they
mean and where do they come from? That is the main
subject I want to speak of.
They are first heard of clearly and definitely about
the beginning of the Christian era, and, after a period of
about 200 years, they became fixed in practically every
country in Western Europe, and to a certain extent in
what is called the Near East. In their earliest form,
which I shall deal with presently, they correspond definite-
ly with the seven planets, and it is practically certain that,
whatever their earlier history may have been, they
established themselves first of all through Chaldean
astrologers in the Roman Empire. From the time of the
invasion of Babylonia by Alexander in the 4th Century
B.C. until the dissolution of the Roman Empire, Chaldeans,
i.e., Mesopotamians, of various kinds came to Rome as
astrologers and diviners and brought Babylonian astrol-
ogy with them. This astrological week became current
about the 1st century B.C., so that we find references
to these names once or twice before Christ and frequently
afterwards.
In A.D. 350 or thereabouts, Constantine the Great
identified the week day of the traditional resurrection of
Christ with the day of the sun and legalized the early
Christian practice of making that day of paramount
importance. He was for the majority of his life a sun-
worshipper, and there is no doubt that he was strongly
influenced by this worship, even in his protection of
Christianity. Whether there is any real conclusion to
be drawn as to his attaching great importance to Sunday
as the day of the Sun is not clear, but it would be quite in
keeping with other events of his reign.
What do these names mean and where do they come

from? In their original Greek and Latin forms they
are as follow, taking them in their regular order: Sunday,
the day of the Sun; Monday, the day of the moon;
Tuesday, the day of Mars; Wednesday, the day of
Mercury; Thursday, the day of Jupiter; Friday, the day
of Venus, and Saturday, the day of Saturn.
This order is one relating to the planets. The seven
planets or the five wandering stars, together with the sun
and moon, have been known for some 4,000 years at least.
This particular order is, however, not a natural one at all.
It is not an order in which you can arrange them from
any of their astronomical characteristics, but nevertheless
it is an order which can be arrived at from the natural
order by a very simple arithmetical process.
The most natural order of the planets is that according
to their speeds, commencing with Saturn. Saturn goes
round the heavens once every thirty years, Jupiter in
twelve years, Mars about two years, then the sun, one
year, Venus, which obviously goes faster at times than the
sun, but averages also one year, Mercury, still faster at
times, but again averages one year, and the moon, one
month. That is the natural order as seen from the earth.
We are not concerned with what the velocities really are,
but what they appear to be.
Now if you take this series and write it over and over
again, and start at any place, you will find that the
1st, 25th. 49th, 73rd, 97th, 121st, and 145th items occur in
exactly the order of the names of the days of the week, and
while there are some alternative hypotheses, it is
practically certain that the rationale of the process is that
astrologers started with a system of days of 24 hours, and
they said that to each hour there is a planetary influence.
These hours were then arranged in the order of the
planets as they are seen from the earth. Thus, starting
with Saturn and running through the day, the first hour
of the day is devoted to Saturn and is known as Saturn's
hour, the 8th is also Saturn's hour as is also the 15th and
22nd. The 23rd will be Jupiter's hour, the 24th Mars' and
the 25th the Sun's. In other words the first hour of the
day after Saturn's day is the Sun's. Now if you start the
same system all over again, you will find that the 1st hour
of the next day is the Moon's, the 1st hour of the following
day Mars' and of the next day Mercury's, and the next
Jupiter's, the next Venus' and we come back again to the
1st hour of the 8th day, which is again Saturn's. That is
perhaps rather difficult to follow, but if you think of it in

this way, that 24 is 7 times 3 and 3 over, you will see that
the new series comes from the first one by moving up 3
places at a time. So if you take the order of the planets
and move along it 3 places at a time, you will find the
exact order in which the days of the week are named.
This system has been in existence for 2,000 years. This
particular origin was known to the Greek writer, Dio
Cassius, who wrote in the 2nd century and said that
the whole system was a new one and had originated with
the Egyptians. There is, however, no trace in Egyptian
chronology of the seven-day week, and in this respect he
was almost certainly wrong, but he was quite right in
saying this was the manner in which this order originated.
In order to understand anything further about it, one
must look for three things. First we must see where the
planets were first discovered, all seven of them. Secondly,
one must find a place where the planet Saturn was
important, and thirdly one must have the 24-hour division
of the day current. All these conditions are met in
Mesopotamia. In Phoenicia, the god El, who corresponds
to Kronos or Saturn, must have dominated the Sabbath
if the Phoenicians knew of that institution, and in Meso-
potamia Ninib was his equivalent.
These points will help us to go on a little further. At
the moment I want to say more about these names. The
first known record of the planetary name of a day occurs
in the writings of the poet Tibullus, a contemporary of
Julius Caesar, who speaks of "The sacred day of Saturn."
In the writings of Justin Martyr and Clement of
Alexandria (2nd Century) we have references to the day
of the Sun and to the days of Mercury and Venus, both
in Latin and Greek forms. As I said, the writer Dio
Cassius, who also lived in the 2nd Century, mentioned that
the whole system was a newly established one.
How are we going to explain the variations which
occur all over Europe in these names, and how do we get
these peculiar English, German and Scandinavian forms?
Do they exactly correspond? In this connexion I would
mention that a well-known writer on Scandinavia, Du
Chaillu, thought there had been once in Scandinavia a
five-day week and that the names followed from the five
Scandinavian gods in the same order as we have now, and
from them have come the names as they occur with many
Teutonic peoples. That the order of the names
originated is quite impossible, because it is the planetary
order, which compels the conclusion that the Teutonic

and Monday are perfectly obvious and occur in Anglo-

present forms go back certainly to the 6th or 7th Century.
Nevertheless, the fact that they come in exactly the
right planetary order makes it practically impossible for
them to be of purely Teutonic origin. Tiw or Tyr is a god
of whom very little is known, but according to the few
records, he was a god associated with fighting. Woden
(generally called Odin) is perfectly well-known and is
quite definitely associated by Tacitus and other Roman
historians with the god Mercury. Thor had certain
attributes in connection with thunder and was definitely
associated with Jupiter by the Romans. Freya is often
confused with Frigg, the wife of Woden. She was also
associated with much the same things as Venus and in the
case of Saturday, which we have in the Anglo-Saxon as
Saeternesdag, this is not even a translation. It has been
suggested that there was an actual Scandinavian god
Saeter corresponding to Saturn, but all the evidence is
that Saturday is what it appears to be, Saturn's day.
With regard to other languages, there are interesting
deviations. It we take the French series, Dimanche is a
corruption of Dominicus or Dominica and this occurs in
similar forms in the other Romance tongues. Dominicus
means the Lord's Day, but a very interesting thing is that
in Wales and Brittany which were, so to speak, shut off
from the world by Teutonic invasion, we have the primitive
Welsh form Dydd Sul and similarly in Brittany Dissul,
both meaning the day of the Sun and not the Lord's day.
Saturday also has some very interesting variations. In
all countries except Scandinavia and the low German
countries (Holland, Frisia, England) you have the word
Sabbath as the equivalent of Saturday. In French you

have, for instance, Samedi, which is a corruption of
Sabbath-Dies. In other countries the forms Sabbato,
Samstag, Sabado, Subbota, Es-Sabt, etc., all of which are

With regard to the other days, Monday corresponds
to Lundi, a corruption of the Latin form Lunae Dies;
Tuesday corresponds to Mardi, Martis Dies, and so on.
There are again some curious variations. Wednesday for
instance in the high German countries is called Mittwoch
("Mid-week") and there is no very definite record of it in
the form of Woden's Day in these countries, but Thursday
and Friday are uniform in all Teutonic countries. One
rather interesting and amusing form occurs in Scandina-
via-where Saturday is called Laugrdagr (Lordag) which
means washing day, or possibly may relate to the ancient
Scandinavian god Loki, who may have been identified
with Saturn.
So you find that throughout Europe these names occur
in forms which can all be considered corruptions or trans-
lations of the Latin style, which was simply a series of
planetary days, Sundays and Saturdays in some countries
being replaced by names meaning the Lord's day and the
Sabbath respectively.
This is a very fascinating subject when you make a
comparison of the linguistic and dialectic forms of the
names, finding the changes and noting the extraordinary
conservatism of man. There is a complete survival of the
Latin forms in Wales, Brittany, Cornwall and probably
also the Isle of Man, also a partial survival in Gaelic.
There is a remarkable stability about the system and the
distribution shows that the names must have been intro-
duced in the Latin form into many places before Chris-
tianity was adopted. Ecclesiastical influences always tend-
ed to abolish such reminders of heathenism, but the names
were so firmly set that, when Christianity was adopted in
these countries, these names survived in most cases, only
Saturday and Sunday being sometimes changed. There
are a few exceptions, as I mentioned before, such as in
Portugal and Russia, where the names were modified.
So much for the history of the days of the week in the
Christian era. Now what about the period before? In
connection with the earlier period it is difficult to get exact
details, because records are so fragmentary. There are,
however, certain interesting data. First of all it should
be mentioned that in 1869 George Smith, one of the early
explorers in Mesopotamia, discovered a clay tablet which

was part of a calendar and in which it was stated that the
7th, 14th, 19th, 21st and 28th days of the moon were
taboo to the King and to certain other people, for certain
purposes. Even the word "Sabbath" is said to have been
applied to these taboo days, but there is some controversy
as to this. A few years later a large number of cuneiform
tablets was discovered which formed part of a collection
of omens drawn up by Babylonian astrologers from the
appearance of the stars and sky (the "Observation of
Bel"). In this collection there was one group of tablets
which seemed to refer to the seven planets in the order in
which they occur in the names of the day of the week.
This particular conclusion is not, I think, quite accepted by
Assyriologists nowadays. They doubt if the seven names
are what they were at first thought to be. At one time it
was thought quite definitely that this order of the seven
planets actually went back to this collection of tablets
which is reputed to have been compiled or partly compiled
in the time of the first Sargon (1700 B.C.), but there are
lots of difficulties about it. These inscriptions are very
hard to translate because they were written in a curious
oracular manner and in a double system of writing, so that
beyond the fact that certain days of the month were taboo,
and that there was in some instances an arrangement of
seven days, it is not yet certain that the ancient Baby-
lonians knew what a week was or anything about the
seven planetary days of the week. When we come down
to a later period, things are rather different. In the so-
called Seleucid period, after the conquest of Babylonia by
Alexander, Babylonian astrology became much more pre-
cise and comparatively valuable for astronomical pur-
poses. There is, however, no indication that the seven-day
week was employed for chronology in Egypt, Babylonia,
Greece or Rome. These all had their own particular
systems which mostly went along lines like those of the
Chinese lunar calendar. It is almost certain that late
Babylonian astrologers developed this planetary week
system purely for astrological purposes. The peculiar
system of naming the planetary day after the first hour
of the day in itself suggests astrological ideas. Long be-
fore that time the seven planets had been associated with
the seven gods of Babylonia, who had a number of attri-
butes which correspond broadly to those which later
European astrological books attributed to the seven
planets.

tory and was not finally destroyed until the 13th Century
A.D. It was in existence 3,000 years B.C. and practically
throughout the whole of that period, even right up to the
10th Century A.D., it was a city of star-worship and a group
of Sabaen pagans lived there. These people must not be
confused with a similarly named Christian sect with whom
they had no connexion. They had this planetary week
before the time of Christ.
The history of Harran is rather complicated. There
was a curious interchange of culture and astrology be-
tween Harran and Greece and Rome on the one hand and
between Harran and Baghdad on the other. This was
quite possibly the centre in which the seven-day planetary
week originated. There is nothing to confirm it, but you
can conjecture if you wish that Abraham got the system
from the same city about 2,000 B.C. and transmitted it in
a non-astrological form to the Jews, but that would be
pure conjecture.
Now about the week in China. As you know the
week is now very well-known among Christians in China
by the name Li-Pai, which is obviously of recent mission-
ary introduction. It, however, entered China much ear-
lier in connection with these peculiar forms of Christianity
known as Manichaeism and Nestorianism and also, in
connexion with astrology, since the T'ang Dynasty. The
seven planet cycle which occurs in China and corresponds
to the order of the days of the week is an astrological one.
From very ancient times throughout Asia there has been
a distinct division of the ecliptic or path of the moon into
28 asterisms (Nakshatras in India, Hsu or "Resting
places" in China). Actually, as I have said, the moon
travels round the stars in 2714 days and consequently the
method of 28 divisions cannot be an exact one. Four
of the star groups of these 28 are alluded to in the
Chinese "Book of History" in Chou times and a 28-day
system might go back to that time, but is not recorded.
These 28 divisions have been allocated by astrologers to
the planets. You will find that the Chinese have a similar
arrangement to that which we have in our week days, but
it almost certainly has been borrowed from the Indian
series, which again probably came from Harran. So all
that you can say about the planetary-week in China is
that the series of planets in the same order does occur in
astrology in China (also in Arabia and India), but purely
in connexion with astrology, not in popular practice or
chronology. As you know, not until comparatively

I would say one last thing in connexion with drastic
reform of the calendar. Mr. Cottsworth, who recently
lectured on this subject in Shanghai, has explained the
proposals of the League of Nations, but there is one
curious weakness about the whole system of revision
which should be considered, viz., it depends entirely on
the predominance of the seven-day which is not a measure
from an astronomical point of view and is an arbitrary
division of time based on a seventh day of rest. It means
nothing except that, so that it is perhaps a little question-
able whether any drastic reform should be tied down to a
seven-day week. In Russian a five-day week has been
talked of. One rest-day in five would be perhaps a more
convenient arrangement than the present, The week is
purely an arbitrary division in time and apart from
traditional religious associations has no real meaning.
The seven-day week is thus, as I said at first, a very
interesting fossil of the ancient life and thought of man.
