SOME TYPES OF CHINESE HISTORICAL
THOUGHT
By CHANG HSIN-HAI
Perhaps no nation is more interested in history than
the Chinese. Certainly the number of historical works
that have been published in China within the last two
thousand years have not been exceeded in any other
country. Beginning with the Han Dynasty, when for the
first time Chinese historical thought took articulate form
on any extensive scale, we have it on record that over four
hundred books of a historical nature were written.
During the Sui Dynasty, the number increased to over
13,000, and during each of the succeeding dynasties, the
number of history books was always over 20,000, with
the Sung Dynasty claiming more than 43,000 books, so
that the total number of orthodox books of a historical
nature cannot be less than 200,000!
Now, a number of questions immediately suggest
themselves for our attention when we are brought face
to face with this vast historical accumulation. How
much of this material is of real historical value? What
proportion of it is able to meet the requirements which
are prescribed within the limits of historical science? To
what extent has this material been guided by any
systematic thought to give it unity and coherence? And,
finally, if it is inspired by any systematic thinking, what
is the nature of this thinking? To answer all these
questions adequately would involve the whole field of
Chinese historiography. The present paper is unable to
be so broad in its scope, but it will attempt to sketch the
conditions which have made the development of historical
writing in China possible, and the direction which
that development has taken. We shall have to consider
the nature of some of the more representative historical
works and the ideas of some of the more pronounced
thinkers who have helped to shape Chinese historical
writing in its present forms.

As soon as Chinese society assumed a settled or
organised state, one institution emerged which laid the
foundation for all subsequent historical undertakings,
and that was the creation of the official historian. We
have now no means of ascertaining the functions which
these historians of the State were called upon to perform
in the earliest days. We know, however, that they
already existed as far back as the Yin Dynasty; and in
the Chou Dynasty our knowledge of these officials
becomes more precise. It seems that there was not only
one such historian, but a large group accredited both
to the ruling house and to the different feudal lords,
each charged with the duty of recording, in chronological
order, events of some importance. Further, the position
as a State historian was one to be highly coveted, because
it meant very great social prestige; so that very
frequently, in addition to being a mere recorder of events,
the man could become a State councillor and be himself
directly responsible for these events. These historians
really constituted an intellectual oligarchy with here-
ditary rights, and towards the end of the Chou Dynasty
became apparently quite indispensable to the intellectual
life of the nation. Some of the eminent thinkers of the
day were enlisted from these ranks, as, for instance,
Lao-tse who held the position as custodian of history
for the Chou Dynasty.
It is unfortunate that not much of the historical
writing from this official class has come down to us. Our
knowledge of it is therefore meagre, but it is not difficult
to draw the conclusion that the writing was no better
than a mere registration of events which did not neces-
sarily have any bearing upon one another, but were thrown
together simply by virtue of their common affiliation with
the house which desired to be remembered. It must have
been very similar to the Old English Chronicles which
were begun in King Alfred's reign and brought down
to 1154. It was very concise; the material was not
organised, and was drawn exclusively from the happen-
ings of the court with no attempt to regard the problems
of a larger world; and, of course, in consonance with the
enormous credulity of a society in the early stages of its
formation, it laid great emphasis on unusual cosmic
phenomena.

Dynasty, there was a marked change in the position of
the official historian. In the first place, the class to

Our present day historians, including those of
modern Europe, may be more scientific, may employ a
more precise methodology; but the question is still a
debatable one whether history will be any better by
merely being under the tyranny of a scientific method.
For, after all, in addition to being accurate, what we
expect from the historian is that he should also give us a
synthesis of the material which he handles-a unity, a
coherence, or a definite attitude which must be consistent
and logical. One cannot very easily escape from selection
in historical writing, and selection is so much a part of
the historian himself that it can neither be objectified
nor made amenable to any scientific rule. It is impos-
sible, therefore, as Ranke would have us believe, for the
historian simply to show "wie es eigentlich gewesen."
All that responsibility for selection, for emphasis, for
colour which no historian of the first order can afford
to ignore will always make history an art rather than
a science.
Now, if we assume this point of view when we read
the Tso Chuan or Ssu-ma Chien's General History, it
is not difficult to see that both are among the earliest of
the great histories of the world, holding a place certainly
as eminent as the history by Thucydides.
The Tso Chuan made three remarkable contributions,
immediately raising the level of Chinese historiography
which was steadily sinking at the hands of the official
historian, and at the same time giving a new direction to
history writing, which culminated in the still more
remarkable work of Ssu-ma Chien's. The official his-
torians had been attached to individual ruling houses,
and their spirit of devotion had no room for expansion
outside the limits of these particular houses. The
so-called Spring and Autumn Annals, for instance, were
a record of events which occurred in the Lu principality.
There were other annals registering the events which
befell the houses of Tsin or of Wei or similar houses.
But although the historian of the Tso Chuan was himself
still living in what is commonly known as the period of

Another contribution lies in the historian's concep-
tion of entirety from a different point of view. The
Tso Chuan is a history not only of political events, but
also of many phases of the social organisation of the
day. Now, this, too, is a radical departure from the
stereotyped annals of the official historian. The annal-
ists were satisfied merely with registering political events
as they occurred in order of time, and with placing them
in juxtaposition with one another even when they did not
have the slightest connection. But the historian of the
Tso Chuan, though not forsaking the purely chronolo-
gical arrangement, had a conception of causation or living
continuity in social organisation, so that events of a
purely political nature could no longer be of any ade-
quate value for him. Instead of recording events as if
they were simply dead facts, he had a sense of value; and,
therefore, even while he was discussing political affairs,
he wanted to see the larger policies and spirit from which
these affairs emerged, and this very frequently brought
him to a discussion of many social phenomena with which
political events were inevitably connected and which,
under the official historian, were, of course, most likely
ignored. The second contribution, then, is the concep-
tion of causation, and with it the whole field of historical
narrative was much widened.
Still another contribution is the emphasis on a unified
outlook. The Tso Chuan is inspired by a dominant
motive, by a consistent attitude towards the whole ques-
tion of social and political organisation. The facts were
presented not for their own sake, but were selected in
order to illuminate the historian's personal conviction of
the direction in which society was developing. The

The Tso Chuan, then, was in every respect a crea-
tive work of immense value. It greatly minimized the
importance of the official annalist and brought Chinese
historical writing from the stage of mere chronicling to
the stage of articulate historiography. A later thinker of
the Tang Dynasty, himself an eminent historiographer
whom we shall consider at some length, justly remarked
that "the Tso Chuan was a work which did not follow
any ancient methodology but which, through the happy
combination of the narration of events and of the presen-
tation of the opinions of important personages, made
itself a model of balance and rationality."
By the time of Confucius' death, in the fifth century
before Christ, we can be quite sure, then, that Chinese
historical thought had already, through its detachment
from official chronicling, become an independent branch
of scholarship where ample opportunity was offered to
the display of individual genius and initiative. But fully
four centuries had to pass before the seeds which were
sown by the historian of the Tso Chuan could come to
fruition in the extraordinary work of Ssu-ma Chien, a
part of which at least is now happily made available to-
Western readers through the distinguished and painstak-
ing translation of M. Chavannes.
In this General History of Ssu-ma Chien, one really
feels the force of a mighty current where the water was
generously contributed by tributaries of all sizes, among
others, of course, the Tso Chuan. The work represented
the climax of Chinese historical thinking, beyond which
no later historian has been able to go in actual historical
productions. For over two thousand years it has served
as a model, any criticism of which would be an act of pure
sacrilege. Scholars after the Southern Sung period
were, it is true, audacious enough to challenge the
accuracy of its material, but it has retained an
overpowering influence which is not likely to weaken,
because it is founded upon genuine merits which even
in our present stage of historiographical evolution we
cannot very easily deny. This General History is
now classed as the earliest of the so-called Twenty-Four
Histories, and is absolutely unrivalled in the breadth and

In the introduction written by himself to his General
History, Ssu-ma Chien made a few remarks which are
of great value in illuminating the purpose and method-
ology of his history. In one place, he says, "My narrative
consists of no more than systematisation of the material
that has been handed down to us. There is therefore
no creation, but a faithful representation." How exceed-
ingly modern does all this sound to our ears which have
become familiar with all the talk about being objective,
scientific, impartial, by the historians of the 19th century.
Neither a Mommsen nor a Ranke can very easily improve
on a statement like this! But Ssu-ma Chien realises
what the more narrow-minded of the academic historians
perhaps do not realise, that the organisation of his
historical material must imply the existence of a definite
and consistent point of view. It has to be submitted to
selection, and selection means a coherent and intelligent
attitude towards life. If, however, a work is merely an
elaboration of that attitude, it would not be history either.
So Ssu-ma Chien says, "I would not deliver myself up
to empty talk, but rather to discovering the hidden mean-

What, then, is the nature of this extraordinary his-
tory which has held a place in the curriculum of Chinese
education second only to the classics of Confucius them-
selves? Who was this man Ssu-ma Chien? What were
his qualifications, and under what conditions did he write
his immortal history?
It is a curious fact that Ssu-ma Chien inherited from
his father the position of an official historian, the very
institution which his own genius was destined to reduce
to a place of comparative unimportance. He was born
about 145 B.C. during a period of great vitality and
attractiveness. The four hundred years which trans-
pired between the historian of the Tso Chuan and the
appearance of Ssu-ma Chien constituted a great formative
period, during which all the energy that was spent in
incessant warfare among the different principalities came
to be directed towards constructive enterprises in the
building up of a new and vigorous nation which had
already become unified under the powerful personality of
the emperor of the Tsin Dynasty. That unity had now
come to be even more firmly established in the Han Dyn-
asty, when expansion took many forms and followed many
directions. The political structure was rapidly consoli-
dated during an era of peace, and pursuits in scholarly
work were undertaken in numerous fields. The desire
for power and the desire for knowledge were now driving
the young energetic race to the confines of neighbouring
countries, and the future historian had all the opportunity
to satisfy his intellectual curiosity. It was, in fact, an
age of extraordinary richness and variety.
Ssu-ma Chien was born in Shensi, the land of Shih
Hwang-ti. The details of his life can now no longer be
ascertained, but two facts are of great importance. One
is that he travelled extensively to some of the remotest
corners of the country. The other is he was cruelly
treated while in gaol in his forty-eighth year for the
release of Lin who had yielded to the power of the invad-
ing Huns. He died around 86 B.C., perhaps at the age
of sixty. The history was begun while in prison.
It is a general history, because Ssu-ma Chien tried
to cover the whole field of Chinese history from, the earl-
iest beginnings of which there was record to his own

The 130 chapters consist of 12 chapters devoted to
the history of the ruling houses, 10 chapters of tabular
presentation, 8 chapters of general discussion dealing
with subjects like rituals, music, law, astronomy, etc.,
30 chapters on the histories of distinguished families and
finally 70 chapters of biographical material, mostly of
men, but occasionally also branching into discussions of
distant lands and social questions. There is one chapter,
for instance, dealing with the economic problems of the
day which is exceedingly valuable from many points of
view.
More than half of the history is of a biographical
nature, but, in fact, it combines all the styles of
historical writing known at the time. The twelve chap-
ters on the ruling classes have chronological arrangement
and are an improvement on the Spring and Autumn
Annals after which they were patterned. The ten
chapters of tabular presentation provide us with a large
amount of statistical material and have for their model
another historical work which has now entirely dis-
appeared. The eight chapters of general dicussion are
devoted to an exposition of the impersonal elements
which underlay social structure of which a beginning
was made in the Shang Shu. And lastly, the chapters on
the biographies of eminent men, made interesting by
the inclusion of colourful details, were composed after
the style of the Kuo Yü.
The General History is therefore a grand symphony
in which all the known types of historical writing were
made use of and harmonised. Although the interest
was mainly a biographical one, we nevertheless see that
Ssu-ma Chien had a conception of society in its entirety
which was possible only when a nation had been com-

familiarity which came as a result of personal contact.
With the possible exception of Korea and of the southern

country under the jurisdiction of the Han Emperors that

All this geographical knowledge gave to Ssu-ma Chien
a comprehensiveness of view and a breadth of vision
which no historian before or since has been able to rival.
And further, it saved history from being merely a record
of court life and enabled it to embrace society in all its
aspects. We have, therefore, accounts of the economic
life of the people, the political structure of the nation,
all manner of social phenomena on which we depend for
our present day knowledge of the society of that time, re-
moved as it is from us by two thousand years, and lastly,
accounts of the distant peoples on the boundaries of the
extensive empire which are of infinite value to us to-day
as affording data of the early history of Sino-foreign
relations. It is no exaggeration to say that no historian
of any nation, ancient or modern, set himself to acquiring
first-hand information with the degree of thoroughness
that we find in Ssu-ma Chien. He heard, he saw and had
personal contact with all manner of people and all classes
of society before he felt qualified to write his history.
The ability to see society as an organic whole and to deal
with that whole in all its manifold phases is a contribution
of the greatest magnitude to Chinese historical thought.
In Ssu-ma Chien, as Dryden said of Chaucer, we have
"God's plenty." We have, in addition, wealth of ma-
terial, sanity of judgment, and an ability to interpret and
many other of those qualities which we usually associate
with the requirements of modern historiography.
With the death of this extraordinary personality,
Chinese history writing has not attained to the same
degree of eminence. The very scope of all succeeding
histories was considerably narrowed with the introduc-

tion of dynastic histories by Pan Ku. Ssu-ma Kuang, the
author of the Mirror of History of the Sung Dynasty,
was an exception, trying to regain the sweep of the
father of historians, but his work was essentially political.
During the Tang Dynasty, however, when the
institution of the official historian was revived with a
vengeance, we find the beginnings of another branch of
history writing which is exceedingly important for our
purposes. It was no longer possible to produce new
histories as valuable as the General History of Ssu-ma
Chien, but historical thought now assumed the form of
criticism, of philosophic or theoretic interpretation, and
the first one whom we wish to consider is Liu Chih-chi.
Born in 661, more than 700 years after the death of
Ssu-ma Chien, this critic of history had the opportunity
of acquainting himself with the tremendous number of
historical works which had been accumulating during all
these seven centuries, and was now in a position to give
a systematic and critical exposition of the whole subject
of historical writing. The result was a treatise on
history consisting of 49 chapters divided into two books
of 36 and 13 chapters respectively. It was published in
711, when he was fifty years old, and ten years before his
death, in 721. Like the illustrious Ssu-ma Chien, he was
destined to be an official historian who once more began
to assume very important functions during the Tang
Dynasty, but he was chagrined to find that valuable his-
torical books were no longer written, and so, instead of
writing a history himself, he thought that it would be best
to clear up our ideas on what history really is, on how it
should be written. In one chapter, he says, "Almost a
thousand years have elapsed since the beginning of the
Han Dynasty, and there have been many historians, but
the number of really good ones is indeed very limited."
Only one thing was therefore left for him to do, even
though it might antagonise himself against his fellow
scholars, and that was, he says in another chapter, "to lay
down the rules and to systematise all historical writings

of this poor scholar; that no true scholarship is possible
without a passion for unity. It is not sufficient simply
to have an analytical mind; it must at the same time be
broad, comprehensive and able to see the relationship

is worthy of our serious attention, but underlying our
world of thought and our world of experience, there is

great mind is a synthetic mind.
On the subject of analysis and of methodology, he

the real significance of a book?" And as regards
