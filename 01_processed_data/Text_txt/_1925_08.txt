SOME NEW LIGHT
ON THE LIFE AND TIMES OF
BISHOP DELLA CHIESA
By J. J. HEEREN, Ph.D.
The discovery of America, in 1492, by Spain had its
repercussion in Asia. Pope Alexander VI's "demarca-
tion line," first drawn in 1493, assigned to Spain all the
newly discovered lands west of this imaginary line and
to Portugal all those to the east of it. In 1585 Pope
Gregory XIII gave to the King of Portugal the "jus
patronatus" for China, to the Bishop of Macao the eccle-
siastical government of all China and to the Jesuits,
under the protection of Portugal, the missionary care of
China. Three years earlier, in 1582, the illustrious
Italian Jesuit, Matthew Ricci, had landed in South China.
Fifteen years later, in 1600, Clement VIII allowed orders
other than the Jesuits to take up missionary work in
China.¹
The most prominent among the newly admitted
orders seems to have been that of the Franciscans. In
1684 a young Venetian follower of St. Francis came to
China as co-adjutor of Fukien. Six years later this
Venetian, Bernadin della Chiesa, at the presentation of
the King of Portugal, was appointed Bishop of Peking,
but he did not receive the bull of his appointment until
nine years later.
When della Chiesa arrived in Peking, most probably
after May 30, 1700, the so-called rites controversy
between the Jesuits, on the one hand, and, on the other,
the Dominicans and the Franciscans was already under
way. This dispute and his difficulties with the Portu-
guese and the Jesuits under Portuguese control caused

During the spring and summer of 1924, while in
London, the writer secured copies of three of della
Chiesa's unpublished letters, now in the possession of the
British Museum. Two of these letters were written
within six months after the Bishop's arrival in the
capital. One is dated October 6, 1700, is written in della
Chiesa's own hand and is addressed to the Cardinals; the
second letter is dated October 7, 1700, is a joint letter of
Bishop della Chiesa and of Basilio di Gemona, Vicar
Apostolic of Shensi, is addressed to the Pope, and, with
the exception of the Bishop's own signature, is written in
the hand of Basilio di Gemona-the latter's signature
indicates that he wrote the letter. The third letter was
written from Lintsing, in Shantung, on October 22, 1701,
is addressed to the Cardinals and is in della's Chiesa's
own hand-writing.
With this brief introduction, we shall let these in-
tensely human documents speak for themselves. Better
than anything else these letters give us a clear idea of
Bishop della Chiesa's difficulties and of his own reactions.

Since della Chiesa was the bishop of the diocese of
Peking for twenty-one years, at a time in which the rites
controversy reached its height, it will be worth our while
to analyse the three preceding letters somewhat minutely
in order to see more clearly what new light they throw
upon the life and the times of the Bishop.
These letters bring out much more clearly than any
other documentary material the writer has seen that the
Bishop's difficulties were not so much with the Jesuits as
a whole as with the Portuguese faction of the Jesuits,
those who were of Portuguese nationality as well as those
of other nationalities who were under Portuguese control
