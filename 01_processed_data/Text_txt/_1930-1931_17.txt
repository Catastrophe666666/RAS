BAMBOOS IN CHINA
A Survey of the Morphological Characters
that Distinguish Species
By W. M. PORTERFIELD
The bamboos of China represent a group of useful
plants not to be equalled anywhere else in the world.
Among the novel sights that meet the eyes of travellers
to far eastern countries not the least are the growing
bamboos. The unique character and beauty of living
bamboos have never failed to impress the newcomer to
China. From Marco Polo down to the present, explorers
and tourists alike have remarked on the bamboos and on
the products made of them. Not only do the beauty and
useful character of the plant, but also some of the habits
discovered by those whose interest have carried them to
more extended observations strike the stranger as little
short of amazing. Robert Fortune (1857), during his re-
sidence among the Chinese, was sufficiently interested to
measure the daily growth of bamboo shoots and found
that "one could almost see them growing." He found
too that the greatest rate of growth took place during
the night. The frequent references to bamboo made by
writers are in part due to the fact that it is so widely
distributed and, therefore, so often comes to notice.
Every section of the country, with the exception of the
extreme north, has its representatives. The present 490
species of bamboo occupy a great deal of the tropical and
sub-tropical territory of the world, and in their adapta-
tions to different habitats and climates, furnish mute
testimony to a long evolutionary history. Though kin in
their universal love of moisture, they have radiated over
many types of environment from the low-lying plains of
China to the valleys and even to the high ranges that
bound the western frontier. It will be remembered how
Capt. Kingdon Ward (1924), while crossing the high
passes between the upper Salween and Taron rivers on

the Burma-Thibet border, had to fight his way through
dense brakes of snow-covered bamboo cane at 11,000 feet.
Wherever they are found bamboos serve as a great source
of satisfaction to the people. The materials furnished by
the various species have for centuries, in one way or
another, met every vital need as well as satisfied the wide-
spread human craving for aesthetic expression in liter-
ature and art. In a word, bamboo in China has provided
such scope for human exploitation that it is not exaggerat-
ing to speak of a once great bamboo civilization which,
even in this age of machinery and steel, survives in the
interior and country districts.
Before passing on to the main body of the paper, a
few prefatory remarks are needed in explanation of the
point of view which I have adopted. The title of this
paper plainly directs attention to kinds of bamboo rather
than to bamboo collectively as found in China. The char-
acter of bamboo as a plant I have discussed elsewhere
(1925) and, therefore, need not take the time now to
repeat myself. I limit my remarks strictly to material
bearing on "bamboos" rather than on bamboo, assuming
that the simple facts are already known. In so doing I
shall avoid the tediousness of definitions and introductions
which, in view of the fact that most persons have already
seen, heard or read about bamboo, I am sure are super-
fluous. The discussion of uses also has no concern with
the present paper. In the first place, to residents of China
the usefulness of bamboo is axiomatic; in the second, a
detailed account of the uses and of the kinds of bamboos
applied to such uses represents the subject-matter of a
book in itself. It is incumbent upon me, therefore, to
adhere to the principles I have laid down and embark
upon a discussion along lines which I have felt have long
been needed and upon which I have long wanted to write.
Bamboo furnishes very fine material not only for serious
consideration from the standpoint of cultivation as a crop
plant, but also for the study of certain physiological
problems of morphogenesis. But unless we know with
what species we are dealing, a great part of the value
is lost. In the past the descriptions of species have not
been carried through systematically enough for one to
identify the kinds with ease. Consequently my object is
to call attention to a possible basis for more systematic
descriptions along morphological lines that will make the
work of identification more possible and less a problem
than a pleasure.

With the exception of India, Brazil and Japan are
China's only competitors in bamboo production, and it is
not altogether improbable that many of the generalized
Japanese forms were originally migrants from the con-
tinent. Since the cultivation of many varieties has been
going on for centuries, it is difficult to state definitely
where these may have originated. They may have been
introduced from other regions and have since become
indigenous, as is the case with many of our garden
species. A very good illustration of the general fact is
the recent introductions of certain Chinese forms such as
Phyllostachys bambusoides Sieb. et Zucc.¹ into the United
States, where they have become fully established.
It is customary to differentiate bamboos on the basis
of their habit into two great groups, the monopodial and
the sympodial 2 types. The former are runners and form
great forests owing to the fact that the culms develop
from buds on the nodes of an extensive rhizome, while
the latter are clump-forming because the shoot-buds
spring from the basal nodes of the old culms. The useful
Chinese species are for the most part monopodial and
are, therefore, from lack of crowding more likely to
produce straight culms than the cespitose forms. As far
as I can be sure there are sixty-six species of bamboos
native to China. These comprise eight genera. Of the
species twenty-four belong to the genus Phyllostachys, all
of which are monopodial; fifteen belong to the genus
Arundinaria; and thirteen are cespitose forms belonging
to the genus Bambusa. The remaining species are scat-
tered among the genera, Sasa, Fargesia, Dendrocalamus,
Pseudostachyum, and Schizostachyum. Most of the east
China bamboos are Phyllostachides while around Canton
there are more species and varieties in the region com-
prised by Kiangsu and Chekiang provinces, seventeen of
them being Phyllostachides. Nine species of Phyl-
lostachys are of recognized commercial value and are sold
by local bamboo merchants. The rest of the forms are
ornamental and are to be found in gardens. A collection
of fourteen specimens sent down from Luchow in the
province of Szechuen does not reveal very clearly their
generic differentiation owing to the smallness of the
sample pieces.

The classification of bamboos is founded, as it is with
the rest of the grasses, on flower characteristics: the
general habit and character of the inflorescence, the
arrangement of the spikelets, the number of fertile and
sterile flowers in each spike, the number of stamens, the
number and character of the various orders of floral
bracts, the seed characters, and the vegetative cycle.
It is a logical and certainly a reliable basis of differentia-
tion, for of all plant characters those of the floral organs
are the least variable. There is in the case of bamboo,
however, an insurmountable difficulty, namely the great
scarcity of flowers. Only in comparatively few cases can
flowers be readily found wherewith to make identifications.
Brandis (1899) calls attention to the fact that by their
method of flowering, bamboos fall into three groups;
those which put out flowering shoots annually, those
which flower gregariously only after regularly recurring
periods of years, and lastly, those which flower at
irregular intervals. Those of the first category might
be identified by floral characteristics because of their
greater availability, but not so those of the other two.
While the specificity of flower characters has been
established, the same cannot be said of their periodicity.
The forms falling into the last two categories flower
rarely, and furthermore the time of flowering cannot as
yet be predicted with any degree of certainty. Investi-
gators like Brandis (1899), Mitford (1896), Seifriz
(1920) and Kawamura (1927) have contributed authentic
records of flowering for many species and have discussed
the causes of gregarious flowering as such, but until the
records are more complete and at least four flowering
dates, i.e. three vegetative periods, for each species shall
have been established, this class of data cannot be said to
determine specificity. It is true that in a few cases the
periodicity is fairly well known. For example, Bambusa
arundinacea Retz' growing in the West Coast districts of
India flowered in 1804, 1836, and in 1868, thus indicating
intervals of thirty-two years. To make our records
complete, we ought to know what happened in those
districts around 1900 and on the strength of that con-
fidently to predict the next flowering in 1932. There is
also a certain amount of positiveness regarding the peri-
odicity of the climbing bamboo, Chusquea abietifolia

Since the method of flower analysis for the specific
identification of our Chinese bamboos is difficult under the
circumstances, one must look for other dependable charac-
teristics by which to differentiate them. A careful study
reveals the fact that there are three general classes of cha-
racteristics which are significant: (1) The morphological
which concerns the general habit of the plant with special
reference to the form and structure of the cuim; (2) the
physiological which more specifically deals with the growth
characteristics; and (3) the ecological which covers its
distribution and special environmental relations.
The morphological characteristics are distributed
between the general habit, and the particular structure of
culm, branches, leaves, and shoots. The flowers I have
already discussed elsewhere in this connexion. The
general habit has been referred to in a previous paragraph
by the terms monopodial and sympodial. This is a
character of major importance and may be used to assist
in the identification of the plant with a major group. A
monopodial bamboo of East China might belong to one of
three different genera, Sasa, Arundinaria, or Phyllostachys,
while a sympodial form might belong to any one of several
genera headed by Bambusa. A further difference among
the monopodial forms can be made by distinguishing
between the loose-running types and those which form
dense brakes. An example of the former is our Mau tsok,
(P. pubescens) and of the latter the brush bamboo
(P. nidularia.) Height, though a variable character

While the diameter of the culm is correlated with
height, not all tall forms are necessarily thick. Slender-

modified by wood quality. All arborescent forms

diameter in cross-section as it were, the wood is harder,

the wood and additional deposits of minerals like silicates
in the periphery, so that the culms are more rigid and
therefore more stiffly erect. This is the "rock bamboo”
just mentioned exactly. An idea of the stony hardness

Yunnan were started by the rasping of bamboo culms on

cauline or pre-eminently foliar in nature? Some types of

greater degree than others and hence it may be used as a
secondary mark of distinction. It may be observed that
Arundinaria and Bambusa species do not exhibit this
tendency. In origin it is undoubtedly connected with
another characteristic of internodes which is of marked

the pressure exerted serves not only to depress the
adjacent face of the expanding internode, but actually
also to divert slightly the axis of elongation. P. nigra
and P. nidularia illustrate these two characters very well.

A very interesting character of the culm, which
bears close examination for identification purposes, is the
length of the internodes. The observer will note that the
distance between nodes varies in different parts of the
culm. A study of this point will show that this is not
haphazard but follows a definite progression. Starting
from the basal nodes, which are short, the length of the
internodes increases to a point usually below the middle
of the culm and then gradually diminishes as the tip is
reached. A chance measurement of internode length,
therefore, will indicate nothing. In general all one can
say is that the internodes are short or that they are long.
But this factor can be made specific, and so of value, by
careful serial measurements which when averaged will,
provided there are enough samples, furnish a specific
index of internode length.

if definite, is usually constant and is an important fact to
note. The fact that adventitious shoots do or do not
appear anew at the nodes each year is likewise note-
worthy. Our Phyllostachides have either two or three

All descriptions of bamboos make careful note of the
leaves. To the casual observer bamboo leaves all look
alike, but there are decided differences. Size and color
are the most obvious characters. In general a bamboo
leaf is oblong lanceolate in shape with an acuminate apex
and tapering or rounded base, but there are variations
and all sorts of distortions of the average configuration.
Arundinaria graminea Makino, for instance, a species
found in Jessfield Park, has a linear lanceolate 22.8 centi-
meters long by 1 centimeter wide, very grass-like as its
name implies, while beside it in the same plot of ground
stands a dwarf species, P. ruscifolia, whose leaves are the
direct antithesis of the other. In the latter the leaves
are disproportionately foreshortened so that, though
short, they are still broad. In shape they are ovate lan-
ceolate and in length 9.5 centimeters by 2.1 centimeters
wide. Sasa tessellata has the largest leaves of any bam-
boo. They are both broad and long, being 45 centimeters
long and 10 centimeters wide. Queerly enough, in con-
trast to those of this dwarf species the arborescent
P. pubescens possesses small narrow leaves.

Of major importance is the character of the venation.
By holding up the leaf of a Phyllostachys species to the
light one can just make out the tiny cross-veins that con-
nect the larger parallel veins running the length of the
leaf. This is known as tessellated venation and is another
mark by which larger groups can be distinguished. For
example, the leaves of the Bambusa forms are not tessel-
lated. They are instead striately veined, i.e., they possess
only parallel veins. It is claimed that foliar tessellation
has an ecological significance and that those forms which
exhibit this feature are hardier than those which do not.
Stipular hairs or bristles flanking the base of the leaf
petiole are present in some species and absent in others
and therefore have a descriptive value. These are easily
knocked off and some fall off naturally, so that the end of
the growing season is the best time for determining this
trait, and not later.

size, the attributes of the protective sheaths must be
carefully noted. It is really this part of the shoot that
tells the story. The sheath is a modified leaf base and the
limb or pseudophyll which surmounts it is the homologue
of a leaf. While the internode is elongating, the sheath
leaf functions, but when it ceases to grow, the sheath
undergoes abscission, that is, it dies and falls off. Some
sheaths, however, are very persistent and never do become
detached, for very much the same reason doubtless that the
leaves of the oak tree fail to fall when they die. Some
Arundinarias are like this. Color markings, texture, and
surface-covering are traits peculiar to the sheath proper,
while average length, color, and habit describe the limb.
At the juncture of limb and sheath is the ligule, a mem-
branous collar that fits snugly against the surface of the
internode. This may be high or low, its upper edge either
entire or irregularly cut and lobed, and, as in the case of
the leaves, its flanks may be occupied by stipules known
as suricles which are usually fringed with bristles or
hairs. Who, having once seen the great shoots of P. pu-
bescens with their broad blotched furry sheaths, can ever
forget them? Just as striking is the annual spectacle
staged in April when like the emerging spears from
Cadmus's crop of dragons' teeth, multitudes of spikelike
shoots of Sasa albo-marginata Mak. et Shib. push up out
of the soil. Finally edibility and the season of germina-

Some shoots are identified with good food and some are
not. The latter are woody or bitter. Among the most
delicate and choice for eating are the shoots of P. quad-
rangularis.

cal significance gives the most faithful indication of the
specific nature of shoot growth in bamboos. A tabular
view of the K values for our Chinese species would de-
monstrate, as much as any other form of analysis, true
specificity.
The distribution of each species of bamboo in China is
as yet unknown. In general it is governed by ecological
factors comprised within the organic and inorganic en-
vironments and by the responsive capacity of the species.
Moisture, altitude, seasonal change, alternation of wet
and dry periods, soil, vegetable and animal life including
man all have a definite bearing. Without discussing the
point further I shall present below the list of Chinese
bamboos together with what facts are known of their dis-

Altogether there are eight genera and sixty-six
species, counting those introduced. A list of Chinese
names of local bamboos is appended to this paper.
In conclusion I should like to repeat that this paper
is not meant to be a taxonomic work on bamboos of
China, but merely constitutes an approach to such a
work in the future. I hope the outline of the problem of
specific identification has been made clear enough and the
appeal for systematic morphological descriptions strong
enough to enlist the attention of some young men among
the ranks of scientists. The way is now open for the
student adventurer with ambition and I think the present
survey is sufficient to make him realize the magnitude of
his task as well as the great need for it. We look for-
ward to the time not far distant when we may come to
know more familiarly the bamboos of China.
