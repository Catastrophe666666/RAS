BURIAL CUSTOMS IN SZ-CHUAN
By Thomas Torrance.
As is generally known, the burial of the dead in China,
especially if the person be aged or wealthy, is a most elaborate
affair. First, there is the laying out of the corpse with its
many details of ceremonial observance. It is followed by
the carrying in of the ponderous tree-shaped coffin, the
"housing" of the deceased therein and a renewed worship of
the ancestral tablet. Then in due course there are the
long chantings of emancipation masses by Taoist and
Buddhist priests, and the careful selection of a grave-site
where the dead person may rest in peace under those
"Feng-shui" currents of heaven and earth which are
felicitous, and out of reach of those which are evil and
disturbing. On the funeral day a long procession of white-
clad mourners, trumpet blowers, banner carriers, gong beaters,
paper cash scatterers and heathen priests winds slowly along
to the marked spot in the field or on the hillside where the
coffin is duly placed in position, the soil piled up around it,
and he (or she) is said to be decently buried. Such, in brief,
is the present-day vogue of burial in China.
Now all this is so different to our ways of burial that we
naturally enquire the "how" and the "why" of it all and take
closer notice of these Chinese interment practices. In this
paper, therefore, I propose to notice briefly the modern burial
customs and methods of the Chinese, and from them trace

First, let us see the curious practice this people follows of
providing the dead with an outfit for the next world.
Occasionally money and valuables are concealed in
Sz-chuan graves, but only the rich do this. The rule is only
to place in the grave a jar filled with the ashes of a passport
to Hades and another with burnt paper money. On the day
of interment they burn paper models of houses, effigies of
men and animals, paper forms of sedans, boxes, clothing,
furniture, household utensils, weapons, and all articles of
interest and use down even to opium pipes and packs of
cards. In addition, big quantities of mimic cash and mock
silver are transported through the sacred medium of the flames
to Hades for the use of the person gone there. This custom,
as a whole, received recognition during the T'ang dynasty,
though, it is said, the burning of paper money extends back
to the times of the states of Wei and Tsin.
The reason of it has its roots in the Chinese idea of filial
piety. They consider it their duty to serve their progenitors,
dead as well as alive, and to make no difference in the manner
of the service. Their sires are dependent on them for rest,
sustenance, and continuance of moral glory. Though they
have gone to Hades their interests have not ceased to be one
with the living. Humanity past, present and future is one
and indivisible; byegone ages are linked up with all the
succeeding ones in a continuous family bond. Such
conception demands that there shall be no transgression of
reverence, obedience or any of the laws of filial duty. A son
has the power of making happy the spirits of his forefathers.
He can even arise and redeem, by some act of merit, the past

reputations of all who have gone before him. It is no wonder

prevails of worshipping at the tombs and invoking the

Honourable sepulture constitutes the first act of piety to
the dead. Unless care is exercised to find a grave-site
immune from evil disturbance the spirit must wander
restlessly hither and thither without a fixed abode. Under
these beggarly conditions he may return to vex the living
with sickness, blasting and mildew. Apprehension of these
evils makes the duty also one of self-interest. What could
better lend strength to the practice of giving the dead a grand
burial? Or keep men from forgetting to supply them with all
the necessaries of existence in the unseen regions? So natural
feeling is aided by superstitious fear and the force of public
opinion to make ancestral worship the strongest religious
force in China. Every year the graves are visited with
offerings of food, drink and cash paper. As far as it requires
the tomb is repaired, and a favourite plan of accumulating
merit to cancel some sin that lies on the conscience consists in
forwarding supplies to those spirits and of piling up a little
more soil on their graves who, from neglect or death of
descendants, have no one to render them the essential
service.
The Chinese mostly inter their dead in mound fashion.
The coffin is placed on the level of the ground, or a very
little way below it, and the soil piled up around it till the
covering reaches a height of three, four, five and sometimes
ten feet. These grave mounds outside cities may be
counted by the tens of thousands. They occupy large
stretches of ground. Had they been left from olden days, and
fresh places sought for the continuous burials, it is open to
question whether by this time there would have been any

It is difficult now to say what was the prevailing manner
of interment in Sz-chuan during the dynasties previous to
the Ming. There are no written data to guide the search
for information. That there were several methods is certain.
The simple raising of earthly tumuli was one. Deep burial
with a stone slab over the grave and the raising of a heap of
earth over that again was a second. This method is still
followed in one district. The building of a brick arch or
stone vault, under or above ground, was a third. The use of
a cave hewn in the solid rock was a fourth.
From time immemorial, when the Chinese who were
paying their funeral duties to the dead could afford it they
have provided the coffin with an outer shell. This fact
explains the diverse ways of burial. The middle and upper
classes have built brick or stone receptacles for the shielding
and better preservation of the coffin, while the poor have
had to be contented with committing it simply to the
embrace of mother earth. Mencius gives the reason of this
as "satisfying the natural feelings of the heart." He meant
perhaps that it was a thought pleasing to the mind that
thereby the remains were longer kept from dissolution, or
that they were thus protected inviolate. An old Sz-chuanese
custom, reaching back as far as the Han dynasty, was to
deposit quicksilver in the tomb. It was thought to retard
the natural process of decay or putrefaction, and often
holes were made under the stone floor of the vault for a
similar reason. In many instances also the coffins were
suspended by irons in the tomb. The object intended was
the same. In the "Annals of the Feudal States" we find
a reference to this. King Hsiang tells the Prince of Chin
that the old Monarchs decreed the exact burial customs, and

From what has now been said it will be seen that the
burial customs of the Chinese present many features in
common with those of other ancient tribes and nations, and also
that their present-day funeral rites are relics of ancient usages.
In Western Sz-chuan, principally on the banks of the Min
river, there are numerous caves cut in the solid rock of the
cliffs and hillsides. These were supposed by foreigners to be
the ancient dwelling-places of the aboriginal inhabitants of
Sz-chuan until the beginning of the year 1908, when I first
discovered them to be, at least in the majority of cases, tombs.
Further investigations have confirmed this opinion. Here
are thousands of Chinese places of interment dating from the
time of the North Tong dynasty, A.D. 960-1126 as far back
as the Han dynasty B.C. 206-A.D. 23. And here is an ideal
field for the study of the ancient burial customs of the Chinese,
and through them of the common life and thought of the
people.
As a result of my investigations, covering more than a year,
it is established beyond all reasonable doubt that these caves,
in the Northern section at least, were tombs of the Chinese and
not of the so called "Man-Tze," or aboriginal inhabitants. Of
the Southern section, as yet I cannot speak, because I have not
had time to investigate fully; as this article proceeds the reader
will see the reasonableness of this conclusion.

Sandals through all the succeeding centuries since then
have not changed the very slightest from the original pattern.
Head-gear and modes of doing up the hair betray a contrast,
but men and women bowed, acknowledged and welcomed

the custom of the race. The likenesses of birds, ospreys for

prosperity, give strong reason to the fact of the Chinese looking
back with pride on their beloved Han dynasty and regarding
it as the high water mark of their national glory.

Whatever may be thought concerning their meaning,
most will concede that the discovery is one of value in giving
back to us such a fine picture of an ancient Chinese chariot.
My first impression was that it was a scene of return from war.
In those days the Chinese were at continual warfare with the
tribes on every side of them. Their territorial expansion
depended on their prowess in battle.
So the martial spirit burned keen, especially in those
parts in the immediate vicinity of the border lines. And
Western Sz-chuan as such must have seen many a bloody
battle. I thought therefore that here was a tomb of some-
one who had gained renown in some of the border fights,
and this showed either the submission of the conquered
people, or, what was more likely, the joy whereby he was
welcomed home.
The cave in which these bricks were found had been
visited and rifled at the time it became exposed. All the cash
having been removed, there was nothing to record its date,
but an image found in it bears so close a resemblance to
another found in a cave several hundred yards away that
you can see both had been cast in one mould. And with
this other image was found the rim of an urn having handles
of the shape of old fork cash. If this may be regarded as
an indication, then the bricks date somewhere very near
the beginning of the Christian era.
Strange to say, few caves are found more than a mile
from the river side. The idea seems to have prevailed that
the "feng-shui" there was good. But they do not always
face the river, large numbers of them having their opening
towards the opposite direction. Wherever the rock was
suitable there they excavated their houses for the dead. So
closely situated are they sometimes to one another that in the

One thing remains inexplicable. How is it that the
people do not know that these caves are the tombs of ancient
Chinese? Perhaps the ruthless exterminating campaign
of Chang-hsien-chong at the close of the Ming dynasty explains
it. The province was then almost depopulated, and had to be
re-peopled from settlers from other parts of China. And
they, coming as strangers, naturally knew nothing of the
local history of the place. Some day I hope to say

In finishing this article I cannot help recording my
sorrow at the work of havoc and destruction the Chinese have
wrought in these caves. They seem to take a wanton delight
in destroying coffins and smashing all the burnt-clay articles
they can find. The sarcophagi are taken out and sold to
rich people for water cisterns. Thus their carvings are
speedily defaced. Old graves are not always respected. I
have seen the vault of one taken down for the sake of the
