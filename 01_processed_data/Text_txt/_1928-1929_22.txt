At the moment it is my duty to say that the learned
geologist of the Shanghai College, Dr. George B. Cressey,
presented a lecture at a Science Congress at Tokio
in 1926. His theme was nearly the same as mine
of to-day, The Climate of the Glacial Period in Eastern
Asia. It is an excellent work, worthy of its learned
author. But this subject, the Ice Age and its peculiari-
ties in Asia is immense. Not in two lectures nor even
in several big volumes could it be exhausted, so I
hope I can find some remarks which are out of the sphere
of his very instructive lecture. This is easier, as, in
the main, Dr. Cressey is a geologist, whilst I am, rather,
a biologist.
It is not impossible that the consideration of the
biological side of the Quaternary Ice period will help us
to approach with a modest step nearer to the solution of
at least some parts of that great enigma which remains
to-day, the Ice Period. Numbers of volumes have been
written about the Ice Age and Ice Ages. Generally,
when the Ice Age is spoken of one understands the
series of glacial and interglacial periods, which constitute
the Quaternary Ice Age. Incidentally I remark that
there have been many other Ice Ages far back into the
Archaic Period. We can say that the Quaternary Ice
period is still in being. The Antarctic, Greenland, great
parts of Norway, are still covered with mighty ice sheets,
which, as it seems, have in Greenland a height up to
2,000 yards. We can very truly say, we are still in the
Quaternary Ice Age, but in its vanishing stage.
As it has been said above, the greater part of Europe
and North America have been covered with an ice layer
of about 1,000 yards. There are unmistakable signs and
traces that the enormous precipitations and very great
lowering of the temperature have extended over the whole
earth. Evidence of this is known from Africa, India,
South America, and other countries.
These enormous precipitations have produced the
great glaciations of Europe and America. I quote from
the aforesaid lecture of Professor Cressey, that Europe
had 55 per cent. of its territory covered with ice and
North America even 82 per cent.
And now I come to the peculiarity of the
Ice Age of Asia. The north of Siberia is also
now perhaps the coldest territory in the world,
but it has a cold age and no Ice Age, it is not
covered with ice. It seems that it has been the same

Anyhow, one class of explanation is flatly contradicted

must have had the same effect on all the northern con-

But I have said that this lecture will treat principally
with the biological side of the Ice Age, especially in Asia.
When these enormous precipitations, and with them
the cloudy cool summers and cold winters came, the whole

one of the earlier Ice periods, that for the first time,
traces of fire-places have been found. It is very probable
that it has been the cold of these periods which taught
early man the use of clothes and the use of fire.
Another condition contributed to this great discovery,

On this occasion I make a short remark about
another great biological issue of another very remote Ice
Age. I have said above that the Quaternary Ice Age, in
whose last stage we are at present living, had several
precursors. One of the most important of them, or
perhaps one the importance of which we think to
recognize, was the Permian Ice Age at the end of the
Carboniferous Age. It is very probable that this was
the time when mammals and birds began to separate
from the common lineage of low reptiles. We must
bear in mind the three principal and distinctive
marks of these two superior classes of Vertebrates.
These three marks are (1) they are well clothed with fur
or feathers, (2) they have internal heating—their warm
blood, and (3) they show extreme care for their offspring,
internal development of the mammals, nesting of the
birds. These three fundamental qualities, I repeat, to
be well clothed, well heated and to show great care for
the progeny, are eminently defensive measures against
cold. Until to-day, after millions of years of evolution,
the reptiles cannot live in the arctic, and are poorly repre-
sented in the moderate climates. Also until to-day,
mammals and birds are in a flourishing condition in arctic
climate, they live and multiply well, and develop in
numerous species to the mightiest types. Polar bear,
musk ox, arctic fox, and walrus are examples of this.
It is well known in what numbers birds live on many
arctic islands.
But the biological situations of the whole of any
unit in time or in space are extremely complicated,
interwoven between all living creatures, the organic
world with the inorganic and vice versa. It is nearly
surpassing human mental capacity to extricate these
Gordian knots even in the present. How much more
difficult is it for the past.
I will give an example. It is conceivable that the
elephant should disappear in cultivated, densely populated
countries. It is natural that such a big consumer
must disappear where the vegetation becomes scarce.
Generally speaking, the elephant is living still in
countries where he finds a warm climate, an abundant
vegetation and not too much persecution from his only
enemy, man.
So we understand that he is still living in India,
Indo-China, and Central Africa. Like the other big
mammals, he never migrated to New Guinea, where he

But another, more interesting, question is this. Why
has Neanderthal man disappeared? This variety of
mankind seems to have been better adapted to primitive
life-conditions than present mankind. His senses, visual,
auditive and olfactory, were much more developed than
ours. In his brain the centre of these senses was much

his descendent; he is not our ancestor. Why has the

same regions. These examples seem to have no connec-

they are necessary to show how far the present Science
of Natural History still is from the explanation of the
connections between the elements of the organic world.
It is very probable that researches in these directions
about the connections between the life of different organic
realms, classes and species, between the organic and
inorganic world, will give explanations of many things
which are open questions to-day. One of these questions
is, why is Northern Siberia not ice-covered to-day, and
has not been ice-covered during all the hundreds of
thousands or perhaps millions of years of the quaternary
series of ice periods? And perhaps questions of even
infinitely greater interest will be brought some steps
nearer to solution by patient researches, by the never
tiring collection of well observed data and facts.

mammoth is a variety of the elephant. The phylogenetic
story of the proboscis family is one of the best known.
It gives some ideas about the reciprocal action of animals,
plants and their surroundings. The oldest known
ancestor of the elephant was a digging animal of the size
of a pig. Every peculiar quality in any animal is always
an adaptation to its surroundings. If a herbivorous
animal develops digging qualities, there must exist bulb-
forming plants, like potatoes. But the plants develop
bulbs as a defense against drought, or against grazing
herbivores. The plants invented subterranean refuges
long before our armies. Drought and great grazing
animals transformed the Eocene forest to the Oligocene
aridity. Later on the forest reoccupied the earth, and
the ancestors of the elephant became the tree-eating
mastodon. The trees answered with defensive measures.
They protected their trunks with thick bark up to the
height to which the great herbivores reached. Until
to-day, the few trees with thin bark have the latter
poisonous or of a bitter taste. Against the bark pro-
tection of the trees the elephant developed his enormous
size, his high columns of legs, his long-reaching trunk.
For the same reason the giraffe developed its great
height. The mammoth is as a tree-eater even further
developed than the elephant. His shoulders and head
are higher. To his grasping trunk he added his long,
round-bent, mighty tusks, not merely usable as weapons,
but excellent tools to pull down the big branches of trees.
So the mammoth became the mightiest tree-destroyer of
the Quaternary period. There is only one enemy of the
forest, whose destructive capacities surpass by far even
those of the mammoth. This is what the great classifier
of the organic world, Linnaeus, called very wrongly
Homo sapiens, "The Wise Man." He should have called
him with much more right Homo destruens, devastator,
"The Destroying Man."
Returning to the struggle between proboscid and
forest, these mighty tree-destroyers, the mammoths,
lived in veritable armies in Quaternary Northern Asia.
But the forest, as a whole, has not accepted the defeat.
It brought new defensive weapons into the Eternal
Struggle. It invented the resinous coniferous trees,
which even to-day are not digestible to most herbivores.

The resinous trees victoriously conquered enormous

But a very great question presents itself at this

amount of the quaternary precipitations had a great part,
perhaps the decisive part, in the origin of the Ice Period.
Perhaps it may be that the great victory of the

elements of the organic world did not cease with the

In the primeval leaf-tree forest, with daily rain,
primitive man could never learn the use of the mightiest
of all human tools and arms, the use of fire. Not only
for the palaeolithic or rather eolithic man, even for the
man of ancient written history, the maintenance of fire
was a very heavy task.
The Vestals in ancient Rome, the Eternal Lamp,
first in the Pharaonic Temples, then in the Jewish Divine
Services, and many actual Rituals show how important
was and what difficulties attended the maintenance of fire
even in advanced civilizations. How could primitive
man in an unceasingly wet forest, without a piece of
dry wood, maintain a fire? The fire could not even
originate in such a forest.
With the propagation of the resinous trees the
situation changed. Although until now, we have no
direct proofs even of the existence of man at the end
of the Pliocene Period, it is to-day generally admitted
that man not only existed at that time, but had
already attained a very advanced development. Several
of the resinous woods burn very well even in the green
stage. The great, new planted forests in the south of
France, mostly resinous trees, and the coniferous forests
of Canada, suffer every year from conflagrations.
Lightning or volcanic eruptions could sometimes
light a resinous tree. The primitive man was in a

In North America an analogous phenomenon can be
observed. Where the Aztec civilization and the following
Spaniard conquista was established, Mexico, South
California, South Arizona and much more, there are
steppes and deserts. More to the north, up to Alaska,
civilization has come in the last century, and only there
are forests, abundant rain and magnificent glaciers on
the high mountains. I believe that the western coast of
the whole of America is an example, that in the same
geographical formation it is the existence or absence of
intensive human civilization that is the determining
factor of forest or desert, abundant rain or dryness.
Now we are a step nearer the facts about the absence
of glaciation in North Asia. But our investigation must
go further. In my preceding lecture I have already

the Cretaceous saurians. With the use of fire as a

suicide.

rapid industrial destruction of the forest travels on the
same road.
