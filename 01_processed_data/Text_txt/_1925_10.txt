By E. Т. С. WERNER
[The word rebuttal (root, old French boter, to thrust)
is not intended to convey any idea of a pugnacious or keratose
feeling on my part toward those who have misunderstood my
views in connection with certain sinological questions. I
regard co-workers in the vast field of sinology as friends,
and to entertain antagonistic sentiments toward them would
be as foolish as for a bricklayer to entertain enmity toward
his fellow on the scaffolding. For, in this immense and most
fascinating task we are, as I have written elsewhere, all
builders, not completers, and the feeling of gratitude seems
to me to be the appropriate one to be entertained toward
those who are helping in the great work. Only when, in
laying his brick, a co-worker dislodges mine, or twists it in
such a way as to give it an ugly or false aspect, it becomes a
duty to point out how it has been wrongly jolted and what
its proper position is. And in making these corrections I
discard the old (and, I could wish, obsolete) idea of intel-
lectual autocracy, of knocking over all your rivals and
setting, or trying to set, yourself up in their place: an idea
more appropriate to the age of Assurbanipal than to that of
modern civilization, and, after re-adjusting the position of
my titled brick, resume my patient labour in a happy and
friendly spirit.]
The Origin of Chinese Religion.—In Vol. LV (1924)
of the Journal, in an interesting and valuable article
entitled "Cult of the Chʻêng Huang Lao Yeh," Mrs.
Ayscough says that "Werner, de Groot, du Bose, and
their school maintain that it ('the character of religion in
ancient China'] was a fetishism, an animism pure and
simple."
Thereat I make my first friendly butt. Animism is
not my ism. I have nowhere maintained, or even stated,
that animism pure and simple, or animism of any kind,
was the character of religion in ancient China.
Ascription to me of the same theory on this subject
as that held by De Groot can, in view of the following

passage in my China of the Chinese (p. 241), only be
ascribed to an oversight:
"Those sinologists who, like Dr. De Groot, argue for the
theory of universal animism as earlier than the theory of the
ghost or other self, do so in face of the existing evidence, and
in contradiction of their own statement that the Chinese
mind in early times was unable to form abstract conceptions."
In the next paragraph of her article Mrs. Ayscough
changes her venue by referring to "the 'original religion'
as far as we can know."
The "character of religion in ancient China" and
the "original religion" are two different things.
(The
"as far as we can know" will be considered presently.)
To imply that they are the same is to imply that birth
and adolescence are the same.
Mrs. Ayscough says that the latter (original religion)
is "that described in the records, and taught by Con-
fucius."
The original religion of the Chinese is the religion
of the Chinese when they first had a religion. Therefore,
since we do not know who or where the Chinese were at
that time, it might be said that we cannot say what the
original religion of the Chinese was. That is not so.
Of this more anon.
To say that the "original religion' as far as we
can know, is that described in the records, and taught
by Confucius; that which is to-day the firm basis of
Chinese belief," is to imply that from the time of the
birth of the Chinese (qua Chinese) people down to the
sixth century B.C. their religion had remained as it was
originally. That is to say, that Confucius taught a
religion which was unique in this, if in nothing else, that
it had remained unchanged for thousands (perhaps many
thousands) of years before his time and for two thousand
years more down to the present day. It is also to imply
that in the time of the records and of Confucius a time
of a relatively high state of civilization-there was still
taught and practised the religion (what that was we shall
see later) of the Chinese at their earliest and most primi-
time stage of their history as a social aggregate. This
is true in one sense but not in another.
Now if Mrs. Ayscough or anyone else would contend
that religion had one origin in the case of China and a
different origin in all other parts of the world, the ques-
tion cannot be profitably discussed. The only course in

that case would be to wait until she or they have published
their arguments and substantiated them sufficiently to
make them worth discussion. I for one hold that all
religions had one origin and one only-as in the remote
future all religions will coalesce into one.
This brings me to the point which especially concerns
me. It is proverbially difficult to outrun a lie, and though
I certainly have no intention of using so harsh a term
with regard to Mrs. Ayscough's misstatement, still that
misstatement has had a year's start, and it is not only
possible, but highly probable, that many who have read
her idea of my idea of the origin of Chinese religion will
never see this disclaimer. Moreover, the outrunning will
be all the more difficult from the fact that the misstate-
ment was not only published in this Journal, but was
first made in a public address delivered in London. It
was thus broadcasted on at least two sides of the world.
To say of me that "Werner
maintains that
the character of religion (why not 'religion' simply?) in
ancient China was a fetishism, an animism pure and
simple," is much the same as calling an evolutionist a
creationist, a Protestant a Roman Catholic, a Conserva-
tive a Liberal (or, having regard to the present micros-
copical nature of the latter party, it would perhaps be
more apt to say a Labourite), an idealist a realist, a
biogenesis-ist an abiogenesis-ist (or any other of the
great historical antinomies). Those who have followed
the tremendous controversies regarding the origin of
religion know with what vehemence they raged. In this
great polemic between men of great philosophical and
scientific knowledge and training, the ultimate question
was one of animism versus the ghost-theory or the
"second self" or "double of the dead." The latter was
unfortunate in not having a name as happy as the
"animism” of its rival, and thus failed to a certain
extent in gaining as ready an acceptance with the oi
polloi, with that large class which takes an interest or
dabbles in these matters without any deep study or
technical knowledge; but this made no difference to it in
the scientific world.
Put in a nutshell, the question is one of relative
priority; and the weight of evidence and argument was
overwhelmingly in favour of the ghost theory. This fact
stands, in spite of the frequent assertion in many scien-
tific and quasi-scientific books, periodicals, and news-
papers that the basis of religion is animism. During the

controversy even such a great authority as Dr. E. B.
Tylor, the celebrated anthropologist, who originally took
the side of animism, and who had read more books dealing
with primitive peoples than any man living or dead, was
converted to the ghost theory.
Primitive man does not regard anything as animated
without reason. When he does so regard anything, the
reason he gives is not something abstract, which he cannot
understand, but something concrete, which he can under-
stand. He argues not in metaphysical terms, but in
terms of his own experience. His experience tells him
that the world is full of duplicates of actually-existing
things. His shadow, all shadows, follow their originals
and do as they do; his face, reflected in the pond, twists
as he twists it; his mind his "second-self "-wanders
away while he sleeps, and does many things which he
believes are realities and not mere figments of the ima-
gination. The "second-self" is actually doing those
things, just as he himself used to do them: he takes it as
a reality, not as his fancy. It certainly is no metaphy-
sical abstraction. One of the beliefs of the Chinese con-
stantly thrust upon one's attention, is that the spirits
were once men; and that the material soul was produced
first. Manes live entirely on charity; the food, etc.,
burnt for their use are material things of this world.
Without these sustained supplies, they die. Were there
a belief in an inherent animism in the spirits, keeping
them alive independently of the material world, these
things would be unnecessary; and the living Chinese
would incidentally be saved vast annual expenditure. So
frugal a people, pressing hard on their means of exist-
ence, would not have been slow to take advantage of such
a situation, had they believed in its existence.
When the "second-self" returns to the body, the latter
awakes. Death (as the primitive man knows it) is merely
the failure of the "second-self" to find its way back; and
after appealing to it in various ways to return, he pre-
serves the bodies of the dead in the hope that his exper-
ience of these returns (after fainting, coma, catalepsy,
etc.) will be repeated. His explanation of these pheno-
mena is not any animism, of which he knows nothing,
but a dualism constantly experienced by him in other
words, the ghost theory is the basis of his religious ideas.
Being concerned here only with the origin of religion,
it is unnecessary to follow out the various ramifications
which grow from this one seed.

Holding, as I do, that religions everywhere have one
origin, and one only, I contend that the origin of Chinese
religion is to be found in this idea of the double self-the
idea arising from the unscientific interpretation of
natural phenomena. And this ghost-theory is not that
of animism.
Coming now to the second issue, that "the 'original
religion' as far as we can know, is that described in the
records, and taught by Confucius," there must obviously
be a difference between the primitive dualism above des-
cribed and the relatively high stage religion had reached
in the sixth century B.C.
The chief differences would be in unification and
elaboration. As the several tribes and States were being
gradually moulded into one nation, so their religious ideas
were coalescing also. Mrs. Ayscough says the religion
as found in the Classics is "strangely uncomplicated."
I hold that it is not uncomplicated as compared with its
pre-Classical original simplicity; but the use of the word
"strangely" by one who thinks she is dealing with a
primitive state seems to betray want of acquaintance with
the law of progress. If we contrast the religion in its
earliest infancy, when each family had (might almost be
said to be) a god unto itself, and the god-general was a
relatively weak and not-highly-exalted ancestor, called
upon not at fixed times in regularly-organized ceremonies,
but only in times of danger or distress, and reviled,
beaten, and even deposed when disobedient, with the
evolved state reached in the Classical period, with its
animism and fetichism, its elaborate ancestor-worship,
its T'ien and Shang Ti, all-powerful and awe-inspiring,
its "Lord of all the Spirits" and his royal prerogatives,
its State hierarchy of greater and lesser gods, its high-
priest and priests, temples, altars, shrines with spirit-
tablets of ancestors, its chief and supplementary sacri-
fices, including the elaborate seasonal sacrifices designed
to maintain union of family connections, its Assessors
and Personators, its detailed codes of ceremonial and
hierarchy of ceremonialists, its prayers, fasting, ablu-
tions, posturing, hymns, etc., we can hardly reasonably
conclude that the religion as described in the Classics and
taught by Confucius is the "original religion" of the
Chinese. An analogy to that argument would be that
Druidism was the religion of England in the time of the
wars of the feudal barons. It would be as rational to
suppose that we are witnessing in the Classics the origin

I said above that the statement that the Chinese
religion had remained unchanged from the earliest times
to the time of Confucius, and onward to the present day,
was true in one sense, but not in another. It is true in

ancestor-worship in one and the same stage of its evolu-
tion. To use a colloquialism, the two are not to be named
in the same week.¹
Idols. I come now to another assertion in the same
article, namely, "Werner goes so far as to make the
astounding statement (for which he gives no authority)
'Idols are spoken of in the earliest times."

in a summary in forty-six pages of four thousand years

Buddhists came to China in 250 B.C.; that Han Kao Tsu
made an image of the Goddess of Fire; that the wu
priests had their temples, containing images of gods, to
whom they paid worship and offered sacrifices; that the

having a little more elbow-room, I can state that theory

interpreted as including astonishment not only at my
statement, but at the idea that there were idols in the
earliest times, perhaps she will in the end come to be
astonished not at the idea of there being idols, but at the
idea of there being none.
Here again there is a question of terms. First, as
to "the earliest times." My summary dealing with a

period of four thousand years, the phrase may rightly be
taken as referring to pre-Chou, pre-Confucian, or even
pre-Han times. Second, as to the meaning of "idol."
The pre-idol is the sacrificed-to preserved body-pre-
served in the hope that the absent second-self may yet
return. The first idol proper is the worshipped figure
of the deceased, made partly of his remains and partly
of other substances. Subsequently, the figures of the
deceased are made wholly of other material or materials.
The idea is, that if the second-self can re-enter the corpse,
it can also re-enter an image of it.
Images, placed in the grave with the deceased, and
later in the crypt with the corpse or the coffin, are later
still placed on or in front of the grave itself. That they
are believed to be imbued with life, is easily proved from
the actions performed with regard to them; they are the
embodiments of spirits which must be propitiated. They
are in fact idols of the deceased and of those appointed as
guardians of his tomb (whatever form-human or sub-
human-those idols may have in any particular epoch or
part of the world). In some cases, tablets, stones, etc.,
are substituted for the more elaborated images. Where
they are worshipped in the belief that they are inhabited
by the manes, they are as much idols as the completely
anthropomorphic images. What, in fact, is an idol but
an anthropomorphized, spirit-inhabited tablet of clay,
wood, stone, or metal?
The existence of human effigies in connection with
the belief in the second-self scarcely needs showing.
Evidence jumps to the eye everywhere in the classical
and historical books. The Hou Han shu says: “Ancient-
ly, at every burial of an emperor or king, human images
of stoneware were used." The Chou li says: "The Officer
of the Grave Mounds addresses the human images." And
so on. Another writer says that "the images of ancestors
were a step to general idolatry." It is also expressly
stated that the wooden images resembled human beings
more closely than the souls of straw which were sub-
stituted for them later (see below).
The gravestone, placed on the altar of the grave, is
a counterpart of the wooden soul-tablet of the deceased,
which is exposed for worship on the altar in his home.
Both the stone and the tablet are supposed to be inhabited
by the manes.
We have the cave or roofed-in burial place developing
into the temple or church, the grave-heap into the altar,

and the image of the dead, or the grave-tablet of stone or
wood (the seat of the soul), into the idol.
It can scarcely be doubted that this form of idolatry
would have increased and multiplied and evolved into the
completely anthropomorphic form found in connection
with Buddhism and other religions, but for some powerful
antagonistic influence which stunted its growth in that
direction. This influence was Confucius. His successful
protestation against the use of wooden images implies
not only their existence, but also their abundance. Не
was clever enough to give for his disapproval a reason
which would go home to the hearts of the people, for he
based his argument on the ground that their continued
use would lead to the sacrifice of the living at the tombs
of the dead. Accordingly, he advocated the substitution
of images made of straw, and these souls of straw are
used even at the present day-the wooden tablet, com-
pletely de-anthropomorphized in deference to his wish,
being used as a more enduring substitute. Confucius
thus stunted the growth of idols (using that word in its
popular sense) in China-a growth which his fear shows
must have been prolific, and likely to be still more so in
the future. Nevertheless, the necessity of idols in some
form is shown not only by his permitting the continued
use of straw idols, but also of his sanctioning the con-
tinued use of wooden images in the popular temples.
Now, when the exotic religions came to China, there
grew up on all sides of the original ancestor-worship a
profusion of anthropomorphic idols. So dense was that
growth that it almost choked-did at times completely
choke the orthodox unanthropomorphic idolatry. Or,
to use a more appropriate metaphor, Confucianism was
like an unidolatrous rock, causing the waters of the
idolatrous exotic religions to part as they flowed past it,
but which was itself at times submerged.
I submit that it would be unreasonable to deny that
this condemnation of these images cannot rightly be
taken as an indication of the absence of idols; also that
it may be reasonably presumed that but for Confucius the
growth would have gone on unchecked, rivalling, if not
surpassing, that appertaining to the other religions; and
the anthropomorphic form would have been the universal
form in China as in India; and that in any case, even if
these hypotheses were (most illogically) to be rejected,
idols in the earliest times existed in the shape of wor-
shipped gravestones and tablets, which, as being the

abode of the manes, are as much entitled to the name as
the more elaborate graven (or cast) images usually
designated by that term. In common with religion itself,
they must have developed from more primitive forms.
Their sudden appearance in Chinese history does not
mean that they were then new-born. We must take a
broader and more scientific view of these matters than
Mrs. Ayscough does. In a world of universal evolution,
it would be strange and astounding indeed if the idol
alone had dropped from the sky ready-made, and re-
mained for ever unchanged.
If the earliest anthropomorphic idols are not found
in later times, their scarcity or absence can easily be
accounted for by destruction of those existing and non-
production of others under the powerful influence of
Confucianism. Those who felt the need of the concrete
human form could bow down to the idols of Buddhism
and Taoism. Though persecuted in their turn by the
Confucianists, these flourished alongside of the state
orthodoxy: flourished indeed to such an extent that China
has been called "the principal idolatrous country of the
world." And so I hold this description should apply
to the earliest as well as to the later times.
Confucius not a god. On page 91 of the same
volume, in Rev. L. Newton Hayes' article on "The Gods
of China," it is stated that "within the last fifteen years,
Confucius was elevated to the position of a deity."
It is not stated by whom Confucius was elevated, nor
what he was made the deity of.
Evidently some misconception exists as to this
alleged apotheosis. So high a position has Confucius
held, on the average (or rather, very much above the
average), in the minds of the Chinese, that, had it been
considered the appropriate thing to apotheosize him, he
would certainly have been apotheosized long ago. But he
was not of that "ilk"; he belonged to another branch of
the constitutional tree. They regarded him as their great

Ask any Chinese scholar, and he will reply that Confucius
is a man, not a god. (Note the 人 ​in聖人​).

Tzŭ Hsi, in 1907, raised Confucius to a rank equal to that

In 1917, I was present at the debate in the Chinese
Parliament as to whether Confucius should be made the

Dynastic Tombs. In the same volume of the
Journal Dr. Herbert Chatley, in a learned article on
"The Dead Hand in China," states in a foot-note on
page 56: "Werner (Myths and Legends of China) says
that all dynasties previous to Mings destroyed their
predecessors' tombs."
My statement, I submit, is correct. The rule was
that not only the members of the vanquished dynasty, but
also everything belonging to them, should be annihilated.
What then is to be said in face of the authorities
quoted by Dr. Chatley showing the survival of grave-
mounds in various places and of much similar evidence
to be given by me presently? Simply this: that the
question hinges on the interpretation of the word "des-
troyed." I hold that it is true that, though grave-mounds
and images of the guardians of the tombs, etc., are to be
found in many places, the dynastic tombs were destroyed
in the sense that little else than these-practically only
the "outer shell"-remains, and that only in a relatively
small proportion compared with the original total number
of tombs. The dynastic tombs, with the exception of the
Ming, may therefore be said to have been destroyed in the
sense that they were rifled, emptied of their contents, and
obliterated except in a comparatively few cases-these
exceptions proving the rule. It is especially to be noted
that the writers (Chavannes, Geil, Segalen, Sowerby)
referred to by Dr. Chatley mention only "tumuli,"
"mounds," and "small circular barrows," - nothing in
the nature of sarcophagi (empty or occupied) or of the
numerous articles it was customary to deposit in the
tombs.

The large grave-mounds-mostly, I believe (subject
to correction), now empty-would cost more money and
labour to remove or level with the ground than would be
worth while; the comparatively few stone figures being
probably allowed to remain in situ for the same reason.
Nevertheless, it will be useful to place on record, as
far as possible, what actually does still exist in the matter
of grave-mounds, etc., leaving to future investigators the
task of discovering whether anything, and if so what,
remains of their interior structure and contents (a task
at present impossible, for many obvious reasons).
Being engaged in literary work which tethers me to
Peking for most of the year, I have not been able to visit
the sites-or supposed sites of the older dynastic tombs,
but I am most fortunate in having the acquaintance of a
gentleman who makes this his hobby, and equally fortunate
in that his generosity has placed at my disposal a budget
of most valuable material relating to this subject. Mr.
Moore-Bennett, a gentleman of independent means resid-
ing in Peking, is a scholar possessed of just the technical
knowledge indispensable for a scientific study of the sites
of these ancient burial-places. He has visited, and in
many cases surveyed the sites of, Ch'in, Han, Sung, Kin,
Wei, and Yüan tombs.
I cannot do better than give in extenso the very
valuable memoranda he has been so kind as to send me,
together with the photographs taken by him. I feel sure
that Mr. Moore-Bennett's kind-hearted generosity will
also admit of the results of his further investigations
being placed at the disposal of sinologists.

If Mr. Moore-Bennett's example be followed by other
competent investigators with regard to the sites, where
ascertainable, of all the other dynastic burial-places, we
would in time have a most interesting and valuable body
of knowledge on this subject. Should these investiga-
tions eventually prove my statement quoted by Dr. Chatley
to be mistaken, no one would be more gratified than I,
for not only would we who are studying things Chinese
then be able to tutankhamen (to invent a new verb) as
jubilantly as the Egyptologists in recent years, but we
would have at our disposal material of the most valuable
kind possible, which could not but throw much light on
the funeral rites and religion of the Chinese in those
earlier times.
I conclude, therefore, with a fervent prayer that the
said statement may prove to be erroneous!
