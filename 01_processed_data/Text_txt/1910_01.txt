THE WAY OF THE SAGES, i.e. CONFUCIANISM.
The way of the Sages, that is to say, Heaven's way,
being that which is necessarily in operation throughout
nature (literally, between Heaven and Earth) is not, as is
erroneously supposed by those who have hearkened to the
transcendental themes of the Buddhists, on the same level
with Buddhism. To regard the latter as superior to the way
of the Learned (i.e. Confucianism), as some do, is simply to
ignore the laws of nature (literally, the ways of Heaven).
The way of the Learned is the way of the Sages. The way

To open up a way is a figurative expression, as when
over a moor or mountain, where no way has been before, a
way is opened up for the first time. For example, on the

dangers and from risk of going astray. Why? Because
Yeki-no Shokaku, a natural genius with an extraordinary
intuitive knowledge of mountain roads, for the sake of those
who were to come after him, opened up the proper ways. In
the same manner the Sages, by means of their extraordinary
clearness of mind and surpassing knowledge, gained an insight
into the principles of the myriad things of Heaven and Earth
and for the sake of the world opened up a constant and
unchangeable way.
At the commencement, after the separation of Heaven and
Earth, the birth of human beings was like that of fish in a
lake of long standing or of insects in putrefying matter,
coming to life by a spontaneous transformation of matter.
Hence, among the men of that time there was no distinction
between noble and base, between upper and lower; but all
were of the same class. They were what we call the commons,
(literally, plain folk). Though human in shape their minds
were not different from those of the animals. Men and
women passed their days living as one herd.

There is no human being but has a father and mother.
Animals when they are young and being nourished by their

The sexual desire is a constant element of human nature,

I should push others out of my way, to get the advantage
I wanted. And others would feel and act in the same way
as myself. It was because men in general did act in this
way that contention and taking by force arose, and there was
incessant disorder among mankind. Their behaviour, in fact,
was as that of the animals.
Vexed at this state of affairs, the Holy Men established
what we call Decorum and taught it. Thereupon people
kept their feelings and desires under control, and each
restricted himself to his own wife, or concubine, and
refrained from violating the wives and daughters of other
people. They put a check on their inclination to contend for
advantage, and in the heat of summer let others remain in
the cool places, and in winter let them keep the warm places
they had. This is the way of concession, and is the
foundation of decorum (i.e. politeness, courtesy). This
decorum and this rectitude are due to the teachings of the
Holy Men, and were not originally attributes of man's heart.

After the teachings of decorum and rectitude were
carried into practice the disorder of mankind ceased, there

first Holy Men at the opening up of Heaven and Earth.

The way of governing the heart was the subject-matter
of the Law of Buddha, and was not included in the procedure
of the Holy Men. The heart of man is a vivacious thing, which
it is not easy to keep still. If you try to reduce it to
quiescence it becomes all the more irrepressible; if you try to
govern it, it remains none the less disturbed. The reason is
that whether it be in motion or whether you are trying to
keep it quiet, it is still the heart. As the heart must be a
unit, to govern the heart by the heart is an impossibility.
The effort to govern it is itself a disturbance of it. The will
to quiet it is itself an activity. The heart is like a child.
You must give a child a toy of some sort to play with and
help it to pass the time. If it has no toy, it cannot stay quiet
in one place even for a short space of time. If you try to
make a child stay quiet in one place by simply telling him to

The teaching of the Holy Men made decorum and
rectitude the chief concern and took no notice of mind-
methods. The passage in the Canon of History, "He
regulated practical matters by voluntary righteousness; he
regulated minds by decorum (ceremonial)," is a description
of the practices of Tang, the Completer, founder of the Yin
dynasty; and is a most important element of the way of Holy
Men. Regulating practical affairs by voluntary rightness
(justice) means that, in all matters whatsoever from the most
important affairs of State down to the petty concerns of each
man with others, if the management of his affairs is left to the
private sagacity of the individual, there will be no risk of his
going too far or not going far enough, but that such success
as the circumstances of each case admit of will surely be
attained. This is what is meant by the expression "voluntary
rectitude government." By government or regulating is
meant the action of the artificer who cuts and proportions the
figure he is working upon. The expression "regulating minds
by ceremonial decorum" implies that there is in the mind

Buddhism makes the complete control of the heart its sole

wild thoughts should be allowed to arise within it. To guard

thing to do.

At first, no doubt, this regulating of the passions by the
observance of ceremonial decorum requires patient endurance
and occasionally lapses may occur, but by constantly keeping
the laws of decorum and by steadily keeping a watch over
one's conduct so as not to violate it, obedience becomes
spontaneous and all our words and actions conform to
Decorum. In this way, by force of habit, a virtuous twist
as it were is insensibly given to the constitution, and the
practice of what is right becomes perfectly correct. Then it
is that the heart also has become perfectly correct in unison
with the conduct. By deliberately and unceasingly pursuing
this course the difficulty of restraining the passions wears
away, and afterwards is not felt at all; and the heart
spontaneously becomes pure and tranquil. And thus as the
tale of years mounts up and a man passes the age of forty,
the youthful spirits wear off, the roving disposition settles
down, and the soul becomes firmly set. The man thus-

The Holy Men of antiquity did not impart to us any
teaching about the governing of the heart. In the Classics,
the governance of the heart is not mentioned, with the

Towards the end of the Chow dynasty and in subsequent
times there arose the various schools of philosophers, savants
and specialists who handed down a great variety of systems
and methods. When we examine their writings at the
present day we find that there is not one of them but has
some principle that helps towards the explanation of the way
of the empire and the nation. But the empire, subsequent
to the Tsin and the Han dynasties, was never governed
by the systems (ways) of the philosophers and savants.
Let me illustrate this fact by a comparison. The way
of the Holy Men is the five cereals. The ways of the
philosophers and savants are medicines. The five cereals
are the staple sustenance of human life, and, from the
highest to the lowest, there is not a man in the empire who
could afford to do without them. But if a man eats too
much of this staple food, or happens to swallow something
that was tainted, his spleen or his stomach gets out of order,
and he becomes ill himself and gives other people trouble.
Then it is that medicines become indispensable, as otherwise
the five cereals would only help to shorten his life. For
the cure of such ailments there are medicines of various
kinds: purgatives, emetics, sudorifics and carminatives-and
the doctors, in order to subdue the fevers of their patients,
administer one or other of these pharmaceutics according
to the symptoms in each case. Then, after being cured of
his illness, the patient resumes his accustomed cereal food.

out of gladness at being cured of his illness, give up cereals

his ordinary food. The way of the Former Kings is perfectly

After Buddhism became generally diffused among us
many of the emperors embraced it and were ardent devotees;
but the government of the empire was never administered in
accordance with Buddha's law. Although Japan was a more
intensely Buddhistic country even than China, and all, high

royal law, yet the state could not have existed for a day had
the (Confucian) principles of decorum and justice been
violated or the social relations been disparaged by the laity.
Then let us pass on to the case of the clergy. Though these
are supposed to have withdrawn from the world and ostensibly
live aloof from it, yet they conform to the laws of the state,
and if they were to violate decorum and justice they also
would be unable to maintain their position for a single day.
The Buddhist law stringently forbids the taking of life;
but the folk living on the sea-coast make fishing their
occupation instead of agriculture. Consequently the bonzes
who live in those localities pray to Buddha morning and
evening and recite the Sutras and chant the Nirvana Sutra,

bonzes become participators in the blessing. Although the

the taking of life as if they had actually killed the fish
