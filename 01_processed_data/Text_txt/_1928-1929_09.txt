THE ORIGIN AND GROWTH OF DESERTS
AND THE ENCROACHMENT OF THE
DESERT ON NORTH-CHINA
By Dr. A. L. ENGLAENDER
The fabulous Atlantis has always evoked the interest
of everyone; and in fact, vanished cities, nations and
civilisations have presented always a romantic interest.
But before I tell my story of disappeared cultures and
forgotten peoples, I will quote some words from the ori-
ginal work where the legend of Atlantis is found. It is
the Timaeus of Plato. The High Priest of Heliopolis in
Egypt addresses the Greek sage: "You Greeks are like
children, you don't know your own history." The
average European of to-day believes that he knows
history. He knows the usual traditional stories about
kings, courts and battles, for one or two thousand years
back; he has some more or less clear notions about Anti-
quity, and that is all he knows. But all these are histories
of recent times. In the last few decades, since what may
be called the Science of the Spade has developed,
since the spade has unearthed Troy, Knossos and
Ur, and the sand-buried cities, of which we know
neither age nor name nor history,
we have
begun to realise that the history of mankind and also
that of culture and civilisation are far, far older than we
learned in school, and than we believed until yesterday.
The Pyramids are nearly six thousand years old, and yet
they represent not the beginning, but a very high degree,
of civilisation. Many thousands of civilisations must have
been successively developed, before such science, such
skill and such administrative power had come into exis-
tence, as was necessary for such huge enterprises as the
Great Pyramids. There are behind the wellknown history
old, more or less mythical, nebulous traditions, historic
facts mingled with stories of gods, goddesses, monsters
and heroes. One and the same tradition returns in the

But he met with important and very unexpected

of big towns, many of them of the area of a square mile
and more.
In the Syrian and Arabian deserts I myself
have seen the ruins of great towns, of castles, of
aqueducts, of reservoirs and all the various manifesta-
tions of sunken cultures, of vanished populations.

serts consists of sand-hills moved by the wind. In reality
the desert presents the same variety of relief as any other
kind of country. There are plains, hills, mountains,
narrow or broad valleys and so on. In the very heart of

bury all their contents, there is nothing to be seen but
the sand-dunes. All the other parts have their history
engraven, but this writing is not easy to decipher. I will
try to tell something about the tales it tells. They are
the saddest and most melancholy imaginable. In nearly

developed river systems. In the Syrian and Arabian
desert, in the Takla-Makan, in the Gobi, in the Sahara,
most caravan-trails go along the the ancient, dried-up
river-beds. All these river-beds, many of them of huge
dimensions, show all the geological signs that they con-
tained plenty of water during long ages. But they show
also indubitable signs that they have dried up relatively
not long ago, certainly in the present geological period.
These dried-up river-beds are by no means the only signs
of the disappeared waters. In the Sahara great numbers
of bones are found of typical forest-dwelling animals,
such as the elephant and the giraffe, and they are of the
recent, contemporaneous varieties. In many parts of
the Sahara petrified tree-trunks are the usual landmarks.

were well-forested, well-watered and well-cultivated in

elephant is a typical forest dweller.

This third thesis runs as follows: Even in countries
seemingly in a perfectly flourishing condition, there are
unmistakeable signs of the progressive diminution of
sweet waters.
Many of us have made journeys along the shores of
the Mediterranean Sea. For the trained eye these
seemingly flourishing, smiling shores tell a sad story of
gradual drying up. In Apulia, in the south of Italy, the

even sufficient drinking water. But in all parts of these
gay shores you see the beds of dried-up rivers. Where
there is yet some water in the river, it is piteously less
than the breadth and depth of the river bed. This is
the same on all the shores of the Mediterranean Sea, in
Europe, in Africa, in Asia Minor, and in all the great
historic islands.

From all I have said there is a general conclusion of
immense importance. It is a thesis of depth, of sadness,
of monumental tragedy. It is human culture and civil-
isation itself which slowly, in the millenial course of its
development, transforms successively into desert, every
country on which it has had sufficient time to develop.
The older the civilisation of a country, the more
approaching to desert conditions is its state. We see
that the absolute deserts of our globe, the Gobi, the Takla-
Makan, the Sahara, show unmistakeable signs of past
intensive life. The traditions of every ancient-cultured
people tell us of immigration to its present fatherland,
from these now desert regions. Now you understand
perhaps something about old Egypt, which from the time
of Herodotus until to-day, has always struck every inves-
tigator. This is that Egyptian civilisation had no begin-
ning. In the time of the builders of the Pyramids, nearly
6,000 years ago, the Pharaonic civilisation was already
at its height. It has never shown its roots and begin-
nings. Yes, it had its beginnings and slow development,
but these did not take place in Egypt. It began on the
now desert tablelands of the Sahara. The old Egyptians
migrated into the then swampy valley of the Nile, when
the Sahara turned to a desert. This conception throws
a flashlight on many hitherto inexplicable facts of history.
I repeat again, that the place of origin of all European
peoples was the east. The Hindus came from the north.
The Chinese came from the west. All these migrations
had their origin in the now desert central regions of Asia,
and now that Shantung is drying up, it repeats again the
tragedy, which took place successively on all the arid
regions of Central Asia and North Africa. It is a deeply
tragic fact that the final result of every great culture and
civilisation has been the invincible and insatiable Desert.

many countries. The Hindu invasion, an occupation of

It is true that actual Arabia is inhabited mostly by
nomadic Bedouins, but it has not always been the same.
I will try to explain. What happens when through slowly
drying up a country can no longer nourish its dense
population? First the big towns shrink away and dis-
appear. I have seen the ruins of Palmyra, with its range
of columns more than a mile long and with its tens of
thousands of ruined dwellings. I have seen the ruins of
Samaria occupying a full square mile, and I have seen
numbers of ruins of unnamed towns in northern Arabia.
None of these towns can be inhabited, for there is no food
and no water. So in the devastation of a country the
first sufferers are the big towns. But very slowly agri-
culture also becomes very scanty, and finally the only
possibility of life in the desert country are wandering
herdsmen like the Bedouins. All the other elements of
the population must emigrate or starve.
Naturally all this takes place with terrible struggles.
The starving people fight cruel and murderous fights for
every inch of land and every drop of water. The chief
of a Bedouin group midway between Damascus and
Baghdad told me that around every oasis and well in the
desert there has flowed ten times more human blood than
the water contained in these wells. Most Bedouin tribes
are by no means primitive people, but the last, degraded
and vanishing remnants of ruined and disappeared civili-
sations, and their terrible, cruel, pitiless struggles
persist even to-day. Now and ever the life of a desert
dweller is a continuous struggle for scarce food and for
the last drop of water. He must kill or he must die.
Naturally the strongest, the cruellest, the cleverest of
these fighters will survive. These conditions are mostly
the same in all deserts, and the same conditions of life are
creating the same characteristics, so one can understand
that the Turkomans of Central Asia, the Bachtiers of
