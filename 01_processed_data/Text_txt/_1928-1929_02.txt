THE JEWS OF HONAN:
A TRAGIC STORY OF SUBMERGENCE
(A Fresh Study of the Stelae)
By THE REV. D. MACGILLIVRAY, M.A., D.D., LL.D.
The destruction of Jerusalem and the Temple took
place in A.D. 70, and since that time all the world knows
the tragedy of the Jewish people. They have been
"scattered and peeled" among all the nations. They have
wandered into every land, but with remarkable tenacity
have retained their separateness as a people and the
purity of their ancient faith in so far as circumstances
allowed. But no one knew until Père Matteo Ricci's
discovery of our orphan colony that some of the diaspora
or Dispersion of Israel were to be found scattered
as far as China. This is the farthest point of their
wanderings. The careful reader of the Prophet Isaiah,
(see Chapter 49: 12-"these [shall come] from the land
of Sinim") might have suspected that some Jews were
to be found in China. Sometime-three different inscrip-
tions give three separate dates for their coming-seventy
families, so reads the record, found their way to Pienliang
City, Honan (now Kaifêng), which was then the capital
of the Sung Dynasty. What Israel was intended to be we
can read in glowing passages of the Old Testament, e.g.
Exodus 19: 6-"And ye shall be unto me a kingdom of
priests and an holy nation"; Deut. 14: 2-"For thou
art an holy people unto the Lord thy God, and the Lord
hath chosen thee to be a peculiar people unto himself,
above all the nations that are upon the earth." Isaiah
5: 7-"For the vineyard of the Lord of Hosts is the house
of Israel and the men of Judah his pleasant plant,'
Deut. 26:18-"And the Lord hath avouched thee this day
to be his peculiar people as he hath promised thee, and
that thou shouldest keep all his commandments; and to
make thee high above all nations which he hath made,
in praise and in name and in honour; and that thou

But in China, as with the Nestorians (), the
middle wall of partition between the Jews and the Chinese
wore thin and finally collapsed, and so what we here pre-
sent is a study in the absorption of a faith by an alien
civilization. Many other small communities of Jews
have shared a similar fate, but left no trace behind. Far
otherwise is it with the ill-fated colony in Honan, for
there are abundant historical remains. Alas! they them-
selves disappeared beneath the all-embracing yellow flood,.
with a greater destruction than that of the waters of
the Yellow River which more than once swept down on
Kaifeng and destroyed their Synagogue and homes. The
swarthy and characteristic features they bore when they
first came are now changed for the most part to the colour
and general facial appearance of the Yellow Race-so
most witnesses agree. They worship their ancestors,
their women bind their feet, the Synagogue is gone; there
is not even a wailing place where they might cry
"Ichabod." It was a branch torn off and soon withered,
(Romans 11: 17); or to use another Biblical phrase from
the Weeping Prophet: "How is the gold become dim!
How is the most pure gold changed!" (Lamentations
4: 1). Their story possesses a melancholy interest both
to Jew and Gentile and is not without warning to all;
"Let him that thinketh he standeth, take heed lest he fall."
The Discovery of the Secret.-For many centuries,
these Jews were lost to the outside world until at the
time appointed, their secret was revealed. Let us recon-
struct the romantic story—a free and justifiable use of
the historical imagination will help us to complete our
tale.
Among the candidates for the degree of M. A. at
the Provincial Examinations in Kaifêng was a young
Jew. Before entering the Examination Hall, he had been
on the Sabbath attending a service at the local Synagogue.
In order to enter for the examination he had of course
to perform certain prostrations before the tablet of
Confucius, but evidently he had overcome his conscien-

The Moslems did the same. The prizes were too glitter-

When the examination lists came out, his name, O joy!
appeared amongst the successful ones, but not yet content
he soon started for Peking to try his luck for still higher
honors at the final trials in the Palace. It was in the
leafy month of June, in the early part of the seventeenth
century. As he travelled along the great road to Peking
he found many inns for the refreshment of the weary,
but none in which the food was guaranteed to be pure
(kosher), in the Jewish sense. As he afterwards naïvely
tells Father Ricci, if he had strictly kept Jewish dietary
laws he would certainly have starved upon the way. As
soon as he reached Peking he heard with surprise that
some white foreigners from the West had lately come
thither, and that they were Monotheists like himself,
but not Moslems. As his ancestors were foreigners too,
he feels an affinity for foreigners and is consumed with
curiosity to see them and investigate the reason of their
coming. He enters Father Ricci's humble dwelling and
announces himself not as a "Jew," but as an "Israelite."
(The reason will appear later). Father Ricci was
greatly struck with his visitor's features; the Hebrew
Bible was produced, but he could not read it. He excused
himself on the ground that his Chinese studies had so
engrossed his time that he had had no leisure to keep up
his Hebrew (which has a strangely modern sound).
Ricci took him at once into a small oratory and shewed
him a picture of the Holy Family with John the Baptist,
and also another picture of the four Evangelists. The
visitor said, "We in China reverence our ancestors, but
do not worship pictures. This is Rebekah and her sons,
Jacob and Esau. As to the other picture, why make
obeisance to only four sons of Jacob? Were there not
twelve?" A long conversation followed and Ricci learned
that there was a small colony of Jews and a Synagogue
some four hundred years old, together with many rolls
of the Law, in the city of Kaifêng. We know the very
day on which the momentous interview took place, for
Ricci tells us it was the Festival of John the Baptist
which comes on the 24th of June. One would like to
know what happened afterwards to the young man, but
history is silent. His name is preserved, Ai ();
perhaps it was "Ezra" in the Hebrew tongue. This

Father Ricci himself is too old to travel (he came to

fully corroborated. The Catholic Fathers,2 about a
hundred years later (1706), spurred on by European
Biblical scholars who had heard about the Rolls of the
Law and were anxious to compare them with the
Massoretic text which they had in the accepted Hebrew
Bible, visited the Synagogue, examined the MSS., took
copies of everything of interest, made plans of the build-
ing, and sent them all to Paris where they are still
preserved in the College of Ste. Geneviève. In 1850,
visitors sent by "the London Society for promoting
Christianity among the Jews" found the Synagogue still
standing, but Dr. W. A. P. Martin, in 1866, found nothing
remaining but a solitary stela (dated 1663) with inscrip-
tions on both sides. Since then a third stone has been
found, but the stone of 1572 erected in Kang-hsi's reign
has disappeared. Dr. W. A. P. Martin said of the stela
he saw: "It deserves to be regarded as one of the most
precious monuments of religious history." (Hanlin
Papers, p. 362). The Stelæ are human documents of
rare interest and replicas should be made of them (as
has been done of the Nestorian Stone) and placed in
Shanghai, perhaps in front of the Synagogue in Museum
Road, next to the Royal Asiatic Society's building.
There they would be seen by tourists and scholars from
every land. In Kaifêng, few will see them.

hardships of the journey were indeed most formidable,

sands. The trail was still open and there must have
been many flourishing towns along the way. Doubtless
they carried with them various articles of merchandise,
but amongst them all nothing was more precious than
some rolls of the Law of Moses. These were placed in
charge of one of the priests or rabbis who undoubtedly
accompanied the little caravan. Their arrival at the
border of China would be immediately reported to the
Emperor who graciously gave them permission to resume
their march to the capital. At that time the Confucian
maxim Jou Yüan Jên A, "Treat foreigners with
kindness," was in force.
Surely a more luckless site for a capital was
never chosen. Kaifêng has been devastated by floods
fifteen times, by fire six times, by siege eleven
times, and is just now (1927) in the central eddy
of the Civil War. Nearby were the high dykes of
the Yellow River, like the levees of the Mississippi, the
danger of which the Jews were to know only too well in
later times when the bursting of the dykes in 1642, or
the cutting of them by an invading army, flooded the city,
and more than once destroyed the Synagogue and the
copies of the Pentateuch and other portions of Scripture
kept in the Bethel, or sacred ark. Kaifêng is now 10
feet below the bed of the River, and even then was an
easy prey to floods. But the city was at that time one
of great magnificence. It is said that there were a
million people there, and its roads, its shade-trees and
its buildings were indeed worthy of an Imperial capital.
The strangers were taken to the Court and brought with
them certain offerings. On the stela these are called,
"tribute." Now this is the same word as the Chinese
applied to the gifts which foreign ambassadors used to
take with them when they went to present their
credentials to the Emperors of China. The flags of their

heart must have often turned to the land of their fathers.
I wonder if they ever sat by the banks of the Yellow
River and were asked to sing the songs of Zion, and hung
their harps upon the willow trees which grow there, as
they did in Babylon, and said, "How can we sing the
Lord's song in a strange land?" (Psalm 137).
They found silk, which perhaps Herodias and the
Roman Imperial ladies wore, here worn by many ordinary
folk, and mandarins were clad in silken robes with breast
ornaments somewhat like those of their own High Priests.
They found a people even then as numerous as the sands
of the sea, or as the stars for multitude. It may well
have seemed to them that the promise made to their
father Abraham, (Genesis 15:5, 12:2, 3,) had been ful-
filled to the Chinese, and not to the Jews, and when they
learned how long they had been in the land they might
also well have thought that Jehovah had granted them
long days in the land on account of their reverence for
their parents (Deut. 5:16). One of their first duties.
after arriving was to fit themselves into the economic
life of the strange city. True to type, they preferred
city life to farming, in which they could scarcely compete
with the Chinese. They were too mentally alert to be
content with manual labour. Then with pious care they
would first get a meeting-place and then build them a
Synagogue. This was not at all like the final Synagogue
of which we shall speak later. The 42nd Psalm might

At first the splendour of the court, the painted idol
temples, and the gilded haunts of vice, must have made
the city seem as Babylon the Great did to the Jews of
the Captivity, a wicked city which was doomed by
Jehovah to destruction for its sins, a second Nineveh,
perchance. But as God spared Nineveh so also he spared
Kaifêng. Presently they began to yield to the seductions
of heathen society, and then set in the sad process of
drift, apostasy and denationalization. A rubbing of a
stone from one of the side rooms of the Synagogue is
still preserved, but only a few disconnected characters
are legible. The pitiless and ceaseless pressure of
elemental forces beating down upon it from all sides has
worn its face away-a fit emblem of the history of the
Jewish colony of Kaifêng. The group was not water-
tight and seeping was unavoidable. The older folk were
immune, but the children would attend Confucian schools
so as to learn the Chinese language, and there was no
"conscience clause" to absolve them from the worship
of Confucius, and fed on Confucian diet they soon became
little Confucianists. A little protective colouring could
not come amiss in case of trouble. At first the Jews
were shocked by the idolatry and kept far from it, but
presently it did not seem quite so horrible and they ate
meats offered to idols. (Cf. 1 Corinthians, Chap. 8).
Some of the customs would be practically the same as
their own, e.g., the lunar calendar, millstones, the use of
middlemen in marriages, polygamy, altars, sacrifices,
priests and divination. They would be pleased to see
that China had a Book of Rites and a Book of Divination.
Chinese Proverbs could be matched out of the Book of
Proverbs. Then there would be Mohammedans who
observed the same dietary laws, at least in part, as
themselves. But at first they would be greatly shocked
to see the yoking together of mules, cows, donkeys, etc.,
to one vehicle or plow, a thing contrary to the prescrip-
tion of their law (Deut. 22:10). The goods displayed
in the shops must have excited their amazement, for at
that time Western products were not equal to those of
China. They could not join in festivals, where pork was

neighbours! The struggle against this corroding en-

be a liberal and a conservative school. The complaisance
of the younger generation would be agony to their elders,
such as we see portrayed in modern times by Israel

no compromise with heathenism. Oft must they have

dealings with them. The Greek influences at work on
the Jews in Palestine, but more particularly upon the
Diaspora, were sufficiently dangerous, but the influence
of the Chinese civilization upon the little colony proved
even more deadly. The weaker culture went to the wall.
The road of non-conformity is always hard to travel and
the relentless pressure of China's millions all doing
something different from the Jews, finally overwhelmed
them. Racial pride, loyalty, and religious fervour all
alike vanished.

The Form and Contents of the Synagogue.-The
form and contents of the Synagogue shew strong Chinese
influences. Strange to say, none of all those who actually
beheld the Synagogue before its final disappearance make
any allusions to the material of which the building itself
was made. I conjecture that, inasmuch as stone is a long

the building was made of the ordinary materials of wood,
adobe, and a foundation of brick. Thus it will be seen

practice of Jews when building their synagogues to

10. Finally, the erecting of a stela or stone is not a
Jewish but a Chinese custom. The Jews were not given
to the erection of memorial stones with inscriptions, save
in the case of gravestones, which supply 99 per cent.
of the surviving inscriptions of the Jews throughout the
world. Perhaps it was because the Law forbade graven
images, or because the finger of God had graven the Ten
Commandments on the two tablets of stone and no one else
should do such work. Thus in the recently excavated
ruins of the Synagogue at Capernaum there are no
memorial stones like these at Kaifêng. The only carved
thing so far discovered there is the vine, a favourite image
of Israel in the prophetical writings. Besides, there was
an express prohibition against using a graving tool on
the stones of an altar, (Exodus 20:25, Deut. 27:5), and
the graven image of the golden calf was an evil omen.
The Kaifêng stone could not have had such a head as
the Nestorian stone with its mysterious beasts, though
one of them has a very faint flat outline of birds, one
on each side. If any tombstones could be discovered,
their inscriptions would be useful as sidelights. But the
Chinese were always scratching or chipping words, from
the oracle bones of Honan to the stone drums of the
Temple of Confucius in Peking, and from that day to
this, memorial stones are everywhere a familiar sight,

believed in numberless evil spirits and practised a form
of exorcism. But their most striking debt to Taoism is
in their constant use of the word "Tao" which is very
frequently used as a term for "God." What this
character signifies in the Tao Teh King is a
matter of great controversy. The book itself says, "The
Tao, so long as it remains absolute, is unnameable, but
obliged to give it a name I made an effort and called it
The Great Way". Whatever was the original sense, the
Jews have used it for "God", and the Christians have
used it for the "Logos" of the first chapter of St. John's
Gospel ("In the beginning was the Word," etc.)
If those translators, who first hit upon this expres-
sion for their version of the Prologue to the Gospel
according to St. John, had known the use the Jews made
of it in these inscriptions, they would have found strong
support for the adoption of the term.

Final Poverty of the Colony.-They alleged that
extreme poverty had forced them to sell the rolls and
synagogue, but the causes of their poverty are not very
self-evident. The ability of the Jew to make a living
anywhere and indeed amass wealth is a well-known fact
but in this case, although a number of them attained
to official positions the mass remained in comparatively
poor circumstances.
1. When the Jews came to China they had no
economic advantage, for they could teach no craft in
which the Chinese were not already far ahead of them.
If they had done so they might have been welcomed as
were the Huguenot emigrés from the noyades and drag-
onnades of France who brought into England the secrets
of weaving of silk and other materials and so laid the
foundations of English prosperity in textiles.

The Final Scene.14-The discussion of the Hebrew
MSS., etc. is not within our purview now. Neither have
we space to enter upon the very fascinating story of the
various attempts to reach the colony made both by Jews
and Gentiles. A second Ezra of Shanghai, alas! no
longer with us, made a brave but futile attempt to
revive the faith of the colony in 1901, in which he was
ably seconded by his compatriots. In fact, before that,
visits were paid by many, and still other visits were
attempted but frustrated by the hostility then prevailing
against foreigners travelling in the interior. Many
letters were written in Hebrew and in Chinese, resulting
in an accumulation of facts concerning their history, but
alas! the colony itself committed the final act of felo de se
by the sale of the materials of the Synagogue and the
precious rolls of the Law, all of which passed into non-
Jewish hands and are now to be found in the various
large libraries of the world. (Siccawei
has one).
Once upon a time a rich Jew had rebuilt their Synagogue
for them, but he had no successor in modern times. With
the loss of their Synagogue the colony lost its last chance.
We venture to think that if the Jews had come to
Shanghai in 1842 when the Treaty of Nanking was made,
they might have stood a much better chance of getting
help and surviving. But what was the use? The
Synagogue was a shell without a soul. The real cause

The Nestorians also have disappeared from China,
