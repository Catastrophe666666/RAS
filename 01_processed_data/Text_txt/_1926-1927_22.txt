SOME CONTRIBUTIONS
TO THE
ANTHROPOLOGY OF THE BURIATS
By GEORGE P. KOJEUROFF
At the present time, part of Irkutsk province, all
Pribaikal province and part of Sabaikal province are
occupied by the so-called Buriat-Mongolian Republic.
The country is comparatively little known in anthro-
pological and ethnographical respects, and certainly
presents a great and wide field for an explorer. The
great bulk of the population consists of Buriats,¹
Russians and Tunguses.
The Buriats are related to the Mongolian Branch
of the Uralo-Altai group of peoples. Before Russia's
appearance in East Siberia, i.e., before the first quarter
of the 17th century, the vast area of the present Yenisei,
Irkutsk and Transbaikal provinces was occupied by
Buriats, and their influence extended to the neighbouring
tribes, Kottos, Beltirs, etc., and also to certain Tungus
tribes.
The settling of Buriats in various places around
Lake Baikal pressed out the Jakuts far to the north, and
these in their turn were obliged to occupy the grounds of
widely-dispersed Tunguses in the north-east of Asia.
With the appearance of the Russians there began
a slow but obstinate conquest of East Siberia, and a
subjection of the population by the comparatively small
number of brave pioneers from the West.
Several times the indigenes revolted against the
governors, their tyranny and heavy taxes; but after
being victorious for a while they were conquered again,
and had to recognize the Russian authority or leave the
Buriats, or Buraty, or sometimes they are called by Russian
peasants Bratsky.

country. Discontent of this kind and competition with
the intruders from the West, were the reasons of the
constant emigration of Buriat tribes from Irkutsk
province to Transbaikalia, and from thence to Mongolia.
So was it in the years 1658, 1659 and 1660, when the
Boulagats left, partly for the valley of the Bargouzin
River and the Koudarinsk steppes, and partly for North
Mongolia. The conquering and settling by Russians of
the newly-discovered lands reacted on the indigenes in
such a way that they were pushed from west to east,
and also from the river-deltas and the rivers themselves.
Through the Russians occupying Irkutsk province and
Transbaikalia the population became composed of Russians
and Buriats.
At the present time the Buriats occupy the territory
from the conflux of the rivers Ouda (Uda) and Angara
on the west to the river Argounj on the east, and from
the upper part of the rivers Lena and Bargouzin in the
north to the Mongolian frontier in the south; the
distance from east to west is 1,500 kiloms. and from north
to south about 600, an area of about 397,000 square kiloms.
As was mentioned before, the population of the
Republic is mixed, for Russian settlements alternate with
Buriat ones. Sometimes the Buriat settlements are like
islands among the Russians, as, for example, on the
lower part of the Selenga river, where the Koudarinsk
Buriats form such an island. And in the mountains and
steppes of Buriatia, among the rest of the population are
also found here and there slowly degenerating Tunguses.
In the far north, in the Bargouzin district, in the
basin of the North Angara and on the middle and upper
parts of the Bargouzin, to the north from the River Ina,
live the Muja-Tungus tribes; and south in the basin of
the river Djida, in the Armaksk district live the so-called
Armaksk Tunguses sent here in the 18th century for the
"defence of distances." Another group of Tunguses live
in the Akshinsk district. In Irkutsk province and in
the Balagansk district live the Malobelsk (Littlebelsk
Tunguses, and on the upper parts of the Lena, Kirenga
and Tutura rivers, the Tuturo-Atscheulsk Tunguses.
I have mentioned already the very varied population,
that from my point of view is the most important effect
of the known assimilation of Tunguses by the Buriats on
the one hand and the Russians on the other. The above-
mentioned groups of Tunguses, especially the Armaksk
and Akshinsk Cossacks, have become so "Buriatified"

that it is very difficult to distinguish them by appearance
from the Buriats. The influence of the Tibeto-Mongolian
culture which the Buriats have also experienced has put
its special impress on the Tunguses also, in clothing,
homesteads, language and religion. Most of them are
Lamaists, but a few are Shamanists.
Another factor, more powerful and more difficult to
resist and moreover destructive to national and original
traits, was the influence of the European culture of the
West, which was brought by the Russian pioneers,
slipping among dangers into far, unknown mysterious
Siberia, searching for "new lands and unknown peoples."
With the development and recrudescence of the culture-
power of Russia her influence on the indigenes extended,
and made for their Russification. This influence,
unfortunately, was not always beneficial or satisfactory
to the Buriats and Tunguses, but this was the fault of
uneducated administrators, and their inability to approach
and to understand the indigenes. The following example
should always be remembered in history, when the
excessive ardour of the governor of the Balagansk
stockade ("Ostrog") John Pahoboff, caused a revolt. In
addition to his administrative duties, Pahoboff occupied
himself with the diffusion of Christianity, as there were
no special missionaries in Siberia at that time. He had a
special liking to “washing in water", i.e., to baptizing the
new subjects. The way of baptizing which he invented
was original enough: "Having cut an ice-hole in the
frozen Angara River, Pahoboff gathered to it the crowds
of Buriats. When the moment for immersion had come,
the Cossacks bound two or three men together, tied them
to the middle of a long pole, and then taking the pole
by the ends, at a given signal immersed the tied men in
the ice-hole, three times without interruption." It is
doubtful whether a baptism of this kind could have made
a good impression on the indigenes. In any case, the
results were shown immediately in the revolt and the
decamping of the Balagat Buriats.
The government, wrestling with such problems,
decided on severely punishing its representatives; so in
1721 was hanged Prince M. P. Gagarin, the first Siberian
governor; in 1736, the governor of Irkutsk, Djolabaff,
had his head cut off, and so forth
But in
spite of this, and mainly thanks to the great distance from
the capital, such "gentlemen" regarded themselves as
absolute sovereigns.

The number of administrators of Pahoboff's type
was not a small one, but that has been a usual thing
during the diffusion of European culture in the countries
out of Europe. Remember only the results of the
appearance of the Spanish conquistadores in South
America, and the history of the Incas; the oppression of
North American Indians and of Tasmanians by white
settlers, etc. All these are sorrowful pages in the story
of the restless white man and his irresistible European
culture.
The highest point of Russification has been reached
by the Buriats of the Western Aymaks of Irkutsk
province-those of Ekherit-Boulagatsk, Balagansk,
Tunkinsk and Alarsk Aymaks. (Afterwards the reader
will meet such words as: Aymak, Khoshoun and ulus,
and for that reason I want to explain them. Aymak=
county, Khoshoun=district, and ulus=a village or
hamlet). The Russified and half-Russified population is
known as "Yasachnoe". They are settled on the land,
and are mostly agriculturists who wear Russian clothes
and live in Russian peasant houses. In their mode of
life they are half-way between Russians and Buriats.
The Buriats of the Eastern Aymaks made a more
steady stand, and the number of "yasachnoe” or “Karimi"
is insignificant among them. These Buriats of the East-
ern Aymaks have maintained their own language, and
many of them do not speak Russian; they have also
retained their own clothing and customs. It is true they
do not live in huts, but in "balagans", or wooden construc-
tions resembling Russian peasants' houses. In some
uluses they have good Russian houses. Most of them are
agriculturists or cattle-breeders. As a relic of their past
migratory life which now is impossible or more or less
difficult, because of the settling of Russian peasants every-
where, they have preserved their "zimniki" and "letniki”.
The "zimniki" are warm and solidly-built constructions
where they live in winter, and the "letniki" are light
dwellings in which they spend the spring and summer.
The "letniki" are built sometimes at a distance of one, or
two, mostly three kilometres from the "zimniki", but also
sometimes just beside them.
Most of the Buriats of the Western Aymaks are
Christians, but that does not prevent them from being
Shamanists; it is true they conceal this, as they conceal
their Shamans too, from the Russian authorities. Mr.
Bikoff, who stayed about twelve years as a teacher

The Buriats of the Eastern Aymaks are Lamaists
in general, though a few of them are Christians or
Shamanists. After the year 1920, with the arrival of
Communists in Transbaikalia, there began an anti-
religious propaganda, the results of which were reported
in 1925 as showing in the increase of the number of
Shamanists, and Shamanism has become a great force.
An ex-lecturer of the University of St. Petersburg,
at the present time Public Commissar of Education in
the Buriat-Mongolian Republic (Mr. Baraidyn, himself
a Buriat), tells me that this growth of Shamanism is the
result of the enfeebled activities of the missionaries and
of the lamas, because they have to use all their strength
against the anti-religious propaganda of the Soviet gov-
ernment. It is possible that this is really so, because
the missionaries now work under such conditions that
they have no possibility of carrying on aggressive work,
and they satisfy themselves with keeping in their hands
what they have already obtained; as to the lamas, they
are only worrying about their own felicity. The Sha-
mans, being left in peace, renew their activities and
shelter themselves behind their followers.

matter, but will give some proofs. The total number of
the Buriats given by the explorer, Mr. V. K. Ansenieff, is
288,599 including 145,717 males; and that coincides with
the estimate given by Mr. S. Patkanoff.

As to the Eastern Aymaks, it is difficult to tell
whether there has been increase or decrease, because the
population used to be reckoned by classes (peasants,
workers, merchants, etc.) and not by tribes.
Also, at one time they migrated from Mongolia, and
since their return they have often been mixed with Tung-
uses, and so on. With all this, Mr. S. Patkanoff believed
there has been some increase, but he could not give exact
numbers, though he reckoned the fluctation as being
between 13 and 14 per thousand.
What can be the reasons of the decrease of the West-
ern Buriats' numbers, and as I personally believe-at
least a suspension of increase among the Eastern ones?
-This is of course a difficult question requiring special
study. But I am going to give some suppositions.
For the Buriats of the Western Aymaks-great mor-
tality and low birth-rate. The great mortality comes from
tuberculosis, syphilis and the abuse of alcohol. The mix-
ing with Russians through marriage plays a big rôle. It
helps on the one side to the Russifying of the indigenes,
for the new type, the "yasachnoe", approach more and
more from generation to generation to the Russians, but
on the other side it must be one reason for such an
evident decrease of West Buriat population.
As to the Buriats of the Eastern Aymaks, tuber-
culosis, syphilis and alcohol are their great scourges also.
Because of the utterly insanitary conditions of life, and of
the existence among Buriats and Mongolians of panmixia,
the spread of such a disease as syphilis assumes very
large proportions. Russians visiting the native villages
always take with them their own utensils, not allowing
the Buriats even to touch them. The latter are more
than dirty; they do not wash, and do not change their
clothing until it is quite worn-out. On their bodies, and
in their long hair and dirty clothes, multitudes of par-
asites shelter. When I was taking anthropological mea-
surements among the newly-mobilized Buriats in the Red
Army, I had many opportunities of observing their bodies
covered with dirt and scratched to blood.

sent here in the sixth decade of the 18th century, (2) by
the so-called "Siberian" emigrants and (3) by the Cossacks.
In 1725 a number of jaitskii (Ural) Cossacks were
transferred to Transbaikalia. Partly they were settled
in mines and manufactories, but most of then were intend-
ed for agricultural work.
As to the Great-Russian Semeiskie, they are of great
interest. They are called "Semeiskie" (from the Russian
word for "family") because they were removed by whole
families. (Professor Selitscheff has a theory that they
got their name from the river Semi on which they lived
in Poland). The forefathers of the Semeiskie as well as
the forefathers of those who are now living on the river
Don and in the Dobrudja, at one time dwelt on the south
from Moscow in Toulskaja, Kalougskaja, Orlovskaja, and
the south part of Rjazanskaja provinces. When religious
dissent was being persecuted in the reigns of Alexis
Mickhailovitch and Peter the Great, a part of them escaped
into Poland. Catherine the Great, wishing to colonize
Transbaikalia, offered them a lot of privileges if they
would return and settle there. This they did. They were
splendid farmers, and in the places they occupied grew
up, like stately and beautiful flowers, the richest villages
in Siberia!
The "Great-Russian Semeiskie,” because of their reli-
gious views of life still remain in their former state, and
have not mixed with the surrounding population. They
are still living together, occupying whole districts. They
are very faithful to their principles; very few of them
are educated, because among them studying is considered
a sin. They do not use alcohol or tobacco, but the
Revolution has had its influence on their young people,
who do not consider smoking and drinking as sins.
Professors Talko-Grincevitch and Selitscheff, and
Mr. Girchenko have written concerning the "Semeiskie".
Two of the works are of an historical character, and one,
anthropological. Both groups, the "Siberians" and the
"Semeiskie", as well as the indigenes, are waiting for
scientific investigators who will have to decide a lot of
new and interesting questions about the Mongoloid type,
the mixing of the tribal groups as well as that of the white
and yellow races, problems of heredity, the rhythm of the
stature of Russians and Buriats in the schools, and many
other matters.

found the Work of Dr. Shendrikovsky, Materials for the

All these works are of great interest, Professor
Talko-Grincevitch's especially, but they are of those
anthropological works where many measurements have
been taken of few individuals; besides this, the measure-
ments are very difficult to collate, as they have been taken
by different persons.
I was led to make this initial essay in Buriat Anthro-
pometry, first, because of the lack of any works giving
an account of the measurements of a few phenomena on
a very great number of individual Buriats; and secondly,
by my desire to apply to the problem of their basic types
the geographical method, of which Dr. Tchepourkovsky
demonstrated the value in his great work¹ on the
primitive (basic) types of the Russian peoples, where he
gives the results of measurements on 50,000 persons.
In this modest sketch I offer for consideration my
insignificant measurements. In the summer of 1925 and
in the winter of 1926 I made two trips to the Buriat-
Mongolian Republic. I had not much time and in 1926
I worked in a very cold Siberian winter. The frosts were
from 35 to 39 C.
I shall compare and complete my measurements,
where it is possible, with those of Drs. Porotoff, Shendri-
kovsky and Talko-Grincevitch. As basic measurements
those which are recommended by Professor Tchepourkov-
sky were taken: i.e., the maximum length and breadth of
the head, and the stature. Besides these, I cut some hairs
from the occiput.
The Buriats whose measurements were taken by me
were from both Western and Eastern Aymaks, the greater
number being from Verkne-Udinsk and Khorinsk Aymaks.
Their ages were from 20 to 27. They were 352 in number;
310 had their index calculated and the stature of them all
was measured. The number of women among them was 43.
One begins one's measurements from the cephalic
index as being basic, and persistently hereditary.
This work was brought out in Russian (Moscow 1917), and
in English in the (London) Journal Biometrika edited by Karl
Pearson, in an article "Biometrical Studies in the Anthropology of
Russia", giving the results of the application of biometrical methods,
detailed curves, indices, etc.

As to the earliest appearance of gray hair I observed
it in some boys of 10 or 11 years old, but the Buriats told
me that it may appear at an even earlier age.
As to the "Mongolian eyelid." This is a fold of skin
upon the interior angle of the upper eyelid which covers
the lachrymal gland, and that is why it seems that the
eyes are oblique. It is strongly marked in the inhabitants
of Central Asia especially, but you can also observe it in
Malays, Tibetans and also Far-Northern Asiatics. In
infants and in children generally it is very marked, and
lasts until 20-25 years of age (approximately) but a little
later it sometimes begins to relax, and it can be seen up
to 40 years of age, though not always. I observed the same
phenomenon when taking measurements among Chinese
and Koreans. In order not to rely on merely personal
observations it would be extraordinarily valuable and
interesting to collect en masse photographs of “Mongolian
eyelids", noting the questions of age, sex and heredity as
affecting the matter.

to the discovery of primitive (basic) types of the
population. In this matter I could not use the results
obtained by other authors, for they did not enquire
to what Khoshoun or ulus the persons they measured
belonged. Tracing on the map the seven khoshouns of
Eastern Aymaks, viz., Eravinsk, Khorinsk, Kijiginsk,
Jhergalantouisk, Koudara-Buriatsk, Batanai-Kharganatsk
and Orongoisk, we do not discover any chaos in the
distribution of the mean indices, but, on the contrary, a
certain degrees of order.
All these Khoshouns situated on the right bank of
the Selenga River, in the basin of the Khilok River and
northwards as far as the Uda River are distinguished by
a lower index, with the centre in Jhergalantouisk (Verkne-
Udinsk Khoshoun (83.00); to the south, Batanai-Khar-
ganatsk has 83.87 and to the north-east Kijiginsk has
83.40; so, as is evident, the indices have begun to rise.
The Khoshouns situated more to the north-east, such as
Khorinsk (85.31) and Eravinsk (85.26) have higher indices,
just like those at the mouth of the Selenga River; while
in the neighbourhood of Lake Baikal, Koudaro-Buriatsk
has 85.24, and to the south, on the left bank, Orongoisk
Khoshoun has the index 84.42, i.e., also rising.
I shall not hasten to state any conclusions as they
might be untrustworthy and distinguished by too much
personal bias.
This short article the first attempt to apply the
geographical method to the anthropology of the Buriats
-I regard as a comparatively insignificant contribution
of new material. I profoundly believe that there will
be other investigators who, applying the geographical
method, will collect much more material than I have done,
and will look into my results.
Far away there, among the mountains and on the
steppes of Transbaikalia there are living the white and the
yellow populations, the "métis" (half-bloods) of Trans-
baikalia, and the "Yasachnoe” or “Karimi”.
The Whites are tall, strong, broad-shouldered, blue-
eyed and light-haired, i.e., the Great-Russian “Semeiskie";
the Yellow have a yellowish skin, with black-brown eyes
and black, coarse hair, and are lower in stature than the
Whites-these are the Buriats; they are all dwelling
around Baikal's sacred Lake, awaiting explorers and
research-workers who will open new and interesting
pages in Science.
