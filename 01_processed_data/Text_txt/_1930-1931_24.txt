THE CHINESE NATION AND THE
DOCTRINE OF THE GOLDEN MEAN
By TSAI YUEN-PEI
The world we are living in is a relative one with
opposing tendencies, and in order to make proper adjust-
ments to it in this life, we usually seek a centre of
equilibrium as our resting point. This may be illustrated
from many examples. For instance, in human and
animal physiology, there are the arteries and the veins
opposed to one another, and the heart acts as the centre
of the circulation system; the assimilative system again,
which has the double and opposite functions of assimila-
tion and excretion, has the stomach and the intestines as
its centre. In our general concepts also, similar relative
differences exist in both time and space. We have the
inevitable opposition between the right and the left, the
front and the rear, and the above and the below in space,
of which we ourselves serve as the centre. The stream
of time is divided by us into the past and the future, with
the ever shifting present as the centre or medium.
these are all natural facts of human thinking, not subject
to our alteration. Now the same principle of balance
should obtain in human conduct also. Among the western
philosophers, this principle has been recognized by
Aristotle in his Doctrine of the Golden Mean, in which he
upholds courage as the ideal mean between cowardice and
recklessness, and temperance as the golden mean
between parsimony and extravagance. Apart from this
notable exception, however, there is a common tendency
to fall into opposite idealistic extremes, either the
extreme theory of non-resistance of Tolstoi, or the
extreme theory of will to power of Nietzsche; or again,
either the extreme theory of laissez-faire advocated by

Jean Jacques Rousseau or the opposite extreme of strong
government control preached by Hobbes. The reason for
this probably lies in the fact that all the other nations
except Greece somehow do not possess the temperament
required by the Doctrine of the Golden Mean, which
therefore does not fit in with their conception of life. In
China, however, the story is quite otherwise; whereas
the extreme theorists have repeatedly met with failure,
once their theory is put on trial, the Doctrine of the
Golden Mean has always met with the approval of the
majority, and has always had the best chance of standing
the test of time. This may be proved from the experience
of two of the most highly respected theories in China:
first, that of Confucianism, which has held uninterrupted
sway for two thousand years, and second, the Sanmin
Doctrine of Dr. Sun Yat-sen.
The personages which have been upheld by the
Confucianists as the ideals of manhood are the Emperors
Yao, Shun and Yu who lived about four thousand years
ago, Emperor Tang who lived about three thousand five
hundred years ago, and finally the founders of the Chou
Dynasty, Wen Wang and Wu Wang, who lived approxi-
mately three thousand years ago. Now the Confucian
Analects says that when Emperor Yao turned over the
government to his successor Shun, he commanded the latter
"to hold on firmly to the mean." Now how did Emperor
Shun "hold on to the mean"? The Chapter Chung-
yung in Li-Ki says that "Shun was fond of asking for the
opinions of those near him, and then, weighing between
the two extremes, he would strike the balance and apply
it to the people." The Book of History, Shang-Shu, says
that Emperor Shun made his minister of music and
ceremony take charge of the education of the people, and
commanded him to teach the young to "be straight-
forward and yet gentle, lenient yet firm, to be determined
without being harsh, and simple without being proud."
Now although straightforwardness, leniency, determina-
tion and simplicity were all good virtues in themselves,
yet it was possible to have too much of these good things.
Too much straightforwardness does not go with gentility,
and too much leniency is not compatible with firmness;
a man of over-powering determination would be inclined
to be harsh, and dogged simplicity may verge on
nonchalance and disrespect. Hence, according to the

Doctrine of the Golden Mean, these should be tempered
with the opposite virtues of gentility, firmness, bene-
volence and humility.
When Emperor Shun turned over the government to
his successor Yu, again his parting injunction was the
same as what he had received from his predecessor,
namely, to hold on to the Golden Mean. How did
Emperor Yu carry out this injunction in his conduct?
Confucius says, "Emperor Yu was satisfied with simple
meals for himself, but was careful about the official
sacrifices; he hated beautiful clothing for personal
decoration, yet was mindful of the beauty of the official
gowns; he neglected his private dwellings that he might
give his all to the public works." One might easily carry
the idea of personal economy too far and apply it also to
the sacrificial offerings, the official gowns and the public
utilities which would then be too simple and slipshod.
On the other hand, one might fall into the other extreme:
because it would be desirable to have dignity and form
in official ceremonies and thoroughness and order in the
public works, one might become extravagant also in his
personal habits. By striking the medium between the
two, Yu carried out the Doctrine of the Golden Mean.
We know less of the personal habits of the next great
Emperor Tang, but Mencius says also that "Tang held on
to the (Golden) Mean," apparently following the
examples of Yao, Shun and Yu. Although the founders
of the Chou Dynasty, Wen Wang and Wu Wang, were
not exactly models in this respect, yet Confucius said of
them: "To push on without relaxation: that Wen Wang
and Wu Wang could not do. To relax without pushing
on; that Wen Wang and Wu Wang would not do. To push
on and then relax alternately: that was the way of Wen
Wang and Wu Wang." Wen Wang and Wu Wang fell
into neither of the errors of insistent expansion or
indolent relaxation, but steered safely between the two,
by alternately playing fast and loose, as it were, which
was their version of the Golden Mean Doctrine.
When we come to Confucius, the founder of Con-
fucianism, we have very explicit statements which clearly
show his Chung-yung temperament and his love for
moderation. Confucius says: "When the Truth does not
prevail, it is because the clever ones overshoot their mark,
and the stupid ones fall short of it. When the Truth is

not clearly understood, it is because the wise ones know

to have summed up the philosoply of moderation in the
one short remark: "To overshoot the mark is the same as
to fall short of it." Commenting on the harmony of
culture in a gentleman, he says: "To have more substance
than form would be vulgar: to have more form than
substance would be only worthy of a government clerk;
to have the harmony of both form and substance there's
your gentleman." Again he says: "A gentleman in this
world does not insist on doing anything or abstaining
from anything; he stops at what is right and proper.'
And of himself, he says, "I never insist on anything
(literally I do not distinguish between what one can do and
what one cannot do)." The Doctrine of the Golden Mean is
here seen to apply to a man's culture and to his personal
conduct and decisions. Here is another picture of the
gentleman from Confucius: he says, "The gentleman
gives to others without incurring much expense; he
works people hard without causing discontent; he takes
from others without being greedy; he is dignified without
seeming proud and awe-inspiring without seeming
physically terrible." His disciples say of him that he
was "gentle and yet firm, awe-inspiring and yet not
rough, polite and at the same time well-poised." These, we
may note, are all virtues of moderation. We may
suppose therefore that when Ts-ssu, the grandson of
Confucius, wrote that chapter on Chung Yung, or
Moderation, he was strictly carrying on the tradition of
Confucius.
Side by side with Confucianism in its earliest period,
there were other extreme schools of philosophy. There
was the extreme right legalist school, based on the theory
of the wickedness of human nature, and believing
absolutely in government control and interférence; on
the other hand, there was the extreme left school of the
Taoists, believing in nature and in absolute laissez-faire.
The theory of the legalist school, however, was tried by
Tsin-shih-hwang, whose imperial rule met with almost
immediate collapse. The Taoist philosophy prevailed
during the Tsin Dynasty, but that did not save the
Dynasty from perishing. In the beginning of the Han
Dynasty, we see three successive changes during three
reigns. Wen-ti was a believer in Taoism, his son and

successor, Ching-ti, swung over to Legalism, and then
the next ruler, Wu-ti, adopted Confucianism and gave it
almost the status of a state religion, banning all the other
schools of thought. Since that time, Confucianism has
kept its position until the very end of the Manchu days.
This seems to show that both extreme right and extreme
left tendencies are not so compatible with the tempera-
ment of the Chinese race as Confucianism, which is
neither right nor left, but stands in the middle, and which
has succeeded in holding sway for two thousand years.
With the contact of western civilization and modern
science, there has grown in China a new school of thought,
which is the well-known Sanmin Doctrine of Dr. Sun
Yat-sen. Now, although the Sanmin Doctrine includes
many things which were never thought of by the Con-
fucianists, it is nevertheless based on the Doctrine of the
Golden Mean preached by Confucianism. We can give
many instances to show its love of moderation which is
so characteristic of the Chinese people. For instance,
we have the two extremes between Nationalism, which
is opposed to the idea of a world state, and Interna-
tionalism, which often takes no cognizance of necessary
or unavoidable national barriers and distinctions. The
Sanmin Doctrine of Dr. Sun stands in the middle between
these two extremes; it stands for
it stands for the national
independence of China, and at the same time for the
ultimate equality of all nations. Again, champions of
the people's rights are usually opposed to a strong
autocratic government, while an autocratic government
generally also tramples upon the people's rights. The
People's Rights principle of Dr. Sun's doctrine provides
for both. It recognizes and stands for the four rights of
the people, which are called their "political rights,"
chiefly concerned with the powers of legislation and
control of government personnel. At the same time, it
gives the five rights of administration, legislation, main-
taining justice, control and examination to the govern-
ment, which are called "government rights." It aims
therefore at a happy medium, wherein both the people's
rights and those of the government are properly
regulated, toward the end that we may have a strong,
efficient and at the same time democratic government.

and labour, there is often the danger of either class
oppressing the other. In the Sanmin Doctrine, the

attitude taken is that capitalism should be restricted by
equalization of the rights of land-ownership, and at the
same time socialist measures should be adopted for the
protection of the interests of the labouring class. Here
we have the Doctrine of the Golden Mean or moderation
applied to the modern economic situation to bring about
an adjustment between capital and labour. In the sphere
of thought, there are conservatives who do not wish to
see China westernized, and there are the radicals who
are not all enthusiastic about the preservation of the
national culture. Dr. Sun believed that we should bring
about a revival of old ethical standards and cultural
attainments, and at the same time that we should learn
what the West has, to teach us. Here we have the
Doctrine of the Golden Mean applied to the question of
general culture. In the system of government, there
are the extreme theories of centralization and decentral-
ization. Dr. Sun believes in a wise and equitable
division of power between the central and the local
governments. The main policies that affect the nation
as a whole will be delegated to the central government,
while those affecting local interests will be placed within
the jurisdiction of the local government.
From all this, we can see that the Sanmin Doctrine of
Dr. Sun Yat-sen is strictly in the true Chinese tradition,
being in keeping with the spirit of moderation inculcated
by Confucianism two thousand years ago, and therefore
seems best fitted for the temperament of the Chinese
people. In this respect, it decidedly differs from the right
nationalists of the fascist type and the left communists.
