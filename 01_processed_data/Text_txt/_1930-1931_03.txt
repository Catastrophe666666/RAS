A JOURNEY TO THE TUNG LING
and a Visit to the Desecrated Eastern
Mausolea of the Ta Tsing Dynasty, in 1929
By EMIL S. FISCHER
Years ago I made a year-long journey of inland obser-
vation through the more remote sections of Szechwan,
Kansu and Shensi without mishap, although the trip
involved ten thousand miles of overland hardship; to-day
a trip of several hundred miles along the coast section of
China presents more difficulties. It is not my aim here to
discuss these difficulties due to hold-ups by Tu-fei-so-
called bandits who are really disbanded soldiers and
deserters.
For a long time I had planned such a journey to the
Imperial summer hunting resort at Jehol, where centuries
ago Emperor Ch'ien Lung erected Imperial residences and
built famous buddhist temples on the crests of hills
surrounding this famous spot. Among these palaces is
a replica of the Patala of Lhassa, the residence of the
Pontiff and Spiritual ruler of Tibet, the Dalai Lama.
But the Councillor of the Waichiaopu refused the necessary
Chinese passport, stating that I was much safer in
Tientsin than in making a journey via the Ku Pei Kao
Pass, outside of the Great Wall, into Mongolia.
Again in 1929 I wished to combine a trip to Jehol with
a visit to Ma Lan Yü, at which market place, inside the
Great Wall, the Tung Ling, or the Eastern Manchu
Mausolea, are situated. I had before visited at various
times the Hsi Ling, or the Western Manchu Mausolea,
and had witnessed there the laying to rest, in his newly-
erected mausoleum, of the Emperor Kwang Hsü and his
Empress, as well as his favourite, Yü Kwei-Fei, the latter
of whom had died at Sian Fu in 1900 during the flight of
the Imperial House, when the Allied Powers pressed on
Peking to relieve the Legations in consequence of the
Boxer troubles. This triplica of an Imperial funeral
took place at Liang Ko Chwang, south-west of Peking, in
December, 1913. Here, along the foot of the Western

This made it comparatively easy to reach the Western
Mausolea. But this was not true in the case of the
Eastern Mausolea, or Tung Ling, which had its more
beautiful outlay about a hundred miles north-east of
Peking in an inner section of the Great Wall, whence a
path leads over the high mountain division into Mongolia,
to Jehol.
Upon studying the Postal Map of Chili Province
(now Hopei) I found among routes which led to Ma
Lan Yü, one from Peking in a north-westerly direction
for about a hundred miles and also another over which
the Chinese postal couriers carry mail. This second route
runs from Tongshan, the industrial centre along the
Peking-Mukden Railway, in a northern and north-western
direction, and its mileage, according to the Postal Route,
aggregated 200 Chinese li, or less than 70 English miles.
I decided to take this shorter route and engaged in
Tongshan at the station a Peking cart, drawn by two
mules tandem fashion. This conveyance could take me to
Ma Lan Yü in possibly two days. As it was at the end
of the rainy season, while we covered half of the distance
in the plain during the first day, the other half we had
difficulty in covering in three days, because we had to
cross a great number of torrents which came southward
from the Great Wall divide of mountains. At this point
mountains reach altitudes of over 6,000 feet and their
serrated tops, gullies and groves brought down from these
hilly sections of the country torrent after torrent whose
rushing waters were five and more feet high. This
brought delay after delay before we could ford them with
the help of peasants who knew the locality and the condi-
tions of the streams.
However we succeeded in safely reaching Ma Lan Yü,
which market town is spread out due north in the rear
of the ridges between which the Tung Ling grounds are
enclosed. It is inside this waste area that each of the

In 1928 suddenly came the widespread news that the Tung
Ling had been looted and destroyed and that even the
bodies of the rulers had been shamefully desecrated and
stripped of their burial gowns and that priceless
treasures which had been entombed with them had been
carried away by soldiers led by their officers.
A first hand knowledge of this region led me to make
some investigation of this terrible state of things. I had
previously visited and examined not only the Hsi Ling,
but also the mausolea of the Ta Tsing rulers, who have
their tombs north and east of Mukden, and had also often
visited the tombs of the Ming, not only in the neighbour-
hood of the Nankow Pass, north of Peking, the Shi San
Ling, where the thirteen rulers of the Ming Dynasty
(1368-1648 A.D.) are entombed, but also the tomb of the
founder of the Ming Dynasty, Emperor Hung Wu, whose

The perspective all over the crescent of hills inside

In the miles of distance towards the central southern
portion, there was the Lung Men, or Dragon Gate,
mountain opening, with a huge marble P'ai-lou divided
into five large portal sections or arches. From thence
in a straight northern direction the mausolea main road
led to the central part of the general outlay of the tombs.
Nearby to this huge P'ai-lou the large mural enclosure, the
Red Wall, with its central entrance portal-a huge
temple entrance of palatial extent with its pagoda-like
roof-could be plainly distinguished.
Near the main portal in the distance a large
enclosure with fine temple buildings and courtyards and
a huge mound in the rear attracted the attention. This
was the tomb known as the Chao Hsi Ling, and contains
the remains of the mother of Emperor Shun Chi, the first
ruler over China during the Ta Tsing Dynasty. She
ought to have been buried at the mausoleum of her
husband at Mukden at the Pai Ling, where the original
rulers of Manchuria have their burial grounds and where
also was the tomb of Tai Tsong Wen Hwang who, ruling
from 1627 to 1643 A.D., closed up the Han Kin (Han Tsin)
Dynasty, being succeeded by the Ta Tsing or Manchu
Dynasty of China. However the mother of Shun Chi had
expressed the desire to be buried near her son, who
erected to her memory the so-called mausoleum Chang
An Feng Chien, which later Emperor Yung Cheng in
1724 renamed the Chao Hsi Ling.

The Tung Ling possessed up to about ten years ago a
most beautiful pine forest which was hundreds of years
old. Some of the trees had a circumference which could
be barely spanned by two men. All this has long since
disappeared. It is pitiful that in addition to the forest
destruction, as in 1928, so in 1930 an actual desecration
of these beautiful mausolea took place. Some of the
destruction which happened to the Hwei Ling has
already been referred to. In addition to the looting these
monuments show a wanton destruction which one cannot
understand. Why did these soldiers tear down parts of
whole roofs of buildings? In some buildings more than
half of the roofs have already gone; no doubt the wind
and the ravages of weather in time will complete the
utter ruin of this monument, unless the government
responsible for these terrible deeds will undertake their
repair and safeguard. This is much to be wished for.
The China Branch of the Royal Asiatic Society which
years ago interested itself in the preservation of
monuments in China, should try to see that this is done
substantially, even if the Tung Ling are far away from the
beaten track. For instance, in the Shwan Fei Ling or the
double green-glazed tiled tombs of K'ang Hsi's con-
cubines, the front roof of the temple entrance was all
gone. When I approached the so-called Shen Chou Kao,
by which the slaughtering compound is meant, which
stands outside of the Ching Ling, or the K'ang Hsi
Mausoleum, I looked into it and found that it was now
the stable of hundreds of Mongol ponies belonging to the
military guard which was stationed at Ma Lan Yü when
I visited the Tung Ling in August, 1929.
