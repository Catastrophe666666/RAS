ARTICLE V.
A SKETCH OF THE TAUIST MYTHOLOGY IN ITS MODERN FORM.
BY THE REV. JOSEPH EDKINS.
Read before the Society, May 17th, 1859.
At the head of this mythology is a trinity of persons, who preside
over the intellectual universe, the physical being governed by
divinities of a lower grade. The title of the first individual in this
triad is Yuen shi t'ien tsun, the honoured one of heaven, first in
time. The heaven in which he resides is called "the upper capital
of the jade-stone mountain," or more shortly, "the jade-stone
pure region." Tauism follows the Hindoo method of imagining a
tier of heavens ranged one over the other, or scattered through
the starry spaces, constituting the residences of divinities. The
entrance to his palace is called, in imitation of the style of an
oriental emperor, "the golden door." He is said to have created
the three worlds. He brought into existence Devas, and other
rational beings, in overwhelming multitudes. He sets the stars
in motion, and causes the planets to revolve. He is the source of
all true doctrine, and is in himself immaterial, and spontaneous.*

Subordinate to this supreme triad are various divinities, of whom
the highest is Yuh-hwang-ta-ti, the great jade-stone emperor. He
is the governor of the physical universe, and as such corresponds,
say the Buddhists, to Indra Shakra in their mythology. He is
inferior to the San t'sing, the above-mentioned triad, as Shakra is
inferior to the Buddhas. His residence is the pure jade-stone
palace in the T'ai-wei tract of stars. An ancestor of the hereditary
head of the Tauist sect, whose family name is Chang, has been
promoted to this rank.
There is a "ruler of earth" corresponding to Yuh-hwang-shang-
ti, the ruler of heaven. This personage is called Heu t'u. From
the earliest periods, the Chinese used language, which ascribed
divinity to earth, or rather the spirit of the earth. He dwells in
a palace made of the seven precious stones. He presides over the
fruits of the soil, and over the mountains and rivers. He is styled
Heu t'u hwang ti k'i, spirit of imperial earth, ruler of the soil.
Next to these occurs a series of star-gods, astrology forming a
principal element in the development of the Tauist polytheism.
The divinity who is called T'ien hwang ta ti resides in the present
pole-star. He is said to rule over the north and south poles, and
to regulate the affairs of heaven, earth and man. He presides
over war and peace, and the revolutions of the stars. In his posi-
tion he resembles the high mountains of the earth. His residence
is called the palace of the Lesser Bear, at the pole of the Tsï-wei
tract of stars.
The lord of the stars (sing chu) is another personage who is
called the great emperor of the north pole. He regulates the
movements of the sun, moon and planets, and presides over the
stars, the great circles and the seasons. His residence is a star of
the same name, near the pole.
The spirit of the south pole is called the original holy honoured
one of heaven, and the long-lived great emperor of the south pole.

The sun and moon are also adored as gods. The former is
called T'ai yang ti kiün, the sun-ruler, or "the honoured one of
heaven that diffuses true light," or "the bright ruler of the palace
of the sun." He is said to preside over the door of light in the
south, and over the soul (hwen) and intellect of man. He is the
most honoured of all the lights of heaven. Wherever he shines,
he changes evil into good.

The spirit of the moon presides over the door of darkness in the
north. She protects the material soul of man (p'eh), and sends
down with great impartiality, happiness and misery on the good
and the bad among mankind. She is spoken of as the bare tra-
versing her nine paths,* and is styled the honoured one of heaven,
the light of whose wisdom shines brightly.

There is a very well known triad of subordinate divinities called
San yuen, the three original ones, or San kwan, the three rulers.
They preside over three departments of physical nature, heaven,
earth, and water. They are styled the three holy men who form
a unity (T'ai kih). They send down good and ill fortune on men,
and save the lost. They are called in their collective unity san kwan
ta ti the three rulers who constitute one great god, and “the
honoured one of heaven, who is easily influenced."
The next deity to be mentioned is Hiuen t'ien shang ti, high
emperor of the dark heaven. He is also denominated Chen wu
ta ti, the great military emperor. He is described as the model
of the true ascetic. He has transformed himself eighty-two times
to become the instructor of men in the three national religions.
He is called the spirit of happiness, and the honoured one of heaven
who overcomes demons (Maras), and who came from the golden
door (of heaven) to assume a human body.
There are several personages, belonging to different periods of
Chinese history, who are worshipped under the name tsu, patriarchs.
Some of them are real and others fictitious. The older five patri-
archs are styled ti kiin, imperial ruler. The first is Shau yang,
or Tung hwa. He is placed at the head of a series of men, who
have transmitted the doctrines of Lau tsu to modern times. Cheng
yang whose real name was Chung-li k'iuen, and who is said to
have beeen a general of the Han dynasty, is the second. The
third is Feu-yeu, who is also called Lii tsu. He is in the Tauist
religion, what Kwan yin is in the Buddhist. He is said to have
been born in the T'ang dynasty, and to have revealed the secret
doctrines of the spirits and genii. The fourth Hai chan, was a
statesman who served the Liau imperial family. His name was
Lieu tsau. The last of the five is Chung yang ti kuïn. His name
was Wang hi and he is styled the teacher of perfect truth, who
communicated to men the mysterious doctrines of heaven, caused
'them to know the way to immortality, and spread far and wide
the principles upheld by the various schools of Tauism.
Another series of patriarchs consists of five distinguished authors
of no great antiquity. They are Chang tsi yang, author of Wu
chen p'ien, book on the discovery of truth, Shil hing lin, author
of Hwan yuen p'ien, book on returning to the origin, Sieh tsï hien,
who commented on the former of these two works, C'hen ni wan,
writer of several noted works, and Peh tsï t'sing, another well-

These men did not live earlier than the tenth
century, and all belonged to southern China. They are styled
chen jen, truc men, and are also with two others called "the seven
true men of the south." The other two are Lieu yung nien (a
commentator on the work Wu chen p'ien and disciple of its author),
and Peng hoh lin (a writer of some Tauist hymns).
There are seven Tauist writers of north China, who are called
"the seven true men," or "the seven true men of the north."
They were principally of the twelfth and thirteenth centuries.
The first is Ma tan yang and the seventh, a female, is his wife.
She is named Sun t'sing tsing. The others are Tan c'hang c'hun,
Lieu c'hang sheng, Kieu c'hang c'hun, Wang yuh wang, and
Hoh kwang ning. The woman is called yuen kiün original ruler,
while the rest are styled "true ruler.”
A female divinity named Teu mu, the aged dame of the great
bear, is of Hindoo origin. She is addressed as "queen of heaven."
Her Hindoo name is Ma-li-che.
The god of literature is Wen c'hang. He is said to have trans-
formed himself ninety-eight times, and to have wrought numerous
wonderful effects. He has promoted all the three national religions.
He equals in authority the three rulers of heaven, earth and the
sea, and assists those who are seeking office, or testing their abilities
in the public examinations.
The divinity who presides over T'ai shan, a high mountain in
the province of Shantung, is called Tung yoh ta ti, the great
emperor of the eastern mountain. There are four other divinities
ruling over the principal mountains in the north, south, west and
centre of China, but this one is more the object of Tauist worship
than the rest. He is said to have appeared in many successive
ages in the person of various teachers of Tauist doctrine. He
rules over punishments in the present and future life. Two series
of rulers in hades, one of ten, and another of thirty-six, are subject
to him. The names of the good he records as deserving of heaven.
Those who are corrupted and deceived, he seeks out, that he may
save them.
Confucius has a place assigned him among the deities of this
religion, and he is addressed as "the honoured one of heaven who
causes literature to flourish and the world to prosper."
The living head of the Tauist religion, a person of the Chang
family, is honoured under the title T'ien shï, heavenly teacher.

Like almost all the deities that go before, he is addressed with the
the title Ta ti, great emperor, and T'ien tsun, the honoured one of

There are some other personages whose names appear in Tauist
history, who are also honoured as gods. Hü tsu, an ancient phy-
sician, worshipped as a medical divinity; Koh tsu, a well known
Tauist author, and an alchemist of the third century; Sah tsu, and
three brou ars in the Han dynasty, called the San Mau, are among
the most conspicuous of these individuals.
In this enumeration, those personages are not mentioned who
have received posthumous titles from the state. The divine honours
paid to them are a part of the national religion, but there is no
special form of worship appointed to be used for them in the Tauist
books. They are such as Kwan ti, the god of war, the protectors
of districts and departments, called C'heng hwang, and of lesser
territorial divisions called T'u ti. They may in some sense be
classed with the personages immediately honoured by the literati
in the Confucian temple, and who receive similar titles.
