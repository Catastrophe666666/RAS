THE PHILOSOPHER YANG HSIUNG
By A. FORKE
Yang Hsiung, or Yang Tse-yün, was born in P'i-hsien,
a district of Cheng-tu, the capital of Szechuan Province.
The name is derived from Yang-hsien, a town in Shansi
which an ancestor of the philosopher held as fief. At
Yang Hsiung's time the family had become rather poor,
but, engrossed by his studies, he did not feel this poverty
very much. In his biography in the Ch'ien Han-shu it is
stated that he died in A.D. 18 at the age of 71 years,
consequently he must have been born in 53 B.C. First he
wrote poetry after the fashion of his famous countryman,
Sse-ma Hsiang-ju, then he imitated Ch'ü Yuan and
composed an elegy, the Fan Li-sao, which he threw into the
Min river as a homage to the drowned poet. About 13
B.C. Yang Hsiung arrived at Chang-an, where he was
recommended to the emperor Cheng-ti, who appointed him
member of the Han-lin and later secretary of the
Censorate. When in A.D. 9 Wang Mang overthrew the
Han dynasty, he was not promoted as many others were
who flattered the usurper. Implicated in a trial of his
disciple Liu Fen, he jumped from the Tien-lu Hall, when
he was to be arrested, and almost lost his life. Then he
wished to retire owing to his age, but Wang Mang
appointed him Ta-fu. The height of the Tien-lu Hall is
no sufficient reason to discredit the incident, as some
scholars have done, for Yang Hsiung may have thrown
himself down from a lower story.
Whereas up to the Sung time Yang Hsiung was placed
on a level with Mêng-tse and Hsün-tse, some Sung
philosophers blamed his moral character and found fault
with his writings. They censured him for having served
under Wang Mang instead of committing suicide, and
accused him of having flattered the usurper whom he ought
to have condemned. These charges brought forward,
especially by Tschu Hsi, are unjust. The praise he gave to

Wang Mang dates from the time of his regency, before he
dethroned the Han dynasty, and was well deserved. His
honour has been vindicated by Tsêng Kung of the Sung
dynasty and by Chiao Hung of the Ming dynasty. As a
philosopher he was highly appreciated by the historian
Pün Ku, by the philosopher Wang Ch'ung, the poet Han
Yü, by Wang An-shi and most of all by Sse-ma Kuang,
the great statesman, historian, and scholar, who edited his
chief works, the Fa-yen and the Tai-hsuan ching, with a
commentary.
In addition to these works Yang Hsiung wrote a book
on music, Yo, in four chapters, and admonitions for
officials in the provinces, Chou-chen, in two chapters. He
is also the author of the Hsün-tsuan, a dictionary of 5,340
characters in fourteen old scripts. All these works have
been lost, only of the Hsün-tsuan Ma Kuo-han has collected
and published some fragments. On the well-founded
testimony of the Fêng-su t'ung-i of the 2nd century A.D.,
the Fang-yen, a dictionary of colloquialisms in various
dialects, has been attributed to Yang Hsiung. The
objection raised by Hung-mai, a writer of the Sung
time, that this work is not mentioned in the biography
of the Han-shu nor in the section on Literature, is
well answered by the editors of the Sse-k'u Ch'üan-shu
tsung-mu holding that probably Yang Hsiung left this
work incomplete, and that it was completed and published
by his disciples.
In the Fa-yen, which is written in the style of the
Sun-yu, we find some fine utterances, e.g., "Temperament
is the horse on which we ride to good or evil, for in human
nature good and evil are mixed up. He who cultivates
the good becomes a good man, and he who cultivates the
evil, a bad one." Yang Hsiung proclaims Confucius as
the infallible teacher but also acknowledges Lao-tse,
though with a limitation, saying, "What Lao-tse says
about Tao and virtue I admit, but when he demolishes
benevolence and justice and extinguishes propriety and
study, I do not admit it."
The Tai-hsuan ching is a book on divination like the
Yi king. Its scientific value is not to be sought in the
principal sections, but in the appendices, which contain
Yang Hsiung's metaphysics. His ontological principle
underlying the whole universe, from which everything is
evolved and which governs every change in nature and in
human life, he calls Hsuan,the Mystery. It has the same
functions as Tao and scarcely differs from it.

Yang Hsiung cannot boast of the brilliant style of
Mencius, his own being often too concise and obscure, nor
of the critical acumen of Hsun-tse, but he surpasses them
both as a speculative philosopher, being undoubtedly the
greatest metaphysician among the Confucianists before
the Sung period.
