Sophism, which came to an end quickly in Greece
because philosophy had found in the teachings of Plato
and Aristotle a system with sufficient prestige to last,
continued in China until the beginning of the Sung era.
Indeed, even at the beginning of the eleventh century the
doctrines of Confucius were in danger of being lost to
posterity. General writers had trifled with them, poets
and philosophers of the T'ang dynasty had hopelessly
mixed them with Buddhist and Taoist thought and no
great thinkers had arisen to point out the lofty ethical
ideals or systematize and adapt them for the use of a
changing age.
Shortly after the beginning of the eleventh century,
however, there arose a whole galaxy of famous teachers-
men like Chou Tun I, Ch'êng Hao and his brother
Ch'êng I,¹ Yang Kuei Shan, Chang Tsai, and most famous
of all, Chu Hsi. From then on, though suffering the
vicissitudes of time and the changing fortunes of war,
this school of thinkers, first contemptuously called the
Yuan Yu Tang2 and later honoured as the real transmit-
ters of the sage's teachings, established their doctrines as
the true interpretation of the Classics, and Confucianism
with Taoist and Buddhist influences became the national
cult of China. It is important to note, first, that the
Neo-Confucianism which developed into a great Ration-
alistic system in the Sung, Yüan and Ming dynasties was
very different from the comparatively simple ethical
teachings of Confucius and Mencius, and second, that
this new philosophy was in the beginning a heterodox
teaching which only succeeded in becoming the accepted
interpretation after many years of bitter struggle with
the political party which was in power.

The teachings of Ch'êng I Ch'uan can be taken as
our point of departure for a survey of the chief doctrines
of the Neo-Confucian school. T'ang dynasty scholars
had been reading the Book of Changes; Ch'ên T'uan, the
Taoist, had expounded his theories of the Extreme
Ultimate and Chou Tun I, influenced by Ch'ên T'uan's
theories, had evolved his "Diagram of the Supreme
Ultimate." Gradually the doctrine of two great forces
at work in the universe was formulated. These were Li
and Ch'i, Law and Ether. This doctrine received its
most complete statement by Chu Hsi but was first clearly
expressed by Ch'êng I Ch'uan, the disciple of Chou Tun I.
Ch'êngtzŭ said, "Apart from the negative and positive
principles there is no ordering of the universe. The
negative and positive principles are the Ether. The
Ether comes after there is form. The order of the
universe comes before form. This is the Law." These
two forces, Law and Ether, play an important part
in the thinking of the Sung, Yüan and Ming dynasties.
Sometimes the universe is conceived in materialistic terms
and these two forces, equally important or one prior to
and more powerful than the other, interact to produce
the ten thousand things of the world; sometimes the
relation of these two forces reminds one of the antithesis
of mind and matter in Western thought, while in the
writings of other philosophers, the Law seems to be an
ethical force which can mould and shape Ether and the
dualism in the universe ultimately resolves itself into a
unity with Law supreme.
This doctrine of the two forces Law and Ether was
found very useful in the field of Ethics to explain the

thinkers. The original nature was good as Mencius had

One other phase of Ch'êngtzu's teachings, though not
so constructive, was quite as important in the develop-
ment of Sung thought. This was the doctrine of "cul-
tivating reverence, "Chu Ching."10 "Ching" was a
favourite word in Confucius' teachings. It meant "self-
respect," "attention to duty," "uprightness of character."
He said, "be ching in business," "in handling public
affairs, be ching,"12 "trustworthy and ching in action."13
In the Sung dynasty this word ching was given a slightly
different content. Buddhism had become indigenous and
its theology had attracted the careful attention of
scholars. The need for a more distinctly religious
element in their national cult was felt by Confucian
scholars. Ching helped to fill this want. The word was
therefore used to indicate a reverential attitude, a spirit
of adoration and worship. Buddhism of the Ch'an school
had emphasized meditation as the way to seek personal
virtue.
The Confucianists endorsed this and taught that
the way to cultivate reverence was to retire from active
life and in the tranquillity of some lonely spot seek to
eliminate the evil desires and passions from human
nature, strengthen the original nature and weaken or
destroy the Ch'i Chih¹¹ or physical nature. Thus Ch'êng-
tzŭ says, "Reverence is the way to keep away evil
thoughts. Keeping away evil thoughts and retaining
sincerity, although they are two things, become one.
When the evil thoughts are kept away, sincerity is
naturally preserved. In the world there is only one good
and one evil. When the good is gone, evil prevails; when
the evil is gone, good prevails."15
Thus at the beginning of the eleventh century we
find a rejuvenated Confucianism, so changed that the
great sage himself would not have recognized it. We
find the scholars dabbling in ontological speculations,

ciples. We find Human Nature much more complex than

of a teacher who had instructed his students in the

Several reasons for the distinctive development of
Neo-Confucian thought may be noted. First, Buddhism
which had been introduced into China shortly before the
Christian era, had by the beginning of the Sung dynasty
developed Chinese schools of thought which profoundly
influenced the minds of scholars. Primitive Confucian-
ism had been lacking in distinctly religious aspects or in
philosophical speculation. The Master had confined his
attention to the outlining of a political ethics designed to
teach men how to be good citizens, which while satis-
factory for some purposes, did not fill the hunger of souls
craving for a deeper insight into the meaning of the
universe. Buddhism and a revived Taoism were supply-
ing this religious need, filling life with new meaning,
throwing back the gates of the spiritual world and giving
to the earnest seeker visions of the realms beyond. Con-
fucianism, in order to maintain its supremacy over the
minds of the Chinese, was forced to modify its views and
give some attention to philosophical and religious
subjects. Indeed, the evolution to this new point of view
came naturally without a conscious decision on the part
of the Neo-Confucian scholars. They had been influenced
by Buddhist teachings to such an extent that it was
inevitable they should drift into an imitation of the con-
templation of the Buddhist, should begin to study man's
emotions and desires and deal with problems of Human
Nature and Heaven's Law. On the one hand, Confucian
scholars bitterly attacked Buddhism and Taoism; on the
other, they incorporated within their own system so many
of the ideas from these two schools of thought that it was
impossible to tell when they were speaking as orthodox
Confucian scholars and when they had adopted the myst-
ical language of the Buddhist monk or Taoist priest.
Fully as great a factor in the rise of these new
doctrines was the persecution the scholars of the Yuan Yu
Tang suffered at the hands of Wang An Shih's party.
Deprived of political office it was natural that they should
grow introspective; unable to serve in practical affairs
their only refuge was the hermit's cell and a life of
meditation. Wang An Shih had no time for idle specula-
tion. He was engrossed with the practical problems of

Rationalists. They may have belonged to different
schools, held different theories as to the exact relation of
Law and Ether in the universe, but they had all received
the same training, had studied from the same books and
had occupied themselves with the same intellectual frivo-
lities. When the country was torn by dissensions within
and threatened with a foreign foe without their patriot-
ism was all on paper, and when the crisis came they "had
no way to help their prince save by dying." It is little
wonder, therefore, that at this time the philosophy these
scholars represented should have been discredited, and
the reformers of the seventeenth century should have
revolted against methods of education which had con-
tributed to the downfall of the Ming dynasty, had
betrayed the country into the hands of foreigners and
had brought untold suffering upon the people.
As representative of this revolt against Sung and
Ming dynasty Rationalism I have chosen three men, all
living in the period 1625-1700. They hail from different
parts of the country and in educational opportunities and
point of view on life they differed widely, but all lived
through this period of revolution, all suffered from the
misgovernment and lawlessness of the times and all with
one voice condemn the Confucianism of the Sung scholars
with its Buddhist and Taoist influences and metaphy-
sical speculations, and insist that philosophy must get
back to the teachings of Confucius and Mencius and the
country must return to the condition where "a father is
a father, a son a son, a prince a prince, and a minister a
minister," if the Middle Kingdom is to be saved.

This study of Ku T'ing Lin, Fei Mi and Yen Hsi Chai
does not by any means exhaust this revolutionary period.
These three are representative only. Coming as they did
from different sections of the country, holding varied
opinions on many subjects, they were in essential agree-
ment as far as Rationalism was concerned. They,
together with others, were the leaders of a revolt against
a system of philosophy which had reigned practically
for three hundred years. They are, however, more than
this. Their influence extended throughout the Ch'ing
dynasty and their spirit was an inspiration and incentive
to K'ang Yu Wei and the other political reformers of
1898. To-day again, those who are seriously seeking an
intellectual basis for the present revolutionary movement
are turning to study anew the writings of Ku T’ing Lin,
Fei Mi and Yen Hsi Chai.
