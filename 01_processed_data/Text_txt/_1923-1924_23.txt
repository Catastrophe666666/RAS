The hand of the dead (mortmain, but not in the legal
sense which simply refers to the continuity of tenure of
an impersonal corporation) rests heavily on the living in
China. The humorous saying that "corporations have no
souls" is in a way the inverse of the truth; it is only a
corporation that has a continuous existence. An appre-
ciable proportion of the family income is devoted to the
maintenance of graves and ancestral halls; a large frac-
tion of the arable area is occupied by graves; the expenses
of funeral and memorial rites are large, and feng-shui
hinders public works and private building operations.
On the other hand this devotion to the dead, which is
part of the whole system of filial piety (#), does act as
a social cement; the mutual respect for the related dead
helps to maintain that family solidarity which appears
to be the mainstay of Chinese civilization and is certainly
an economic insurance for the bulk of the population.
The dead do in a manner live again in their descen-
dants and psychology sees in some of the phenomena of
instinct and subconsciousness the vestigial reminiscence
of the ancestral life. The law of inheritance in all lands
implicitly recognizes the rights of the dead.
From one point of view the living are merely the
temporary vehicle of the flame of life which passes from
ancestor to descendant and, in a time dimensional sense,
all living beings (and especially those of one genus) are
one organism. Buddhism and its Karma becomes almost
a reality if Karma be regarded as the continuous stream
of life. Retribution in a physical sense is in a manner
inflicted upon the descendants if they are regarded as
one with the ancestors, although one may question the
justice of its incidence. Chinese magic more or less
implies that the dead can be evoked in the descendants¹
and it is not beyond possibility that the dominant cha-
racteristics of an ancestor may be perpetuated in such
a way that a certain subconscious dissociation may form
a fractional or sub-personality which does resemble that

In China the dead are kuei shen, i.e., wandering,

William Simpson, a well known artist and archaeo-
logist, many years ago propounded the hypothesis that
the most predominant feature in religious practice is the
worship of death. Sir James Frazer, in his encyclopaedic
work "The Golden Bough," provides a wealth of collateral
evidence to this effect without intending to do so and
Grant Allen in his book "The Evolution of the Idea of
God" crystallized the idea so that it may be regarded as
a definite theory. He remarked on the support which
Chinese practice gives to the hypothesis, but, so far as
the writer is aware, no very serious attempt has been
made to study this aspect of the matter except in so far
as Johnston has done so on general folk lore lines in his
"Lion and Dragon in Northern China."
The theory, briefly expressed, is as follows:-All
supernatural beings worshipped are either dead men or
derive their authority from a direct or indirect association
with the idea of dead men. All temples and sacred places
are tombs or derive their sanctity from direct or indirect
association with the dead. All idols are emblematical
representations of the dead or derive sanctity from
necrolatric customs. Nature spirits and those gods who
are no longer consciously associated with death are really
sublimated ghosts (often of remote royal ancestors) who
have become delocalised by mythology. All ritual and
conjuration is essentially necromancy. Hero worship is
a generalisation of ancestor worship, the hero being as it
were adopted by his admirers.
In China there is a good prima facie case for this
hypothesis. With scarcely an exception all the objects
of worship are known by history or legend to have been
once men. This Euhemerist theory (Euhemerus or more
correctly Euemeros was a Greek philosopher who lived
300 B.C. and asserted that all the Gods were once men) is
not much favoured by mythologists and folk lore students
but agrees very well with religious custom as distin-
guished from mythology, which last is chiefly a matter
of word-play.

Although Tylor, Avebury, Frazer and others have
sufficiently established the general uniformity of tribal
practice, it is very interesting to compare the Chinese
family system with that obtaining among Aryan tribes
of Europe who are the ancestors of most of the
European peoples. The villages were essentially family
groups (as denoted by names like Cott-ing-ham, the home
of the Cott clan) and obeyed a system of clan laws, of
which the name still survives in the word Bye-laws
(Byr-laws: Byr means an inhabitant modn. dutch
"Boer"; the root occurs in the verb "to bide"). The
laws were promulgated at annual (and other) "folk
moots" or "things" (whence "meeting"=moot-thing) held
in the open on a moot-hill or "thingwall" which was
often a barrow ("bury"-ing place) with a stone pillar
and sacred tree. There can be little doubt that where
the village was of longstanding the barrow stone and
tree were sacred to the ancestor of the clan, but in the
case of invasion settlements, as in England, the ritual
might adopt older nameless barrows. Criminals were
executed near by and probably originally on the ancestral
grave and to the manes of the dead. The headman' and
the elders (redesmen) in some cases sat upon the stones
of the dead or on circles of stones, the original idea
probably being the personations of the ancestors, who
witnessed the moot. In some cases a sacred symbol or
staff was used to summon the folk to the moot, and from
the reverence and quasi-human customs with which it
was treated, it may be presumed to have been originally
a sacred image of the first ancestor. The principal
subjects of discussion at folk moots were the control of
animals and the tenure of land, but in earlier times
personal disputes were judged and sentences of death
and outlawry were given. Tenure and distribution of
land were particularly important legal issues.

mitive mound builders. The thesis of that book is like
that of Grant Allen that all these monuments are grave
stones or cenotaphs, the larger circles and avenues refer-
ring to the deceased of a battle.
The practical bearing of worship of death is an
important sociological question. The recent war, with
its immense cemeteries, cenotaphs and the cult of the
"unknown warrior," shows that the same tendencies
persist. The repugnance to cremation and the desire
to have the dead in an accessible cemetery bear a like
testimony.
The psychologist would probably hold that by provid-
ing a discharge for the emotions consequent upon the
loss of the dear departed, funeral rites are inevitable.
There is no necessary relation to belief in postmortem
existence as may be seen from the funeral practices of
modern sceptics. The fact is that personal association
must focus on whatever remains of the dead and so long
as there is any real or symbolic relic the memories of
the past life will be revived in the survivors by seeing
such relics but in a degree diminishing as other activities
gradually absorb the mental energy which was at first
locked up with those memories.
In so far as there are in all of us memories of the
dead which may by suggestion and contemplation be
stimulated, funeral rites will probably always have a
strong social effect, and the hypothesis that this fund
of emotion (with or without the amative instincts to
which modern psychology attaches so much importance)
is the main basis of practical religion as distinguished
from theory, has a high degree of probability.

With Shang Ti the conditions are somewhat simpler.
In the first place the word Ti is unquestionably human.
Chavannes mentions the marvellous birth of Hou Chi
() from the footprint of the Ti as a "precious
souvenir of the ancient belief in an Emperor from on
High who was but a deified man" and, after mentioning the
confusion in Chou times of Shang Ti with T'ien, remarks
that finally T'ien became equivalent to the sky itself, just
as the local land god finally became the second member in
the Grand Duality of Heaven and Earth.
The writer believes that all anthropologists and
modern sinologues hold that the primitive Chinese were
a savage people who worshipped local nature spirits and
dead men with bloody sacrifices to wooden images just as
modern savages do and our own ancestors once did. By
the fusion of the clan divinities of tribal chiefs, parallel
to the fusion of the tribes into the State, China early
developed a vague monotheism alongside the polytheism
of the people as a whole. The latter still survives in
much the original form. The Tu Ti (±) of the field
shrines and the tablets in the halls of the clans still
preserve the primitive practices in a slightly modified
form, and the cross associations between the two cults
dimly indicate an original unity which conforms to the
"Golden Bough" theory.
Thus the old "Term" controversy ceases to have any
meaning. T'ien and Shang Ti were both originally human
tribal gods and at some very remote epoch were probably
men.
