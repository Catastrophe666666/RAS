I have described above some twenty different kinds
of foot-measures. Generally speaking this list includes
all the important measures from the Han dynasty down
to recent times. The foot-measure is important, not only
because in every country it is related to the affairs of
daily life, but because in China especially it was used by
the government to harmonize the musical scale, and to
fix the time of day on the sun-dial. It is for this reason
that the various dynasties took great care to fix the
measures and to study the systems that had prevailed in
previous dynasties.

(A.D. 265-274) Hsün Hsü (4) made a comparative
study of two kinds of musical scales, two kinds of bronze

can now confidently lay it down as a general law that
there has been a steady tendency for the Chinese foot-
measure to increase in length, but that the rate of increase
has not always been uniform. This increase was greatest

dynasties; that is to say, between the years A.D. 265 and
554. From the T'ang period to the present the increase
is very slight; especially so since the Sung dynasty. The
reason for the rapid increase during the above-mentioned
three hundred years will be found in the fact that after
the close of the Three Kingdoms in A.D. 265, the govern-
ments of the various succeeding dynasties began to collect
part of their revenues in silk and other kinds of fabric.
The standard of value in terms of silk or cloth was called
a p'i (or bolt) which was required to measure forty ch'ih
(or Chinese feet) in length and two ch'ih and two t'sun
(inches) in width. The officials who collected the
revenues, fearing on the one hand a shortage of measure,
and desirous on the other to collect more than their due,
did what they could to increase the measure from time to
time. Especially was this true of the dynasties that ruled
over North China.
Beginning, however, with the Chin, southern Sung,
and Yüan dynasties (11th, 12th, 13th centuries), the
government no longer demanded taxes in silk and cloth.
Hence, for the past eight hundred years the foot-measure
has maintained approximately the same length that it had
in T'ang and early Sung times.
That the two motives mentioned above for the
lengthening of the foot-measure were actually operative
during the centuries in question, is amply proven by the
contemporary dynastic records. The Kao Tsu Chi section
of the Wei Dynastic History states that in the nineteenth
year of the reign of the Emperor T'ai Ho, that is, in
A.D. 495, an edict was issued to alter both the foot-measure
and the measure of capacity on the ground that these
had become too long and too large. Twenty years passed,
however, with no perceptible change in the official corrup-
tion. For we are told in the same history-in the biogra-
phical sketch of Yang Chin-that when the latter was
appointed magistrate of Hua Chou, Shensi, the foot-
measure then in use for collecting silk was especially long,
that the officials profited greatly, and that the people
suffered much at their hands. Consequently Yang Chin
commanded that the official foot-measure should again be
the standard. The Chang P'u-hui section of this same
Wei dynasty history states that in the middle of the Shên
Kuei reign-period (A.D. 518) not only were the required
dimensions of silk and cloth excessively large, but that an

that the Official Cloth Foot-Measure of the Sung dynasty
was shorter than the foot-measures ascribed to the T'ang
dynasty. This seems, at first glance, to be an exception
to the law which we laid down, that the foot-measure had
a clearly-marked tendency to increase as time went on.
A closer study will show, however, that this seeming dis-
crepancy is not in reality a contradiction of the law, but
is in fact a confirmation of it. We must bear in mind
that there were two distinct types of foot-measures in
use during the T'ang period: one, the large foot-measure
listed above; the other, a so-called "millet" foot-measure
which was two t'sun (or Chinese inches) shorter. This
is clearly stated in the Chin Pu Lang Chung section of the
T'ang Liu Tien () where we are told that the foot-
measure was determined by the ratio it sustained to the
black panicled millet (E) grown in North China. The
diameter of one grain made a fên, ten fên made a t'sun
(inch), ten t'sun made a ch'ih (foot), twelve t'sun made
a large foot, and ten feet made a chang. The smaller or
millet measure was used to harmonize the musical scale,
to fix the divisions on the dial, to compound medicines,
and to regulate the official head-dress. But the large foot
was used in all other governmental transactions. This
had been the practice in the Later Chou and the Sui
dynasties, hence the T'angs followed their example.

But the more I acquaint myself with the system that

these variations have their roots in antiquity. According
to the regulations of the Board of Revenue () of the
Tang dynasty, foot-measures were determined by the
millet grown in North China. One hundred grains of
millet made a foot. Having fixed this foot, one may
obtain the large foot by the addition of two inches. In
the T'ang period forty large feet made a p’i. This was
equivalent to forty-eight millet feet. Now the govern-
ment in collecting silk requires forty-eight feet to make
a p'i. This is exactly equivalent to forty Huai Ch'ih
(ER). As in other things we have followed practices
set by the T'angs, so it appears that our present Shêng
Ch'ih (R) was determined by the millet foot of T'ang
times. If this were not so, there is no sufficient reason
why it should continue to be used in government offices,
especially in view of the fact that the Huai Ch'ih is
already used for measuring silks."
What Ch'êng Ta-ch'ang called the Kuan Ch'ih and
the Shêng Ch'ih were the Official Cloth Foot-Measures of
the Sung dynasty. This is confirmed by Chao Yü-shih's
remark in the Pin T'ui Lu () that the Shêng Ch'ih
was the Cloth Foot-Measure. From this we know that
the Official Cloth Foot-Measure was the smaller foot-
measure of the Sung dynasty, and for that reason is also
smaller than the ordinary or larger foot-measure of the
Tang dynasty. In other words, the Official Cloth-
Measure of the Sung dynasty is larger than the millet
measure of the T'ang dynasty. Hence I say that the
length of the Sung Cloth Foot-Measure does not contradict
the law which I laid down, but in reality helps to
confirm it.
