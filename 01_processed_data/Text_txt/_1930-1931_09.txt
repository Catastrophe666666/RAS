THE I WEI
A Problem in Criticism
By PERCY BRUCE, M.A., D. LITT.
The subject I have chosen may appear a somewhat
strange one to select, and it may be well to say a word
or two by way of explanation.
To begin with I have been anxious for some time
to trace to their source those philosophic conceptions
which are characteristic of the Sung period, but instead
of starting at the Sung end and working backwards I
decided for various reasons to start with the Han period
and work downwards.
But why the Han period? One of the answers is
this:
There has always appeared to me to be something
specially attractive about the Han period as a field for
literary investigation for the reason, if for no other, that
it immediately follows the Burning of the Books by the
First Emperor.
That tragic event was like a spiritual Great Wall
separating us from the Ante-Ch'in period of Literature
by a barrier which it is very difficult to pass, so that the
Han Period may almost be regarded as the beginning
of things in a literary sense. Whatever there was before,
and survived the ordeal of the Ch'in persecution, passed
through the hands, or minds, of the Han scholars, either
as editors, or as memorizers.
I said just now that I was anxious to trace to their
source the conceptions of the Sung philosophers. The
task, however, has already to a very large extent been
most ably done by Professor Forke in his work The
World-Conceptions of the Chinese. He has covered
almost the whole field in every period, but on parallel
lines, if I may use the expression. That is, the Professor
has taken individual conceptions and followed their
development separately and on parallel lines down
through the successive generations of literature.

A task which still remains to be done by scholars is
to give us a presentation of the philosophy of each period
as a whole. Suzuki and Hu Shih have done something on
this line, but we still wait for something exhaustive and
comprehensive. From this point of view the Han period
is a tempting field for study.
The question still remains-Why the I Wei? There
are of course other works of the Han period, such as the
writings of Tung Chung-shu, of much greater importance
from the point of view I have just mentioned, and a
further word of explanation may very fairly be asked
for. I might give a two-fold answer to the question and
say: The I Wei fascinates me while it irritates me. The
questions which repeatedly assert themselves-What is
the I Wei? What constitutes the I Wei? What does it
all mean as to its content? When and how was it
produced?-are irritating by reason of the perplexity
they cause, and yet fascinating because one finds it almost
impossible to leave them alone.
That probably, to be perfectly candid, is one reason.
But it seems to me that involved in obscurity and mystery,
as it is, the work is not without considerable importance
if we are rightly to understand the period to which it
purports to belong, if it belongs to that period; and if it
does not belong to that period it is also important that
we should know that it does not.
Now it is obvious that, comparatively small though
the work is that goes under the name of the I Wei, it
would be impossible to give a complete and comprehen-
sive treatment of it in the time allotted to this paper.
But as I have just indicated, the prior problem to be
considered before the treatment of the subject matter
can be of any value is that of its origin and date.
did it come to be and when? That is the question to
which I am addressing myself in this paper, and shall
only touch on the subject matter in so far as it bears
on this question.
How
Let me say I do not for a moment claim to have
solved the problem; I might almost say I can do no
more than state it. But what I have tried to do is to
gather up the salient points furnished us by Chinese
editors and to offer some reflections of my own which
a study of the work has suggested.
The first of those irritating questions which I just
mentioned is "What is the I Wei?" The work which
goes by that name appears to consist of a number of

writings more or less independent of each other, or
more or less related to each other, but what really con-
stitutes the I Wei itself it is difficult to say. There is,
as I say, an irritating vagueness about the term, and yet
though it is difficult to attach it to any one concrete work,
it nevertheless stands for something.
The term itself, of course, suggests some relation to
the term Ching, a relation corresponding to the relation
between the warp and the woof which are the respective
primary meanings of the two words. We are told that there
were several Wei corresponding to all the Ching. There
were the Shu Wei, the Shih Wei, the Ch'un Ch'iu Wei,
the Li Wei as well as the I Wei, and all were largely
composed of what purported to be sayings of Confucius.
The work which we have now is in the Ssŭ K'u and is
substantially the same as the work which was included
in the Yung Ho Ta Tien. In the Index of the Ssu K'u
the editors have treated fairly comprehensively the
problem of criticism, and they tell us that the Wei were
supposed to supplement in some way the Ching, that the
I Wei, in particular, had its origin in the I Ching, but
in its application of the I conceptions passes on to other
subjects not treated of in the I Ching. These other
subjects, even a cursory glance of the work will tell us,
are associated with fortune telling, and the theory of
numbers which was so exhaustively developed by Shao
Yung in the Sung period. So then the I Wei is allied to,
and yet to be differentiated from, the I Ching.
Similarly it is allied to, but is to be differentiated from,
the Ch'an Shu. The expression Ch'an (Fortune Telling)
Wei occurs in several writers leading us to suppose that
they are of the same class, if not synonymous. But the
Ch'ien Lung editors adjure us to avoid that mistake.
The Wei are the Wei, and the Ch'an are the Ch'an. The
distinction is that the latter deal wholly with fortune
telling whereas the I Wei, though including some of the
Ch'an ideas, also deals with Philosophical, or rather
Cosmological conceptions, as such.
The fact appears to be that the I Wei occupied a middle
position between the I Ching and the Ch'an Shu, or
rather was a composite of the two.
It is interesting to note that the I Wei, if such a
work existed before the beginning of the Ch'in dynasty,
while it was spared the general massacre of the innocents,
owing to the Taoist leanings of the First Emperor, was
itself destined for the stake in a later period.

The Ch'ien Lung Editors in their critical research

be said perhaps, and more is said by the Ch'ien Lung

What is more valuable from our point of view is the
Internal Evidence. Here we must to some extent part
company with the Ch'ien Lung Editors and be more on
our own. The question before us is: Does the subject
matter stamp the work, or the several sections of it, as
belonging to the period to which the external evidence
points?
In answering this question I wish first to draw
attention to certain conceptions characteristic of the
work as indicating the period to which it belongs, and
second to point out certain passages which, either verbally
or in their subject matter, are common to this and other

To go back some distance in the Chou period, the
philosophers who became known later as Taoist Philoso-
phers with Chuang Tzu as the most famous, were the
authors of, or gave currency to, certain mystic legendary
and even fantastic conceptions.
Of course there were not at that time two distinct
schools-Confucian and Taoist. It was much later that
this distinction became clearly marked. They were
simply philosophical teachers with different philosophies,
such as we have had in the West, and they taught their
several systems as individual teachers rather than as
representatives of schools. But the process of what
De Groot calls bifurcation was in progress, and one
element in that process was the emergence of what I have
just referred to, viz:-
Credence for the legendary, the fantastic, the
mystical and magic.
By the end of the Ch'in dynasty these ideas had
become so distinctive that the term Huang Lao, either
then or later was applied to them.
The term Ch'an, referring more specifically to
fortune-telling also came into use at the end of Chou, or
the beginning of Ch'in, together with the growth of a
class of fortune-tellers called.
This stream of thought and practice was notably in
the ascendant under the Ch'ins. The burning of books
was accompanied by the ostracism of orthodox Confucian-
ism, whereas Taoist books and the Yi Ching related to
them in substance, were spared.
With the coming of the Hans, however, a reverse
process set in. Respect for Confucian books was revived,
and then followed a commingling of the two streams.
The Confucianists borrowed Taoist ideas, perhaps to
make their teachings more popular, and the Taoists
borrowed from Confucianists, possibly to give greater
credence to their teachings, even to the extent of falsely
attributing sayings to the Sage which be never uttered.
This commingling is just what we have in the I Wei.
The collection of writings collated under that title are
largely made up of what purport to be sayings of
Confucius, and, on the other hand, their teaching is
largely Taoist in colour and tendency; and the suggestion
I wish to make is that it was a product of the period to
which the Ch'ien Lung editors assert it belongs.
