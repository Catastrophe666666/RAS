DID MANICHAEISM INFLUENCE
LAMAISM?
By J. HUSTON EDGAR
The following article does not attempt to prove any-
thing but Social Contact. I have tried to make this clear
wherever an opportunity presents itself. It would have
remained probably for ever among a mass of other
material of doubtful value, but that a letter from Dr. F. C.
Burkitt, author of The Religion of the Manichees-the
Donnellan Lectures for 1924-gave me hope that the waif
might be worth some attention. The quotations from the
Fihrist and Al Biruni are from the above volume.
I. The Disciples of Marcion and Bardaisan. What
is Manichæism? Cumont speaks of it as the "last form
of Idolatry received by the Latin world"; and "the heir to
the cardinal doctrines of Mithraism". Harnack calls it
a "union of mythology and materialistic dualism with a
simple worship and a strict morality". According to
Burkitt it combined practical pessimism with ultimate
optimism; and he traces the Christian element to the
heresies of Marcion and Bardaisan. Fragments from
Eastern Turkestan, also, suggest that Manichæism is a
combination of heretical Christianity and Persian Magian-
ism with Buddhist accretions later. It is impossible now
to give a detailed account of Mani, its martyred founder,
or the fantastic philosophy of the system and its wide
acceptance and final extinction. But about the 8th
century A.D. it was common enough in China to be banned
as a perverse doctrine with a Buddhist veneer. In the
thirteenth century, also, Rubrik describes the religion of
Turkestan as a mixture of Buddhism, Nestorian
Christianity, and Manichæism.
II. Life in the Deserts and Wastes of the Earth. It
there any proof that Manichæism, pure or adultered, has
had an opportunity to mould the religious ideas of the
Tibetans? An affirmative answer to this question is im-
portant, because it would be easy to explain all suspected
affinities without reference to Mani or his disciples.

But social contact with Manichæists is beyond doubt. In
the 8th century, Ynigur tribes, the allies of the Tibetans,
accepted Mani's doctrines and for centuries we have proof
in manuscripts and historical records of its vigorous
growth in Eastern Turkestan. Now at different times
during the formative period of Lamaism this region was
not only in close contact with Tibet but was occasionally
occupied by her armies. Indeed, according to the Fihrist,
a Moslem work, about A.D. 1000 a Manichee "King of
China"-probably a chief in the Lob Nor region-was in
a position to obtain better treatment for his co-religionists
in Samarcand by threatening reprisals if the occasion
should demand it. About the same time the Arab, Al
Biruni, claims much more than social contact. We are
told "most of the Eastern Turks, of the people of China
and Tibet, and some of the Hindus adhere to Mani's
law and doctrine." We suspect that the murderous raids
of Genghis Khan gave the death blow to Eastern
Manichæism, but did not Tibet, so near at hand, and the
great asylum of persecuted peoples, offer many of them
a refuge?
III. Buddhas of Light still illumine the Dark. The
above scraps of information are important enough to allow
us to assume that Manichæism definitely affected Lamaism
at one of its most susceptible periods. Naturally, then,
we would look for traces of it in their theology and social
organization. We shall examine some aspects of
Lamaism, therefore, with the hope that we may streng-
then the testimony of history.
The central doctrine of Mani was the conflict between
the light and the dark. The light was good and the dark
evil. This point, as far as I know, is only suggested in
Lamaism when dealing with their Gods and Buddhas. In
some Tibetan sects we find a super-God, or Adi Buddha.
It is said of him, "The Light Rays proceeding from the ten
points of the Adi Buddha purify the whole animate crea-
tion." Again, he is in Tibetan the supreme overlord of
all the Celestial Buddhas who are so often described, as
will be shown, in terms of Light. Now the God of Mani
is said to occupy five dwellings which are correctly
described by the Hebrew word Shekinah. Again in the
system of Mani provision was made at opportune times
for great teachers to appear whose work it was to instruct
men and lead them into Truth. Mani was the last of the
series and claimed to be the Paraclete promised by Jesus.
In Eastern Turkestan he eventually became sublimated

Ch'en Lamas have a peculiar place in the Lamaist theology,
which may be partly explained by a former contact with
Manichæism. In any case, as there was "no knowledge of
the Transmigrations in China before 1430" we may at
least doubt an immediate Buddhist origin. Their inner
meaning is even more un-Buddhistic. The Dalai, for
instance, is the Saviour and Paraclete of the Tibetans, a
God, a Pope, and a Secular Ruler. The Chinese "Treatise"
on Tibet asserts that he is a "Light Emanation" from
"Kwan Yin" or the Fourth Celestial Buddha, who is also
known as the "God of Infinite Light." The Pan-Ch'en, or
Great Teacher, is an Emanation of Light from the same
source, and, in some ways occupies a more exalted position
than the Dalai. The five Celestial Buddhas also may have
been adapted from the "Five God" idea of the Manichees.
They are really the five elements, but their relation to the
Light which is such an important element in the Manichee
theology must be emphasised. For instance, the first
Celestial Buddha is "The One Completely Filled with
Light"; and "Infinite Light" is the name of the fourth.
Then we have "The Guardian of Light," "Dispersing
Light," "Light Rays," "Fiery Crest," "Radiant Vision,'
"Flashing Light," and "Self Luminous" applied to
"Emanations," "Buddhas," and "Gods." Does this
imply Manichæism or even more ancient Magianism?
IV. Why are the Lamas supreme in Tibet? The
monastic system in Tibet is not a coincidence. The high
altitudes make it economically desirable and Buddhism
might have given it authority. But it may be doubted if
Buddhism would have forced it into its present form or
proportions. Could Manichæism? To the disciples of
Mani society consisted of Monks and Laymen or the Elect
and the Hearers. The former were the Cream of the land
and the Saviours of men. As the ethics of Mani demanded
abstinence from every thing connected with the Dark, the
life of his disciples was severely ascetic. Indeed the Elect,
who were to save men by freeing them from the Dark,
abstained from wine, flesh, women, and property. They
lived a wandering existence and were absolutely forbidden
to produce or take life. The system was organized on
hierarchic lines. Beginning with auditors, we follow with
the electus, presbyter, episcopus to the Magister or Pope
who was the successor of Mani. The auditors, or hearers,
formed the bulk of the community. They were allowed to
marry, hold property, and live very much like ordinary

folk. But the real justification for their existence was the
fact that they ministered to the Light-freeing Elect. Now
in many ways this social organization reminds one of
Lamaism to-day. The ordinary Tibetans are "those in the
world," but the lamas are the elect, a special class, the
guides, and in a sense the saviours of the people. They
too, are a sacred class with a definite purpose in view; and
in many ways are accorded similar favours, while limited
by like prohibitions, as the Elect Manichees. Then their
hierarchic organization and steps up the ladder of office
are, mutatis mutandis, very similar.
V. Are they Gods of the Ynigurs or Semites? The
litholatrous survivals, also may be a Manichee influence or
older than Abram. Shoues as gods or talismans, are
found everywhere on the Sino-Tibetan frontier among
what may be the more distinctly Turkish tribes. But why
associate them with Mani and his system? Sacred shoues
were common enough in the Near East-Europe and Asia.
Cumont, however, mentions "in the Syrian mysteries it
was taught that the shoues ('Bethels) were animated,
and the same doctrine perpetuated itself in Manichæism."
With the above mentioned trend of Manichæism in view,
and a suspicion of a relationship to the first Ynigur
Manichees, the Litholatry might have a parentage other
than the pre-historic immigrants from the Euphrates, or
historic ones from India.
VI. A coincidence or influence-which? In a
Chinese History of Tibet there is an account of a feast in
Lhasa which may be in no way related to Manichæism,
but is worthy of mention even as a coincidence. The
Manichees commemorated the death of their founder in
March by the Feast of Bema or the Teacher's Chair. The
faithful prostrated themselves before an adorned but
empty chair which rested on a podium five steps up. Now
in Lhasa-in the past, at least-about the end of March
"the Dalai ascends a mountain, and precious vessels, gems
and ornaments are spread out in the Imperial Temple.
The exhibition is called the 'Treasures of Light.' The
next day an image of Buddha, decorated with coloured
silks and embroideries is suspended from the fifth floor.
A lama simulating spirit control circumambulates
the temple three times worshipping the Buddha with
dancing and singing. This goes on for a month." The
date, March and April, the five fold elevation, the adorned
representative, the Treasures of Light, and the prostra-

VII. The Prayers of Tibet may yet be explained.
The last suggested Manichee influence will not find much
favour in some quarters. Is Om mani pad me hum an
invocation to the Persian Saint? Perhaps not. But the
late origin of the prayer, the widespread and fanatical
use of the formula, and the unsatisfactory explanations
of its origin have puzzled us for years. No one doubts
what has been said of its meaning in Sanscrit now; but is
the Sanscrit, after all, an afterthought? Would its popu-
larity as a talisman not be better explained by its having
been at one time an invocation to Mani? The Manichee
formula "Monastra Hirza," "Oh! cleanse me from all
spots," may have suggested the need, form, and object of
such a phrase. This may strike some as far fetched, but is
it more so than some of the explanations which make sense
out of what is to most Tibetans a meaningless phrase?
But there is another point: The Bons or Black Lamas
reject Om mani pad me hum and use in its place Om
Madre mu ye sale du. The meaning of the phrase so
far is unknown, but if by any chance, Madre is another
form of Mithras it would confirm units that the Bons
are the survival of what was at one time a Persian cult
which threatened to conquer the world. But that would
make the antagonism of the Black and Yellow Lamas one
between Mithraism and Manichæism. But Madre may
mean anything.
