THE GODS OF THE CHINESE
L. NEWTON HAYES, M.A.
Dr. George Rawlinson for more than twenty years
Professor of History in Oxford University declares that
"Religion is the most important element in the thought
of a nation; and it is by studying their religions that we
obtain the best clue to the inner life and true character
of the various peoples who have played an important part
in the drama of human affairs.
In the above quotation Dr. Rawlinson has in mind
the religions of the Egyptian, Assyrian, Babylonian,
Greek and Roman peoples, but what he says is fully
applicable to the religion of the Chinese. There is pro-
bably no more affective way to arrive at a sympathetic
understanding of this interesting people than through a
knowledge of their religious beliefs and practices.
Many volumes have been devoted to the philosophy
of Chinese religions, a number more have been written
to describe their superstitious beliefs and practices, but
up to the present time very little study has been made
of the divinities of the Chinese pantheon and the worship
that is accorded them. The gods of the Greeks and
Romans are well known to students of Ancient History.
The divinities of Egypt, Babylonia, Assyria and India
have been studied with great care; but thus far practi-
cally no attempt seems to have been made by Chinese or
foreigners to classify the gods of China or to under-
stand their individual characteristics, powers and fields
of influence.
There are many who would minimize the value of
studying about the gods of a disintegrating pantheon
and a dying religion or group of religions.
As one
travels throughout China, he discovers that temples and
pagodas everywhere are being allowed to fall into decay;
in many parts of the country, idols have been removed
from their shrines and have been pulverized or buried
or thrown into rivers and the worship of images, in and
near port cities, is found to be on the decline, but it is
probable that many years will come and go before the
Chinese as a nation will give up their belief in Yü
Huang-the "Pearly Emperor"; Ts'ai Shen-the "God
of Wealth"; Kuan Ti-the "God of War"; Kuan Yin-

the "Goddess of Mercy," and perhaps fifty other deities

of centuries. If the Chinese continue to worship these
gods, then the problems caused by their belief in these
deities will remain as problems for many years to come.
We believe that a knowledge of the objects of their
worship will help one to understand the background of
the life of the world's most ancient race. But if the
securing of this understanding is not a sufficient incen-
tive to inspire one's study, then he may seek information
about their gods in order that he may have the satisfac-
tion of recognizing the features and knowing some facts
about their representations that may commonly be seen in
pagodas and temples and shrines in nearly every city
throughout the length and breadth of the land of
"Eighteen Provinces."
Whenever any one visits a city or village, travels to
the hills or goes to the seashore, he can see in temples,
in pagodas, in shrines and in homes idols that he has
probably often seen before. Many have a familiar ap-
pearance and he wishes he might identify and recognize
some of them, but he has never taken the trouble to learn
their distinguishing marks or characteristics. He has
heard that the number of Chinese idols is infinite and has
therefore thought it a hopeless task to learn to recognize
even a few in such a vast array. One who holds this
view and who at the same time would like to know more
of the gods and their representations would be interested
to know that idols may readily be classified and the more
common ones identified in a way that he can recognize
them, with few exceptions, whenever they are seen.
Many centuries ago, the greatest man of his time
made a visit to the capital of Attica, Athens the glory
of Greece. As Paul walked along the streets of that
metropolis and saw on every hand countless evidences
of worship, he formulated an opinion which he later
expressed in his Areopagus address in these words, "I
perceive, O Athenians, that ye are a very religious
people."
A foreigner visiting a truly Chinese city for the first
time makes a similar observation about the people of this
country. On every hand, he sees temples, pagodas and
shrines. Candles and incense burn not only in places of
worship but in the homes of rich and poor. Paper money
and other accessories of religious ceremonial may be
found for sale on almost every street. The sounds

are legion and the number of their representations beyond
the bounds of comprehension.
Wilkinson, in his work entitled "Ancient Egyptians,"

India number 3,339. And Bishop Thirwell, author of
"A History of Greece" is our authority for the statement
that the gods of various orders in that country were
spoken of as 30,000.

Ten years' study of the gods of the Chinese leads
us to feel that it is safe to say that there are probably
even more recognized deities in the Chinese pantheon
than in those of any other race, and more images in this
country than are found in the temples and homes of any
other land. Indeed, the number of gods is so great that
de Groot, not only characterizes it as "infinite," but
almost in desperation resorts to hyperbole, declaring
that in the creation of new gods for their "unlimited
polytheism," the Chinese are "bounded by nothing but
the circumstance that even the human art of inventing
gods has its limits."
The spirit world of the Chinese is indeed peopled to
capacity with gods and goddesses, genii and fairies.
There are gods good and bad, powerful and weak, hand-
some and ugly, kindly and cruel. There is a god to
control nearly every phase and department of life. There
are deities of the elements, the sun, the moon, the stars
and the constellations; rulers, too, of the four directions.
There are gods of mountains, rivers seas and cities;
gods of the living and gods of the spirit world. There
are gods of war, literature, wealth, mercy, medicine and
disease; gods of the home, gods of the farm and even
gods of individual streets and bridges. There are gods,
too, for every important trade.
Cows, horses, snakes and many other animals and
reptiles, and even some insects which prey upon the
human body have worship accorded them by human
beings. Inside the northeast gate of Soochow is a temple
to the God of Snakes and outside the west wall of the
same city, there was a few years ago a place of worship
dedicated to the human parasite whose common name is
unmentionable, so we refer to it by its Latin designation
-cimex lectularius.
Some idols are worshipped only in temples and
shrines, others are found exclusively in homes, and there
are at least two which, we are told, are never worshipped
at all.
Two of the first questions, which come to our minds
when we consider the vast number of gods of any
polytheistic people are; What is the the origin of their
polytheism? And, how is it possible for men to have
such a multitude of different divinities? In his volume
"Religions of the Ancient World" Dr. George Rawlinson,
previously quoted, gives us his theories of the origin of
polytheistic religion. He says, "The religions of the

ancient world, if we except Judaism, seem to have been,
all of them, more or less polytheistic; but the polytheism
grew up in different ways, was carried out to very
different lengths, and proceeded upon considerably vary-
ing principles. In some places natural objects and
operations appear to have presented themselves to the
unsophisticated mind of man as mysterious, wonderful,
divine; and light, fire, the air, the sun, the moon, the
dawn, the cloud, the stream, the storm, the lightning,
drew his attention separately and distinctly, each having
qualities at which he marvelled, each, as he thought,
instinct with life and each, therefore, regarded as a Power,
a Being the natural and proper object of worship and
reverence. Elsewhere, men seem to have begun with a
dim and faint appreciation of a single mysterious power
in the world without them, and to have gradually divided
this power up into its various manifestations, which by
degrees became separate and distinct beings. The process
in this case might stop short after a few steps had been
taken, or it might be carried on almost interminably,
until a pantheon had been formed in which the mind lost
itself."
"Where the polytheism grew up out of an analysis,
the principle of the division might be either physical or
metaphysical; a separation of nature into its parts, or
an analysis of the Being presiding over nature into his
various powers and attributes. Or these two processes
might be combined and intermixed, the patheon being
thus still further enlarged at the expense of some
confusion of thought and complexity of arrangement.
Again, occasionally, there was a further enlargement
and complication, in consequence of the desire to embrace
in one system analyses which were really distinct, or to
comprise in a single national religion local diversities of
arrangement or nomenclature, or even to admit into a
system based on one principle elements which belonged
properly to systems based upon others. The whole result
in such a case was one of extensive complexity, and even
contradition; a tangle was produced which it was scarcely
possible to unravel. The system, however, gained in
richness and variety what it lost in logical sequence and
intelligibility, and continued to have firm hold on the
minds of many when religions of greater internal con-
sistency had lost their power."

In East Asia ancestors, so de Groot thinks, were
probably the first gods, before mental development and
culture had caused that part of the world to invent other
deities. In his Prolegomena to the Shih-King. (the
"Book of History"), Dr. James Legge says that "while
the ancient Chinese believed in God, they believed in
other spirits under Him, some presiding over hills and
rivers and others dwelling in the heavenly bodies. In
fact, there was no object to which a tutelary spirit might
not at times be ascribed, and no place where the ap-
proaches of spiritual beings might not be expected and
ought not to be provided for by the careful keeping of
the heart and the ordering of the conduct.
We find sacrifices offered to the spirits of the ground and
the four quarters of the sky, to the Father of Husbandry,
the Father of War and the Spirit of the Path."
From these statements and from our own study of
original sources, we are led to conclude that China's
polytheism came from a primitive worship of the bodies
and forces of nature, and gradually developed in com-
plexity until it passed from a veneration of merely visible
objects of nature to a stage in which it included, with
these, the gods that were believed to supply all the needs
to which the human heart and mind are subject.
In making a study of the Chinese pantheon, one
searches in vain for a supreme creator and ruler of gods
and men. Egypt had her Kneph and Phthah, Assyria,
the god Asshur, and Babylonia, the Great Bel, who, we
are told, made the earth and the heaven and formed men
by a mixture of his own blood with earth. India has her
Brahma, Greece her Zeus and Rome her Jupiter, but China
has no deity that occupies exactly the same place in her
catalogue of gods.
Two complicated theories of the creation of the
universe are accepted by large divisions of the Chinese
people. According to one, Panku, a mythical being,
chiseled out the universe with his herculean mallet and
iron. From a vast primordial mass, he formed the earth,
the sun, the moon and the stars. On the completion of
his task, he laid down to die, whereupon the various parts
of his body underwent transformation and became the
mountains, seas and rivers and even the animate beings
of the earth.

The second and more philosophical theory is that
there was in the beginning a great force or power known
as the Wu Chi or great Infinite which divided itself and
became the Yin and the Yang elements. The Yang breath
or spirit, which embodies light, fire, warmth, productivity,
and life, separating itself from the Yin element, arose
and, as a result, there were formed heaven, the sun, the
stars, the thunder, fire, winds and mountains. The Yin
element or spirit embodying darkness, water, cold and
death, descended, and, as a result, there appeared the
earth, the moon, water, snow, clouds, rain, rivers, seas
and finally Hades. Each one of these bodies and elements
came in time to be deified.
The control of these natural powers and the authority
over the gods of these nature-forces, as well as over the
deities of lesser orders, in other words, supreme power, is
divided between three claimants for the honor. Some
give it to "T'ien," some to "Shang Ti" and some to "Yü
Huang", (the Pearly Emperor).
The name "Tien" is used when speaking of the
highest deity, not only as a being or personality, but also
somewhat loosely, as a place and also as a principle. The
name "Shang Ti" is usually reserved by non-Christians
to denote the spirits of departed emperors and by others,
chiefly scholars, as the ruler of the universe; and "Yü
Huang" (the pearly Emperor), by reason of his human
origin, would seem to many to be unqualified to take the
highest seat in the pantheon.
Since "T'ien" is regarded by a large proportion of
Chinese as the name of a place or a principle and not
exclusively as the name of a person, since "Shang Ti"
is the name sometimes given to deceased emperors and
was worshipped by the living emperors, and not by the
people, we are inclined to regard "Yü Huang" as the
supreme ruler of the majority of Chinese deities. When
the kitchen, or household, gods are burned one week
before the end of the Chinese year, we are told, they
go up to heaven where they report to "Yü Huang" the
affairs of the homes which have entertained them.
Among Chinese deities, we may therefore say that the
"Pearly Emperor" occupies the place that most nearly
corresponds to that of Zeus in the Olympian Council and
of Jupiter in the Pantheon of Rome.

It is impossible however to say who gave the gods
their ranks and positions in the pantheon of China.
A popular fable says that in the Twelfth Century A.D.
Chiang Tzu Ya, the aged councillor of Wu Wang, of the
Chou dynasty secured from three genii the sacred list of
gods now well-known as the "Feng Shen Pang." It was

Upright men of history, from time to time, have been
exalted by the people, and, by the decree of the emperor,
have been raised to the rank of god. Within the last
fifteen years, Confucius was elevated to the position of a
deity and Yuan Shih Kai, in his brief enjoyment of imperial

god. The latter was created a deity to preside over war,
to rule over the destiny of Chinese military affairs and to
be co-equal in rank with Kuan Kung, already an occupant

newly created deity has just been completed on the north
shore of Hangchow's beautiful lake. This is in a portion

man who is one of the most honored patriots in Chinese

Johnson, in his "Buddhist China," upholds this view

On the other hand, the vast majority of Chinese

De Groot's position probably represents more justly
than does Johnson's the common conception of the people
and it is logically supported by the method employed to
endow an idol with the all-essential spirit. It is not
everyone who can make an idol. These images are
constructed by a special guild of craftsmen who have
the power, assumed or delegated, to construct and to
give life to the works of their hands.
The material of which an image is made is of no
consequence. Any medium in the wide range from paper
and clay to jade and gold may be employed. The only
stipulation rigorously heeded, when clay is used, is that
the earth must be secured from beneath the surface of the
ground. The sun must not have had a chance to shine
upon it. It is very apparent that the science of geology
is unknown to the workers of this craft, else they would

be aware that the sun in some previous age must have

The size of an image or its surface covering have
little bearing on its effectiveness. The one and only thing
that counts is whether or not the spirit of the god has
been introduced into its body.
The induction ceremony is most interesting and
consists, wherever properly performed, of three parts;
the dotting of the pupils of the eyes, the introduction of
the heart, lungs and other vital organs, and the placing
in the body of some living insect, bird or reptile.
If one visits the shop of an idol maker, he can usual-
ly see many images in various stages of construction.
There will be some on the shelves, to all appearances
fully completed. The craftsman, however, will point out
two deficiencies which the visitor might never notice.
A close scrutiny of the eyes would reveal the absence of
the pupils, and if the image were reversed, he would see
a small trap-door in the middle of the back below the
shoulders still unsealed.
After all external decorations have been completed,
the idol is ready for sale but not to be worshipped. The
purchaser takes the idol to his home, it is enshrined in
a temple, and on the lucky day the idol maker is called.
A feast is made. Candles and incense are burned,
prayers are chanted and then the maker places the silver
vital organs inside the body. He then takes some living
creature and puts it through the little door which he
immediately seals up before the life can escape.
Idol-makers evidently believe that idols should have
organs similar to most of those which are found in the
human body. These are generally made of silver and
are strung together in a series, suspended from a hook.
Lungs, heart, liver and even windpipe and oesophagus
are included among the other essentials. Sometimes, in
smaller images precious stones are employed instead of
silver representation. Whenever temples are robbed or
idols sold for profit, the first effort of the plunderer or
purchaser is said to be to get at the silver or precious
stones. I have seen in Chengtu scores of brass idols
which arrived in Szechuan from Tibet, each of which had
been pried open to get out the jewels, that were thought
to be enclosed in the bodies when they were made in
Lhassa.

Immediately after the fall of Nanking in the first
days of the Revolution, soldiers of the Republic spared
few idols in the lesser temples in their search for silver
hearts. A few days after the city fell a Chinese gentleman
told me that he watched a group of soldiers destroy three
clay images, each ten feet high. When these men came
to the hearts and livers, much to their disgust, they
discovered that the idol maker had cheated the purchaser
and had inserted silver organs only one half an inch in
diameter. The gentleman in question overheard one of
the plunderers remark to his companions, "No wonder we
smashed those gods so easily. Images with such small
livers were of course unable to protect themselves from
our greed."
In the choice of creatures for enclosing in new
images, the idol maker has a wide field from which to
select. We are told that flies, mosquitoes, snakes, centi-
pedes and even small birds are very frequently employed.
Occasionally, articles like threads of the five colors, rice,
tea and beans are added for good measure.
Although an idol may have the silver organs in place
and the living insects, reptile or bird sealed up within
its body, yet the image is still incomplete. The final
ceremonial act yet remains to be performed. The idol
maker sets the image in place. He takes a brush and dips
it in ink and then, at a moment which he has previously
determined is most propitious, he motions to the guests
who have assembled for the ceremony to maintain ab-
solute silence. While they hold their breath, he repeats
an incantation and touches each eye with his brush.
This act is called "adding or opening the light." Imme-
diately following its completion, the god is declared to
be filled with power and is ready to be worshipped.
There is sometimes a variation from the above practice.
The idol's eyes are dotted in the store, but immediately
afterwards are pasted over with small squares of red
paper. It is then carried to the temple or home. In
this case, the "light opening" ceremony consists of re-
moving the papers before the assembled crowd.

The worship of idols is always accompanied by the
use of incense and generally by the use of candles. When
incense only is used, the worshipper need not kneel before
the shrine. When candles are employed, the person kneels
and strikes his head three or nine times according to the
degree of his earnestness and his desire to have some
particular prayer answered. Incense is usually made of
sandal or cedarwood, and is burned in little bundles of
about twenty-five sticks wrapped in thin paper. When
less than one package is offered, the usual number of
sticks that are lighted is three. These are always placed
in an incense urn directly in front of the idol.
Candles, we are informed, are always made of vege-
table fat. Animal fat candles would give offence to the
idol. Never less than two candles are lighted and these
are always offered in pairs.
Candles are never lighted without the use of incense,
though incense is often burned before some inferior idol
without the accompaniment of candles. Worshippers are
supposed to wash their hands before lighting candles
or burning incense. This rule is better followed in the
breech than in the observance. Candles and incense are
sometimes seen burning at the foot of a wall, across the
street and opposite to some gateway. The reason for
this strange practice may be one of two possibilities;
either a servant such as the gate-keeper, who is not
permitted to worship in his master's home, is making an
offering to his ancestors, or the family is "sending away"
evil spirits who have caused sickness in the household.
In many homes, there is a daily worship of the an-
cestors, the God of Wealth and the Kitchen God. Before
the establishment of the republic it is probable that there
was scarcely a non-Christian home in all the country that
did not perform this rite of worship on the first and
fifteenth day of worship on the first and fifteenth day of
every lunar month. The majority of stores and shops
made a regular practice of worshipping the God of Wealth
every fifteen days.

In order to attract the attention of the gods to the
petition offered by the worshipper, many expedients are
resorted to; percussion instruments of various kinds are
employed. The most frequently used are bells, gongs and
drums made of iron, brass and even wood. No one who
has ever heard the sounds of the wooden "fish heads"
in the midnight ceremony of some Buddhist monastery
will ever forget the boom of those quaint instruments.
In some temples, it is not unusual to hear firecrackers
exploded. It is explained that this practice is observed
partly to waken the gods to receive the worship and partly
to frighten away evil spirits that hover about.
When one is particularly anxious to have a petition
granted, an offering or sacrifice of meat and other savory
dishes is often made. This offering is placed on an altar
before the idol. The idol supposedly smells the aroma, is
pleased, and is thereby induced to grant the petition.
The worshipper may leave the bowls of meat at the altar
for the officiating priests, but more frequently he returns
with these to his home where he can himself enjoy the
food of the gods.
People rarely worship at a temple without having
in mind some definite petition which they wish granted.
Many years of observation and study lead us to believe
that worshippers never go to a temple with the purpose
in mind of communion with the deity, or with the desire
to get power for a better and higher life. They go
invariably with the object of getting some physical need
supplied.
On the altar before the image and beside the incense
burner usually stands a bamboo receptacle full of sticks
of the same material. Each of these strips of wood is
numbered. A tube will commonly contain thirty-six,
seventy-two, or one hundred. The numbers on these
strips correspond to numbers on narrow sheets of printed
paper in the keeping of a priest or temple attendant.
After a worshipper has lighted his candles and
incense, the usual procedure is for him to take the tube
from the altar. He first waves this receptacle three or
four times over the burning incense and then retires to
the mat in front of the altar. After performing the
usual number of obeisances he begins slowly to revolve
the bamboo tube. The sticks within push each other
from side to side until one is forced out and drops to
the floor. Let us suppose the worshipper is concerned
over a lawsuit. He picks up the stick, looks at the number

Some idols are worshipped at stated occasions, others
as need requires. The fire god is worshipped during or
after a conflagration, when the "red crow" is supposed
to be flying about ready to ignite unburned buildings.
The Dragon King is worshipped when rain is needed, the
God of War, just before a battle. Most gates of large
cities have enclosures between the inner and outer gates.
Within these spaces, one may generally find a small
temple to the God of War. Before generals would leave
a city to engage in battle, it was a practice of old to halt
at the gate through which they led their armies and pray
for success at the temple of the Chinese Mars.
Each god has a birthday and is specially worshipped
on that occasion. Shihkiamuni (or Buddha) and Kuan
Yin are particularly fortunate in this respect, for they
are each conceded three birthdays. Most of the principal

In many temples a careful observer will notice, placed

In Manchu days, processional journeys were periodic,
in the cases of some gods; with others, they depended
upon circumstances. The Ch'eng Huang Lao Yeh, or God
of the City, in most instances was scheduled for three
such journeys each year. On such occasions not only
were principal thoroughfares visited, but frequently the
processions went out through the city gates into the
suburbs.
A second god who was assured of a chance to leave
his temple at stated intervals was Tung Yueh, the God
of the Eastern Mountain, T'ai Shan. He is recognized
as the chief deity of the nether world. The Tung Yueh
Ta Ti was carried out each year on the anniversary of
his birth, the 28th of the second month. He was then
supposed to visit each one of the different city gates of
the city over which he presided.
Other gods sometimes carried in processions were
Yu Huang, (known as the "Pearly Emperor") Kuan Ti,
(the God of War); Huo Shen, (the Fire God) and Lung
Wang, (the Dragon King). Not infrequently, when fires
had ravaged a city, the Fire God was taken out to inspect
the damage that had been done. He was thus given a
chance to repent for allowing the disaster. When long-
needed rain was withheld by the supposed indifference
of the Dragon King, that god was borne from his temple
to the country and given a chance to see for himself how
sadly the parched vegetation craved mercy at his hands.
Idol processions were always filled with interest to city
crowds. It was once the custom for each family to light
two candles and to burn three sticks of incense before
the home as the god passed by.

The maintenance of temples aside from those sup-
ported by the state, is usually provided by guilds or
societies. Each principal temple god, with only a few
exceptions (Yu Huang, Yen Loh and Ti Tsang among
them) has a guild. The members are like directors of
a club. They contribute to its support and direct the
affairs of the temple. Subscriptions for repairs of
buildings or renovation of the idols are raised in its
name and feasts and theatricals on the temple stage are
given under its auspices.

In learning to distinguish idols, there are six factors
that help one to determine the god's identity. These are
its head-dress, face, clothing, hands, posture and atten-
dants. A few illustrations will make this clear.
Milei, the little fat god, and most other Buddhist
idols are never seen with any head-dress, others like
Wei T'o and Yu Huang invariably wear helmets or crowns
and it is not unusual to find that the only distinguishing
mark on the person of the God of Happiness is the hat
with two extended wings.
The color of the face in the case of the God of Fire
and the God of Disease is always red. Most images of
Kuan Ti, the Chinese Mars, possess a countenance of a
similar color. Certain Buddhist idols are identified by
spots or discs on their foreheads, while possibly a dozen
important gods have three eyes. We may recall that
Bhikshatanamurti, one of the incarnations of Siva, the
Hindu deity, also has three eyes. The little god who
produces thunder, has not only his mouth but also his
feet made to resemble those of a rooster.
The clothing in which we find gods dressed greatly
aids in their identification. Coats of mail, imperial robes
and priestly costumes are very helpful in giving clues to
some gods otherwise difficult to distinguish. One of the
commonly seen incarnations of the Goddess of Mercy
(known as the Pai Yi Kuan Yin) appears only in a
dress of spotless white. We can never mistake Mi Lei,
the "Laughing Buddha" in his priest's robe;-the little
fat gentleman who sits in negligé costume at the entrance
of many Buddhist temples.
The positon, number and contents of the hands are
often worthy of attention. One is fascinated by the mar-

hands. But Tou Mu, like Siva of India, possesses six
in three pairs. The Ch'ien Shou Kuan Yin has a thousand
hands. Each of these has an eye in its palm. We recall
one of the stories told to explain this strange deity. It is
connected with the illness of the evil Miao Chuang Wang.

The most common minor grouping of idols is in
sets of three, usually known as triads. Most ancient
religions seem to have had one or more such groups.
Egypt had her Osiris, Isis and Horus; Babylonia and
Assyria, their Anu, Bel and Hea; Greece had her Zeus,
Athena and Apollo; Rome her Jupiter, Juno and Minerva;
and India her Brahma, Vishnu and Siva.
China has many such groups, and infinite are the
temples which contain three great images side by side
behind the altar of the main hall. Shops and homes with
pictures or images of three gods in one group are very
common. Among the triads most frequently seen are the
following:-
(1) The San Ch'ing. These are "the three pure ones"
of Taoism. The senior member of this triumvirate is
known as T'ai Ch'ing. He is Lao Tzu, acclaimed by some
as the founder of that religion.
(2) The San Yuan. This group is also known as the
San Kuan. These are the primordial sovereigns. Their
names signify the three sources and the three officials.
The first of this group represents heaven, the second,
earth and the third, water.
(3) The San Hsing. The third triad, is named the
three stars. These are respectively the gods or stars of
Happiness, of Office and of Longevity. This is a very
common group and is frequently seen in homes and stores.

of agalmatolite, the substance which resembles soapstone.
The God of Long Life is the well known old man repre-

holding a peach in one hand and leaning with the other
on a long staff, often as tall as himself.
(4) The San Huang or "three emperors" are very
well known, but are not met with as frequently as the
preceding groups. These reprensent Fu Hsi, Shen Nung
and Hsuan Yuan.

There are many goddesses in the Chinese pantheon,
but comparatively few of them are ever represented by
images. The best known goddess, of course, is Kuan Yin,
the Goddess of Mercy. Like Vishnu, the Hindu deity,
Kuan Yin is said to have many avatars or incarnations.
Some tell us that this goddess has appeared on the earth
in thirty-three different forms, others say seventy-two
and still others claim that there is no limit to the possible
incarnations of the Goddess of Mercy. She has appeared
we are told, in various forms of beautiful women, she has
assumed the guise of a man and she has even taken the
body of a devil and entered Hades to save some person
from torment.
Less prominent goddesses are P'u Hsien, who carried
the Buddhist classics from India to sacred Mount Omei
in Szechuan, T'ai Yin, the Goddess of the Moon, Tien Mu

god may see his way in the dark, Tou Mu, the Goddess of
Ursa Major, the "Great Dipper" and finally Tien Ho
Shen Mu, the Queen of Heaven, who is the protector of
sailors and the patron deity of the people of Fukien
province. Many of the gods have wives but few of these
are ever worshipped and still fewer represented by images
in homes or temples.

(1) Gods of natural phenomena. (2) Gods of the dead,
or of the spirit world and (3) Gods of the living.
In the first group the gods of natural phenomena we
find Heaven, the Earth Mother and the gods of the sun
the moon, thunder, lightning, the elements, fire, water
and mountains.
