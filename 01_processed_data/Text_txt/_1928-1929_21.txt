GOLD IN CHINA
In Its Historical, Practical and Financial Aspects
By E. KANN
Since the remotest historical ages gold (aurum) has
been known and appreciated owing to its comparative
rarity, its shining splendour, its beautiful and unchanging
colour and its capability of resisting the influence of
almost all chemical reagents. Gold is found either as
pure metal embedded in rock, usually quartz (placer
gold), or in the sediments of rivers which have originated
in the course of centuries from the pulverised masses of
deteriorated rocks (alluvial gold). Heated up to 1,240
degrees (Celsius) gold turns into a bluish-green liquid.
Of all the metals gold possesses the greatest ductility; in
consequence of which the finest wires can be drawn from
gold (2,000 meters weighing 1 gramme) and the thinnest
leaf produced from the yellow metal (of even less than
0.0001 millimeters thickness).
The world's annual output of gold, measured by
present-day standards, approximates nineteen million fine
ounces, valued at nearly U.S.$ 400,000,000. Seventy-five
per cent. total is produced in Great Britain's colonies,
Transvaal supplying by far the biggest portion.
The production of gold within the extensive domain
of China is infinitesimal, both relatively and absolutely.
Possibly the country might not be rich in gold deposits.
But it must be recognised that the deposits actually
existing have remained almost undeveloped, due to a long
chain of reasons, of which the following form some of the
links: lack of communications, general insecurity and
inadequate protection against robbery, absence of modern
mining laws, ignorance in general of modern mining
methods and superstitious beliefs which often end in
aversion to the opening of mines. The following
short historical sketch about mining in China affords
many instances of obstruction, but practically none of
assistance, on the part of the ruling authorities.

HISTORICAL SKETCH ABOUT MINING IN CHINA:
From the earliest records available it appears that the
Chinese were settlers in the valley of the Yellow River,
where they occupied themselves with agriculture. There-
fore it is not to be wondered at that Chinese ancient
history has but little to record about metallurgy or mining,
arts which were most likely introduced from abroad.
The teachings of Confucius (born 551 B.C.) and his
disciples favoured the despising of riches. The treasures
of the earth were to remain hoarded there until they
were required by the emperor. The rank of the Chinese
farmer was an honourable one in olden times; lowest on
the social ladder came thieves, soldiers and miners.
Until comparatively recent times metals have not been
recognised as of utility to the State, except for currency
purposes. But even in this direction their employment
was restricted, judging by the fact that the circulation of
metallic money was repeatedly abolished. Consequently
the mining of metals was, on many occasions, interdicted,
the last prohibition having been issued under Emperor
Tao Kuang in 1850.
It may be said without limitation that all Chinese
emperors have consistently discouraged mining of the
metals, except to create currency for the purpose of
paying the troops or meeting war indemnities. Metallic
money was known in China in the time of T'ai Hao
(2852-2737 B.C.), being styled Chin (). In about

2000 B.C. there were three kinds of metallic money known

The first mention of Chinese mining refers to copper
at Shuo Shan in Honan Province, about 2600 B.C. During
the reign of Shun, a legendary ruler (2255-2205 B.C.)
the working of gold (metal ?) mines was supposed to
have been prohibited.
Due to the growing influence of Feng Shui, which
science began to make itself felt shortly after the begin-
ning of the Christian era, mining was often officially
forbidden. "Feng" means "wind," while "Shui" means
"water," the male and female elements in nature. The
science of Feng Shui is based on ancestor worship and a
scanty knowledge of arithmetic. Feng Shui, based on a
materialistic scheme of philosophy, teaches that:
(1) Heaven rules the earth.
(2) Heaven and earth influence all living beings,
and it rests with mortals to turn this influence to good
account.
(3) The fate of the living depends largely on the
good-will of the dead.
As the miner is likely to begin his initial work on a
mountain side, he is bound, by digging deeply into the
ground, to wound the spirit of some dragon, tiger or
phoenix. Under the doctrines of Feng Shui gold is chief of
the sacred five-system of the elements, the other four being
water, earth, fire and wind. The deeper meaning of Feng
Shui would entail very long and complicated explanations
for which space cannot be found here. Its influence on
mining has, until recently, been disastrous.
Between about A.D. 400 and 500 Chinese records
report that there were 1,000 gold washers working in Han
Chung (Shansi). Up to the end of the eighth century
gold and silver were used uncoined as commodity money.
The Sung Dynasty (A.D. 960-1127) was endeavour-
ing to assist in the promotion of industry; amongst other
reforms it organised the mining administration. The
dynastic records of the period contain a series of man-
dates dealing with gold mining in Shantung. In A.D. 1023
the emperor wished to praise the officials and miners for
the satisfactory output, but was dissuaded by a Grand
Councillor who complained that so many labourers had
been attracted to mining that none were left for farming.
According to the state of agricultural crops gold mining
was alternately forbidden or encouraged in those districts.

Between 1078 and 1084 an Imperial decree seems to
have been issued prohibiting mining.
In 1114 numerous gold nuggets were found in Kuang-

permitted in Kuangtung. In 1119 gold mines in Sze-
chuan had to pay 20% of their output as royalty to the

In 1126 the Kins marched to the Sung capital, then
Kaifengfu, demanding a war indemnity of five million
ounces of gold, besides fifty million ounces of silver and
oxen, horses and silk. But it was not possible to raise
more than 200,000 ounces of gold and five million ounces of
silver. In the following year the capital was removed to
Hangchow. Some years later an annual indemnity of
250,000 ounces of gold and 250,000 pieces of silk was
agreed upon. This shows that the mining of gold had to
be enforced. Many families were forcibly removed to
work as miners. Only in 1260 was the compulsory
labour system discontinued. The Sung Dynasty, which
ended in 1280, having been continually at war, did very
little for the promotion of mining in China.
It was followed by the Mongols who attempted to
force paper money as sole legal tender. After a pause
of only three years (in 1263) compulsory recruiting of
miners was resumed and many thousands of families were
forcibly sent to the mines. An order was issued that
4,000 people were to dig for gold in Tsi-hai (Teng-chow
prefecture) in Shantung province. In 1278 another 2,000
gold washers were recruited by the magistrates of T'sing-
chow and Lui-chow. Gold washing in Manchuria is
recorded at that period.
In 1287 an official manager of gold mines near Nan-
king was nominated. Under him were engaged 7,365
families conducting gold digging in seventy localities of
Kiangsu Province. But no gold was found. At the same
time gold mining was carried on in the provinces of
Anhui, Hupeh, Hunan, Szechuen and Yunnan.
In 1286 the export of gold and silver from China was
forbidden. In reply to a petition to work certain mines
the emperor remarked: "Under the last dynasty gold
mining produced a certain amount of revenue, but as
time went on the financial strength of the people failed,
with the result that there was much suffering in the gold
mining districts. Continued mining must sooner or later
exhaust the country's mineral resources."

In 1421 the Ming capital was removed to Peking.
Emperor Suan-Tsung decided to close down all mines in
the empire.
The Ming emperors in 1596 ordered mining in Chihli
and the neighbouring provinces, but ten years later all
mines were compelled to close down. Emperor K'ang Hsi,
on ascending the throne in 1662, did much to encourage
mining. At that time the royalty payable to the Govern-

Viceroy of the Hukuang Provinces petitioned that mining

were apt to be unnecessarily disturbed. In 1776 it was
decreed that mining should be permitted only when it
was proved that it was not interfering with farms and
graveyards.
The Taiping Rebellion undoubtedly acted as a
serious impediment to the mining industry in South
China. In the course of the Panthey Rebellion, which
began in 1855 in Yunnan, the Mohammedans did particu-
larly extensive harm to the mining industry of that
province, destroying almost every mine there.¹
From these only incomplete instances it will have
become apparent that the mining industry in China,
during the Imperial regime, was not promoted by the
State, but instead hindered and ruined throughout many
centuries. From the frequent orders issued in connection
with the prohibition of gold mining it might appear that
gold was plentiful in this country; but this was at no
time the case.

SHAPES IN WHICH GOLD IS USED IN CHINA:
Leaving aside the existence of gold in its crude state
in the shape of nuggets, gold sand or dust, we find that
the yellow metal is employed in China for the manufacture
of jewellery, gold leaves and gold bars. Its use for
currency purposes has hitherto remained extremely
limited. As gold has at no time been adopted as a standard
in China, it has remained to this day an article of trade,
subject to wide price fluctuations.
With regard to jewellery it can be asserted that this
consists mainly of golden rings, rarely studded with
precious stones, of bracelets and hairpins, usually made
from metal as nearly as possible 1000 fine. These articles
are almost always of crude finish and are acquired for
their intrinsic value, because they can be quickly hoarded
in the event of danger and readily turned into cash in the
hour of need. In China gold ornaments are usually
bought and sold by weight, the fineness of the metal being
uniform and known. There is another factor which
makes the acquisition of golden jewellery desirable,
namely, the element of chance. A ring is frequently
bought by the small man when gold is thought to be cheap
in terms of silver. The same article is disposed of
remorselessly by the owner whenever gold becomes

GOLD AS CURRENCY IN CHINA: 4
Gold has never served as a standard for China's
currency system, although it has, at times, served as
a quasi-currency. In about 1100 B.C. gold acted, as a
medium of circulation in this country; not in a minted
state, but in the shape of a cubic inch (tsun), weighing 1
catty (kin).
In A.D. 10, Wang Mang abolished knife money and
created a new currency in gold, silver, copper, tortoise-
shell and cowries. The gold unit was called kin and was
valued at 10,000 cash.

GOLD BARS:
Enormous significance has, during recent years, been
attached in an evergrowing degree to the existence, or, as
some choose to assert, to the non-existence, of gold bars in
China. The general public looks with serious misgivings
and with suspicion on the mysteries surrounding the soit
disant fearfully intricate mechanism connected with gold
bars. It is human nature to mistrust and even condemn
problems the aims and purposes of which are strange to

the mind of the concerned. As a matter of fact, a gold
bar in China is a small, brick-shaped piece of gold,
weighing about 10 ounces, (10 Chauping taels) or 366.71
grammes, containing 978 parts of pure gold and 22 parts
of alloy. That is all there is in the matter. It has no
mechanism at all, and its inside is composed of exactly

To be still plainer:-the exact translation into Japan-

Surely this is simple enough. In German, one is entitled
to call one gold bar of Shanghai origin, 1,000 gold marks; in
French, 1,230 gold francs; in English £48; in American,
240 gold dollars. These are round figures, as near as
possible to reality.
So far we have had clear sailing, and even the
pessimist will have to admit that no mystery surrounds
these plain statements of fact. But the suspicion of the
uninitiated is apparently not yet allayed, for he asks:
"Why is gold in China presented in the shape of bars?"
The answer will either be, "Why not in bars?", or else,
"Because all the nations of the world hold and conserve
their stock of unminted gold in the shape of bars."
After this rather primitive, yet plausible, explanation
another obstacle has apparently been removed. In olden
times unminted gold in this country was cast into
small shoes of 10 taels weight, similar in form to sycee
silver. The transformation into bars is a comparatively
recent innovation, denoting foreign influence.
And yet the pessimist is still unconvinced and asserts
that he has heard that, in reality, effective gold bars are
not to be found in China; their existence is to be likened
to the treasure unearthed in fairy tales. Wrong again,
Mr. Pessimist, for only since the beginning of the current
century (i.e., between 1901 and 1927) more than 200
million taels worth of gold bars have actually been
exported abroad from this country. Therefore gold bars
existed yesterday in China and will put in an appearance
to-morrow at a given signal. This signal has as its watch-
word "parity." Gold bars, though cold inanimate beings,
are undergoing an eternal metamorphosis. To-day their
origin is sown by the arrival of an American war vessel
at Shanghai; the sailors, upon landing, change their
American gold coins into local silver currency. The
American double eagles find their way into the melting

since they are of a standard weight and fineness, are
distinctly gold currency, which, though not legalised by
the Chinese Government, is fully recognised as valid all
over the world. Shanghai gold bars are based on the
gold yen, owing to the proximity of Japan, whence
or to which country bullion shipments can be effected
from or to Shanghai in the course of a few days. As
soon as the embargo against the export of gold from
Japan will have been removed (as a consequence of which
the yen would revert to gold parity) one will be enabled
to see how very real gold bars actually are. One would
observe some day the import of Japanese gold to
Shanghai for conversion into gold bars. As soon as the
parity permits it, a movement in the other direction will
set in, namely export from China of gold bars to Japan
or to America.
A heavy fall in the price of silver abroad will induce
owners of gold rings, bracelets and hairpins, to dispose
of their possessions without hesitation, simply because
they would then obtain much more silver than they
disbursed when originally buying these articles. This is
one of the ways in which gold bars come into existence,
and it is one of the causes why gold bars are exported
from China abroad in return for silver bullion.
Gold bars do exist in China in quantities, the extent
of which depends upon conditions that are conducible to
an increase or decrease. One often hears contemptuous
remarks about "fictitious gold bars", or about "paper
gold bars." But the uninitiated public forgets that the
so-called fictitious gold bar is a legitimate measure of
values, exactly to the same extent, for example, as the
Japanese yen of to-day. In view of the still existing
export prohibition of gold from Japan the value of the
yen is to-day about 7% below parity. Japanese currency
in the shape of gold coins, or even gold bars, has, since
1917, been unobtainable. Nevertheless yen currency is
by no means a phantasm, but a most potential factor in
world economics. As the Shanghai gold bar is closely
interwoven with Japanese currency, these two are bound
to march together. As soon as the yen has regained its
full gold value, the much abused Shanghai gold bar will
automatically regain its old glory. Then there will be
opportunity to observe one day the import into China of

The conditions under which the topic is being dis-
cussed here mark it imperative to exclude technicalities,
methods of calculation and formulæ. All these are found
profusely in The Currencies of China. Therefore only
some general terms relative to the gold bar market can be
briefly referred to here.
All transactions in gold bars have to be concluded at
the Shanghai Gold Stock Exchange or at the Shanghai
Produce Exchange. Business transacted at the former
institution is about 80% of the total, as compared with
20% at the latter. But operations entered in the Gold
Exchange must be liquidated there and cannot be set
off at the Produce Exchange, or anywhere else. Only
members (who have to be shareholders) are entitled to
transact business at the Exchange as brokers.
It rests with the seller either to deliver, during he
currency of the contract, actual gold bars, or else to
set off his previous sales, receiving, respectively paying,
differences in silver currency. If the seller fails to do
either of the two alternatives, the existing contract will
automatically be liquidated at a price based on the official
selling rate of the Hongkong & Shanghai Banking
Corporation for cable transfer on Japan, plus 3 taels
per bar.
This means that the official T.T. rate for
yen, on the last day of the month concerned, has to
be multiplied by 480, and 3 taels have to be added to
the result.

Another lawful mode of operating in gold bars is to
be seen from the following example. If an arbitragist
desires to buy on the local market a large amount of yen
at a certain limit, it might happen that there are no
sellers at that moment, or else only sellers at dearer price
levels. The gold bar market at Shanghai is much wider
and affords the operator the opportunity to buy the
requisite amount of gold bars instead at a parity
quotation which he judges to be reasonable. As soon as
the opportunity presents itself the arbitragist will sell
the gold bars he had previously bought (in case of need
in partial lots), purchasing at the same time the needed
yen which, at a former occasion, were unobtainable. In
this way gold bars serve as a most convenient and
perfectly legitimate hedge, obviating almost all elements
of speculation.
The export of actual gold bars from China are
undertaken by banks only, who, in turn, sell cable
transfer against such shipments. This means that they
are exporting gold from China and bringing silver here
instead, a simple transaction which is perfectly legitimate
and free from the risks of speculative dealings.
The few instances quoted here are only vague outlines
of a problem which has very wide ramifications and
which can only be justly treated by a tower of calculations.
