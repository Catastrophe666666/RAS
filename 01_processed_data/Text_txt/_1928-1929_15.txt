THE KITE'S
KITE'S MESSAGE
Translated from the Chinese
By ELFRIDA HUDSON
It was a beautiful spring day, the day for kite-flying
in the Celestial Empire.
The young son of Duke Tsi did not like to stay in the
schoolroom. "What do I care about studying?" he said
to himself. "Such old fogies as Confucius and Mencius
weary a person to death. It is fortunate that there were
people in olden times who knew how to institute some-
thing worth while, like this kite-flying, for instance, or
where should we be? At this time people are flying their
kites on the city wall, and I must go and join them and
show my skill." So he ordered his servant to paste a
kite for him.
Meanwhile his young companion, Han, was sitting
in the deserted schoolroom. Han was an orphan who
was allowed to study with the Duke's son to spur him on
to greater effort. "Young master Tsi has run off again,"
he was thinking to himself. "His father has built him
this splendid study pavilion, but of what use is it? The
flowers around it are all sickly looking, the weeds are
growing in abundance; instead of seeing pretty butterflies
sporting around, we can see hardly anything but noxious
spiders." Despondently he picked up his "Dictionary of
Rhymes" and began to turn its leaves. He rubbed his
ink-stick on the slab and was just beginning to write
down some verses, when in rushed a servant with a kite
in his hand.
"Master wants you to paint something on this kite
for him," he said.
"Here I was just beginning to write some verses,"
said Han impatiently, "when you come and interrupt me
and stop the inspiration! I have no colours for painting.
He surely does not want me to scribble characters in
black and white on the kite. I cannot even afford to buy
a little red ink to punctuate my "Book of Changes", much

'Tis spring-time! With delirious joy all nature smiles,
The flowers display their varied hues.
The azure sky, the song of birds, nothing beguiles
My grief. On my sad lot I muse.
With strangers it is cast. No parent's heart of love

The servant took the kite to his young master who
was impatiently waiting for it. He was disgusted to
see the characters written on it, instead of the expected
painting. "That's just like Han's mean way of acting,
he said pettishly. "Nothing but verses from right to
left and from top to bottom! He thinks so much of his
verses. I am sure if his life were forfeited, he would
think he could easily redeem it by writing verses. Now
there is no help for it, I shall have to take it as it is.”
"By the Sages! What a marvel!" exclaimed the
young duke. "I have hardly loosened the string before
it flies upward like a mad thing."
High above all the others the kite kept sweeping
upward in ever widening gyres until it disappeared in
the azure sky.
