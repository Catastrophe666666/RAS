CHINESE POTTERY AND PORCELAIN:
AN APPRECIATION
By R. D. ABRAHAM
The present Paper was prepared during a holiday
where no books of reference were available.
A break from the deafening roar of Shanghai traffic
and busy days very soon brought images to mind of a
bygone age, and the fascination lay not a little in the
knowledge that the art pertained to the land we live in
and to the people we live amongst, and more so at a time
like the present when the country is in turmoil and the
majority of the people live in terror of their lives.
It is at such a time that Art, the delightful and pure
art of China, conjures up thoughts that inspire one to
philosophical thinking along paths that lead to a strange
yet happy calm.
An inspiration was felt to record these feelings, but in
a subject like this-Chinese Pottery and Porcelain: An
Appreciation-it was, I fear, great presumption to make
the attempt without books of reference. However, the
subject has been attempted, and your kind indulgence is
craved for this Paper in that it lacks scholarly references
and scientific data. It is, indeed, a Paper for the layman.
About a decade ago, Dr. J. G. Andersson discovered
by excavation in Honan fragments of pottery of which
the body, the glaze and, what is most important, the
designs, resembled similar fragments discovered in India,
in Persia, in South-western Russia and on the Mediter-
ranean Coast; the geological strata in which these frag-
ments were found were at least 5,000 years old.
This discovery upsets our preconceived notions as to
the age of pottery, the use of glaze, the design for its
embellishment and the connexion which evidently must
have existed between peoples so far distant.
We discuss affairs to-day which happened millions of

communication with India and Europe at least 5,000 years
ago, and that Pottery was world-wide in its use.
The potters must have lived in a civilised age, to
judge by the pottery produced, of which specimens have
been discovered.
The impression that these specimens make on one
certainly leads one to ponder, for here we have irrefutable
evidence of Pottery, and good specimens of that ware, of
more than a thousand years previous to the Chow Period,
which formerly was thought to be the oldest period with
which pottery, consisting of coarse clay-vessels crudely
shaped with matted or striated design, could be connected.
Sir Aurel Stein refers in his book Innermost Asia to
discoveries he has made in Seistan in Eastern Iran of
fragments similar to those of Dr. Andersson's discovery.
This is all that we know of the history of these remote
times. Further discoveries must be made, more scientific
data obtained, for, as Mr. G. K. Chesterton states, science
must make further headway to correct perhaps many of
what are our present scientific beliefs. When reading
Mr. Chesterton's article on this subject, these excavations
of Andersson and Stein were called to mind.
One is reminded, too, of a comparatively recent article
in a leading magazine in which certain oriental archaeo-
logists claim now to have made discoveries, which very
discoveries were made known to the archaeological world
some years ago, and yet no acknowledgement is made of
this fact in their report.
The next period is the Chow, and of this period
numerous specimens of bronze and jade have come down
to the present day, many of the pieces being in perfect
preservation. It gives us an excellent idea of the arts
and crafts of that period, and an insight into the minds of
the people, to realize that all of the bronzes (if "all" is
not too sweeping a statement) were made for ritual
purposes while jade pieces, too, had ritual significations.
This was the age of Confucius, and propriety held sway.
Bronze vessels were made in sets to stand before the
ancestral shrine. Everything had its proper signification.
Jade, in particular, was carved for symbolic purposes.
Jade was worn on ceremonial occasions, and advantage
was taken of its resonance to cause a pleasant tinkling
when the wearer moved.
It is a strange thought that Aaron, Israel's High
Priest, should in his canonicals have golden bells so that

It is a far cry from the Sinaitic Desert to the State of
Lu-yet there are these "touches of nature" which shew
that "the whole world is kin." A little thought on this
matter will impress us as to the futility of war and hatred
amongst men, and of how pleasant it is "to dwell as
brothers in unity."
But what of the Pottery of the Chow period?
These do not give any indication of the splendid works
in bronze and jade. What has become of the early
"pottery" civilization that we discussed at the beginning
of this Paper? Have there been civilizations equal to ours
in bygone years and now lost? Will the same thing
happen in our own case? "There is nothing new under
the sun" preached Koheloth.
We now come to the Han Period, a most prolific period
in the history of Chinese art, in magnificently wrought
bronzes in which the soul of the artist poured itself into
his handiwork, making his creations pulsate with the very
essence of life and energy. They are small pieces very
often, but are none the less monumental in conception and
in execution.
The pottery of the Han period shews the same genius
that we see exhibited in bronzes and sculptures of this
age.
The biscuit is usually reddish-not too thick, and in
some instances quite thin-and baked hard, affording an
excellent example of, as Hobson calls it, porcelaneous
pottery, the fore-runner of true porcelain.
An illustration will be seen in the picture of the flat
shaped bottle of the Han Dynasty, Fig. 1.
The biscuit is reddish, thin, and there is for this type
of ware quite a sharp resonance on percussion.
The piece is finally modelled and there is balance,
proportion and technique.
You will notice that there is a gentle swelling at the
bottom of the piece which gradually narrows down as it
reaches the top, culminating in a comparatively large
spout and capped by a stopper. Allowance is made for
the vessel to be carried in suspension. Presumably this
piece is a copy of a soldier's portable water bottle in bronze
and perhaps shews Siberian influence in modelling and
design. The copying of bronze pieces in pottery was
quite common in China.

There are also clay figures of men and animals
executed in this period: some of them are painted. This
class of funeral ware was carried on down to the Sung
period and later.
The execution of these pieces of the Han, Tang and
Sung Periods is, as I have already said, remarkable for
strength and life.
The thought often comes to mind of how Chinese art
compares with Egyptian, Roman and Hellenic art, and
very often one is at a loss what to think. The discoveries
in Tutankhamen's tomb are gorgeous. There are gold
plated furnishings, beautiful ebony carvings, inlay work
of the most intricate and delicate nature. We are over-
come by the pomp, glory and luxury of Egypt. Look at
the marvellous statuary of Rome and of Hellas and one is
dumbfounded! Chinese art, and Chinese civilization (for
art is the crystallization of civilization) seem to shrink
before these giants of Egypt and of Greece.
Yet in quiet moments the simple calm of Chinese art
will steal upon you unawares and give you comfort. A
fleeting colour of russet autumn, the fresh green of spring
-these seem to be caught and detained for your delecta-
tion. It is as if the philosopher-artist, the nature-loving
artisan, had gone out into the green fields and woods
and, sitting by limpid streams, was taught that beauty
lay in simplicity, in line, in proportion and in colour. With
these reflections, perhaps the gorgeousness of Egypt will
not carry all before it.
And again, with European art the human body has
been emphasised; the perfect human form was the highest
form of beauty. In China, man is but a dot in the
universe; he is of small account. But nature is incom-
parable. Colour has a prepossessing charm.
Such thoughts begin to bring relief, but how shall we
know that they are not erroneous, seeing that our only

The following dynasty, the Tang, was also conspicuous
for its great art achievements. The painting of this
period was remarkable for its strength and virility. It is
said that when the painting of a certain period is of a
high order then it follows that all the works of art of that
period must be good.
Clay formed by the potter, in the beginning by hand
and later by the wheel, into vessels which were baked,
made the first earthenware vessels, but these were too
porous to contain liquids. Later on, in the evolution of
the clay vessel, came the introduction of powdered stone,
making it harder and able to hold liquids; this was the
second stage of what Hobson calls porcelaneous stoneware.
It is now easy to imagine that enterprising potters experi-
mented both with clay (chiefly Kaolin) and stone, until
the former clumsy vessels were later made light to handle,
smooth to the touch, safe for the retention of liquid and
modelled either for beauty or for utilitarian purposes.
This brings us to the third stage in the evolution of
porcelain. In the Tang period, as far as is known, the
culmination was reached, i.e., the evolution of pottery into
porcelain. True porcelain must possess three qualities:-
(1) Hardness, (2) Translucency, (3) Clear resonance on
percussion.
The word "evolution" has been used quite often; it is a
favourite term in these days. It correctly defines the
word "change," and instinctively the feeling comes to
one that changes were not wrought by chance nor of
their own accord. We see that anxious thought and
experiment were required, and that there were direction,
system and method. Perhaps those artist-potters felt
that when they pondered over the problems of the universe

The two-handed vase shown in Fig. 2 shews that
shapes were borrowed from foreign lands. This specimen
is in the shape of a Greek Amphora, but it is already being
adapted to suit Chinese symbolism for, as you will notice,
the handles are composed of two dragons, one heavier than
the other, representing the dual principals Yin and Yang.
Chinese artists have borrowed ideas from foreigners here
and there, but these have not been of any particular
importance: the art of China in the main is indigenous.
This piece is also classed as porcelaneous stoneware.
While on the question of shapes due to outside
influences, Figs. 3, 4 and 5, all of the Sung Dynasty, will
prove interesting.
It is generally known that pigments were used for
the decoration of pieces in these early days. At first
designs used to be impressed on the soft clay, e.g., a leaf
would be used to give a naturalistic design, but it is not
generally known that enamel colours, over glaze, were
also employed with success.
Chinese Records describe a ware, called Ch'ai, made
about the end of the five dynasty period. The records
state that these pieces were as thin as paper, the fragments
sharp as a razor and the colour like the "blue of the sky
after rain." For many years this was considered a fanci-
ful description by enthusiastic antiquarians. A few years
ago, however, excavations on some ancient sites revealed
porcelain answering to the above description with the
one exception that the pieces are not quite as thin as the
paper we know to-day. We can add one more quality to
those described above, and that is Translucency, for if
the pieces are held up to a strong light peculiar flakes,
resembling snow flakes, may be noticed: the combination
of age, and the infiltration of soil has caused this beautiful
effect. The workmanship of these pieces exhibits the
highest skill in the art of pottery and in the kiln.
Bearing in mind these beautiful pieces, chaste both
in colour and form, we will leave this epoch and now
glance at the Sung Period.

the Tang, but the art of Sung was mellow, as if the youth
and energies of the Han and Tang periods were softened
and harmonized in the age of Sung. Mellow tones con-
stituted the chief attraction of the Sung Dynasty, rather

The glaze of the type known as Chun Ware was of a
strawberry purple hue breaking into lavender mottling
and grounds: this is the fore-runner of the typical flambé
glazes of later times.
The colours are beautiful, but the forms and shapes
are heavy.
In several cases of this type of ware, the biscuit,
together with fluxing material, was put into the kiln,
whereupon air was introduced; the oxygen assisted in
a chemical combination which produced purple and red
patches on the surface, but these patches could not be
produced in any required position nor could their colours
be determined; the result was considered a "trick of the
kiln."
The Lungchuan Ware of the Sung Period has been
well known almost all over the world for several centuries.
The ware is heavy and capable of rough handling-hence
it was suitable for export. In Africa this ware was
venerated in that it was claimed that if poisoned food
was placed on a plate of this ware, the glaze would change
colour. In Europe it was prized as the famous celadon
are, for the colour of the glaze.
The colours of the Lungchuan glaze are known as
olive green, onion green, etc., from the various tones
of green employed. Some of the colours approach a
bluish-green of the Ying Ching type. The decorations
of the bowls and vases are very often in relief and
sometimes incised on the body, the glaze covering the
incised design giving an artistic effect of high value.
The celadons of the Sung Period were one of the
popular wares coming from China; another fairly
common ware of the Sung Dynasty is the Tzu Chow
Ware. The glaze is yellowish-white on which are decora-
tions in black, the brushwork being particularly "lively."
Another form of decoration was the design in high relief,
with two shades of the same coloured glaze to accentuate
the decoration,
Another variation of the Sung glazes is the Chien
Yao Ware-mirror black glaze, with brown fleckings:
this was known as the "Hare fur" design owing to its
resemblance to a hare's pelt. These bowls are also
decorated with russet spots known as "iron rust" designs.
Beautiful beyond description are the bowls of this type
decorated in the interior with light brown flecks in the

The next glaze to discuss is the beautiful Ting Yao
glaze. The biscuit of this glaze is of a fine-white porce-
lain on which a lustrous creamy fluxing material is
used. The result is one which causes the beholder to
experience a thrill of pure delight. The effect on the
ware is further enhanced by the decoration in low relief,
or incised design under the glaze, of peonies, lotus,
phoenix, geese, etc., all executed with a symmetry and
harmony possible only in an artist possessed of a deep
love of nature. In the Ming Dynasty this, it is said,
was the most prized of all Sung wares.
There are three important types of this ware:-
Tu Ting-earthy or yellowish in colour;
Fen Ting-flour white in colour;
Pai Ting-the white Ting.
The Ting ware was a highly prized Imperial ware;
no defects or black spots were to be allowed on the
surface.
The next period is the Yuan period, which endeav-
oured to keep up the Sung Tradition. This dynasty
lasted only eighty years and the ruling house was
foreign. There were many famous artists who lived
during this dynasty and whose works are admired to
this day. There was, however, no special feature in
the works of art of this period to comment upon.
The next epoch was the Ming, and those dealing
with this period are always severe on the works of art
of this dynasty. They say that Ming Dynasty art is
influenced by Confucianist teachings, which bound life
by a system of codes of propriety and correctness, thereby
stultifying enterprise and keeping genius in check.
This is true to a certain extent, but that Confucian
teaching successfully held the population of China in
the path of peace and common-sense, and encouraged a
sincere respect for "right reason" or Justice, is no mean
achievement.
Many years ago-before China became "civilized"—
a dispute could be settled as soon as one of the contending
parties would admit, "This belong reason." That was

what Confucianism did for China-made these "farmers

And what of art? A troubled sense possesses one:
shall licence destroy the joy of beauty and harmony? Is
there no middle course? When we look at the art of
Ming we find the answer to our question: it is in the
affirmative.
The Blanc de Chine and Blue and White Wares of
this period are well known and highly esteemed.
"Kwan Yin" was very often made of this ware and
what greater tribute to the genius of Ming than that
Ming Blanc-de-Chine "Kwan Yins" have been piously
reverenced in Europe as representing the Madonna?
Kwan Yin, or Avalokitesvara of India, as modelled by a
Chinese artist in beautiful white porcelain to become
later an object of deep religious veneration in Europe!
Confucius said: "All within the four seas are brothers.
It is true that many of the Ming pieces are heavy,
that the figures and designs are very often grotesque,
and the colours are florid: while recognising these faults,
let us also in justice to this dynasty record the great art
of the era.
The next period, the Ch'ing Dynasty, was fortunate
in that two of their long-lived rulers, though of Tartar
origin, worked devotedly for the advancement and glory
of China-Kang Hsi and Chien Lung. Art, more especially
porcelain, developed under their encouragement and
patronage. Pottery became a glorious art, and decoration
became intricate. Egg-shell porcelain was made so
tender and delicate that it seems almost sacrilege to
handle these pieces to-day. A delightful riot of colours
prevailed, and three and five-coloured pieces became the
vogue.
The Sung traditions, however, were not lost, and
charming pieces with glazes of a single lustrous colour
were highly prized and to-day are the objects of deep
admiration. The rich claret red, sang-de-boeuf, is one
of these.
The glaze beloved in China and the delight of Europe
and of America is that famous glaze with the fanciful
name "Peach bloom.”
This glaze is of an attractive rose pink in which
may be seen green spots, the whole effect being reminis-
cent of the bloom of the Peach. This glaze was used
only on small pieces-vases, stamp boxes and water pots

Much has been written on the origin of Chinese Art
and of the outside influences on it-Greek, Buddhist,
Mohammedan, Siberian and European.
Other art has also been compared to Chinese art
and speculation is rife on this matter but the preponder-
ance of evidence is that the art of hCina is indigenous.
Where pottery and glazes actually originated is
unknown. May we not take a lesson here from Natural
History and plead for a common origin? Consider that
well known bird, the Kingfisher, whose original habitat
is believed to have been in Malay. To-day over one
hundred and sixty specimens of this specie are known to
science and the bird is to be found almost everywhere.
We have not discussed designs, methods of decora-
tion, nor shapes; suffice it to say that many of the designs
were religious in significance; some of them conveyed
good wishes and felicitations; others were purely decora-
tive. The shapes were probably at first formed on
utilitarian grounds only, and later became elaborated.
