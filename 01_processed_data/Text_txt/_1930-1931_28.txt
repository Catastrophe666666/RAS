THE ASIATIC ORIGIN OF SOUTH
AMERICAN MAN
By J. J. GAPANOVICH
The aboriginal population of America is generally
considered as native to this continent, disconnected from
the European or Asiatic peoples; that is a commonplace
assertion, being found in many text-books and sometimes
corroborated scientifically. Thus, they speak of the
American race or "Red race" having peculiar character-
istics unlike other races (in fact it is not red but dark or
light yellow) and even now, when we have learnt that
"pure" races do not exist at all, while existing races are
more numerous than we are accustomed to think, even
now they represent the American race as an exception,
because it is supposed to be homogeneous all over the con-
tinent; on the other hand, "red skin" peoples are frequently
represented by the public as having striking character-
istics, which are well set forth in the heroes of J. Fenimore
Cooper or Jack London (romantic figures so unlike the
modern Europeans or Asiatics).
Indeed, such are ordinary opinions on the subject; but
the same assertion is made by some scientists, who
corroborate the notion of the American race from
anthropological, linguistic and ethnological points of view.
As to the American race, taken in anthropological
aspect, J. Deniker held not long ago: "The existence of
an American race, or rather a group of American races,
is generally conceded-a group to which all the native
populations of the New World belong." It is true,
according to somatic characters, he differentiates the
Eskimo, North, Central and South Americans, and
Patagonians, but it seems much more a geographic dis-
tribution than an anthropological classification, neverthe-

In connection with recent investigations of the blood

idea of an isolation of the American race. According to
a more or less degree of the agglutination of different
bloods, there are distinguished four groups: I, agglutinat-

agglutinating mutually, the former more, the latter less.
As some American investigations of the northern Indians
show, they belong 78% to the Group I, 20%-II and

mankind on the iso-agglutination, stated that an Oceanic-
American race exists which has 67% belonging to the
Group I, 29%—II, 4%—III, the third thus being incon-
siderable, while the fourth is not represented at all. This
may be on account of a very early separation of the
American race, which happened before the bio-chemical
differentiation of mankind.
From the linguistic point of view, the American
languages are characterized by the so-called polysynthetic
structure, which means that they prefer to indicate a
connection of ideas by using connecting syllables instead
of inflexion and suffixion; they like to vary the meaning of
words by using numerous prefixes and frequent repetitions
of syllables; to connect an object with a verb belonging to
it, they use incorporation. They mix a pronoun with a verb
and still keep the dual and triple number. These methods:
polysynthetism, prefixion and incorporation are so essen-
tial to the American languages that comparative philology
separates them as a special stock of languages unlike the
principal linguistic branches. This, however, does not by
any means solve the problem of their origin and forma-
tion, one can only state that these languages stand alone.
From the ethnological point of view, we cannot speak
of the homogeneity of the American race in the definite
way that we have spoken concerning linguistics. Visible

More suggestive may be reference to those unique

corresponds to them. Yet the Spanish conquistadores
looked with wonder at the richness of these states,
their admirable technique, and last, the cult of the Sun
with its splendid rites as well as horrible blood sacrifices.
The Aztecs, Maya, Kechua were in a period of metals,
when gold was extensively used; but this Metal Age
seemed to have surpassed the European culture, as the poor
Spanish invaders thought. At present we have learnt
what the Metal Age in ancient Europe meant, especially
since the Minoan culture, with all its richness and beauty
(3000-1400 B.C.) was recently discovered in the eastern
portion of the Mediterranean. So our attitude toward
the ancient American civilization must be other than that
of the Spanish.
However, for us, too, the state of society existing in
these realms has nothing to compare with the oldest
civilizations known to us, and perhaps, only remainders
of such a system could be observed in the history of
Sumeria and Egypt (6000-4000 B.C.).
If that which may be called "culture" in America
originated in so isolated a way and had no connection
with the Old World, is it not an extra evidence in favour
of the independent beginnings of the American race?
It goes farther than the facts allow : all the above mentioned

Thus the attempts to find evidences of man's early

It is not our task to enter into a discussion as to which
are the original homes of the human ancestors, according
to modern hypotheses on this subject. Here it is enough
to state some inferences. All suppositions are based on
finding the remains of any human primates, such as the
Rhodesian skull found in Africa (perhaps, belonging to
Homo Neanderthalensis), or the earlier specimen of

the previous ones. On the other hand, continents reveal-

ancestors of anthropoids may more or less claim to be the
cradle of mankind as, for instance, the case of America.
This line of human ancestry is accepted by such
prominent scientists as Prof. G. Elliot Smith and Prof.

ancestor, who lived in trees and was like a gibbon, but

Northern Migrations to America from Asia.
If man did not originate in America but came
here very late, a question arises-in which way did he
choose to arrive? It means we must learn which continents
were still connected with America, in the Pleictocene,
or, at any rate, could have communication with it after
that time; since archaeological evidences are lacking
as to traces of such a migration, one must look for eth-
nological counterparts of the American aborigines some-
where else, so far as one can speak of the present
primitive tribes, as revealing some analogies, if not
affinities, to the civilized peoples of ancient America
and of the Old World as well. Finally, some particulars
should be given concerning the migrations, mainly with
regard to their possible time, which, indeed, may be
stated only proximately.

parallels existing between the Old and New World in

Such may be the origin of the northern Indians. But
what can be said concerning the civilized peoples of Central
and South America? The theory in question suggests that
mankind migrated here on a level with the Neolithic; then
it spread southward, the geographical conditions being
very favourable for such a movement, and it elaborated
its culture independently here: cultivation, weaving,
ceramics, metallurgy are original inventions of America.
By no means is this a solution of the problem. The
supposed Asiatic origin of the American race is based on
ethnological (and partly anthropological and linguistic)
evidences; why should the analogy not be continued
farther though in another direction if there are con-
siderations in its favour?
At present one may reasonably suppose that the
Mexican, Yucatan, Peruvian cultures were not so isolated-
that is to say from Asia. Let us briefly summarize essen-
tials of these cultures. The American civilizations, which
arose around about 1000 B.C., were familiar with pottery,
weaving, dyeing; their community was based on a temple,
having abundant blood sacrifices; and an intensely astro-
nomical priesthood, their rulers being under a rigorous
law of omen; they (the Maya) invented writing used
mainly for keeping the calendars in order, while the
Peruvians adopted a method of knotting cords used mostly
for financial purposes. And what was especially remark-
able was this obsession of shedding human blood, which
was like a general mental aberration.
These striking features are not, however, unknown
in instances of some ancient civilizations, so far as we are
familiar with them by recent discoveries.
The civilizations existing in the Nile Valley and
Mesopotamia flourished around 6000-3000 B.C., before
great Semitic invasions, and they were the most ancient in
the world. Their level was not unlike that of the American
civilizations which emerged 4000-3000 years after. The
people were busy with irrigation and cultivation, having
no money and no interior commerce; they were familiar
with bronze, copper, silver, gold, but not with iron, metals
being used by the rulers mostly for ornamentation
purposes and incidentally for trade. The temple domin-
ated the life, being a high tower in Sumeria and a massive
pyramid in Egypt; the ruler was the greatest priest in
one country and the incarnation of a god in another.
Both peoples invented writing which, cuneiform as well
as hieroglyphic, was adapted to their languages made up

South Pacific Migrations to America.
The previous survey of hypotheses on the peopling
of America allows for the following statements: (1) Man
did not originate in this continent; (2) he migrated here,
undoubtedly, from Asia, having crossed the Behring
Strait; (3) he appeared here relatively late, probably,
being on a level of the Neolithic. The admixture of
Asiatic elements being beyond doubt, there are in the
American aborigines other constituent characteristics
which cannot be reduced to the same source; such are the
features of the civilized and semicivilized peoples of
ancient America, and particularly is this the case with
regard to South America; there are considerable differ-
ences between North and South America in all aspects
previously discussed, and one may reasonably suppose that
the migration was going here from another direction.
First of all, in contradistinction to a geographical
connection which exists between North America and
North Asia, the two American continents are completely
separated in spite of the Isthmus of Panama, or rather on
account of this Isthmus. It is formed by inaccessible
mountains together with swamps abounding with mos-
quitoes, being one of the most insalubrious areas in the
world. The civilized peoples living southward and north-
ward from it never crossed it and were entirely unaware
of each other before the Spanish conquest. Even the
animals could not migrate by this way; thus fauna was
quite different in North and South America; the reindeer,
caribou, bison, bear are typical of the former, and the
llama, guanaco, parrot, monkey of the latter. This state-

Ways of Southern Migrations to America.
As may be seen, there are considerable points of
distinction between the North and South American abori-
gines; South American mankind is heterogeneous in
many respects, undoubtedly indicating Australian,
Malayo-Polynesian and, possibly, East Asiatic elements.
This being the case, the migration of mankind to
South America must have proceeded in ways otherwise
than to
North America; peoples starting from
Australasia crossed the South Pacific.
It is, however, easier to say than to do: there is no
"bridge" between Australasia and South America like
that between North Asia and North America; while
Asiatic aborigines could quite naturally have crossed the
Behring Strait, peoples who attempted to cross the South
Pacific would have been hindered by the immense distances
between the continents. How was the migration to South
America, if any, made possible?

South America was insufficiently peopled even at the time
