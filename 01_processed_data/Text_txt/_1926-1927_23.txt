Reputed Founder of the Chin Tan Chiao
(金丹​教​)
By C. E. COULING
Of all the secret sects of China, so mysterious, so
baffling, so tragic in their fortunes, so persistent in their
growth, perhaps the most interesting to the West is the
Chin Tan Chiao (sometimes also called the Ssŭ Ch'uan
Chiao) because of its supposed, or suspected connection
with that Nestorian Christianity which came overland
to this country in the T'ang dynasty.
The name Chin Tan Chiao has been most unfor-
tunately translated "The Golden Pill Sect"; unfortunately,
if for no other reason, because it gives a touch of the
ludicrous to what has been the cherished religion of
millions of earnest people through the ages. The name
is not easy to translate worthily; the middle character
tan might have a dissertation to itself; but we may
suggest "The Cult of the Precious Elixir" as somewhat
less inadequate, as well as more dignified. We must
premise, however, that etymologically, the word elixir has
no special reference to a liquid-that is an adaptation of
the druggists. El iksir is any preparation designed to
change baser metals into gold and/or to prolong life
indefinitely.
How long the Chinese have been cultivating the
occult art is unknown. Some think the West got it from
China, and some, that China got it from the West.
Perhaps the pursuit of the transmutation of metals and
the quest for a means of abolishing death are not equally
ancient, though we commonly put them together. One
would think that men must have dreaded dissolution long
ages before they desired gold. As far as the Chin Tan
Chiao is concerned, at any rate, the occult path to riches
is not sought; it is mentioned only to be rejected as a
proof of covetousness and a prostitution of the divine
secret. What seems to be desired is the sublimation of
the physical body into a worthy dwelling for a purified
soul.

The search for immortality has been going on in
China certainly since the time of Ch'in Shih Huang Ti¹,
and probably long before that. Indeed, the Taoists claim
that the Yellow Emperor attained to perpetual life by
means of the divine elixir, and that he owed this, not to
his own discoveries, but to a supernatural being called
Kuang Ch'êng Tzŭ, reputed to be an earlier incarnation
of Lao Tzŭ himself. Compared with all that, the Chin
Tan Chiao is almost modern. It is specially devoted to
the person and teachings of a Doctor of Literature of the
T'ang dynasty, the Patriarch Lü, or in Chinese, 呂祖 ​Lü
Tsu.
Lü Tsu is regarded as the third of the Eight Great
Immortals of Taoism, and as such is worshipped by all
Taoists, and not merely by the Chin Tan Chiao. His
cult is very widespread; his temples may be found all
over North and Mid-China, at any rate, and private
shrines are also numerous. He has the name in religion
of "Ch'un Yang Tzŭ”(純陽​子​),“The Perfect One",
in whom is no darkness at all; he is the Great Healer
of the sick, the giver of oracles par excellence, and
he is credited with having more love for men and
more willingness to serve them than any Immortal
of them all. For some obscure reason, he is the
patron saint of barbers; and the late Mrs. Timothy
Richard put forward the interesting suggestion that
possibly in one of the many persecutions the sect has
suffered, its members were degraded to this class which,
under the old régime, was one of four whose descendants
were excluded from the Government examinations for
three or four generations.
Whether Lü Tsu really founded the Chin Tan Chiao
is uncertain; no account, as far as we are aware, of any
such formal founding is given by anyone. On the whole
it would seem more likely that he is the foundation of
the religion rather than its founder; or it is even possible
that a cult already in existence adopted him as its patron
saint. In any case, true to Chinese custom, the sect
does not call itself by his honoured name.
To understand either him or the cult of him, it first
seems advisable to note certain ideas and beliefs taken
over by him and his followers from their environment
and their past.

First of all, taking our place at their viewpoint, we
naturally find ourselves in a geocentric universe; and
just as the earth is the centre of all things, so China is
the centre of the earth; it might even be said that China
is the earth, except for the recognition that Buddhism
came from abroad.
In this geocentric Cosmos, there is no hint of a per-
sonal God, as we understand the term; though it is
spiritual through and through, personality is a sleeping
fire in the bosom of the Infinite, the Divine coming
to self-consciousness in MAN. We discuss sometimes
whether the soul is immortal or not, but here it is
accepted that the soul is a spark of the Divine and as
eternal as that from which it came, existing from
everlasting to everlasting. To attain immortality is
indeed one of the two great aims of the Chin Tan Chiao,
but it does not mean to prevent the extinction of the
soul; rather to deliver it from transmigration, from
the weary round of births and deaths, and to ferry it,
once for all, across the Sea of Sorrows we call earthly
life, to the farther shore.

There are nine heavens ruled by a Trinity known
as the Three Pure Ones. One of these, Yü Huang
(玉皇​) is a deified member of the Taoist Popes' family
rejoicing in the name of Chang, and now has charge
of all human affairs, also controlling the physical world.
There do not seem to be any angels in these heavens;
the inhabitants are (as Christians would say) all saved
sinners. Though they have escaped from the wheel of
transmigration, their chief delight is not in the joys
of Paradise, but in the salvation of men. They are able
to assume any shape at will, and are busily engaged
in works of mercy. There is a kind of Jacob's ladder,
one might say, in constant use.
There are nine hells to match the nine heavens,
another idea taken from Buddhism; but whereas the
Buddhist 地獄 ​are places of purgatorial purification,
Taoism believes in the possibility of eternal ruin. In
the Chin Tan Chiao books, this generally takes the form
of an irrevocable descent to an animal's body. Thus
Lü Tsu in one place says:
"The path to Heaven has but few travellers;
"At the door of Brutedom men fight to get in."

His sect is eclectic. In addition to taking from
Buddhism its doctrine of metempsychosis, and its
heavens and "hells", the members of the Chiao Tan
Chiao whole-heartedly accept Confucian ethics as their
moral standard;忠孝​, loyalty and filial piety are their
twin virtues, and in their liturgy they do homage to
the good and great of their land who have surpassed
in these.
But the noblest ideas and precepts embodied in
their books, come direct from the higher Taoism of
Lao Tzŭ and Chuang Tzŭ. The sublimity of the Infinite
and Eternal Source of all things, unfathomed, and un-
fathomable, yet to whom the dying go as to a home; the
matchless worth of the human soul; its wondrous destiny
in the Life Beyond; the divine beauty of meekness, of
silence, of patient endurance; the sufficiency of the hid-
den life to satisfy man's deepest longings-all these
things come to us here in Taoist guise, and are at least a
partial answer to the oft-asked question, "Where can the
Higher Taoism be found to-day?"
The occult beliefs and practices from which the name
Chin Tan Chiao comes are also wholly Taoistic, but
belong to that later decadent Taoism which began,
perhaps, after Ch'in Shih Huang Ti burned the Confucian
books, and of which the advent of Buddhism hastened
the corruption. These occult beliefs and practices, though
new in Taoism, were not new in China. Some, at least,
were derived from superstitions and theories which long
antedated Confucius and Lao Tzŭ. The Book of Changes
(易經​) may almost be regarded as the foundation of the
whole; but now and then we seem to get a glimpse of
something older still, of prehistoric beliefs and guesses
which arose when men first began to think things out,
with no equipment but their own five wits.
Such is some of the material with which the Chin
Tan Chiao was builded; and without at least so much
explanation, one could hardly hope to do justice to its
reputed founder.
There are five accounts of the Patriarch Lü's life to
which we have access. All are more or less embroidered
with legend, and all naturally begin with his birth in the
T'ang dynasty. But in one of the Chin Tan Chiao Scrip-
tures we are let into the secret of what had gone before.
The first scene of the drama, like that of the Book of Job,
and of "Faust" and of the story of the Goddess of Mercy,
is laid in the unseen world.

Finally, in the reign of Sung Hui Tsung, at some time
in the period 1110-17, it was decreed that temples to Lü
Tsu should be erected all over the Empire. The reason
was this:-goblins had made their appearance in the
secluded parts of the palace, and in the very daytime
would steal gold and other treasures, and even certain
concubines were spirited off by them. The Palace eunuchs

more than give temporary relief, and His Majesty was
reduced to praying and fasting in earnest. In the event,

and declares he has come to put down the nuisance. He
calls in a military spirit as executioner, and after all is

This paper may fitly close, perhaps, with some brief
remarks on the surmised connection of the Chin Tan
Chiao with Nestorianism, to which reference was made
in our opening words.
The theory is that this Assyrian Christianity, when
suppressed by the Chinese authorities, "dug itself in" as
a secret sect, and that in spite of the accretions of the
centuries, a kernel of Christian truth has been preserved
in it and handed down to the present day.
These ideas arose, as far as we are aware, with the
late Dr. Timothy Richard, and were developed further by
Professor Saeki in his book, The Nestorian Monument
in China.
Dr. Richard had a way of his own as a Christian
missionary. It was his rule to seek out the worthy, i.e.,
those who were recognized by their neighbours as such.
This brought him into touch with the leaders of various
secret sects, of whom he bore witness that they represent
"the spiritual cream of the land". And the Chin Tan
Chiao leaders in Shansi and Shantung with whom as well as
with a number of their followers, he became acquainted,
told him (he reported) that their religion was the same
as his own.
Probably during the reading of this paper certain
similarities have been noticed between the two creeds,
and, the reader can understand the claim these leaders
made. Immense differences exist, it is true; but (as they
said) they too worship one whom they claim to be not a
mere man-in their case, a Doctor of Literature of T'ang
times-but a radiant preëxistent Being, who left his
heavenly home to take flesh and blood for the sake of
mankind, and still lives to answer prayer and give help to
them. Furthermore, with these Chin Tan Chiao leaders'
beliefs, it was easy for them to regard Christ as an earl-
ier incarnation vouchsafed to the West of the great
Immortal who came as Lü Tsu to the East; or, alterna-
tively, as an incarnation of some other high Immortal.
Such, indeed, is their view of the Goddess of Mercy, whose
story is one of their favourite books.
To Dr. Richard's conjectures, Professor Saeki added
what he considered convincing proofs.

was well known in Ch'ang An, the capital city where he
must have taken his Doctor's degree, and where, as we
have seen, he first met his great teacher, Chung-li Ch'üan.

Professor Saeki, therefore, proceeds to identify him
with our Lü Tsu. But, even if they were one and the
same, the writer of the Tablet is not thereby proven to be
a Christian; still less can we assume that he taught
Christianity secretly after it was proscribed.
As a matter of fact, we do not know for certain when
Nestorianism was suppressed, nor, as was said before,
whether the Patriarch Lü founded anything at all.
The chief positive obstacle to Professor Saeki's theory
is presented by the different dates given for the Patri-
arch's birth. Giles gives A.D. 750, we have not yet learnt
on what authority; the "Collected Works of Lü Tsu," the
memoir given in which may be considered the Chin Tan
Chiao's official account, gives 799, the commentary mean-
while mentioning several other dates which are given in
various records, covering in all a period of more than a
hundred years.
If the Chin Tan Chiao is right, then 799 was the date;
but this is eighteen years later than the erection of the
Nestorian Monument, which was set up in 781, and the
whole argument from similarity of names goes by the
board.
After this, the lesser arguments put forth by Saeki,
do not carry much weight. Such things as Lü Tsu turn-
ing water into wine, healing the paralytic and blind, and
reviving dead fish, hardly seem worth mentioning, nor are
they very relevant in any case.
Personally, the writer finds in the Chin Tan Chiao
books studied hitherto nothing which is not in one or
other of the Three Religions the cult professes to unite,
viz. Confucianism, Taoism and Buddhism, with one pos-
sible exception, which occurs in the liturgy and which we
will now mention.

the Central Asian Christians, i.e., Nestorians, for there
were no other Christians there.
In conclusion, the best authorities known to the
writer do not admit the claim that the Chin Tan Chiao
derives in any way from Christianity. By the best
authorities we mean, in France, Professor Pelliot of
Tun-huang fame, and, in England, the Rev. A.C. Moule of
Cambridge, who is the greatest British expert on early
Christian missions in China. Whatever fresh light may
be thrown on the subject when and if the archaeologist's
spade gets busy at Hsi An Foo, at present the safest
verdict that can be given is the cautious Scottish one,
not proven.
