STORIES IN CHINESE PAINTINGS, II
By JOHN C. FERGUSON
In my book Chinese Painting I suggested in the
introductory chapter that in judging pictures a know-
ledge of the spirit of people as expressed in their historical
development, literary production and poetic conceptions is
helpful in correcting the vagaries of personal taste and
peculiarity. Such knowledge is not needed to enable one
to appreciate the flow of line, the spread of colour or the
composition of a Chinese painting. These characteristics
speak for themselves in all countries and at all time.
Chinese paintings in these respects have no standards
different from those of other countries but in the realm
of the subjects chosen by Chinese artists we find everything
unfamiliar until these are explained. In the Journal of
this Society, Vol. LVI, 1925, p. 110, I gave brief explan-
ations of thirty-nine subjects of well-known paintings and
arranged them according to their historical sequence. In
this paper I have followed the method of grouping paint-
ings together according to their common subject or, in the
case of Li Kung-lin, the paintings of one artist.
THE CITIZENS OF YAO'S EMPIRE SINGING
堯​民​擊​壤​圖
​By Han Kuang (), T'ang dynasty,
and Li Sung (#), Sung dynasty.
Yao is regarded as the ideal ruler of ancient times.
It is said that when after years of administration he
wanted to find out what his people thought of his govern-
ment he went out in private clothes and wandered about
the country. He met with some farmers who were enjoy-
ing themselves after their hard day's work. They sang
and danced. They had no musical instruments but they
regulated their singing and dancing by beating against the
ground. Their song was:

SUN WU DRILLING AN ARMY OF PALACE LADIES
教​習​女兵 ​圖
​By Ch'iu Ying (t), Ming dynasty.
Sun Wu (it) lived during the time of the Warring
States and became a great expert in military tactics. He
served as commander of the army of the State of Wu under
Prince Ho-lu () and through his military success
the State of Wu became powerful. When he was first
introduced to the prince he talked freely on military
tactics and Ho-lu was greatly pleased. Ho-lu then asked
if what he said was practicable for ordinary soldiers.
Sun Wu replied that it was not only practicable for
soldiers but for any one, including women. The prince
was amazed at this statement and called out his palace
ladies to try an experiment. Sun Wu took charge of them,
dividing them into two companies and appointed two of
the most beautiful, who were most loved by the prince, as
captains. He proclaimed martial law which meant that
anyone who dared to disobey the orders of the commander
would be beheaded. He then instructed them how to
march forward and backward, right and left. This was
simple and every one understood, but when he gave his
first order the ladies began to laugh. He called out a
second time and then a third time but they continued to
laugh. He then became very angry and had the two
captains beheaded. The prince, who was sitting on a
tower, tried to save them, but in vain. Su Wu then
had new captains appointed and nobody dared again to
disobey him. In a short time the ladies were marching
like well-trained soldiers.

SU WU (A)
Su Wu was an official under the Emperor Wu Ti of
the Han dynasty. He lived at a time when the Hsiung-
nu were a source of great trouble to the empire. He was
sent to the Hsiung-nu to carry on friendly negotiations
but was detained by the Khan who wanted him to throw
off his allegience and enter his service. When he refused,
he was thrown into a dungeon and was left there without
food for several days. He kept himself alive by sucking
snow and gnawing a felt rug; and at length the Hsiung-
nu, thinking that he was a supernatural being, sent him
away north and set him to tend sheep. In the meantime
one of his friends named Li Ling (), a military
official, surrendered to the Hsiung-nu after a bad defeat
and was given a high post. They were good friends and
were all the more intimate when they met again in a
foreign land. At length, peace negotiations between the
Hsiung-nu and the Han were concluded and Su Wu was
permitted to return to China. It was with great distress
that they departed. Two paintings of which Su Wu was
the subject are:

CHUNG K’UEI ( từ)
According to the Tien Chung Chi (2) Chung
K'uei was a spirit capable of devouring demons. During
the T'ang dynasty when the Emperor Ming Huang was
once suffering attacks of intermittent fever he saw in a
dream a large spirit with a blue coat and a broken hat
catching small demons and devouring them. The spirit
said that his name was Chung K'uei and that he was a
native of Chung-nan (). He had attained the rank
of Chin-shih, but, failing in an examination, he had killed
himself by knocking his head against a flight of stone
steps. When the emperor awoke from his dream he
found that his fever had all gone. Upon this the artist
Wu Tao-tsu was ordered to make a portrait of the spirit
and this is the origin of the legendary figure Chung K'uei.
His portrait was hung up in doorways on New Year's
Day to keep off wicked spirits during the T'ang, Sung,
Yüan and Ming dynasties, but now it is used on the
Dragon Boat Festival in some parts of the country. Just
when this custom arose it is not known. Chung K'uei
has been a constant subject for paintings and in every
case he is depicted as being followed with demons. The
following are examples of such paintings:

REFUSING TO SHELTER A WOMAN IN A RAINSTORM
魯​男子​閉​戶​圖
​By Wang Mêng (*), Yian dynasty.
According to the Family Records of Confucius
(F) there was once a man of the State of Lu,
who lived in a house near to one occupied by a widow.
One night there was a great rainstorm in the midst of
which the house of the widow collapsed. The woman
went to the man's house and begged for shelter. He
refused and would not open the door saying that according
to the rites it was not proper for a man and a woman
under 60 years of age to stay in a room together. The
woman contended that it was quite proper and cited the
example of Liu Hsia-hui (T) who once held a lady
in his lap without suffering the slightest imputation on
his moral character. The man replied that this might
have been possible with Liu Hsia-hui but not with him.
The woman was kept outside in the storm.
