THE BURIAL-PLACE OF GENGHIS KHAN
By E. T. C. WERNER
"They made neither grave-mounds nor planted trees.
When the burial was over, the place was trodden level by
ten thousand horses, so that no one could tell the location
thereof."
This custom of the Mongols, adopted with the object
of ensuring safety for the bodies of their dead emperors,
resulted in the sites of these graves being unknown to
any but the relatives or other initiated persons. An
example of one means of how this knowledge was
prevented from reaching outsiders, is found in the case
of Genghis Khan. In order that it might not be known
to what place his body was being taken for burial, no less
than thirty-six thousand people who happened to meet
the funeral cortГЁge on its journey, are said to have been
instantly put to death.
Where was this burial-place? Over this question
great controversies have raged, both among Eastern and
Western scholars. The two principal theories were: (1)
the site is in the country of the Ortous Mongols; and (2)
near the Kerulen River. (The supporters of the latter
theory did not assert burial in the bed of the river,
though the method of burial in the bed of a temporarily-
diverted river was also adopted in some cases).
Having stated the principal point at issue, it is
unnecessary here to go into details of these controversies.
A translation made by me from the Chinese of a lengthy
polemic on this subject was published in the Chinese
Political and Social Science Review for December, 1918.
I also contributed an article thereanent to the New China
Review for October, 1919. The verdict on these contro-
versies up to that time certainly seemed to be in favour
of the Ortous site. But there had appeared in the
Persian La Shih-t'ГӘ's Complete History of the Mongols
and in P'ГӘng Ta-ya's Record of the Black Tartars argu-

There is no monument on the spot. The site is
occupied by two large white felt Mongol tents of circular
shape adjoining each other. In the first are some tables
and chairs, two swords used by Genghis, and implements
of worship, which are taken out for use at the annual
sacrifices. The latter, known as the ta chiеӨ§вҖӢзҘӯвҖӢ, principal
sacrifices, take place on the 21st day of the 3rd moon (not
the 6th moon, as stated by Potanin, though the time of
year may formerly have been different; Golovkin mentions
the 7th day of the 7th moon, but his account refers to the
alleged site of the tomb on the hill in the vicinity of the
Kerulen River). On this occasion every Mongol ch'i ж——вҖӢ,
Banner, sends its representative to assist at the worship,
which lasts for fifteen days. They bring with them
sacred remains of Genghis, usually preserved at their
various localities in the Ortous country. These relics,
ten in number, are specified by Cordier in Yule's
Marco Polo, i. 250. The only one which is not brought
is Genghis's great battle-standard, an enormous black
wooden lance with diamond-shaped iron point (which
never rusts), planted in the desert about sixty li south of
the burial-place.
In the second tent is a very large engraved chest, or
coffin, made of red copper, ornamented with gold facings.
Inside this is another of similar make, and inside this
again is another, made of silver. The latter, which has
not been opened within the knowledge of anyone now
living, probably not since the remains were placed in it,
is said to contain two sable coats, one enclosing the bones
of Genghis Khan, and the other those of his wife.
Thus, it would seem, Genghis was not buried at all,
but at his resting-place under the great white Mongol
tent at E-chin-ho-lao (EtjГӘn Koro), sleeps his long sleep
in his silver coffin.
This is what lawyers call "best evidence," and
agrees generally with the upshot of the controversies
mentioned at the beginning of this paper. Thus do we
"settle the doubts of a thousand years" (strictly speaking,
some hundreds less), and it would therefore now seem
possible to write amen to this long and interesting, if at
times somewhat acrimonious controversy.

The five hundred T'ГӘ-ГӘrh-han(зү№зҲҫзҪ•вҖӢ) families(жңҲвҖӢ),
the official guardians of the tombs, live in the vicinity of

of its own accord on the day of the annual sacrifices, is
not altogether incredible. It is said that a young camel
used to be killed, in the presence of its mother, on the
grave of the deceased khan. "Afterwards, when the
time of the usual offerings of the tomb approached, the
mother of this immolated camel was set at liberty and she
came crying to the place where it was killed; the locality
of the tomb was ascertained in this way." (Ts'ao-mu-tzЕҜ,
quot. in Yule, Marco Polo, i. 248). White is the Mongol
"colour of respect." Genghis had eight white horses of
which he was extremely fond. Therefore (if I may
hazard such a suggestion in the face of Weissmannism-
now wisely more and more discredited in the scientific
world) we may here have an instance of the inheritance
of acquired characteristics arising from habit plus
motherly instinct! But how the horse (unless a foal was
killed in the same manner) learnt the habit from the
camel, whether by imitation or instinct or in some other
way, is not clear!
The custom of causing the site to be trampled level
by thousands of horses was, so the prince says, not
observed in the case of Genghis Khan, but only in those of
the other Mongol emperors. This would naturally be
omitted if the body was not buried.
The Japanese claim that Genghis Khan was their
Yoshitsune, and that the Tartars near Nikolaievesk wear
the ancient Japanese armour with the Genji crest, is
laughed at as pure fiction. It may have arisen from a
general similarity of the armour, and of the pattern on
Genghis's robes to be seen in Japanese decoration.
The Russian tablet engraved with historical details is
also discredited by the prince.
The portraits of Genghis shown by Buddhist priests,
Russians, Japanese, and others, not having been taken
from any authentic original, are all incorrect in one
respect or another. The true likeness is seen in the
portrait preserved in the old Imperial Palace at Peking
and in photographs shown me by the prince of paintings
made of Genghis Khan during his lifetime. One of these
is reproduced here.

The body was probably cremated. At that time the
Mongols were not settled, and this method would enable
them to convey the remains of their great chief with
them without detection, and so keep them in safety from
their enemies. This would account for Genghis not being
buried at or near the sacred mountain of the Mongols.
The Lama Mongols all use the process of cremation
to dispose of their dead; of the rest, some do, and some do
not. The cremation of Genghis would of course explain
the coffin being preserved above ground at its present
