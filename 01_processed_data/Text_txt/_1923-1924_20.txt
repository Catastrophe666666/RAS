WHO ARE THE NORTHERN CHINESE?
By S. M. SHIROKOGOROFF.
The anthropological investigations (I will use the
terms anthropology, anthropological and so on in the
sense of physical anthropology only) of different peoples
of the Earth are so numerous that for some parts of the
world more or less detailed descriptions and generaliza-
tions can be made. The population of Europe, that of
the parts of Asia controlled by France and Russia, also
that of Africa and partly of America, has been measured
in such large numbers that they may warrant some final
deductions. But Northern China has not been so lucky
and before my measurements of the Chinese the anthro-
pology of Northern China was just a project. The
measurements carried on by the expedition of d'Ollon in
Southern and Western China, also those of Prof. Maloff,
of the Kazan University, in Western China, deal unfor-
tunately only with the non-Chinese populations of these
regions. I want to note here the very important studies
made by the Christian Missions, Rockfeller's Institution
and others, but these investigations deal mostly with the
anatomical and physiological problems and are not
systematized for anthropological purposes. Lately Dr.
Davidson Black and Dr. Stevenson of Peking Union
Medical College started very valuable measurements.
Also, Dr. Chi Li expects to begin the measurements in
Northern and Central China.
The measurements of the Northern Chinese and
other ethnical groups in neighbouring regions that I
made from 1912 to 1918 made up the first statistical
material which could be and was worked out by statistical
methods. This society helped me to carry out the
publication of this material with the deductions that I
have set forth in my study entitled-"Anthropology of
Northern China". Last summer I made some very
interesting measurements in the Municipal Gaol. Also
Prof. Kulp, whose help I enlisted last spring and summer,
made measurements of the Chinese from the Chekiang
Province and Swatow region.

From the above it can be seen that the anthropo-
logical investigation of the Chinese has been started and
it is to be hoped that it will be carried on.
Anthropology as a science has its roots in the scien-
tific movement that preceded the creation of Darwin's
theory of evolution. Its beginnings were as hard as those
of all sciences and it bears several traces of the struggle
of scientific with political ideas. The development of this
science was especially successful during the American
Civil War when were created the theories of monogenetic
and polygenetic origin of man and different races with
the view to support either Northern or Southern Parties.
Since then the problem of the origin of races has been
more or less solved by the archaeological discoveries made
in Europe, Africa, Asia and America. These discoveries
prove that,-first, the roots of man and of different races
are lost in the darkness of prehistoric times as far back
as some tens of thousands of years. Secondly, that the
culture of man for some 200 thousand years as well as
the culture of the period immediately preceding the
beginning of our civilization does not show very striking
variations. Thirdly, that the races alternated already
in very ancient times and that during the stone age there
were already formed several anthropological types, so
that the question of monogenetism and polygenetism lost
its former importance. Anthropological investigations
that were made in all parts of the world and the
collecting of new data concerning the origin of man and
other animal species furnish us with a deduction not
less important, that is,-that man originated from some
animal species and later was differentiated into races.
Of course, it is very difficult to say where we can find
the traces of "first man", but this question seems to be
now rather a scholastic problem. In fact, one of the
predecessors of the present man, namely so called
Neanderthal Man, who by his "human" knowledge more
or less assured his existence among the most dangerous
animals, had already his culture of stone implements,
but he was so different from the present man that if we
should try to let him go on the streets of Shanghai, he
would be treated as an extraordinary animal, and on the
other hand he would consider us as not less dangerous
animals for him than were the bear of the cavern, the
tiger of the cavern and others. It is clear that under
such conditions he would not leave any posterity. By
this example I want to show that the present man and

During the following period of anthropology there
were created several theories which were very useful for
classification of the living "races", but this classification
with the progress of science and spreading of investiga-
tions among unknown peoples led us to create more and
more new anthropological units so that finally some
anthropologists refused even to use the term "race" as it
had lost its original meaning. In this line of destruction
of old anthropological theories lie also the results of my
investigations.
One of the most stable theories was that of the
"Mongol Race". This "race" was characterized by a
moderate stature (average about 162), a round head
(with cephalic index about 85), short legs, very close
eye-lids-the "Mongolian Eye"-black straight hair and
yellow or brownish skin. Such a race was created by
the anthropologists and adopted by the public opinion
because the observations that were furnished by inci-
dental travellers were not scientifically gathered and were
more or less superficial. However the searching spirit of
the scientist could not be satisfied with such sources of
knowledge and it wanted some exact data, some "ciphers".
The expeditions and different researches operating during
the last period shocked this theory. My investigations
added new data.
Having begun my investigations in Siberia and
having studied the material collected previously by other
anthropologists I formulated in my first anthropological
study the deduction that the "Mongol Race" is not at all
a race but an agglomerate and amalgamation of different
anthropological types. After that I had the opportunity
to measure some Chinese workmen in Siberia and they
were so distinct from the so-called "Mongols" that I
stopped my observations, supposing the subjects mea-
sured to be a selected material. I continued later my
investigations in Manchuria and China and I saw that
my hesitation was not right. In fact the physical

The Chinese of Northern China of my study are
composed of three groups,-those of Chihli, those of
Shantung and those of the three provinces of Manchuria.
These groups can be considered as forming two main
groups, that of China Proper and that of Manchuria.
The average stature of these two main groups differs
about 3 centimeters,-163 and 165; the average cephalic
index for these two groups gives the difference of
about 5 units; other characteristics are also in positive
correlation with the stature and cephalic index. The
nasal index and frontal index that correlate anatomically
neither stature nor cephalic index, show also very
striking differences of these two main groups, so that
comparatively the Chinese of China Proper can be
characterized as a people taller, with more elongated
head, open forehead and broad nose; and the Chinese of
Manchuria are shorter, with round heads, small forehead
and narrower nose. The cause of these differences is
intrusion of some foreign blood into that of the Chinese of
Manchuria,-immigrants from China. These foreigners
are evidently natives of Manchuria, that is the Manchus
and, maybe, Koreans. The anthropological data concern-
ing these groups confirm absolutely this supposition.
In fact, the Manchus are shorter than the Chinese of
China Proper, they have rounder head, narrower nose
and small forehead. Thus the differences between two
main groups of the Chinese is due to foreign influence.
But the problem is not so simple. The Chinese of China

There arise now several questions which I will
try to answer. Who are the Chinese, if they are so
amalgamated? How can be explained the presence
among the Chinese of type П, which was supposed to
be characteristic of the Tungus? How can be explained
the presence of types B and A?
-
On my first question, who are the Chinese, I
answer that the Chinese are not a race, a physical race,
but they are a nation, some time ago united into a state;
using the same written, not spoken, language; composed
of different ethnical units speaking different dialects,
some of which are not even apparented with the Chinese.
All these ethnical groups composing now the Chinese are
also of different culture and, may be, the Chinese civiliza-
tion is not so uniform as it seems to be. The closeness
of the culture of the peoples and especially the common
method of writing, also the closeness of political interests
and physical amalgamation of these peoples in comparison
with the Europeans, give us an impression of the Chinese
uniformity. The presence among the Chinese of the

This process seems to me to run as follows.
"The original place of the Chinese up to the present
time is not yet known. Different theories unsuccessfully
tried to resolve this problem by supposing the non-asiatic
origin of the Chinese, or by supposing an organic develop-
ment and differentiation of various "tribes", "races" and
"clans" in the present territory of China. Of course,
because of the lack of archaeological and anthropological
evidence all these theories are more or less admissible
"working hypotheses." However, we must consider as a
fact the Chinese radiation eastward, northward and
southward, that was marked by historical data. Hence
it might be concluded that the Chinese original place,
"the Chinese motherland", must lie somewhere west of the
present Central China. Then taking into consideration
the former relations of the Chinese with the population of
Western Asia, and also that the population of Turkestan
at that time was speaking Indo-Iranian dialects, it may
be supposed that the Chinese were in very close contact
with other centres of civilization of that time and that
these relations were broken off only by the invasion of
Turkestan and generally territories lying on the west of
China by the Turko-Mongolic ethnical groups. Besides
the direct relations between the Chinese and Western
centres, indirect ones were possible through the Altai
metallurgic centre, whence two great ways led to Western
Asia and China. Also it might be agreed that the Chinese
appeared as an active ethnical unit in Asia at time of the
Stone Age and they were more advanced from a cultural
standpoint, because, according to the historical data, they
were living since the earliest time by hunting, fishing,
and agriculture. The development of this culture was
followed by the natural increase of population and drove
the Chinese in the search of new territories outside their
former area lying somewhere on the west of the present
Central China. In this movement, I have supposed, they
were opposed by the Mongol and Turkic ethnical groups.
Then they went eastward assimilating and amalgamating
the local population. These were the Tungus, who partly
accepted the conquerors and remained in the territory,
partly pushed back the palaeoasiatic tribes and passed
throughout Manchuria to reach the semi-empty regions
of Siberia. Thus the Chinese successfully amalgamated
the type and reached the Shantung Peninsula and

The anthropological evidence concerning the pro-
pagation of the type, that was connected with the
Tungus, can be supported by the ethnography and history
of the Tungus.
"The sole possible region of habitation for the
Tungus was Northern China, because the Turko-Mongols
since early time occupied the Mongolian Plateau; the basin
of the Amur River was populated by the palaeoasiatic
tribes; and Western China was occupied by the Chinese
and other tribes, traces of which can be observed now in
Western and Southern China. At that time when the
Chinese were in the west, the Tungus were living in the
territory of Eastern China, i.e. somewhere in the basin of
the Yellow and Yangtse Rivers. Then the Chinese invasion
into this region pushed back the Tungus and they went
eastward and northward into Amurland, viz., the present
Manchuria, also Transbaikalia, Amur Govt. and Maritime
Govt. of Siberia. On the banks of the Amur River they
met the ancient population composed of the palaeoasiatic
tribes who lived there since prehistoric time. This
population was comparatively dense and was probably
divided into several groups living by hunting in the forest
region and fishing on the banks of the Amur River.
Also in the forest region they perhaps domesticated
reindeer. The Tungus could not expel these well organized
aborigines and went through this region into Siberia.
During this migration the Tungus appropriated some
cultural characteristics of the palaeoasiatic groups, but
conserved their tongue, clothing, social organization and
so on. In fact the Tungus dialects of Siberia, Mongolia
and Manchuria are very developed in the direction of the
agglutination of several suffixes, also the grammar of
those dialects is very complicated. Meanwhile, the
Southern dialects, as for example the Manchu, from this
standpoint are more simplified. The appropriation of
new vocabulary, and sometimes of grammatical forms
and phonetics, show their relatively later origin. Though
the Yakuts, Mongols and different palaeoasiatic tribes
influenced, of course, the Tungus dialects, their tongue
remains always intact. In comparison with the dialects
of Southern Tungus (the Manchu, the Goldi dialect and
others) the Northern dialects are pure, or more indepen-
dent and developed, but these seem to lose or, may be,
to stop the development of the peculiar Tungus characters
of this linguistical group. However, the Northern and
Southern Tungus recognize the common origin of their

aboriginal type which in some early period occupied not
only the valley of the Amur River but also present Korea,
probably the sea coast, and so on. With the course of

skin must be yellow, and hair-black. My observations

who do not expose their bodies and faces to the influence

But can we assure that the populations of Southern
Europe such as the Spanish, Italians and Greeks are
whiter? I have already noted that the Tungus and some-

lighter coloured than the Chinese, though they were.
decidely included in the "Yellow Race."
As regards the hair colour, it is not so variable among
the Chinese, and black hair is more characteristic of them.
But there are many Chinese, and especially among the
children, who have dark and light brown hair. The
Tungus include a large number of individuals with
characteristically light hair and grey eyes. I conclude,
-the yellow skin and straight black hair are not at all
characteristic of all the groups which now compose the
Chinese.
I want to add some words about the "Mongolian
Eye." It is not so common among the Chinese, but it is
absolutely characteristic of the Tungus and other groups
living in the region where the snow is abundant during
winter and spring, when the sunlight is so intense that
the inhabitants of those regions are accustomed to put
on their eyes a kind of protector with very narrow
space to look through. But as shown, other "Mongol"
characteristics are not peculiar to the Tungus.

in particular who are the Chinese? In my preceding
exposition I have already shown that they are amalgama-
tions of different types which can be connected with some
ethnical and national groups only after a minute analysis,
so that they have now lost their former importance.
The physical characteristics thus have no actual impor-
tance but they are for us only a method which help us to
discover the components of these amalgamated groups.
Therefore we must allow to Anthropology a place among
historic sciences like that occupied, for example, by

The
I think that the characteristics of the Chinese consist
principally in those elements created by the Chinese
psychology and may be physiology which cannot be
registered with anthropometrical instruments.
Chinese culture, or speaking more exactly, complex of
knowledge, their method of thinking and acting, are
absolutely different from that of other peoples. Their
art, literature and especially peculiar writing, have no
parallel among other living peoples except those which
are bound up with the Chinese civilization. Their technical
culture-houses, food, clothing-are not less different
from those of other peoples. All these elements result
in the peculiar features of the Chinese, the expression
of the face, formation of muscles, and so on. Thus the
problem of the differentiation of the Chinese from other
peoples must be brought to the field of the psychic and,
may be, physiological functions. But this does not
simplify the problem of the racial and ethnical differen-
tiation of the Chinese, and if I assert that the Chinese
are not of the "Mongol Race," because this does not exist,
I daresay that the Chinese exist as a powerful ethnical
group, which has absorbed many peoples of Asia,
subjugated them to its cultural power, and finally stands
face to face with the other peoples of Earth.
The discovery of the real elements composing the
Chinese complex is the next problem to be solved. In
order to facilitate the solution of this very complicated
problem we must not repeat our preceding error in
trying to treat the Chinese as a united group of the
"Mongol Race." It seems to me that as the Chinese do
not show uniformity in their physical features, so they
will not show uniformity in their psychic and, may be,
physiological features, and after more detailed investiga-
tions they will appear as an amalgamation of different
elements. This study, I suppose, must be made parallely
