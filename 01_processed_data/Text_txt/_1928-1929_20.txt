DID ANCIENT CHINESE CULTURE
COME FROM EGYPT?
By HERBERT CHATLEY, D.SC. (Lond.)
[Note: This is an abstract of a lecture given on the
18th October, 1925.]
In recent years a good deal has been heard of a
"diffusion" theory of culture. Dr. Elliott Smith is its
principal protagonist but many anthropologists have
accepted it as a good working hypothesis. Briefly stated,
the theory is that human culture is a unique development
that could and did arise at only one centre and has spread
gradually from that centre. Egypt is alleged to have been
that centre.
On this hypothesis all ancient cultures, necessarily
including that of China, originated from Egypt and may
be traced to that country.
This is by no means the first time that an association
has been asserted between ancient Egypt and ancient
China. Many years ago, some writers¹ held that there
was a definite connection (shown chiefly in the hierogly-
phics) and a few seasons back Mr. C. Kiene indicated a
number of interesting mythological parallels.
It is moreover true that a number of resemblances
do exist between the Chinese and the Egyptians:-
(a) Both peoples are black-haired and are said to
have called themselves so.
(b) The ancient culture includes in both cases an
extraordinarily elaborate development of the worship of
death.

(c) Both used ideographs and some of the ideographs
are identical in the two writings.
(d) Both are expert in irrigation.
This is just the sort of evidence that causes a case
hastily to be declared proved. It is, however, practically
valueless. In regard to (a) Asia has many black-haired
people; while the anatomical differences between the
Chinese and the Egyptians are considerable. It is more-
over not proved that the Egyptians commonly spoke of
themselves as "black-haired," and even if so, it might be
a coincidence.
As to (b) all ancient cultures (and a good many
modern ones) attached great importance to the worship
of death, and the resemblances do not extend to practical
detail.
As to (c) only very simple and obvious ideographs
are alike in the two writings, while the languages and
systems of writing are quite different.
As to (d) this was in both countries a matter of
necessity and is more likely to have been a common cause
for stimulating culture rather than the result of a common
culture.
Something has also been made of the rule of priest
kings in both countries, but this is so universal a feature
of ancient civilization that no importance need be attached
to it as a special evidence of connection.
From a contrary point of view, the evidence against
such a connection is very strong. The geographical
separation of the two countries is very considerable and
in addition to this is the fact that other old cultures occur
in the intervening regions which should show some signs
of the transmission if it had occurred.
Chronologically there are great difficulties. Egyptian
culture reached its first well known maximum in the fourth
or "Pyramid" dynasty, between 4000 and 3000 B.C. The
hieroglyphics were already stereotyped at this time and
the use of alphabetical symbols to phoneticise ideographs
was well established. In China, in 2000 B.C. to 1000 B.C.
(i.e., about 2,000 years later), the ideographs on the Yin
oracle bones were still in a state of flux, almost wholly
pictorial and devoid of all alphabetical character.
The Egyptian hieroglyphics are about 1,000 in num-
ber, but at a very early epoch a small selection was made on

and an Egyptian word is therefore usually represented

Papyrus, the pith of a reed, glued into a fabric, occurs
at quite an early time in Egypt, whereas in China, engrav-
ing on wooden or bamboo slips appears to have been the
standard method down to Han times. The columns in
Chinese manuscripts and books show distinct traces of
the wooden slips from which they originated. If Egyptian
hieroglyphics were the source of Chinese writing, it seems
incredible that the more convenient_means of writing
should not have been transmitted. It is interesting to
remark that our own Teutonic words "write," "book," etc.,
all allude to the practice by early Europeans of cutting
marks ("runes") in wood just as the early Chinese did.
An enquiry into any of the fields of culture shows
similar divergences and chronological difficulties. Archi-
tecture and art motives show no important analogies,
except perhaps in such simple matters that a very remote
and primitive common origin might be predicated. In
Egypt Sun-worship was a vital element in all religious
practice and symbolism at an early period, but never
amounted to very much in China. The funeral barrows
of China are undoubtedly analogous to the pyramids, but
it is not even certain that mound burial was the practice
in pre-Chou times, while the most curious features of
Egyptian Soul-worship, such as the use of statues in
special niches with incense holes, are quite unrecorded in
China.
The principle of an hereditary monarchy is reported
not to be primitive in China (this may be merely a political
story invented in Chou times), but it certainly goes back
to a very remote date in Egypt.
Time does not permit of an exhaustive comparison,
and it suffices to say that while there are resemblances,
and even in some minor respects identities, the evidence
is too slight to afford any serious support to the hypothesis.

The conclusion, then, seems to be either that the
hypothesis is incorrect or that any common origin is so
remote that its traces are imperceptible. As to the
cogency of the hypothesis of common origin, biology shows
generally that while identity of structure invariably in-
dicates community of origin, identity of function in an
organ may result along various lines of evolution. Thus
the wing in insect, pterodactyl, bird, bat and aeroplane,
serves an identical function. The organ itself has
originated in different ways but externally tends to be
rather the same. Similarly fish and the aquatic mammals
are externally much alike and have similar functions and
yet their origins are quite different. Broadly speaking,
it seems clear that different sources may produce similar
but not quite identical results. The same state of things
is seen in matters of thought. Unconscious plagiarism is
a common literary and scientific phenomenon. If, how-
ever, it extends to the most minute details it is not "uncon-
scious."
In other words when there is actual community
of origin it usually bears definite evidence of same, but
similarity of function or form is not of itself any proof
of similarity of origin.
The alternative suggestion that Egyptian culture may
have diffused to China in a remote period is too vague to
lead to any very final conclusion. History only begins
when there is a written language and in these two countries
this appears to be about 4500 B.C. and 2500 B.C. respec-
tively. Undoubtedly primitive culture existed for many
millenia before that in both countries. Professor Flinders
Petrie has arranged in a chronological series a number of
cultural periods in ancient Egypt, going back perhaps to
10,000 B.C. or earlier, but there is no exact means of dating.
The key to this chronology is the development of pottery
forms and decorations. The same method would doubtless
serve in China if sufficient records were available.
Neolithic pottery discoveries in China show relations of
form to the later bronze vessels and possibly to certain
Central Asian forms, but, as far the author knows, there is
no proven connection with Egypt. Man is thought by
many to have originated in Central Asia, but if so this was
at such a remote epoch (more than 100,000 years ago?)
that the statement throws no light on the question before

us.

Anthropological connection is practically

between them are far more noticeable than the resem-

One may thus broadly conclude that the evidence is
not in favour of any direct transmission of culture from
ancient Egypt to ancient China,* and that any community
of origin occurs at such a remote epoch that no data are
available in the matter, and necessarily there is no evidence
that Egypt was the source.5 Similar arguments seem to
apply to Mesopotamia as to Egypt and the writer is of
the opinion that most of the features of early Chinese
culture are indigenous.
There is no question as to the indirect transmission of art
motives, etc., originating in Egypt, in Han times, but this has no
bearing on the basic culture of ancient China.
