EARLY CHRISTIANITY IN JAPAN
By LAURA M. WHITE, B.A.
The casual visitor, travelling along the beautiful road
from Nagasaki to Unzen, frequently receiving a gracious
welcome from happy children and hospitable villagers,
does not know that every step of the way might be called
holy ground-consecrated as it is by the faith, heroism
and sacrificial service of hundreds of Catholic Fathers,
and hundreds of thousands of Japanese Christians whose
marvellous endurance has won for them the admiration
of all Christendom. The writer, being a Protestant,
desires to say that she has consulted only Japanese and
Protestant historians in gathering information for this
wonderful story.
In 1542 Ignatius Loyola, founder of the Jesuit Order,
commanded Francis Xavier to set out at once for Goa,
India, to open up missionary work in the Far East. He
only had a few hours in which to mend his old robe, and
say good-bye, before starting on the hazardous voyage
to India, to lay the foundations of Christianity in that
country.
After seven years of service in India, Francis Xavier
felt the call to Japan and in 1549 arrived at Kagoshima,
accompanied by the missionaries he had chosen to assist
him; Father Cosmo Torres, Brother John Fernandez,
and several Japanese helpers, including Paul Anjiro who
had been converted in Malacca. Kagoshima belonged
to the Daimyo of Satsuma. He and the Daimyo of Bungo
were the most powerful lords, "kings" the Fathers called
them, in the islands of Kyushu.
Xavier writes:
"We have been received with great kindness and
friendship. They are surprised to see priests from the
lands of the Portuguese, and approve of Paul's accepting
Christianity. The door is open. Paul has been active in
preaching. His family, also a large number of kinsmen

The Daimyo of Satsuma had hoped for commerce
with India; but Portuguese vessels some time later
became diverted to Hirado in western Kyushu. This
angered the king. Also, Buddhist priests circulated
tales of Christians eating human flesh, etc. The king
forbade any one becoming a Christian under pain of
death. After two months the three foreigners were
compelled to leave; but Paul, in charge of the church,
stayed behind.
At this time, Xavier's great desire was to go to the
capital, Kyoto, to convert the Emperor.
On the journey he stopped at Hirado, where he was
greatly honoured by Portuguese merchants in the harbour.
The young kinglet of the country was so impressed with
the salvoes of Portuguese artillery, and other marks of
profound respect to the Fathers, that he promptly gave
orders that the missionaries must be listened to with the
utmost respect. During the ten days they remained
there, Xavier and his colleagues baptized over a hundred
converts.
Leaving Cosmo and the servants at Hirado, Xavier
and Fernandez continued on their journey, stopping at
Yamaguchi. There they had an audience with the Dai-
myo, who had expressed a desire to know the Christian
doctrine. Xavier explained the creation of the world, the
ten commandments, etc.; but condemned vice. This
greatly excited the Emperor, who was a sex pervert.
The missionaries were ordered to leave the palace and
Fernandez writes: "I was afraid the king would cut our
heads off."
Fernandez continues:
"The next day, Father Francis decided that we should
preach in the streets. We stood at the crossings. I read
of the creation of the world, and spoke of the sins committed
by the Japanese. They had forgotten God. Instead of
worshipping Him, they adored wood and inanimate things.
Likewise the men gave themselves up to the abominations
of Sodom. Then I spoke of the sins of the women in
slaying their offspring before or after birth. While I
preached, Father Francis prayed that God would bless my
words."

The missionaries were ignominously driven out of
the city with stones, the riff-raff mocking their poor attire
and bare feet, as they started for the capital. They were
ignorant of the roads. They walked most of the way to
Kyoto, in winter time, fording rivers where the icy water
was waist deep. The two months' journey was a terrible
one. On more than one occasion Xavier was forced to
carry baggage for mounted merchants who kept him
mercilessly on the trot, tearing his legs with briars and
bamboo grass. The missionaries were pelted with stones
at Sakai, and when at last they arrived at Kyoto, in
piercing cold January weather, the city had been so re-
duced by wars and fires, that whoever lived there ran the
risk of being slain, burned, or starved. Xavier's poverty
at the capital city made him despised. He could get no
audience with the Shogun (the Mayor of the Palace).
He learned that the Emperor was a mere puppet. When
they preached in the streets, nobody listened. After
two weeks of vain effort, bitterly disappointed, the mis-
sionaries left the city chanting "In exitu Israel de
Ægypto."
In India poverty and humility had commended Xavier
to the natives; and in Japan he had tried to make headway
relying on the primitive simplicity of the early gospels,
and the example of the Apostles. But his experiences
in Yamaguchi and Kyoto convinced him that Japan was
different. He returned to Hirado for better clothes, and
armed with his credentials from India and with presents
such as a harp, clock, etc., again Xavier and Fernandez
presented themselves to the king at Yamaguchi, and this
time received permission to preach the gospel.
They had scant success until the occurrence of this
beautiful incident. As Fernandez was preaching to a
crowd, one day, a man edged his way up to him, and,
bending forward, spat on his cheek. The crowd laughed.
Not even turning to look at his assailant, Fernandez
continued speaking, quietly wiping his face with a
handkerchief. A prominent man who had already become
an avowed enemy of Christianity reasoned with himself:

From Yamaguchi Xavier made a visit to Bungo.
Here he engaged in famous public debates with Buddhist
priests. The king became favourably impressed with
Christianity, and Bungo soon became a Christian centre.
In fact, the harvest seemed so ripe, that after twenty-seven
months of service Xavier accepted the invitation of a
Japanese captain to return to India for more workers.
Another reason for his departure was the fact that China
had not accepted the gospel. This was an impediment
to the Japanese receiving the truth. He writes in 1550:
"The Japanese defer to the Chinese as being first in
wisdom and knowledge. I trust to penetrate to the Chinese
king. If the Chinese adopt the Christian religion, the
Japanese will then abandon the religions that they have
received from China."
The king of Bungo sent with Xavier a Christian
envoy to the Viceroy of India, asking for an alliance with
the_Portuguese, and for more missionaries. Arriving
at Goa, Xavier busied himself with missionary matters
for Japan, and made arrangements for his own journey
to China.
Setting out for this country, he reached Shang-Chuan
(Sanchian), an island not far from Canton. He had
hoped that some Chinese merchants might secretly convey
him to the mainland. The Portuguese feared that such
an attempt might lead to the Chinese withdrawing per-
mission to trade. While trying to surmount the obstacles
they put in his way, he was struck down with fever and
died, December 2, 1552.
Xavier had to a wonderful degree the gift of inspir-
ing his followers with holy enthusiasm, making them
willing to work and suffer with him. To him belongs
the honour of opening the door to Japan. But his suc-
cessors were equally consecrated. Father Torres had
sailed with Xavier from India, a buoyant, portly, practical,
energetic man. His sagacity and suavity made him a
worthy successor of Xavier as head of the mission.
Bravely he worked, in spite of his enemy, malaria. He
lived on Japanese food, and was happiest at times of
severest persecution. He gave to Japan twenty-one

Xavier's other companion, Fernandez, was the best
linguist, and succeeded even better than the others in

Xavier had wished him to become a priest, but he begged

Soon new missionaries arrived, and Father Torres
called a conference to regulate the mission policy. An
impressive ritual was arranged to suit the Japanese taste
for pageants and spectacles. Great attention was to be
paid to education. It was resolved to establish hospitals
and an orphanage. This was made easy by the consecra-
tion of Dr. Almeida, a skilled Portuguese surgeon, who
had amassed a large fortune in the East, and placed it
at the disposal of the mission when he joined their ranks.
He built a hospital for lepers, and in Bungo, an asylum
for children whose parents were too poor to provide for
them. The reputation that he gained as a surgeon, his
charity towards those in distress and poverty, and his
unceasing evangelistic efforts made him one of the most
successful Jesuit labourers.
During the next twenty years Christianity made
great progress in Japan. Many interesting stories could
be told of debates with Buddhist priests, and evangelistic
trips over the islands. In Hirado children were attracted
to the church by bell-ringing. Inside they were taught
to sing hymns and psalms to repeat to their parents.
They were also organized into attractive bands that
paraded the streets with banners, singing and chanting
the fine old hymns of the Church.
In this short paper one cannot even touch on the
wonderful results of the evangelistic work at Goto and
other islands off the west coast of Kyushu; of the
calumnies and persecutions suffered in Hakata at the
hands of Buddhists; of the crafty letters written by
different kinglets to the Provincial of the Jesuits in India,
speaking of their admiration of Christianity; of their
desire for more missionaries, and calling attention to the
excellence of their respective harbours, affording fine
accommodation for foreign commerce.

About this time in Kyushu, Civan, the king of Bungo,
was baptized with all his household. And later a college
was established at his capital, Funai; the pictures show
an interesting building, in European style.
Prince Omura, whose fief lay around Nagasaki,
became an enthusiastic Christian. In 1579 he invited
Portuguese merchants to settle near Nagasaki, then a
mere fishing village. He begged missionaries to make
it their headquarters. Moreover he said that only their
religion could be promulgated. It became the Shanghai
of Japan, governed by the Jesuits, fortified by the mer-
chants. A seminary, printing press, hospital and
orphanage were built there.
To the east of Nagasaki, the king of Arima also

Christian ruler, and here there were established a print-

Several other cities of Italy were visited by the
ambassadors, who then went back to Spain. Philip II
fitted up a ship for them at his own expense, adding rich
presents to the many that they had already received; and
they embarked at Lisbon, April 30, 1586, for their return
voyage. At Goa they were joined by Valignani and
reached Nagasaki, July 27, 1590.
Great changes had occurred in Japan. The new
regent, Hideyoshi, had issued an edict banishing all mis-
sionaries! What caused this sudden change? Alas,
their old friend, Nobunaga, had been assassinated.
Hideyoshi felt that the great influence of Christianity
was a menace to the consolidation of his power. Never-
theless, Valignani and the four returned students were
granted a courteous reception. They presented a great
show of gifts: priceless pictures, maps, globes, clocks and
watches. The emperor greatly enjoyed the students'
music. He made tempting offers to them to enter his
service, but in spite of much opposition from friends and
relatives, all four went into the service of the Church as
priests.
One reason given for the edict of banishment is that
certain Christian girls at Arima had refused an invita-
tion to become Hideyoshi's concubines. He was already
drunk when his procurer brought the news that his
favours had been rejected, and roared out that he would
cut the throats of all the Christian women in Arima.
Next morning the edict was issued. But in explanation
of his change of heart the emperor wrote to the viceroy
of Portuguese India:
"The Fathers have come to these islands to teach
another religion. This can only serve to introduce into
Japan a diversity of religion prejudicial to the State.
Because of this, I have forbidden them to preach, and have
ordered them to leave Japan. I am determined not to let
anybody come here to retail new opinions. But I still desire
to keep up commerce with India. Nothing is more tranquil
at this time than Japan. Even distant countries send to
render me the obedience which is my due. I expect soon
to conquer China; and as I have no doubt of succeeding in
this enterprise, I hope we soon shall be very much nearer
to each other."

In 1596, a Spanish galleon with rich cargo on its way
to Mexico went ashore and was captured and confiscated
by the Japanese. Its pilot in protest tried to make an

The Japanese asked how Philip II obtained such

soon as they have converted a part of the inhabitants, they

soon succeed in subduing the country." European

In 1622, on September 10, occurred at Nagasaki what
is called the "Great Martyrdom," when thirty were be-
headed, and twenty-five burned. Thousands of spec-
tators were present. The local Christians were required
to witness the sufferings of their teachers and fellow-
believers as a warning. Those to be burned were
fastened to stakes, and the severed heads of the decapi-
tated placed in front of them. The most famous of the
martyrs was the nobly born Father Spinola. As the fires
commenced to burn he started the 17th Psalm, "Laudate
Dominum, omnes gentes; laudate eum, omnes populi.
Quoniam confirmata est super nos misericordium ejus;
et veritas Domini manet in æternum." The other martyrs
responded antiphonally. The crowd, and even little
Catholic children joined in the chant. The martyrs con-
tinued singing, praying and exhorting, while the fires
slowly burned, water being occasionally added to prolong
the agony. The historian adds: "Three Japanese
Brothers disturbed the joy of that day, by breaking the
bonds, rushing out from the flames, and begging for
mercy. Then one, as though ashamed of his cowardice,
returned voluntarily to the stake."

Yet an uncommon steadfastness in the faith must at the

Once a year a general and strict search is made
throughout all the territories of the empire. All the in-
habitants are assembled in the 'pagodas,' where they must
sign with their blood that they are true Japanese and not
Christians; or, if they are Christians, they must abjure
their faith. But this measure has not produced the effects
the Emperor expected from it; as not one year elapses in
which several hundred Christians are not put to death.
All these persecutions and massacres have, in fact,
considerably reduced the numbers of Christians, and the
Court had directed, in order to discover those that remain,
that if any one was found to be a Christian he should be
relieved from the punishment to which he would otherwise
be liable, upon making discovery of a fellow-Christian.
If he could, but would not, point out another, then he
should suffer the penalty affixed to the profession of his
religion; namely to be hung up with his head downward.
It is generally supposed that this measure will be more
efficacious for the extirpation of Christianity than all the
punishments that have hitherto been devised.
An accurate register is kept of those Christians who

Japanese who were well informed and experienced in

Professor Murdoch writes:
"The grounds for the ruthless extirpation of Christianity
in Japan were political, merely. To find fault with a nation
for being determined to maintain its political integrity is at
once unreasonable and unjust; to refuse to record our meed
of respect to the Christian missionaries in their devotion to
what they conceived to be their duty, would, on the other
hand, be ungenerous. The persecution was a duel to the
death between Christian priests resolved to carry out the
command of the Founder of their religion, and of Japanese
equally resolute to preserve the independence of their
country."

After the Shimabara Revolt, the ceremony of
"Figure Treading" was introduced into southern Kyushu.
Images of Jesus or Mary, usually of brass, were carried
into every house at New Year's time. Each member of
the family was required to tread on them. Even the
little babies were held in their mothers' arms so as to
touch the image with their feet. This ceremony was not
abolished till after the Perry Expedition in 1854.

Thus Christianity seemed to be extirpated. Its very
name excited only contempt and hatred. It had ap-
parently received its death blow about 1620, and the
dying Church had sent to Rome an almost hopeless prayer
for succour that seemed unanswered for nearly two
hundred and fifty years.
Just about that same time the "Mayflower" carried
its band of pilgrims to New England. Some of their
descendants under Commodore Perry re-opened the closed
Japanese door in 1854. As a result of the treaty made

For several days there continued continuous streams
of visitors to the church. They still make the sign of the
cross, but use the thumb to sign their forehead and breast.
Japanese officers redouble their vigilance. We have caution-
ed the Christians to come in smaller numbers for we know
there are spies who watch us. The Christians lift their
hands to their breasts when their eyes meet ours-a private
symbol that they, too, are of the True Faith."
Missionaries soon learned of Christian communities
in other places. A visitor from the Goto Island was
amazed at seeing in the Nagasaki Cathedral the Cross
and other symbols that were known to him. He told
that on his island were 1,000 Christians. Later the
missionaries learned of 1,500 living among the mountains
twenty-five miles away. In all it is estimated that about
50,000 Christians were scattered over Kyushu, but many
never returned to the Mother Church.
The organization of the communities was nearly
always the same. There were two leaders. One, who
generally could read and write, presided at the beds of
the dying and also at prayers on Sunday. The other was
the baptizer. They had even retained the Latin formula
for baptism! The Christians had some books that had
been handed down for generations. One was a treatise
on Contrition, printed in 1603.
Mgr. Petitjean visited an island near Nagasaki and
writes:
"The general spirit of the Christian community is ex-
cellent. The doctrines of the Holy Trinity, the Incarnation
and Redemption seem as familiar to them as to most of
our French Catholics. They know and keep the command-
ments. They lead a life of penitence and perform frequent
acts of contrition."
Then follows a sad story of renewed persecution by
the government. Many were banished. Many were
tortured. Some were sent to the coal mines, sentenced
to hard labour. Prisoners suffered from insufficient food
and clothing. Some died in consequence of privation.
In 1870 all the Christians of Urakami, near Nagasaki,
about three thousand in number, were condemned to exile,
and embarked on two steamers. The men were first sent
on board and were followed by the women and children.
Those of the women who had been admitted to the
sacraments wore upon their heads the white veils that

In 1873 the government relented and an order was
issued for the return of the exiles. Prisoners were freed,
and edicts against Christianity removed. Soon orders
were sent for the return of all Christian exiles. What
joy for them to come back to their beloved island, which
some of them had not seen for more than five years!
What joy in the reunion of families that had been long
separated! But those that returned were in great
poverty. Many at the time of their departure had sold
their lands at any price they could get, and they were
now forced to cultivate less fertile ground on the sides
of the mountains. The Government constructed bar-
racks for those that had no other shelter. Christians
of other places came to the help of their brethren, and
as the first harvest was bountiful, the severest stress was
soon relieved.
The exiles had not suffered in vain. They earned
religious liberty for all Japan, and the admiration of the
whole world; for there is nothing finer in history than
the faith and heroism of those Catholics of Kyushu. The
standing miracle is that, without the inspiration of
church services, or the comfort of the sacraments, with
no Bibles, or Prayer Books, with no priests to minister
to their religious needs, with the fear of torture and
death always before them, nevertheless they actually kept
the fires of faith burning for nearly two hundred and
fifty years!
In 1890 occurred the 25th aniversary of the
discovery of the Christians. Pilgrimages were made to
the Church of the Martyrs in Nagasaki. The pilgrimages
were inaugurated by twenty-five hundred Christians
from Urakami. Early in the morning six hundred of
them had received the Communion in their own valley, and
then, forming a procession they marched over the same
road that had been trodden by their Christian ancestors,
and by many of themselves twenty years previously, as
they had gone into exile. The women, as before, wore
the white head dresses, received at the time of their
baptism; but now, if tears were in the eyes of many, it
was as a sign of joyful thankfulness, and not of grief.
With banners and songs the pilgrims made their way
through the streets of Nagasaki and up the flight of steps
that led to the Church.

Certain days had been appointed for the coming of
Christians from other communities. As the ancient
Jews made their way to Jerusalem at the time of the
Passover, so the Japanese believers came from different
parts of Kyushu. Some journeyed by land, bands of
happy pilgrims rejoicing in the religious liberty that had
been assured to them a year before, by the National
Constitution. Dwellers on the islands came by boats, on
which they lived during their stay in Nagasaki. At early
dawn and in the evening, their prayers and songs could
be heard floating over the placid waters of the bay. On
the day assigned to a company for its visit to the church,
its members gathered upon the shore, and awaited the
summons for climbing the hill. When the bell sounded,
the missionaries in charge of the section of the country
from which the pilgrims came, would put themselves at
the head of the procession as it made its way to the
church. Often the number of those who came was too
great to allow all of them to enter the building at one
time. It was estimated that in all some ten or twelve
thousand Christians took part in these pilgrimages.
The great day of the feast was March 17, the anni-
versary of the discovery of the Christians. Throughout
the commemorative service that was observed, two
women remained kneeling before the tomb of Mgr.
Petitjean. Twenty-five years before, this mother and
daughter, the latter then but a little girl, had been in the
company of visitors who had said to him whose body was
now lying within that tomb: "We have the same heart
as you.'
