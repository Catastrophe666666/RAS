The Christian Monument at Hsi-an fu
(as described by Father Havret, S.J.)
By A. C. MOULE, B.Α.
Trinity College, Cambridge.
When the missionaries of the Society of Jesus entered
China towards the end of the sixteenth century they searched
in vain for any sign that Christianity had been preached there
before.
Matthew Ricci, when he visited Chinkiang in January
1599, did not dream that the ground had been consecrated
three centuries before by the building of several churches in
honour of the true God; nor, in the nine years of his stay at
Peking, had he the least idea that Cambaluc had been in the
fourteenth century an Archiepiscopal see. Yangchow which
Ricci passed through three times; Hangchow (Marco Polo's
Kinsay, the capital of China A.D. 1127-1280) whose first
convert, Dr. Leo Li, was baptized by Ricci; Ch'üanchow
(Marco Polo's Zayton³) in Fukien, where Ricci's successors
were soon to display their zeal-in all these places the
fourteenth century had seen Christian churches and Christian
communities. Yet the great Jesuit could find nothing but
vague traditions of worshippers of the Cross; and he died
without having discovered the secret of the merciful work of
Providence which had been buried in oblivion by the
ingratitude of a proud and sensual people.
God indeed had not left Himself without a witness. But

Fifteen years after Ricci's death this argument was put to
silence completely and for ever by an event little short of
miraculous.
Early in the year 1625-perhaps about the beginning of
March-trenches were being dug for the foundations of some
building near the district town of Chou-chih thirty or forty
miles to the west or south-west of the city of Hsi-an when the
workmen came upon a great slab of stone buried several feet
beneath the surface of the ground. The purpose of the
excavation is not, however, quite certain. One author says
that a grave was being dug for the infant child of a popular
but very superstitious Prefect of Hsi-an: and again Etienne
Faber was told about ten years after the event by an old
native of the district that it was notorious that for several
winters it had been observed that snow would not lie on a
certain small patch of ground, the spot under which the stone
was, on investigation, found. In any case the stone was
carefully raised and cleaned and was then seen to be covered
with an inscription of the T'ang dynasty, in Chinese and in
some foreign characters, of beautiful workmanship, and in a
perfect state of preservation.
Such a discovery attracted the greatest attention and was
at once reported to the Magistrate of Chou-chih who came to
see the monument and gave orders for its removal to the
Ch'ung-jên ssŭ, a temple which lies on the north side of the

But among those who had seen the monument in 1625 at
Chou-chih was a scholar named Chang Kêng-Yi. This man
had met Ricci at Peking eighteen years before and at once
recognized the doctrines of this ancient inscription
practically the Gospel which Ricci had preached. He had a
rubbing made and sent it by special messenger to his Christian
friend Leo Li who was then living in retirement in his house
at the foot of the beautiful wooded hills which rise to the west
of the lake at Hangchow in the far off province of Chekiang.
Dr. Li lost no time in having the inscription (which can
scarcely have reached him before the latter half of April)
printed with an explanatory note which bears a date
corresponding to 12 June, 1625.
Shensi had been visited before 1620 by the energetic and
restless Jules Aléni, but there was no permanent mission
station in the province until this very year, 1625, when
Nicholas Tregault went there at the invitation of a Christian
named Philip Wang. As soon as Trigault reached Wang's
house at San-yüan in April he fell desperately ill, and it was
not until October that he was able to accompany his friend to
Hsi-an to make arrangements for the purchase of a house and
the erection of a church; and then, in October 1625, it probably
was that he, first of all Europeans, set eyes on the newly
discovered and already famous monument which he had been
specially charged to examine.
The monument is formed of one slab of stone. The upper
part contains the title in nine large characters surmounted by
the cross and enclosed in hornless dragons. The lower part,

containing the inscription and lists of names on the front face
and the two edges, measures approximately 3 feet 3.8 inches
wide by 11.3 inches thick at the bottom, 3 feet 0.75 inches by
10.4 inches at the top, and is 6 feet 4.83 inches high. The
height of the whole stone is about 9 feet 1 inch. It is called
fêng pei and so may have marked a Christian cemetery or
grave.
The form of the inscription-a long prose narrative
followed by a summary in verse-is like that of the
biographies on stone which are either buried or set up above
ground in cemeteries; but on the other hand the subject
matter and the titles may tend to show that the praise of
I Ssŭ was at most only a pretext for publishing an account
of Christianity and of its introduction into China.
By a most curious coincidence the temple where the
stone was erected in 1625 appears to be not far from the
site of the first Christian church which was built in 638,
within the huge limits of the ancient walls of Ch'ang-an
although five li outside the modern city of Hsi-an. The
history of the stone before 1625 is not known. No allusion
to it has been noticed in native books of the Sung or Ming
dynasties and the perfect condition in which it was found
makes it probable that it was either buried on purpose or
thrown down and allowed to bury itself soon after the edict
of A.D. 845 (p. 111). The fact that the original church
at the capital seems, as we shall see, to have survived
that edict is thus an argument in favour of the first erection
and discovery of the monument at Chou-chih rather thau,
as some early authorities state, at Hsi-an itself.

Four times at least some attempt has been made to protect the stone.
In 1625 it was placed on a pedestal under a roof supported on four pillars.
In the XVIIIth century Wang Wen-chih says that an official of Shensi
had set up an edifice in which he placed the stone. In 1859 Han T'ai-hua
rebuilt a pavilion, and a sketch by Mr. Jonathan Lees in 1866 shows the
monument let into brick-work in the manner which is common in North
China. In 1871 the stone was without shelter. In 1891 the government,
under foreign pressure. put up a shed which disappeared in a few months.
In March 1893 eight or nine courses of bricks were still standing behind
the stone.
