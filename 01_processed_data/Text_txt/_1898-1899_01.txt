Dr. Legge in his Prolegomena to Vol V. of his great
work, The Chinese Classics, tells us, apropos of previous
statements of his regarding the supposed early Calendar of the
Chinese :-
"I take this opportunity to touch on another subject
which has often perplexed students of ancient Chinese
history-the different commencements of the year in the three
great ancient dynasties of Hëa, Shang and Chow. According
to the representations of the scholars of the Han, and all
subsequent dynasties, the beginning of the year was changed
to signalise the new dynasty by an exercise of the royal
prerogative." He then proceeds to correct an error into
which he had fallen through too closely following the later
scholiasts in his translation of the Shu King, and goes on to
say:-

As a matter of fact, our direct sources of information on
the ancient calendar of the Chinese, such as it was, are
confined to the twenty-sixth chapter of the Shi ki, the Lih shu
歴​書​, one of a series of eight antiquarian essays inserted in
the Shi ki, though bearing strong internal evidence of having
been composed by another hand than Sze-ma Ts'ien's. Other
accounts have been taken at second-hand from this, and by
repeated copying have not only lost in definition, but have
picked up a mass of extraneous rubbish for which no authority
can be shown.
The Shi ki is, however, a work not in favour with the
ordinary "teacher," who much prefers to trust to the
scholiast, and take from him on trust these glosses. He finds,
indeed, a difficulty in grasping the language, and the meaning
of the original, often, and especially in this instance, far from
clear. Even Dr. Legge did not venture on carrying his
chronology beyond 775 B.C., yet it is characteristic of the
school to which he belonged that he was content, almost in
the same paragraph, to accept not only the so-called Hia, but
even to allow a place for the spurious Yin-Shang to be
interpolated.
His explanation, that even up to the close of the Cheos
there " was not sufficient science to correct" the period of
the vernal equinox, otherwise than by the most empiric of
formulæ, may be accepted as a statement of fact.
Returning to our authority, the Lih shu, it is of interest
to discover what its author really did say, and how far it
may be made to support the claims of a high antiquity for
the Chinese computation of time. It begins, as Chinese
essays usually do, with general statements to which little
exception can be taken.
"By the ancients the calendar was first established in
order to correctly define the beginning of spring. That is

lose ground, but when these were forgotten, at the time

There is thus no difficulty in explaining the practical

space of sixty years. In both can be recognised the work of
the great Hipparchus, the true founder of scientific astro-
nomy, who was certainly alive B.C. 125 or only twenty
years before Wuti's proclamation. Hipparchus estimated the
length of the tropical year at 365 days, 5 hours 551 minutes.
It would have required 300 years before the difference
between this estimate and 3651 days would have amounted
to a day, and the astronomers who drew up the Roman and
Chinese calendars evidently assumed that this difference could
be safely neglected.
Any student of Chinese will recognise the fact that the
cycle names given by the Emperor Wu to the years in his
calendars are not Chinese, the characters being simply used
phonetically. Though the great change from ancient to
modern Chinese, which marked the period of the later Cheos,
and which culminated during the iron rule of T'sin and the
subsequent revolutions, was by the time of Wu practically
accomplished, there were still many remains in current speech
of the ancient language. The present dialect of the city of
Canton is probably the nearest approach to the language
of the court of the Emperor Wu. It was only in the year
111 B.C. that the states of Eastern and Southern Yue,
Kiangsi, Fuhkien and Kwangtung, were annexed and settled,
and the immigrants there as elsewhere carried with them
their speech and customs.

It will be noticed that in the Chinese concept of the
Zodiacal relations, the signs are made to run backwards, and
this might be supposed to arise from want of knowledge;
the explanation is that the sequence is intended to follow
Jupiter's retrograde passage in his twelve years' journey
round the ecliptic. As a fact, it has been observed, that while
many nations have essayed [on paper) to make Jupiter's
yearly motion through a sign a time marker, in practice none
have ever done so. Jupiter's synodic revolution occupies
378 days, the earth's revolution round the sun 365, and the
relations become too complicated for ordinary use. The two
hands of a clock coincide 11 times before they finally meet
at the starting-point, but no one has ever taken this coinci-
dence as a time measure. But this connection of the Yin
Sui with the motions of Jupiter seems to indicate an older
use of them as a year measure than is usually accepted.
Though the cycle of sixty years was placed on a regular
footing for the first time by the Emperor Wu, it possibly, or
rather probably, was in existence in early times; though it
never was taken into official use, and its pretended application
to chronology, as Dr. Chalmers has well shown [Chinese
Classics, V; Prolegomena 99] must be accepted as a mere
interpolation.

But if the early Aryan name of the planet has been

Amongst the Semitic peoples he is universally the Just Man,
Çedük, so called evidently from his adjusting the calendar, in
his twelve years' journey to his starting-point round the

the mysterious Cabiri, the inventors of navigation. So in
Chinese lore, Jupiter descends as the planet having specially
to do with the year, but whose influence is by no means
confined to it.

In the eighth year of Duke Ch'ao of Lu, the Tsochwan

In the thirty-second year of Duke Ch'ao we find a more

Wu attacked Yueh (Cheh Kiang). This was its first hostile

There is then considerable reason to believe that the
fashion of naming the year from the position of Jupiter
existed long before the time of the Emperor Wu, The
modern Chinese historians allege indeed that antecedent to
the great regulation of the Calendar in the period Tai Ch'o,
the cycle names were never applied to years; but the extracts
I have quoted seem to prove that a cycle of twelve, at least,
existed, though probably very irregularly kept.
This connects the old Chinese computation of time more
