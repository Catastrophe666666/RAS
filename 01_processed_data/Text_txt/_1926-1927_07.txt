OR "FAITHFUL PRINCE"
("The faithful and devoted of a myriad years")
An Episode in the Taiping Rebellion
By J. H. TEESDALE
I
During my researches into the history of the Taiping
Rebellion I have found a book often referred to of which
a copy seemed unprocurable in spite of all my efforts.
This was The Autobiography of the Chung Wang, trans-
lated by Mr. W. T. Lay, late of the Customs Service, who
I believe acted as an interpreter to General Gordon.
ransacked the local libraries, communicated my needs to
second-hand booksellers in London and even wrote to
Mr. Lay who was then alive in England, but it was only
when at last I had tried the Library at Siccawei that
success attended my efforts. The Fathers rightly refused
to allow the book to leave the premises, so I engaged the
services of a Chinese who attended there and copied it
out in long hand. From this I had several copies type-
written, one of which I deposited in the Library of this
Society.
I found my prize more interesting than I had anti-
cipated.
In the first place the circumstances under which it
came to be written were exceptional.
Nanking, which had been the capital of the Taiping
Kingdom since the 18th March, 1853, fell to the Imper-
ialists on the 19th July, 1864, and the Chung Wang was
captured. He begged his captors to be allowed to write
his autobiography before being led to execution. This
concession was made and the remarkable work to which
I have alluded was the result. It was written in prison,
as Mr. Lay avers, in the Chung Wang's own handwriting
between the 30th July and the 8th August, 1864, at the
rate of 7,000 characters daily-a truly wonderful
performance.

But I must not anticipate.
Let us proceed chronologically and follow the for-
tunes of Li Sin Cheng, of whom Dr. Williams says: “He
was the solitary ornament of the whole movement during
the 14 years of its independent existence and his enemies
would have done well to spare him."
Li tells us in his autobiography that he was born at
Hsin Wang, a small village in Kwangsi, not very far
apparently from where the Rebellion originated and
pursued its rapid and devastating advance via Changsha
into Hunan.
He states that he was at home when he heard that
the Tien Wang's troops would pass by on their way
North. The great distress in his family was the sole
cause of his leaving it. He says: "Our family led a very
precarious existence, having to subsist the best way we
could. From the age of eight to ten I was engaged in
study, but at that period I was obliged to assist my father
and mother in working for our daily sustenance."
What work this was he does not reveal.
At the age of 26 he joined the Taiping Army on its
lightning march to the North and served in the ranks.
As we all know, Chinese armies of the last century
had a reputation of moving slowly. The Taipings in their
march from Kwangsi to Nanking formed an exception,
and the importance of speed to success in military strategy
is well exemplified in this extraordinary incursion. Start-
ing from Yung Ngan by breaking through a besieging
force of Imperialists on the 7th April, 1852, they moved
on Kweilin, the provincial capital, but, having no cannon
fit to besiege a walled city of that size, they crossed the
border, and town after town fell to them in rapid succes-
sion. Checked at Changsha, they took Yochow on the
13th December. Ten days later they advanced upon
Hanyang and Wuchang and capturing them moved East
down the Yangtsze, occupied Hwangchau and Kiukiang
on the 12th January and 18th February, 1853, while
Nanking fell a week later. Nothing seemed able to
resist, and on the 8th March, 1853, they encamped before
Nanking.
This important city fell after only a ten days siege
and became and remained the capital of the new kingdom
for 12 years, the Tien Wang being shortly after pro-
claimed there the Emperor of China and his four sub-
ordinate leaders raised to the dignity of Kings (Wangs)
viz: the Southern, Eastern, Western and Northern Kings.

The Chung Wang tells us that now a proper admin-
istration was set up and the Tung Wang, or Eastern King,
was entrusted with the duties of Minister of State. A
regular army was formed, a military system introduced
and laws were enacted.
He adds "a distinction was made between male and
female, each had his or her proper share of duty allotted
and a hundred things were put into practice
Males
were disallowed to converse with females and mothers
were not allowed to hold communication with their sons.¹
The measures were extremely strict and proper and the
popular feeling exhibited great satisfaction. A stringent
order was enacted for giving peace to the people. It was
that no officer or soldier should be spared if he entered a
person's house when the work of restoring order after
the capture of a place was in progress."
Impressed no doubt at the success which rapid mar-
ches had rewarded them with, the Taipings, before the year
1853 had closed, sent a powerful expedition to the North
to capture Peking, and they actually reached a place
situated within 12 miles of its walls. But the communi-
cations of the force were long and vulnerable, winter
overwhelmed them, Sankolinsin, the Tartar General, com-
pleted their discomfiture, and it was a mere remnant of
the expedition that succeeded in returning to Nanking.
The Chung Wang does not appear to have served in this
ill-fated campaign.
This was the first serious set-back the new Kingdom
of Heavenly Peace had suffered. Others were soon to
follow.
The Tung Wang arrogated to himself such majesty
that jealousies arose and he was eventually assassinated
at Nanking by the Pei Wang with all his followers num-
bering a cool twenty thousand of men, women and
children. The Yi Wang, determined on revenge, marched
on Nanking but learning that the Pei Wang was bent on
killing him also, he escaped to Nganking, the Pei Wang
wreaking his vengeance on the Yi Wang's followers, of
whom he slew all he could lay hands on.

We did our best to get the Soochow people to return, but they

In three days I had pacified the Yuanho people, and in seven
days had quieted Yuanho, Wusien and Changchow, and the people
of the districts, far and near, then acknowledged allegiance, which
induced the people of Soochow and Hangchow to do likewise.
After capturing Tsingpoo, some 50 miles from
Shanghai, we read of the Chung Wang's first contact with
the foreign devils, of whom he speaks with the greatest
contempt, and of his celebrated march on Shanghai. His
reason for not attacking the place is very quaintly given.
When everything had been prepared for a march into the city
and arrangements made for my reception, when I had reached there
the atmosphere became darkened, the wind rose and thunder re-
verberated in the heavens. The ground was rendered so slippery
from the immense quantity of rain, that neither men nor horse
could obtain a firm footing, and so the march into Shanghai was
unaccomplished and the devils who came out to meet me had the
satisfaction of returning without me.
The probable explanation for his not doing so, how-
ever, is pretty clearly explained in other works, such as
Andrew Wilson's Ever Victorious Army and The Taiping
Rebellion, by Egmont Hake. The fact was that it was
outside the walls of Shanghai that the Chung Wang first
experienced the precision and range of foreign firearms.
He soon realized that until his men were similarly armed
he stood a poor chance of success against them. After
moving to the Church of the Red Heads (Siccawei) he
hastened away to relieve Kashing, which was reported to
be in danger. Having raised the siege of Kashing and
taken Bingoo and Kazay the Chung Wang returns to set
Soochow in order. This is how he does it.
