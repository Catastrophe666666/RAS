a Leguminoseae with simple leaves, which very much
excited the curiosity of botanists because of the impossi-

1814, an amateur, who was fond of Natural History,

GUELDENSTAEDTIA
Family of Leguminoseae, Diadelphia, Decandria.
Generic Characters: Calyx with two bracteoles at base, campanu-
late, five parted for two-thirds its length, upper teeth the
broadest. Corolla papilionaceous, five petaled, standard entire,
wings nearly length of standard, carina minute. Stamens
diadelphous. Pod subcylindric, many seeded, valves spirally
twisting on drying, median partition evanescent at maturity,
stuffed. Seeds reniform, scrobiculate.
Plant acaulescent, leaves odd-pinnate or multifoliolate (central
one only remaining), peduncle ascending, flowers umbellate,
red or violaceous.
Related to Coronilla by the umbellate inflorescence, Trigonella
by the nearly three-petaled corolla, Lotus, Indigofera and
Caragana by the pod, Astragalus by the habit of the peduncle."
For the continuation of this paper see under G.
monophylla.
The generic characters, as above enumerated, will
have to be modified if the twenty-two " species" described
to date are to be included. The characters of the calyx
teeth vary considerably, the standard is often notched
or emarginate at apex, the wings may be considerably
shorter than the standard, the pod is reported as
compressed in some species, the valves twist after having
split apart, branched species occur, the flowers may be
white, or any color or shade which includes red and blue,
and yellow. The word multifoliolate is evidently an error
for unifoliolate.
The only really characteristic structures, taken in
combination with the others, is the extremely short keel
and lack of septum in the mature pod (this last has not
been ascertained for all the species).
As thus constituted, the genus extends from northern
Korea and Shantung, north to the northernmost bend of
the Amur and Lake Baikal, west to the Siberian Altai
and the mountains of the upper Ganges, and south to
Shantung, Hupeh, Yunnan and the Himalayas. Thus
it is not found in Japan, central and south Korea and the
lower Yangtzŭ valley. It is thus characteristic of the
semi-arid part of the eastern extension of the great
central desert and the semi-arid belt on the mountains
of east and central Asia.

The species show a decided habitat preference, being
found almost exclusively in dry situations, bare open
fields and grassy places in full exposure to the sun.
For instance, about Tsingtao they are found along the
shore from highest tide line to the top of the hills, but
rarely under the trees; in the hills, they are found chiefly
along dry, grassy banks and slopes; in the fields, on the
grassy retaining banks and graves; at Chefoo, on sand
dunes; in Yunnan, on the arid belt of the high mountain
ranges. For such existence, they are particularly fit by
reason of their long, fibrous, perennial tap-root, their
hairy, compound leaves, articulated leaflets and their
early flowering season. Thus it seems to be a genus
typical of semi-arid, sunny, grassy situations in east
temperate Asia. The same species may be found on
alkaline or non-alkaline soil.
The diffusely branching species seem to be confined
to the south-western portion of the range of the genus.
This character, however, is not definite, as all grades of
branching occur. This is in part due to the fact that the
long tap root (characteristic of the genus) buds profusely
about its apex. These buds form long or short rhizomes
covered by the closely crowded stipules. Thus, although
there are many of these short rhizomes or " branches",
each forming its own rosette, the general effect is that of
a dense rosette, the branching often being lost in the mass.
In those species studied in the field, the leaves form a
flat rosette while the peduncles hold erect their crown of
blossoms. After fertilization and during the growth of
the pod, the peduncles of some species gradually turn
back until the pods, which stand at a sharp angle from
their peduncle, are thrust against the soil and hidden
among the leaves, being thus protected from browsing
animals; in other species they remain erect.
SPECIFIC VARIATION
The amount of specific variation, especially of parts
that have been used for specific discrimination, is
discussed in the following general description of the
generic characters. Root perennial, usually simple, in
some species quite napiform, long, fibrous, tough, with
few fine rootlets; stems single to several, short, thick,
sprouting from about apex of the root, covered by
the more or less imbricated, triangular, opposite stipules;
plants may be glabrous though usually pilose to villous

their fall appearance is much more developed than at

factors, namely, (1) age or development of plant (large,
robust plants averaging a higher number of leaflets than
small, stunted plants growing under extreme conditions
of soil and moisture), (2) time of development (the
earliest leaves to develop always having fewer leaflets
which never develop in size or shape, like the later ones),
(3) a certain amount of variation within the individual
(though the majority of the leaves have the same number
of leaflets). Some leaves, through a symmetrical growth
at the tip, have an even number of leaflets. In G. giraldii
the maximum number is nine pairs (plus the terminal).
In the largest plants (exceeding others by four or five
times) the number is still nine pairs, the difference
in size being made up by increased size of all parts
as well as increased spacing. Shape of leaflets various,
usually oblique at base and on short petiolules, speci-
fically constant within certain limits, the character
of the base and specially the apex often being used
for specific determination. In G. giraldii, although
the apex is usually rounded and apiculate, some leaflets
are distinctly notched. These are usually found near
the proximal end of the leaf while the apical leaflets are
entire. Furthermore, this notching is never found on
big healthy plants of this species, while it is fairly
common on the earliest, dwarfed leaves of poor plants.
This species slightly resembles G. multiflora in this leaf
character but the notches are never as large and generally
developed. The leaflets turn upward during excessive
heat or drought. Peduncles usually longer than petioles.

that of the bracts appears inconstant. This is due in
part to the growth of the pedicel with the maturing of
the flower, making the bracts appear longer in buds and
newly opened flowers than in old or withered flowers of
the same plant. The calyx of some species is more or
less densely covered with black hairs. In G. giraldii the
copiousness of these hairs varies with exposure. For
instance, they are fewer to lacking on under or sheltered
side of the calyx and more abundant on that part exposed
to the sunlight. In very woolly plants they are actually
less common than on plants with a thinner growth of
silky hairs. The calyx teeth are usually unequal in
length, the lower one being the shortest in most species
though the upper two are shortest in three species.
length of the teeth relative to the tube varies a little
within the species, robust plants having slightly the
longer teeth. The base of the various teeth is at approxi-
mately the same distance from base of tube. The amount
of red coloring on the calyx and its parts varies with
exposure to light, thus plants growing in sparsely vege-
tated habitats have a great deal of red while those
growing in a close growth of deep grass have very little
red. This applies also to the amount of red on the rim
of the leaflets and on the peduncles, petioles and stipules.

than that part turned inward. The color of the flower

form to any of the known forms, it is here left as a distinct
species. For comparison with its nearest ally see remarks
under G. giraldii glabra. It is also close to G. delavayi.

PHYLOGENIC RELATIONS
The genus, by the reduction of the keel and lack of
septum in the pod, clearly marks a specialization away
from the Astragali. Its progenitor should be sought for
in eastern Asia undoubtedly among the mountains of
west China.
A survey of the more highly specialized forms shows
that the tendency to reduced number of leaflets is
accompanied (1) by a tendency to correspondingly
increased size of leaflets, (2) more specialization in shape
of leaflets and (3) acquisition of coloring in the hairs.
In analysing the group it has been found more
convenient to consider first the paucifoliate species, i.e.,
G. nonophylla to G. tongolensis as above presented. Of
these paucifoliolate forms, G. mirpoureana may be con-
sidered the least highly specialized by reason of its equally
long and slender calyx teeth; white and poorly developed
pubescence; entire standard and rounded leaflets with
short apicule. By contrast, G. yunnanensis, G. coelestis
and G. flava-tongolensis have the lower calyx teeth the
longer, in the latter two, the upper two teeth being almost
entirely connate. This specialization is accompanied in
G. coelestis by a black haired calyx and golden "body
hairs" and in G. tongolensis by brown hairs on calyx and
yellowish "body hairs." Some of the leaflets of both show

a tendency toward emargination while in G. yunnanensis
they are quite often obcordate. Thus one may infer that
in this genus, presence of apicule may be a primitive
character while emargination is a specialized character,
that coloration of hairs is a specialized, absence of hair
color a primitive charater. Finally the greater develop-
ment of the lower calyx teeth in contrast to the greater
development of the upper, may also be considered a
specialization, though connation of these teeth is a more
certain index of such specialization.
Moreover, G. monophylla, G. yunnanensis, G. coelestis
and G. flava-tongolensis, the most highly developed species
of the genus, lie on the periphery of the Tibetan plateau,
namely Siberian Altai, Yunnan triple sierra, and eastern
Tibet respectively. Although G. mirpoureana lives in
extreme western Himalaya it is quite primitive in all its
characters except the number of leaflets, thus making a
subgeneric division based on number of leaflets entirely
unnatural. It may be pointed out that the most highly
developed species are often located at the ends of
mountain ranges radiating away from the center of
origin, as G. monophylla west end of the Mongolian Altai,
G. yunnanensis and G. coelestis on the Yunnan triple
sierra, while G. flava-tongolensis has not migrated to any
distance. G. mirpoureana with its reduced number of
leaflets is situated beyond the west end of the Kuen Lun
mountains.
The multifoliolate group including the remainder of
the species, forms a compact group separable chiefly by
characters of the hairy covering and shape of the apex of
the leaflets. Loss of hairiness, loss of leaf surface,
dwarfing, etc., are quite evidently modifications away
from the primitive form.
The two stenofoliolate forms are clearly a distinct
off-shoot from this multifoliolate group. Of the two,
G. stenophylla has gone farthest (1) in the narrowing
of leaflets, (2) in producing some emarginate leaflets,
(3) in having dark hairs on the calyx. Its short,
subequal calyx teeth and short, white "body hairs" are,
however, primitive characters. G. pauciflora does not
have emarginate leaflets nor are they so slender, but has
yellow "body hairs" and long upper calyx teeth. Thus,
although both have specialized in narrow leaflets, they
seem to have diverged from one or more intermediate
forms rather than that the one sprang from the other.
Their distribution is also suggestive. G. stenophylla

The eight broad-bladed multifoliolate species are
difficult to arrange in linear or arborescent form of
descent chiefly because their differentiation from the
original form is of the rosette type.
Of the three species with emarginate leaflets, the
Himalayan G. uniflora is most specialized by reason of
its rusty hairs and connate upper calyx teeth (which are
also the longest). G. multiflora of the north China moun-
tains is next in order because of its yellowish "body
hairs," while G. brachyptera of central China, with white
body hairs and long, slender, subequal calyx teeth is the
most primitive of these three. G. brachyptera has one
advanced character not shared by the other three, black
hairs on the calyx. G. uniflora alone is inapiculate.
Their migration has been in opposite directions from
G. brachyptera.
Of the five with entire leaflets, it is interesting to
note that none of them have colored body hairs (the color
of the nearly glabrous G. henryi is not mentioned), that
G. giraldii alone has dark hairs on the calyx though these
are often very scant to almost if not quite lacking in some
individuals, and that all but one (G. diversifolia) are
apiculate. G. henryi has a winged peduncle and is nearly
glabrous. G. diversifolia has a bidentate calyx and is
the only one without apicule. G. guilloni is entirely
glabrous; as are two sub-species of the same province.
G. delavayi of Yunnan has no outstanding characters
except an apicule and is thus the simplest form of the

G. mirpoureana which has remained unchanged only at
the western end of the Himalayas.

say that the group originated in west central China.
