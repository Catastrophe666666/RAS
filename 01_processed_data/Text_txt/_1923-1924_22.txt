BY THE REV. G. G. WARREN.
Christian Sinologues of the last century, Catholic and
Protestant alike, held a view which, with no disrespect I
may entitle the Received Opinion, concerning the progress,
or, regress rather, of Chinese religious thought. Back in
the days of the highest antiquity, the people had the
knowledge of a Supreme Ruler, one who was necessarily
a Person. In the days of Confucius, the substitution of
T'ien for Shang-Ti showed a method of thinking that
might be about a Person, or might be about some imper-
sonal Supreme. In the days of Chu Hsi, and largely
through his influence, the substitution of Li () for T'ien
banished all possibility of personality in the highest
power acknowledged. From Chu Hsi to the present,
Chinese scholars have all been materialists and practi-
cally atheists.
Let me give a few extracts from the sinologists of
those days to illustrate the statement:
Dr. Williams in the Middle Kingdom, II, 200:
"Chu Hsi resolved the few and obscure references to
Shangti in the Shu King into pure materialism.
His system of materialism captivates his countrymen, for
it is far more thoroughly worked out than any other, and
allows scope for the vagaries of every individual who
thinks he understands and can apply it to explain what-
ever phenomena come in his way.
With regard
to the existence of gods and spirits, Chu Hsi affirms that
sufficient knowledge was not possessed to say positively
that they existed, and he saw no difficulty in omitting the
subject altogether-a species of agnosticism or indiffer-
entism therefore-which has become the creed of nearly
the entire body of educated men in the empire." (I could
understand the reference to "the vagaries of every in-
dividual" if the author had been referring to the trans-
lators of Chu Hsi; but I fail altogether to catch the drift
of his meaning when the remark is referred to the Chinese
readers of Chu Hsi. It is a mistake, as we shall see, to
say that Chu Hsi omitted the subject of "the existence of
gods and spirits" altogether).
