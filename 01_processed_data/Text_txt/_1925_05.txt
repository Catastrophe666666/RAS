THE SPRINGS OF TSINANFU
By GEORGE B. BARBOUR
(Professor of Geology, Yenching University, Peking.)
The capital city of Shantung owes its situation to the
springs for which it is famed throughout North China.
In the days before the Yellow River held its present
course within two miles of the city, the economic import-
ance of such a supply must have been even greater than
it is to-day. As it is, the low-lying edge of the plain
watered by the overflow of these springs is one of the
few spots in North China where rice is cultivated. In a
region frequently tormented with drought, it is easy to
see how such an abundant flow has, during the centuries
since the earliest settlement of the district, acquired a
supernatural reputation, and has been an object of super-
stitious reverence.
The Springs of Tsinanfu have an interesting geolo-
gical basis and are sufficiently unusual to merit descrip-
tion.
Historical. Writing of the historical background of
Tsinanfu, Parker notes that "tradition says that more
than 4,100 years ago the famous Emperor Shun ploughed
over a part of the area that is now within the Chinese
city. A little later the Kingdom of T'an was established
within the environs of the present city.
About
25 miles East of T'an in ancient times there was another
small kingdom called Tsinan. In the fourth century A.D.
the capital of the kingdom was, for some unknown reason,
moved to the present site of Tsinan."
Tsinan is built on the lower slope of the northern
margin of the Taishan massif just where the limestone
spurs plunge under the alluvial plain of the Huang-Ho.

The highest point of the city wall (built in A.D. 1371)
is about 120 feet above the plain.
The Springs.—The “72 springs" all rise through the
sloping ground enclosed by this wall, and the water is
guided back and forth across the city in open channels
until most of it finds its way, through the lake or directly,
into the Hsiao Hsing Ho—a canalized stream that has
served as a waterway for much of the commerce upon
which the trade of Tsinan has built up.
Though popular report gives the number of the
springs as 72, it is a mistake to think of the entire flow as
limited to the more spectacular gushers which are to be
seen welling up in the center of pools all over the city.
At a great number of other points bubbles can be seen
rising, and careful observation at these places often
shows the sand at the bottom in constant agitation from
below. It is also not improbable that the mouths of the
underground flow change in importance from time to
time, those at higher levels or, possibly, those more
distant from the source, flowing less strongly as others
develop larger mouths and handle more water at their
expense.
No reliable estimate of the total quantity of water
involved is in existence. In 1920 Mr. Oliver J. Todd
measured the water rising from the three main springs,
which, with their tributaries, feed the stream flowing
past the electric light station. The discharge as cal-
culated from the readings of a Price Current-metre was
at the rate of 70 second-feet; allowing for the seasonal
variation Mr. Todd estimates that the discharge through-
out the year must average 60 second-feet.
Temperature. -The reports of variation in tempera-
ture are due mainly to the contrast with the temperature
of the surrounding atmosphere at different seasons of the
year, and in part perhaps to the personal factor of the
observers. In mid-winter the pools appear to "steam"
with the rising vapor, while in summer children spend
half their days bathing in the "ice-cold" water. A recent
determination made with the assistance of Doctors
Wright and Adolph of Shantung Christian College gave a
reading of 60.5° F. (15.3° C.) as the temperature of the
water rising in the Pa Tu Ch'uan. It being early March,

Source. The ultimate source of supply is, of course,
rain-water which has sunk into the ground at higher
levels. Parker gives the average rainfall of the district
as 20 inches, concentrated mainly in July and August.
In other countries 20" is regarded as the limit between
humid and semi-arid regions. The force with which the
springs well up points to a generally southern direction
for the source of so large a supply. In other directions
the ground falls away too much to give the "head"
needed to produce such pressure. V. Richthoven 4
explains the springs as due to the porosity of the loess
soil which lies like a veneer over the bed-rock. Water

There are several objections to this explanation. If
the underground structure is so simple, why are such
springs not found wherever these conditions are dupli-
cated, as happens in hundreds of localities in China?
Moreover the bedrock of the slope is lime-stone which is
notoriously ineffective as a water-retainer; in fact, it is
the wide-spread occurrence of this rock in Chihli and
Shantung that makes the hill districts so barren of water.
Also well-borings on the site of Shantung Christian
College did not strike water in any quantity till the lime-
stone had been penetrated to a considerable depth. It is
true that in limestone areas water often flows through
solution channels underground and emerges in springs
upon which the district depends for its supply. But the
position of such springs always bears some relation to the
stratification or other structure of the limestone. There
are districts in the east of France where the villages are
placed in lines, having grown up along the loci of such
springs.
In many parts of China the limestone is covered with
a mantle of residual clay which forms a more impervious
layer at the base of the loess. But here too it may be
asked why such a universally found sub-soil formation
has not provided an equally abundant supply in a dozen
other places as well.
On the other hand recent geological study seems to
show that the masses of igneous rock (gabbro) which
forms the rugged hillocks several miles out on the plain
are all connected underground and form a unit at least
as large as the area they encircle. Moreover both to the
East and the West of the city there are outcrops of diorite
which seems to be the marginal phase of the same large
igneous mass.
These occurrences ring the city round like a horse-
shoe, and the springs rise midway between the tips. It is
impossible to resist the conclusion that there is a con-
nection between the occurrences, and that the igneous
body occupies not only all the area between the two points,

For it is possible to show that, though the gabbro under-
lies the limestone, it did not solidify as a lava flow upon
which the limestone was deposited at a later date, but
that instead it invaded the already solid limestone from
below, forcing itself as a molten mass along the strati-
fication and other planes of weakness in the sedimentary
rock.
A glance at the block-diagram (Fig. 2) will make this
clear. The near (left) side of the block is to be imagined
so cut as to expose the structure of the underlying rock
on a section right through the centre of the horse-shoe.
The black hillocks on the plain lie on the outer curve of
the shoe; the east tip of the shoe as exposed to-day lies at
the point x but the wedge probably extends underground
considerably further south. The likelihood of this exten-
sion, either as a single layer or inter-fingered with the
limestone in a series of wedges, is strengthened by the
discovery by Prof. Jacot and Dr. Adolph of several
diorite intrusions in the limestone and of a number of
deposits of clay scattered through the hills just to the
south. This clay may well indicate wedges of igneous
rock from which it has formed by the decomposition
through the action of weather.
After consolidation a mass of rock of this kind is
virtually impervious to water, so that it acts as a rubber
sheet and collects on its upper surface all the water that
sinks down to that depth from above. Further, such a
body of once-molten rock, not being stratified, is under no
obligation to have a regular upper surface, but is moulded
against the contact-surface of the limestone it has in-
vaded. Undulations in the original roof of the molten
gabbro chamber may thus, at a much later date, become
the controlling factors in directing the flow of under-
ground water from a relatively large gathering-ground
into the comparatively restricted area within the wall.
The effect of the igneous body is to prevent water from
sinking and, by thus raising the level of saturation in the
ground, forcing the water-table to near the surface.
Wherever an open way of escape presents itself it will
come out as a free-flowing spring.

The Tsinan springs offer, then, an unusually beauti-
ful example of the control of ground-water by an erratic
mass of buried impervious rock, whereby the rainfall of
the locality is carefully husbanded within the earth and
given back to the fortunate inhabitants in a well-
regulated unfailing supply, which may well be the envy
of the settlements of the hills and of the plain alike.
