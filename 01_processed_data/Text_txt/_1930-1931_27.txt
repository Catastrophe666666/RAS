THE COSMIC SPIRIT
Its nature, operations and influence
By Dr. EVAN MORGAN
Milton pictures the delights of reading the philo-
sophy of the ancients in these words:-
"Or let my lamp at midnight hour
Be seen in some high lonely tower
Where I may oft out-watch the Bear,
With thrice great Hermes, or unsphear
The spirit of Plato to unfold
What Worlds, or what vast Regions hold
The immortal mind that hath forsook
Her mansions in this fleshy nook:
And of those Daemons that are found
In fire, air, flood, or under ground,
Whose power hath a true consent
With planet or with element."
I hope that a short stay with some old philosophers
of China will afford some delight as we consider their
view of the Universe and what they found in it. It has
not been very easy to find out what they really did think
of it all.
I shall quote largely from the first essay of Huai
Nan-tzu and also refer to the Tao Te Ching of Lao Tzu.
Their great theme is the TAO. What did they mean by it?
How much background they had to work on in their
interpretation of nature and life it is not easy to say.
It can only be affirmed that they had infinitely less than
we have to look to. They had the intuition of their
minds, beside outward phenomena and some history to
guide them. They had also much folklore and some
general ideas of cosmogony. There were the great
rituals, especially the worship of Heaven in the Suburb
and the cult of the worship of ancestors. They were
not altogether without landmarks. Nevertheless the void
was still great. What conceptions they had of the
universe were meagre and could not have satisfied the
enquiring mind.

The universe will not permit man to be indifferent to
its mysteries. It compels men to become philosophers
and scientists; the common person looks up and down
and within and is forced to ask what it all means.
"There is in the human mind an instinct that drives for
personality and for righteousness as the necessary basis
and condition of their relations. The vacuum must be
filled by the achieved results of thinkers." Every race.
has made its contribution to the solution. Epicureanism
and scepticism had its representatives in Roman Society.
The former found an expositor in Lucretius, "a genius
such as it had never known elsewhere."
Yet even Lucretius had in him an anti-Lucretius, and
in a famous passage he confesses to an instinct of the
heart quite contrary to the reason he works out in his
head. He says, "When we look up to the great expanse
of heaven, the ether set on high above the glittering stars,
the thought comes into our minds of the sun and the moon
and their goings: then in hearts laden with other woes,
that doubt too begins to wake and raise its head-Can
it be perchance, after all, that we have to do with some
vast power that wheels those bright stars each in its
course?" (T. R. Glover). So enquired Lucretius as he
contemplated the vast mysteries about him hidden in the
depths of heaven and in the visible world around.
Lucretius had his occasional doubts as to the truth
of his formulated philosophy. There were others like
Socrates and Plato who were more certain of their
interpretation of the universe. They were convinced of
some supreme reality at the centre of things which was
not less real because it was invisible. And the Chinese
school of philosophy that we shall consider now has more
similarity in its certitude and conceptions to the school
of Plato than to the uncertainties of Lucretius. I refer
to the Taoist school. Two names will only be briefly
considered, Lao Tzu and Huai Nan-tzů.
The problem of the universe pressed on these. Lao
Tzu found his solution in the postulate of an Eternally
Existing Spirit, the Originator and Sustainer of all
created things. He named it TAO.

word or words could really express the reality of the
universe in all its comprehensiveness. At best words
are only indicators. They are mere symbols, something
like X to express an unknown quantity. Words are
conventional terms to guide the mind to something.

They are signposts to guide the traveller along the way.
The word "God" that we use to signify the Supreme
Being is no more than a conventional term. We mean
very much more by its use than the original word
implied.
So the word "tao," in the beginning, meant no more
than "way," "path," "principle.' But the word grew.
Lao Tan gave it a new idea, an enlarged connotation.
He gave it a spiritual significance and a religious sense:
so we have "tao chiao," the cult or religion of the "tao."
Yet in his first essay of the Tao Te Ching, Lao Tan
warns the reader that the word "tao" cannot convey a
full meaning of the reality that he saw behind and in
all phenomena. He says, "The 'tao' that is expressed
is not the unchanging tao "." The word is no more than
a conventional sign. It is a gate that opens out on the
great mystery, the entrance to the most profound.
The translator meets with a fresh difficulty. How
shall the word "tao" be translated into English. Many
names have been tried. No word can be found to
correspond exactly. "Way," "Nature," "Principle," "the
Absolute" have all been used. Dr. Legge and others use
it untranslated, and speak of the Tao. Mr. Chaloner
Alabaster, years ago, speaking generally of how to unlock
the treasure-room of the Chinese mind, says that an
accomplished scholar told him one day, "It is no good
trying to get the meaning through a dictionary, you must
take each sentence and ponder over it and then, in time,
the light will burst upon you and you will apprehend the
teaching far more clearly than through any commentary."
And so in order to get the ideas that hang round the
word "tao" we must ponder what the original writers
wrote about it as they tried by exposition and illustration
to convey their thoughts regarding it. In the meantime
I venture to give it a new translation and call the "tao"
the Cosmic Spirit.
As these early sages pondered on the mystery of the
universe they became aware of something which they
called the "tao," the Cosmic Spirit. They said this some-
thing was fountainless, that is to say, it was self-existing
and uncreated. It was the author of all change, but itself
did not change. All things proceed from it, without any
diminution of its own power. It works unobtrusively,
but always effectively. It is the cause of all law and
order: nothing failed in its operation because everything
was sustained by it.

These early philosophers felt there was Something
existing before all created things; which was infinitely
above all and which will continue after all else passes away
-Something uncreated, self-existing, unchanging and
eternal. In Nature and man's attributes they saw a
manifestation of an Immanent, Indefinable Spirit of the
Universe, by virtue of which all things are what they
are, by which they exist, and by which they are co-
ordinated. All attributes imply an underlying substance.
All appearances imply an underlying reality. All that
which is to be implies an underlying of "that which is."
All passing phenomena imply a permanent entity.
The Cosmic Spirit is that which Is: it is the Mother
of all things. It is the great Unity, the harmonizer of
spirits and men and things. It is the great Mystery
enfolding everything: the great Illuminator enlightening
all: the great Truth from which all virtues and perfect
Wisdom springs.
It may be asked if they regarded this as personal.
If by "personal" is meant a conditioned being under limits
of time and space, then it is not personal Yet there can-
not be found any grounds for saying it is impersonal.
Chuang Tzu says, "The Tao cannot be a real Being.”
A passage from Huai Nan Tzu, which will be read
later on, says "fathers do not grieve for the untimely
deaths of their children, because of the care and protection
of the Tao." It would not be justifiable perhaps to
draw any conclusions from this as to the providential
forethought of the Tao. Nevertheless it should be
stated that the general impression left on the mind after
reading the descriptions of the Tao and its operations,
is that the Cosmic Spirit is not heartless.
In approaching the problem of cosmology these
ancient philosophers postulated the existence of a Cosmic
Spirit. It made the Universe a living whole. Unity is
one of their fundamental ideas. To get this unity they
regarded it as necessary to assume this cosmic spirit.
In this they are similar to Newton. He did not hesitate
to introduce God into his system of nature. To under-
stand nature it required the "concourse of God," so he
postulated it. For him the world is not intelligible
without God. His words are not unlike the words of
Huai Nan. He says: "He is omnipresent, not virtually
only, but also substantially; for virtue cannot subsist
without substance. In him are all things contained and
moved: yet neither affects the other: God suffers

nothing from the motion of bodies: bodies find no

But He acts in a manner not at all human, not at all

It is true of the theory of Huai Nan, as it is of
Newton, that it did not regard human beings as "mere
sightless lumps, blindly colliding and aggregating or
separating in space, but that the world is permeated by a
Creative Spirit and owes its existence and maintenance
to this fact. This makes the world intelligible, beautiful
and beneficent." (Boodin).
There is only one way, then, in which we can conceive
the cosmos as a going concern and that is by conceiving
it as, in some sense, dominated by life and mind. Modern
astronomers put it this way: "the universe is full of
thought" (Jeans).
Sir James Jeans says:-"To-day there is a wide
measure of agreement, which on the physical side of
science approaches almost to unanimity, that the stream
of knowledge is heading towards a non-mechanical
reality: the universe begins to look more like a great
thought than like a great machine. Mind no longer
appears as an accidental intruder into the realm of
matter: we are beginning to suspect that we ought
rather to hail it as the creator and the governor of the
realm of matter, not of course our individual minds, but
the mind in which the atoms out of which our individual
minds have grown, exist as thoughts." (The Mysterious
Universe, p. 148).
Han Fei-Tzu stated the doctrine thus: "All centres
in the Cosmic Spirit." Creation, life, beauty, law, order,
continuity, permanence emanate, in the first place, from
it. Because it is hsu wu, impalpable, and immaterial
it can do such mighty work as it does.
It should be said that in lecturing on this subject
Dr. Hu Shih is rather scornful of the whole idea. He
says the Tao as an entity is an unwarrantable assumption
and unworthy of serious consideration. It may be
questioned whether he was quite just in his criticism of
the view of the ancient philosophers. He is hostile to
any spiritual view of the universe. He fears that the

Let us now turn to hear what the old writer Huai
Nan says of the properties of this Cosmic Spirit.
"The Cosmic Spirit embraces Heaven and supports
Earth. It stretched the four quarters of the universe
and generated the eight points of the firmament. There
is no limit to its height, and its depth is unfathomable.
It constituted heaven and earth and endowed them with
the primary elements, when as yet they were without
form. Flowing like a fountain, bubbling like a spring,
impalpable, its energies bubbled forth in the void and
filled space. Continuing to effervesce, it transformed the
murky air of chaos into crystal clearness. Hence it
filled heaven and earth and stretched the uttermost parts
of the sea. Distributing itself without any exhaustion,
there was no morning or evening, nor rise and decay.
"Expanding, the Cosmic Spirit overspread every
part of the firmament, earth, time and space. Rolled
together, it was not a fistful: compressed it can expand:
abstruse, it can yet be clear: yielding, yet strong: soft,
yet firm. It is a macrocosmos as well as a microcosmos.
It holds, as in a net, the four poles and comprehends the
active and passive forces of creation. It links the
universe together, and makes the sky luminous. It is
most substantial and full of sap: most tenuous and fine:
so delicate is it, that it penetrates every pore and crevice.
It gives height to the mountain and depth to the abyss.
It fashioned beasts to walk and birds to fly. Sun and
moon are luminous by its power, and the planets revolve
in their courses because of it: the Chilin comes forth
through its energy, and the phoenix wheels in the
empyrean through its might.

Thus, then, in the view of this ancient writer the
present order of the Universe was formed and established
by the Cosmic Spirit. Matter and phenomena were
produced by the Positive and Negative forces which were
the agents of the Cosmic Spirit. All things were unified
and harmonised by the all penetrating influence of the
Spirit. It was the Transcendental and Immanent power
that created all things and was the guarantee of all order.
In all the innumerable fluxes and motions, there was no
failure and no disorder. The constellations kept to their
orbits and the seasons revolved with perfect regularity.
Men and animals received their endowments, each for
each, and all things had their appointments.

But what of exceptional talents, the brilliant endow-

be above the order of nature? How account for men of
genius and the genii? These gifted and extraordinary
beings, too, were the product of the Cosmic Spirit. These

a summary of this part of the essay.
In ancient times there were two great charioteers,
Pang I and Ta Ping. They rode on the clouds and flew
through the lambent air. They raced into the infinite
distance and to great heights. Earth was the chariot:
the four seasons the steeds and Yin and Yang the drivers.
They brought all under the sway of the Master Spirit.
Their exceptional powers came from the resident spirit.
There is much of this magniloquent language reminding
one of the descriptions of Apollo driving the chariot of
the sun.
I cannot help thinking it was a mistake for
these writers to introduce such magical powers in the
description of the Tao. This justified other writers in
adversely criticising the idea of the Tao, a criticism
quoted by Dr. Hu Shih, who agreed that the Taoist
conception of the Tao was not rational. Instead of
giving an enhanced value to it, as they intended, it robbed
it of all worth.
Fortunately, however, Huai Nan soon leaves the
mythical and returns to speak of the operations of the
Cosmic Spirit in a rational way. They say that great
rulers of men who are under the sway of the Cosmic
Spirit carry on their work perfectly. If the heart is free
from human accretions, then it is possible to have the
impress of the spirit and all their actions will be without
effort. In their work the Cosmic Spirit will be mirrored
as truly as the image in the water. But they have to be
careful. The impact of matter and the seductions of the
senses easily contaminate the heart. The incipient rise
of love and hate easily beguile men and then their actions
lose the impress of the Spirit and the sense of absolute
right is destroyed. Then arises human wisdom and the
art of policy and schemes. Therefore it is essential to
keep the pristine purity of the spirit. "Walk after the
spirit and not after the flesh," to use the words of the
apostle Paul.

Again it is the statesman working in line with the
Cosmic Spirit that can do great things. Politicians with
their little schemes and opinions and plans can do little.
They are like fishers with rod and line. On the contrary
the great man controlled by the Tao is like a net: his
catch is great. He can accomplish much with little effort.
He works in the way of the spirit and can stem wicked-
ness by his nobility of character. The opportunist, armed
only with his plan, tries to do so in vain.
An example is given of this theory. Emperor Kun
was an opportunist and to be safe he built a great wall
and filled his treasury with money. The people rebelled.
But Yü came: he had the spirit of the Tao. His
counsels prevailed. He obliterated all military signs and
distributed money. Harmony and peace were established.
Here, perhaps, is the proper place to say that the
Cosmic Spirit is against all militarism. Militarism
begets militarism. A fortified wall implies war chariots:
a coat of mail by one party leads to the sharpening of
swords by the other. To pour boiling water on boiling
water makes the whole more violent. "To be prepared
for war" is not the best guarantee of peace.
Further, the use of severe punishment in government
is hostile to the true idea of rule. To whip a restive
horse is the very way to make it vicious. There is an
art in dealing with all things. Be in harmony with the
Cosmic Spirit, and you will not fail in the true art of
government. This harmony will put one in touch with
the nature of things. Naturalness is the key-note of
success. Nothing should be done through the agitation
of the senses. Heaven is never agitated. The men who
are in alliance with the Cosmic Spirit follow its methods.
That method is absolutely reposeful and unostentatious.
An example of such a man who acted in unison
with the Cosmic Spirit is found in Shun, the farmer, who
became an illustrious emperor. In ancient time, Shun
for a whole year tilled the Land at Li Shan, and as a
result of his example, the tillers of the soil struggled for
every crooked and awkward corner, each anxious to
concede the fertile plot to his neighbour. He also fished
for a whole year on the river's bank, and as a result the
fishermen strove together for the currents and rapids,
each keen to concede the deep pools and quiet corners
to the other. During these times, Shun gave no lectures
on morality or on conduct but, maintaining the great
way in his heart, his influence sped as though it were

Thus we see that the silent Cosmic Spirit has a

nothing left undone. Nonchalantly he acts as though
no authority was exercised, yet there is nothing which

What is meant by "Wu Wei" is that there is no
going in advance of things. What is meant by "Wu pu
wei" is "there is nothing undone," that in following the
course of nature everything is done. What is meant by
"wu chih cho," "not governing," means that there is no
interference with the natural order. And the phrase
"there is nothing which is not governed" means that the
end is attained in correspondence with the mutual fitness
of things.
"Wu Wei" has a fundamental place in the theory
and requires some further explanation. Taken in its
literal sense of "not-doing" it does appear to be some-
what absurd. There is no place in life for people who
"do nothing." For the perfect man to be a man of
inaction is a bad example for others. To explain it in
this way gives ground for the criticism of Dr. Hu Shih,
who points out the futility of such a doctrine in society.
May it therefore not be possible that there is an explana-
tion of the term other than "non-action"? The general
impression left on the mind from the descriptions given
of those who act after the Wu Wei method is that they
had great energy and accomplished great things! In
their outward demeanour they seemed to be indifferent
to the affairs of life, but in reality that appearance belied
their powers.

If "Wu Wei" is interpretated in the sense the old
writers intended, there may be a very reasonable and
potent meaning in it. "Wu" is the negative "not." But
negative in the sense that the heart is free from all
contamination of the senses. There is no lust, no desire.
Action, to be correct, must be free from these sources
of bias. The heart is free from impurity and all that
springs from the earthly clod. Such freedom implies a
readiness and a capacity to receive the impress of the
Cosmic Spirit and to act in line with it and to move in all
actions under its direction. "Wu Wei" then is nonaction
as regards the impulse of the senses and human cleverness,
but in the positive sense it is action controlled by the Spirit.
In this way the term may be translated "spirit-action.'
In further explanation of this somewhat difficult
and perhaps misunderstood term of "Wu Wei" it may be
said to describe the method of action rather than inaction,
It may well be asked how they arrived at this significant
term. Possibly from observation of the motions of
nature. They saw energy everywhere, but energy that
worked without apparent effort, silent but irresistible.
"There is no sound; their voice is not heard." The great
examples were air and water. These are the most
yielding and penetrating things. Yet they are firm and
accomplish prodigious works. They give an indication
of how man should carry on his life. "Yieldingness,
tenderness, softness as of the downy feather." These
qualities are not much esteemed by the world: but they
are the triumphant qualities. "The meek shall inherit
the earth." These qualities are the endowments of the
Cosmic Spirit.
If these old writers had lived in modern times, the
discoveries of science would have afforded them even
greater examples of "Wu-Wei," than was possible for
them then. They would be, overwhelmed by the example
of Wu Wei, in the sense of a silent operation carried on,
afforded by the loss of weight in the sun. It is calculated
that the sun loses 360 thousand million tons of weight
every day by radiation, and this radiation, so far as
direct observation goes, is destined to journey on through
space until the end of time. "What is the cause of the
varied animation that we see around us in the world?
The answer is, in every case, energy. Light and heat are
forms of energy, and a million ergs of light-energy can be
transformed into a million ergs of heat with the utmost
ease." This in their view would be "Wu Wei."

The man who is under the guidance of the Cosmic
Spirit and who follows the Wu Wei method of action,
is a strong man and can do great things.
His nerves will be firm and strong. The sapient
ear and eye will have a comprehensive penetration and
not err.
Being resolute and firm he will never break
down. He does not overshoot the mark nor does he come
short of it in his actions. He will not be unhappy in a
lowly position: great duties he will not avoid. His soul
is not impetuous, his spirit is not unstable, neither is it
perturbed. Profound and undefiled, serene and repose-
ful, he is the autocrat of the universe.
The Great Way is broad and level, and not far away.
The seeker finds it is in himself: going outside for it he
must return within. He responds to the pressure of the
Cosmic Spirit: he answers its promptings: profoundly
recondite as it is: its changes are unspeakably fine. The
varying duties of life are discharged, everything is done
with deliberation and serenity: to every matter a suitable
solution is found, as fittingly as an echo answers the
sound, or as an image reflects the object. Whether in
a high or inferior position, the Tao-man never loses his
grasp of it: treading the way of danger, or walking
in the paths of peril, there is no forgetting of the Tao.
He who abides in this frame, is ensured against loss of
energy and virtue: he disposes successfully of the myriad
affairs of every description that crowd before him. In
attending to the affairs of empire, he is expeditious, like
(a boat) sailing with the wind. He is rapid and easy
in the discharge of business. This is the meaning of
Supreme Ability and with this supreme ability comes

There were men of old who lived in hermit caves
without losing their high spirit. Men (Kings) in later
times, exercising great power, had daily anxieties and
sorrows. We may gather from this that perfection does
not lie in ruling, so much as in getting the Tao: Joy
does not lie in riches and honours, but consists in the
possession of virtue and harmony. Knowing the great-

of empire is indeed to be near the Way.
