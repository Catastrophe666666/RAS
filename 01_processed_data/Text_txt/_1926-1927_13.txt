THE SIEGE OF SAIANFU
AND THE MURDER OF ACHMACH BAILO
(Two chapters of Marco Polo)
By THE REV. A. C. MOULE, M.A.
Marco Polo's story of the murder of Achmach Bailo
has long interested me, and in the Jami' ul-Tawarikh the
same story, of which Mr R. Levy has now most kindly
given me a full translation, is connected with the siege of
Saianfu. The first of these stories is one of the most
remarkable additions to Marco Polo's text in Ramusio's
edition, and the second, found in all texts, is one of the
most conspicuous instances of a statement in Marco Polo
which is mistaken if not deliberately false. In the follow-
ing pages the Persian, French (or Italian), and Chinese
accounts of the two events are given in what I hope are
fairly correct versions, though some phrases in the three
last named languages have puzzled me, in the hope that
readers and future editors of Marco Polo may be glad to
have them thus collected together. The comparison of the
different documents will show the extreme reliability of
them all as regards the main facts, and the divergence in
details which is possible even in contemporary records.
Marco Polo's book was written in 1298, the Persian in
1304, and the earliest of the Chinese texts not (in its
present form) until several years later.

The Amir reached the summer-quarters and presented
himself before the Qa'an with a black dish which he had
obtained and in which he had poured pearls of every kind.
On top of them he had placed a knife, and covered the
whole with a piece of red silk. When he presented it, the
Qa'an asked what it was and what it meant. He replied:
"When I, your servant, came into your service, my beard
was as black as this dish. In the course of that service it
has become as white as these pearls. Kau Finchan seeks
with the knife to stain my beard as red as this silken
cloth". He described also the circumstances of his jour-
ney, and the messengers who had been witnesses testified
to the truth of his statements.
The Qu'an gave orders that they were to go and seize
Kau Finchan, but he, knowing that report of the affair
had reached the palace, fled to the town of Sayan Fu which
is on the borders of Manzi on the banks of the Kara
Muran. Half of the town lies on one bank and half on the
other, and in times past one half used to pay tribute to the
kings of Cathay and the other to the kings of Manzi; but
at this period there was agreement between them. When
Cathay came into the possession of the Mongols, the king
of Manzi seized the whole of that town. There are on this
side a well-defenced fortress, a strong wall, and a deep
ditch; so that the Mongols never succeeded in taking it, in
spite of numerous attempts. When Kau Finchan went
there it had as its Amir a person of considerable note who
was warned of his approach and looked for his support.
And Kau Finchan too became a highly regarded Amir
here.
The Qa'an then gave orders to Bayan to set out in pur-
suit of him with an army. Before that time there was no big
Frankish mangonel in Cathay. From this kingdom Talib
the mangonel-maker from Ba'albek and Damascus, with
his sons Abu Bakr, Ibrahim, and Muhammad and his
assistants made ready seven great mangonels with the
purpose of reducing the city. Kau Finchan sent a secret
messenger to the commanders of the army to say that he
had committed no crime, but that there had been enmity
between him and the Amir Ahmad, that they had attacked
one another and that on this occasion he had fled to that
city through fear. If the Qa'an would spare his life he
would betray the city into their hands. He added that on
the city was based the whole of the empire of Manzi and
with its capture the whole of the empire would be subdued.

At the end of these nine years, during which Kau
Finchan had acted with malice and enmity, he again
attacked the Amir Ahmad, in the following circumstances.
A certain Cathayan, who claimed to be a man of
suffering and chastity and had made himself renowned in
the various camps for his asceticism and piety, one day
fell sick and sent disciples to the Amirs to say that he
was about to die and that he would be restored to life
again after forty days. They delivered their message
and the Amirs sent a committee of men to investigate.
Meanwhile he lay in his house as one dead and his sons
lamented and wailed over him, picturing him as one really
dead. After forty days he emerged, shouting out that he
had come to life again. The Cathayans gathered round
him and he gained great notoriety. Kau Finchan and his
friends came to Daidu and consulted the saint on the
question of contriving the downfall of the Amir Ahmad.
Since he (Ahmad) was a person of alert and wary disposi-
tion and constantly had men on guard with him and his
sleeping place was uncertain, the plan on which they
determined was to send a body of two thousand men to a

parasangs from Daidu, there to keep watch. A thousand
