PASSING OF THE R.A.S. OLD PREMISES
AT SHANGHAI
By ISAAC MASON
On Thursday, March 26th, 1931, the Council of the
Society met for the last time in the old Library, and
transacted business surrounded by empty shelves and
piles of boxes full of books ready for removal to tempor-
ary premises. The Building Committee made the pleasing
announcement that about Tls. 100,000 had been con-
tributed towards the new building, and operations would
commence as soon as possible.
99
On adjournment being made to the Lecture Hall, the
last lecture of the Society to be delivered in our historic
building was given by Dr. Coching Chu, on "Climatic
Changes during Historic Times in China.'
There was,
unfortunately, only a small attendance, the subject
possibly being too technical for some, while others trusted
to reading the Paper at their leisure when it appears in
the Journal. It is interesting to note that the very first
Paper read before the Society, in 1857, was on a kindred
subject, being "On Cyclones, or The Law of Storms."
One could hardly sit through this last lecture in the
old hall without some thought of the past, and imagination
conjured up some of the scenes of long ago, and the
stalwarts who have kept the Society going since the days
when the hall was first built, sixty years ago. There
was probably as much rejoicing in 1871, when "the
required Tls. 2,700 for this building were subscribed,
as there will be in 1932, when our new building, costing
over fifty times as much, is erected and paid for!
We would like at this time to recall the names of
some who have sat in these ample seats, or have given
lectures, or shared in discussions within the hall which
is now passing, after sixty years of honourable service.

Any attempt to describe those mentioned below, or even
give full titles, would expand this Note far beyond what
is intended; but the mere mention of the names will be
refreshing, will stimulate memories in our older members,
and help all present members of the Society to realize
what a fine heritage we have. The list is by no means
exhaustive, but outstanding names include those of
Alexander Wylie, Joseph Edkins, Thos. W. Kingsmill,
S. Wells Williams, Alexander Williamson, D. J. Mac-
gowan, W. A. P. Martin, Wm. Muirhead, Ernst Faber,
Emil V. Bretschneider, Henri Cordier, P. G. von
Mollendorff, G. Carter Stent, Robert Hart, Friedrich
Hirth, A. J. Little, Robert Little, Thomas Hanbury,
Byron Brenan, Pelham L. Warren, H. E. Hobson, John
Fryer, H. A. Giles, E. H. Parker, H. B. Morse, E. T.
Williams, Timothy Richard, A. P. Parker, Alexander
Hosie, F. S. A. Bourne, E. D. H. Fraser, Samuel Couling,
Arthur Stanley, Sidney Barton, J. C. Ferguson, Mrs. F.
Ayscough, and Mrs. S. Couling. To these and many
others the Society owes much, and we trust the new
building will see a worthy succession to carry on the
noble traditions of the North China Branch of the Royal
Asiatic Society.
